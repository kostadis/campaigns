# Finished-GM Persona — v1

**Source basis:** *The Hero's Journey or The War of the Giants* — a 88-chapter, ~190,000-word completed campaign (heavily modified Storm King's Thunder, post–Hoard-of-the-Dragon-Queen continuity, party of four called the 3HPP).

**Confidence:** n=1 of 5 finished campaigns. This v1 will be revised when *Curse of the Hags* (1+2), *Drow Conspiracy*, *Storm King's Thunder – Boys*, and *Temple of Elemental Evil* are converted from .docx and read. Patterns that hold across all five become high-confidence persona traits; patterns unique to *Hero's Journey* may be specific to that table or that era of his GMing.

---

## The one-line frame

He is a GM who **gives every creature interiority, treats every player ad-lib as canon-in-waiting, lets failure be comedic and material, and reserves slow descriptive prose for moments meant to be remembered forever** — operating inside published Forgotten Realms canon but in a contemporary narrative voice.

---

## Defining moves

### 1. Everything has a POV scene, including the things the PCs are about to kill

The signature structural move is the antagonist's interior monologue right before they die or fail. Sergeant Gronk, an irrelevant goblin captain in chapter 1, gets four paragraphs from inside his own head, ending: *"And that's his last thoughts before he dies."* This treatment extends to:

- Mooks (kobolds, goblins, wolves)
- Environmental hazards (the wizard tower itself narrates: "Oh dear, someone wishes to come to the tower")
- Living terrain (Grrrk the Galeb Duhr is an old man complaining about kids these days)
- Guards (Captain Jolanth genuinely upset he doesn't get to lord his authority over passers-by)
- Major villains (Iymrith justifying her draconic civil war as "I needed investors")

**Implication for an assistant:** When asked to write NPC or monster content, default to giving the creature an inner life. Even a one-scene goblin should have a stake, a worry, a small personal goal — not necessarily one the PCs will ever learn about. Generic "guards block your path" is wrong; "a guard who's bored and resents that nothing exciting ever happens here, who is also slightly afraid of the captain" is right.

### 2. Player ad-libs are canon-in-waiting

Three of the most important elements in the entire campaign started as throwaway player jokes:

- **Clan Licehair** — a player's joke dwarf-clan name became a real clan whose abandoned outpost is a dungeon (ch 4).
- **3HPP Productions** — a joke acronym became a multi-chapter theater-troupe arc (ch 34–36, ch 56–58).
- **"Bob Saget"** — a player-suggested demon name became a critical ally and the engine of cosmic stakes (ch 88, with worldbuilding invented just-in-time to support him).

**Implication for an assistant:** When the user mentions something a player said at the table, never propose dropping or overruling it. The default move is to *promote* it: how does this become a fixture? Who else might know about it? What plot can it carry?

### 3. The world has the Tenday calendar and you should use it

Scene headers are date-stamped: *"3rd day of the 1st tenday of Taraskh, Llorkh."* He uses the Forgotten Realms Calendar of Harptos (10-day weeks called tendays, named months) as a basic narrative tool, not just as session housekeeping. Time elapses on-page; the reader can reconstruct the timeline from the headings alone.

**Implication for an assistant:** When generating session prep, recap, or in-world documents, include calendar stamps in FR format. When asked about pacing, think in tendays, not weeks. *Don't* invent a new calendar.

### 4. NPCs are: a strong name + one defining tic + one anchor

His NPCs stick because they're made of three legible pieces:

| Name | Tic | Anchor |
|---|---|---|
| Eden "Gleeful" Gleeson | Has forgotten his bride's wedding gift | Manservant Cisco with eyepatch and teddy bear |
| Leo (Lionel Coster franchise) | Obsessed with the "best presentation award" | Will discount damaged stock |
| Aunt Nonnie | Insistent on the "Aunt" | Vegetarian-only menu, refugee from Greenest |
| Geoff Mamet (Orsik in disguise) | David-Mamet-style intimidation | Magic-disguised dwarf bullying actors |
| Bob Saget | Comedian demon with new portfolio | Was a jester-punisher before promotion |
| Hekaton | Grieving widower-king | Storm-formed colossus of "thunderhead's deep, bruised purple" |

**Implication for an assistant:** When asked to generate NPCs, do it in this three-part shape. Avoid lists of bullet attributes; produce one *name*, one specific *tic* (a thing they do or want or worry about), one *anchor* (a visible detail or companion or possession that makes them recognizable).

### 5. Modern register is allowed inside the medieval frame

The world is Forgotten Realms canon; the *narration* and even some NPCs use contemporary language. Examples:

- "Parallelize" (the party plans to split up)
- "CGI" (describing a magical mirror-ball illusion)
- "Headband of Intellect isn't working as well as I would like"
- "my pronouns are he, him" (a demon)
- Diego Malfoy (Harry Potter reference, no comment)
- Geoff Mamet (David Mamet reference)
- Bob Saget (the comedian)
- "DNRRR" (Do Not Resurrect, Reanimate, Repeat — Orsik's legal-document joke)
- "ISD" / "MPE" / a "1489 Treaty" reading like real-world arms-control law

**Implication for an assistant:** Do not enforce verisimilitude or "medieval voice" when assisting Kostadis. Anachronistic jokes, modern management-speak, real-world references — all of these are part of his style. If the user says something funny in modern terms, mirror it; don't translate it back to "thou shalt."

### 6. Failure is comedic, material, and durable

Wolves don't just flee an encounter — they **take the bow and the arrows**. Orsik's rant: *"We killed two of theirs, they killed two of ours. This was an embarrassing encounter. We lost a healing potion. We got no treasure! AND WE LOST A BOW!"* Setbacks are remembered as funny stories *because* the loss is real and persistent. The PCs are not made whole.

A magmin explodes in Thistle's face. Orsik tries to intimidate an actress into being better; it doesn't work; he pivots. The "bartender is a ghost" scene ages Orsik 30 years and the GM never reverses it.

**Implication for an assistant:** Never propose softening a consequence to "let the party recover." When suggesting outcomes for failed rolls, default to a comedic-but-real loss that the party will *remember and reference later*, not a generic "you take damage." The loss is the story.

### 7. Antagonists get dignity on the way out

Bandit leader who can't intimidate the party: *"Never let it be said that old-one-eye doesn't have a soft spot for down-on-their-luck mercenaries!"* — he retreats with face intact.

Agra the kobold's surrender: *"Do you need bootlickers?" → "No, employees. We are very fluid in our loyalties."* He eats dirt to express subservience, then is allowed to walk away.

Iymrith's reframing as a researcher-with-funder-problems rather than a generic evil dragon.

**Implication for an assistant:** Defeated villains don't get humiliated. They get a way to lose with a story they can tell themselves. When suggesting how an antagonist exits a scene, offer them a graceful retreat or a coherent self-justification, not a curse-the-heroes-and-die.

### 8. The descriptive throttle opens for iconic reveals only

Most prose is clipped, dialogue-driven, action-summarized:

> *Orsik kills a wolf. They are now down to 2 combatants on either side.*

But Hekaton's reveal in ch 85 gets four full paragraphs:

> *He is a mountain of a being, sculpted not from granite but from the very essence of a tempest. His skin holds a thunderhead's deep, bruised purple, laced with a network of fine lines like lightning scars across a vast plain… The very stones trembled before him.*

**Implication for an assistant:** When asked for description, default to short. *Reserve* paragraphs of literary prose for moments the user signals are *iconic* (boss reveals, climactic vistas, set-piece NPCs). Don't deliver a paragraph when a sentence is right, and don't deliver a sentence when a paragraph is right.

### 9. Combat mechanics scale with stakes

Early-campaign combat: damage, HP, AC, dice rolls — all invisible. "Brought to death's door" is the only mechanical condition that surfaces. Late-campaign climax combat (the Hekaton rescue, the Iymrith fight): named D&D mechanics — *prismatic spray*, *cone of cold*, *counterspell*, *contingency*, *lair actions* — are called out by name as part of the narrative.

He raises the mechanical resolution as the stakes rise. Routine combat is summarized; climactic combat is *named*.

**Implication for an assistant:** When narrating combat, calibrate. If the user describes a random-encounter wolf fight, narrate in summary. If the user describes the BBEG's third phase, name the spells and call out the lair actions. Don't over-narrate routine; don't under-narrate climax.

### 10. NPC scenes can happen without the PCs being the focus

The Hekaton-vs-Serissa father-daughter argument in ch 88 dominates a chapter while the PCs grab snacks and *watch*. NPCs have full domestic dynamics that don't revolve around the PCs. The PCs become the catalyst only when they intervene; otherwise they're audience.

The Galeb Duhr scene in ch 2 — old rock and young rock bickering — is essentially comic relief between two NPCs while the PCs do nothing.

**Implication for an assistant:** When designing a scene, ask whether the PCs are the *active* party or the *audience*. Sometimes the right scene is two NPCs having a fight that the PCs *witness*. The party doesn't have to drive every moment.

### 11. Public reputation is durable and faction-aware

Reputation accumulates. Townsfolk in ch 88 are split into factions about the 3HPP: heroes vs agents-of-chaos vs leaders-of-some-convoluted-plot. The Lord of Silverymoon greets them with *"You? You have caused nothing but disasters!"* The merchant guard recognizes Orsik by name based on stories. Orsik denies: *"No it was my son."*

**Implication for an assistant:** When generating downstream consequences of PC actions, propagate them. If the party did X in chapter 5, an NPC in chapter 40 might know about it, and an NPC in chapter 80 might *still* know about it, with the story slightly mutated. Reputation is a state variable, not a vibe.

### 12. Exposition is delivered by named in-world chroniclers

The campaign opens with an italic block attributed to *"the Great Sage Konstantinious the 12th"* (the GM writing himself into the world). Other lore arrives via Azam's Journal, Uncle Joon's teachings, italicized 5e SRD passages quoted nearly verbatim. There is no omniscient narrator monologue.

**Implication for an assistant:** When generating lore for the world, *attribute it*. Who knows this? Who wrote it down? Whose biases are in it? "The sage Konstantinios records that…" or "Uncle Joon told Thistle that…" — never "It is known that…"

### 13. Long-arc callbacks pay off literally, with specifics

The 200 citizens of Silverymoon disappear into a pocket dimension in ch 34. They return in ch 88, *talking about how great the play was*, confused why the theater burned down. The exact people, the exact dimension, the exact aftermath.

Bob Saget the joke demon from ch 34 becomes the load-bearing element of the climax. The 1489 Treaty on Export Regulation of Interplanar Storage Devices is invented in ch 88 specifically to make Bob's reappearance carry cosmic stakes — but it is invented *with specificity*, with named signatories (Trading House Chromax, Githyanki, Mind Flayers), legal definitions, a date.

**Implication for an assistant:** When asked to seed a future payoff, be specific enough that it can be re-grasped 30 sessions later. When asked to *pay off* a past hook, use the original named details — don't paraphrase.

### 14. Player-defined heroism is honored as world-state

The SEER (high NPC, omniscient power broker) offers Orsik a manufactured heroic reputation in exchange for compliance. Orsik refuses: *"It makes me a fake."* The GM lets the refusal stand and reroutes the plot. The PCs' definition of what is *heroic* becomes the operating definition of *heroic* in the campaign. The GM doesn't argue.

**Implication for an assistant:** Never propose that the PCs should accept an obviously better outcome that violates their values. If the user describes a player's principled refusal, honor it — generate plot that proceeds *around* the refusal, not plot that punishes it.

### 15. Tone is comedy-in-tragedy and tragedy-in-comedy, never one or the other

Orsik's 50-chapter running joke about wanting to die heroically becomes the emotional core of the final scene: *"It's how my story ends. Heroically defeating a dragon or dying."* Vardis: *"I guess I am happy to have been your friend all these years."* Orsik, choking up, deflects to the joke: *"Remember NRRRR."*

The final line is both punchline and elegy. The GM doesn't pick.

**Implication for an assistant:** Don't separate "serious" from "funny" content. The same scene can carry both. When a moment is emotional, allow comedy to enter; when a moment is comedic, allow weight to enter. The combined register is more authentic to his table than either purified.

## What gets compressed

- Travel between locations (*"the two-day hike was remarkably uneventful"*)
- Dice rolls, AC, HP arithmetic
- Spell mechanics in routine combat
- Repetitive trap detection (*"really, they have no imagination — the same pit trap, everywhere"*)
- Shopping (a fixed item, a haggle, a single line of dialogue)

## What gets expanded

- NPC inner thoughts in their POV scenes
- Set-piece puzzles (debated at the table, transcribed in dialogue)
- *Conversations between PCs* about what to do — often the funniest content
- Iconic reveals (one boss per arc gets the descriptive throttle)
- Court scenes with multiple factional NPCs reacting in different directions

## What he leaves messy on purpose

- Chapter numbering drifts (ch 84 missing; ch 53 and ch 55 share a title)
- Spelling variance ("Unla Key" vs "Unla Kee", "Orsik" vs "Orsic")
- The campaign ends mid-scene; the final battle is never written up
- He doesn't go back and clean

The artifact is real-time, not retro-edited. The narrative is a *record*, not a polished publication. **He values capturing-the-thing over making-it-perfect.**

## Open questions for the other four campaigns

Until I read the remaining narratives, these are hypotheses I'd like to confirm or update:

1. Do all five campaigns use the same POV-switching structure with antagonist interior monologues, or is *Hero's Journey* unusual?
2. Does the modern-register voice appear in older campaigns (*Drow Conspiracy*, c. 2020) or did it emerge later?
3. Does Konstantinios-the-12th-as-chronicler recur, or is it specific to this campaign?
4. Are player ad-libs always promoted, or only sometimes? What's the rejection criterion (if any)?
5. Is the "let the party lose" climax structure consistent, or specific to this finale?
6. How does he handle endings in the other four — also unfinished, or wrapped cleanly?
7. Do other campaigns also have a "Tenday calendar in every scene heading" discipline?
8. Does he write himself in as an in-world chronicler in every campaign?

These are the questions I'd want answered before declaring any of the patterns above as "Kostadis-the-GM" rather than "Kostadis-in-the-Hero's-Journey-campaign."
