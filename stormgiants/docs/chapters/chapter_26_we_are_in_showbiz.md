# Chapter 26 - We are in showbiz!
## Orsik
Orsik stares at Darz Helgar, and the ex-con knows he will have to spill the beans.
A thieves guild in Waterdeep has to be a highly organized and secretive group, with a strict hierarchy, with the bosses giving orders that the rest of us have to obey. Any guild worth that survived for any period of time would rely on informants inside the city guard and connections into the local borough to stay one step ahead of the law. 
My guild was the Coffin Nail, and it was a well-oiled machine run by a ruthless leader who commanded a legion of skilled thieves and killers. They had a code, strict and unforgiving, and those who dared to cross them paid the price. Make no mistake, the Coffin Nail was a force to be reckoned with, and those who underestimated them did so at their own peril.
But that was ten years ago when I got caught, and since then, I have lost contact with my guild. And I am not even sure they exist. We had a lot of enemies, and a guild doesn’t survive long. 
Orsik is dissatisfied, “where could I find the trace of this guild?”
Darz takes in the dwarf, thinks, and then says, “I would go to Skullport.” 
At this point, Darz makes a hand signal that marks him as a member of the Zhentarim. Orsik reciprocates. 
“You’re a member of the Black Network? The Black Network in Waterdeep is a bit of a mess. There are two factions there. They say one is the real black network, and the other is led by Manshoon.”
He pauses, waiting for Orsik to recognize the name.
“Manshoon was killed by Fzoul but cloned himself. And the story goes that all the clones were killed. But I’ll tell you that I know Manshoon is alive and well. But then again, what do I know? And it’s been ten years.” 
Orsik, whose intelligence was recently augmented, recalls a story about a spell caster that could create copies of himself that he stashed around as a form of immortality. At the time, the story made no sense, but now he realizes the story was about cloning. And for all he knows, it was about Manshoon. 
Orsik turns to Thistle, “we promised him to keep quiet about his role in this crime. And we promised to deal with Kaelen Sarssir. We don’t have to keep quiet, but we did promise.”
“I’m here for the ride,” says the fairy. 
Orsik, Vardis, Thistle, and Unla head to Talking Troll, determined to discover more about the thespian’s plans. Kaelen Sarssir has dreams of putting on a plane in an off-high-road production in Waterdeep. 
## Thistle
“Let me see if I follow this. Darz is a city of Waterdeep criminal who was blackmailed by Kaelen to steal from the Lionshield Coster.”
“Yes.” 
“And Darz told us about Kaelen because he wants us to get some kind of revenge on Kaelen.” 
“Yes.” 
“And Darz doesn’t care if we rat him out.” 
“Yes.” 
“So then to the Talking Troll.” 
And Thistle, once again, picks up a bucket and fills it with water before he arrives at the inn. 
When he enters the Inn, Kaelen shouts, - “he’s already in debt. Drank the money you gave him; now he owes even more.”
“That’s uncle Joon.” 
Thistle sighs and dumps the bucket on his uncle. 
As Thistle stares at his drunk uncle in the other corner of the room, Vardis and Orsik talk to the drunken priest of Helm. 
The cleric raises his head, smiles, and asks - “Did you find the dead body?”
After Vardis nods, the priest mumbles, “Helm spoke truly to me.”
Orsik adds - “the mayor has possession of the body right now. We’re waiting for a cleric who can cast speak with the dead. You’re a cleric?”
“I don’t like talking to the dead.” Then the cleric starts to cry, “I used to live in Neverwinter. I had a real life there. I got to meet people, have a lively social life, and then I got into one theological argument with the high priest. And then I am called into a meeting, and before you know it, I am banished to the Allfaiths Shrine in Red Larch. I was a victim of temple politics!” 
Thistle turns back to his Uncle and mutters, “what have you done, Uncle?” 
Kaelen shouts, “are you here to cover his debt? He said you were good for it.” 
“No. He can stay in debt. In fact, spending time in a debtor’s prison might be a good thing for him.” 
The bartender grumbles, “will you have something to drink, or is this some kind of social call.” He then pauses, “unless you’re willing to review my script? Maybe you might be willing to do a few scenes with me?” 
Thistle is about to object when he realizes Orsik has a plan. The suddenly genius-level Orsik turns to the bartender with a malevolent smile and asks, “That sounds like a fantastic idea. But do you have a back room where we could act out?” 
Kaelen smiles. Thistle thinks, “not what I would be doing right now.” And when Kaelen leads them down to the cellar, the fairy is convinced that this will not end well for the thespian blackmailer. 
The cellar underneath the tavern is dark, dusty, and cramped. The ceiling is low, and the air is thick with the musty smell of old wood and mold. Cobwebs are draped over everything, and the dim light from a few flickering candles casts eerie shadows on the walls. The various shapes and sizes of the bottles are stacked haphazardly on shelves and in crates, covered in a thick layer of dust. Some bottles are caked in cobwebs, and others are empty and discarded. The whole scene feels of neglect and decay as if the cellar had been abandoned for many years. 
Orsik examines the collection and turns to Kaelen, “how did you afford this?”
“The former owner was desperate and sold it to me cheap. His name was, I know this sounds like a joke, Ha-Ha Pinkerton, a short man with bright, cheery cheeks, always smiling. He disappeared after selling it, and I ended up with this place. I was just as surprised as you were to find out how well-stocked the cellar was. Honestly, I didn’t know much about him, and I thought the place was overpriced given the required roof repairs.”
Kaelen pulls out the script and then describes the scene they are to act out. It’s a moment of high tension between a rogue, and his victim, the owner of the cellar, who is, in reality, a vampire. The Rogue doesn’t know that he, not the owner is the real prey. 
After his unfortunate acting performance earlier that day, Thistle defers to Unla Kee, who performs brilliantly as the vampire. 
Kaelen is delighted and is about to propose they try another scene when Orisk interrupts - “we’re looking for a special key, a key we are told you might know about, a key that belongs to the Lionshield Coster.” 
Kaelen turns pale, and despite his poor acting skills, Orisk immediately knows that his denials are all lies. 
Thistle smiles, the poor actor is about to have a new scene for his play. 
“Look, I am an actor, and the keys are worth a lot of money to the right buyer. And I just wanted to sell them to turn this bar into some kind of theater that people would come from all over to see.”
He sits down, “ever since I saw that play The Inn of Shadows off Highroad, I have wanted to be on that stage. And I thought I would get my big break if I put on a play here, where famous people from Waterdeep come.” 
Thistle turns to Orsik, “we could just take the key. But there is value in having the owner of a dive bar in our pocket.” 
Orsik surprises Thistle with a better plan, “what if we had an actor who had a theater house that the rich and powerful supported, and we were the people who discovered him?” 
Thistle has no interest in social prestige in Waterdeep, “we could sell the key? I mean, it’s worth over 5000 gold to the right buyer.” 
The old Orsik would have gone for that, but this one thinks through the problem and says, “this looks like the kind of thing that the Lionshield Coster would hire bounty hunters for. I mean, I would take that job.” He pauses, turns to Kaelen, and says, “I want the key and a bottle of Champagne du le Stomp.” 
“That’s my prized bottle.”
“That’s the price of harassing my friend.”
“Your friend?”
“Yes, and we want to be investors in your dream.” Orsik's investment is more of a loan-sharking arrangement, but Kaelen is so pleased with the idea of investors that he misses the malevolence in Orsik’s eyes.
He walks around the cellar, “I’ll be able to fix the roof, a special effects crew of magic users, not ALM, mind you, but still.”
He then pauses, “that will be at least 5000 gold!” 
Thistle whistles, “are you trying to con us? That’s outrageous.” 
Orsik steps forward, “how about 500 as an initial investment?”
Kaelen is disappointed. “Very well, I suppose I could put you on the leadership circle plaque. The founder’s circle is reserved for investments over 2000 gold.” 
Vardis is about to object, but Orsik has plans. “That’s fine. But when are we going to get a return?” 
“Well, it will take about a month for me to fix the roof and put on a show.” 
“And how many people will be at the show? You have to do marketing.”
“I have to hire actors and bouncers and put on a beautiful production. We will make money from the tickets and from the cellar! See a show, and drink some of the finest wine!”
“And what’s our cut?”
Kaelen suddenly loses his far-off dreamy look and turns ice cold. And Orsik realizes how this actor could have blackmailed a criminal. 
“We’re your partners. It’s an investment. This is not a charity. And for that cut, we’ll keep quiet about your role in the lost key.”
Kaelen stares.
“We’ll just say we found it under a rock or it fell off a wagon.”
Kaelen gulps.
“I think that’s worth a 70/30 split.”
“Of the profits.”
But Orsik has worked with actors and knows that no production ever has a profit “of the gross.” 
And with that deal, the companions head out to Lion’s Share. 
The owners, Alaestra and Narth, are delighted to see their key. Alaestra, in particular, looks relieved. Sensing Orsik is trying to renegotiate their deal, they don’t budge. Orsik gets the sense that as scary as he is, their employers are scarier. 
With their 15% discount, the companions buy healing potions and new weapons. Thistle splurges on some boots of elvenkind that he has to wait for until tomorrow. Apparently, they need to get new inventory, and for that, they need the keys. 
The companions head out to Urgala Meltimer’s Northshield House Inn as the sun sets. 
Urgala is curiously well-informed about what had happened in the Sheriff’s office. When asked why, she says, “rooms are not as private as people think in a small town.” She cries for a moment and then says, “I suppose that affair was never going to end well.”
She stops. She wipes her tears away, smiles, and says, “you brought some measure of peace to me. For that, you can stay one night for free with breakfast in my inn.”
But Thistle  can’t resist pushing his luck, “and your eternal gratitude?” 
And then Urgala flashes the fearsome temperament that made her a deadly warrior, “You’re a sweet talker fairy. But I was an adventurer for a long time, and I have dealt with your kind before. One night, a free dinner and breakfast, and say yes before I change my mind.”
