# Chapter 55 A deal is made, and the Ravens cry Nevermore. 
## 1st day of the 2nd tenday of Eleasis at Raven’s Rock 
Bressik watches the Dragon’s Breath, the airship of the cult whose commander is Delsephine, approach Raven’s rock.

The area is a geographical anomaly. Regardless of the time of year, it is always covered in snow. It’s unclear what the cause is. And his reading has revealed nothing. 

It is a mystery, and there are so many in the north. 

But he has a paid gig as a scholar, which is way better than being a deckhand, so he pulls out his map of the area and tells the 3HPP of his research.

He coughs, takes a swig of some brew, and begins - 

“On the icy slopes of the Spine of the World stands the spirit mound of the Black Raven and Gray Wolf tribes. Raven Rock gets its name from a 100-foot-tall stone carved in the likeness of a perching raven, sitting in the center of the highest plateau of the mound and facing west. The Black Ravens leave carcasses by the stone raven’s feet as offerings. At dusk, giant ravens gather to feast on the remains. After devouring the carrion, the giant ravens perch on timber roosts and squawk at one another until nightfall, when they fly off. Warriors of the Black Raven tribe sometimes ride these giant ravens into battle.

Surrounding Raven Rock are four 50-foot-tall menhirs that the Uthgardt shamans use to track the changing of the seasons and the movement of the stars.

The Gray Wolves aren’t allowed atop the high plateau. Instead, they gather around an altar mound in the heart of a wolf-shaped depression southwest of Raven Rock. Their altar is a rectangular slab of stone with the phases of the moon carved into its sides.”

 Orsik, irritated at all the digging at Morgur’s Mound, declares, “To the altar.” 

Bressik is surprised, “Yes, but what about Raven’s Rock?” 

Orsik cuts him off, “The altar is where it is. It’s always at the altar. We aren’t looking anywhere else.” 

Bressik splutters but remembers Orsik's blade and temper and is silent. 

Unla Kee flies to the altar with the wings of a pegasus. The comical effect of the 30’ wingspan attached to a 3’ tall, 1’ wide halfling never ceases to amuse Delsephine. 

Orsik and Vardis climb down the rope ladder and begin the long trudge to the altar. 

Thistle flies around, scouting with Thuul.

Bressik, who found the relationship between the two fascinating, spent the evening chatting with Thistle. He discovered that the Ranger’s relationship with his drake was more of a partnership. Thistle described it as anarcho-socialist and said they take turns being in charge. 

“Fascinating,” he mutters. 

While the cleric, the fighter, and the mage dig, Thistle notes some ravens and approaches them. 

They are these giant creatures flying, just out of range, and yet just in range. 

“Have you seen any Uthgardian Barbarians?” 

The darkest of the ravens craws. “Who are the barbarians, the people who respect their ancestors or the group tearing up a religious site to steal an artifact?” 

Thistle pauses, “Well, I mean to say the nomadic peoples that are not residential to a specific location, and the term is…” 

He realizes he has just opened his mouth and firmly planted his wingtip into his mouth. 

“Craw! Craw! Craw!” the murder of giant ravens goes. 

“You mean the people who respect the Raven’s Rock? Those who treat it’s residents with care?” says the other Raven. 

“Yes?” asks a meek Thistle. 

“Craw! Craw! Craw!” go the ravens. 

“Who knows? It could be today, it could be tomorrow, it could be five from now,” says another raven.”

Thistle says, “Well if you would be so kind, do not tell them who took this artifact.” 

“Craw! Craw! Craw!” go the ravens.

“Nevermore!” says the first raven that spoke. 

Thistle is alarmed. He doesn’t know what that means and can’t remember what Uncle Joon said. 

He flies to Vardis and Orsik and shouts, “When will you guys be done?” 

A wet, snow-covered, steaming Orsik shouts, “We found it!” 

“Then we need to get out of here ASAP. I think we may have incoming.”

Vardis and Orsik hoist the thigh bone on their shoulds and march back. 

Thistle and Unla Kee talk to Delsephine, who agrees to bring the Dragon’s Breath closer to where the rest of the group is. She fears the Ravens may not view her as an innocent bystander. 

Delspehine points her airship towards Great Worm Cavern. 
## 2nd day of the 2nd tenday of Eleasis The Great Wyrm Cavern
As they approach the Great Wyrm Cavern, flying over the world's spine, Bressik ponders how to make his last presentation memorable. 

Somehow, the conversation turns to the fact that Bressik and Delsephine have a contract that Klamath the Red Dragon has in his hoard. 

“He’s very supportive of your plans.” 

Orsik is surprised, “He’s supportive?” 

“Oh, yes. Dragons view the Giants as an obstacle to their eventual domination over the North. They are uniquely capable of surviving in similar terrains and territories that dragons are.” 

Orsik can’t help but poke Thistle, “You okay with us working with Dragons?” 

“Yes,” says the fairy. “I am not opposed to all dragons. I am opposed to the Cult of the Dragon.” 

Bressik interjects, “And as I clarified earlier, we were not members of Severin’s faction that misread Samaster’s text on Dracoliches. Tiamat or another Devil manipulated Severin to create a summoning ritual for Tiamat. That would have prolonged the eternal conflict between the dragons. We believed in the original Dracolich vision of creating a new undead race of dragons that would transcend the petty differences of the different groups. The war of the Dragons was a coup by Severin and Tiamat to subvert our original plans.” 

At this point, Thistle is annoyed: “This is all academic nonsense. It was about power-hungry individuals.” 

Bressik is offended, “I beg to differ. It was about a contrasting view of the natural order. We believed that the undead Dracolich represents a unifying force between the dragons and death and that unification will lead to peace and prosperity.” 

Unla Kee is intrigued, “Why is it Dracolich and not Lich?” 

“Excellent question. My linguistic research suggests it was done to emphasize the different nature of the ritual and the draconic aspect of the undead creature.” 

Unla Kee looks at him, bored. 

Bressik returns to the initially planned presentation, realizing he's losing his audience. 

So he stops, pulls out his final map and begins his narration.


“The spirit mound of the Great Worm tribe is inside a spacious cavern at the northeast end of the Valley of Khedrun, deep in the Spine of the World. Thick ice hangs above the pillared entrance to Great Worm Cavern, and the interior walls of the place are covered with an icy glaze and lined with massive icicles that gleam and glitter in reflected light. The walls are too slippery to be climbed without gear or magic.”

Delsephine interrupts, “We do have such equipment for sale.” 

Orsik mutters, “We’ll take two sets.” 

The captain pockets the 40 gold into her purse and crampons and other equipment materialize from under the hold. 

Bressik continues. 

“The cavern floor is a sheet of slippery ice flanked by 70-foot-high ledges of rock, atop which stand two totem poles of chiseled ice with carvings that resemble winged snakes. The walls of these ledges are riddled with caves in which the Great Worm tribe members live. Natural steam vents in the rock keep the caves warm throughout the year. Above the ledges, dug into the cavern walls, are narrow crypts where the honored dead are placed, their frozen corpses propped up in a standing position.”

Bressik pauses for a minute and wonders if he should continue helping with this assault on ancient artifacts and this destruction of ancient tribal norms. He then remembers what he was paid for and his audience and continues. 

Rising from the back of the cavern is a triangular promontory of ice-covered rock, 120 feet above the cavern floor at its peak. Thereupon stands an altar of frost-covered stone, carved in the shape of a coiled serpent with great wings. The altar represents Elrem, the Great Worm. Here, the tribal warriors make sacrifices to the Great Worm in honor of Uthgar. Doing so, they believe, ensures that Elrem doesn’t return to devour them.

Orsik asks, “What can you tell me about the gong?” 

“Two possibilities. One is that it is the artifact itself. The gong a circular disk 13 feet in diameter, was once the shield of a frost giant champion, but it was broken in combat and is now missing a large piece. What remains of it weighs 250 pounds. The shield is made of red dragon scales bolted to a beaten copper frame. Its leather arm straps are long gone. The ropes holding up the gong can be cut with two swings of a sword.”

Bressik pauses, “Given that all other artifacts were under the altars, I don’t give much credence to that theory.” He then assumes his professorial tone and says, 

“The second theory says that the gong is a gong. It’s decorative and serves no purpose but as part of some local ritual.” 

Orsik nods in agreement, “To the altar.” The dwarf turns to his friend, “We should get the gong. We have a bong and a gong, which would be a great addition. And we could give it to Harshnag if it’s a shield.”

Bressik takes his notes and descends into the lower decks of the Dragon’s Breath.

Orsik walks up to Delsephine with a sly smile, “Bressik is a very capable man of artistic and literary pretensions, and I was wondering if we could hire him into our show.” 

The captain looks at him, her eyes blazing, her face covered in her dark mask, “He has a contract. But there is a payout clause.” 

Delsephine chuckles, “No one reads the fine print. See, Klamath is a necromancer. And teh contract is for two lives, a living and an undead one. So we assume ten gold a year, and he’s 30 years old, that means we get to 300 gold for the living part. And for the undead part, we assume another hundred years, so we get to 13 000 gold pieces.” 

She pauses, “Klamath is willing to negotiate, and I am happy to negotiate for him.” 

“So what would happen if he died?” 

“His value would decline clearly, but remember the undead part.”

“What if his body was desecrated?”

“Well, we can use the parts in our necromantic constructs.” 

“Can we buy the living part?” 

“Certainly. But you must understand buying the living part releases him from his obligations to Klamath. The obligations don’t transfer to you. He is free at that point to do whatever he wants.” 

Orsik is intrigued, “I have got to meet this dragon. Where is he?” 

Delsephine laughs, “I won’t tell you. But I can promise that he will talk to and reward you once you have dealt with the giants. Now, do we have a deal?”

Unla Kee says, “We need to structure this contract differently than just a straight-up cash deal. How about we give you residuals in the play we are running? So, a residual stipulates how much money you will get from future earnings. These can be bounded or unbounded.” 

Delsephine thinks, “Very well. But since residuals are inherently risky, I will demand an upfront payment of 500 gold, and then the residuals will be capped at 6000.” 

Unla Kee shouts, “Done.” 

And then he turns to Orsik, “Of course, she doesn’t know that I signed a deal for the current play. Our deal will not burden the sequel with her residuals.”

Orsik looks at him admiringly, “You can do that?”

“It’s called showbusiness.” 

Thistle wonders, “Do we want to piss off a Red Dragon?” 

Orsik laughs, “We won’t. What will be funny is that we’ll have to tell Bob to wind down the play so we can start a new one. We have two gongs, and we’ll call it the Gong Show.”

Later that day, they finally arrive at the Great Wyrm Cavern. 

As they enter the cave, they notice members of the Great Wyrm tribe getting ready to defend their totems.

Unla Kee and Vardis exchange a glance and incinerate them all with fireballs. 

They smile smugly. The defenders are dead. The altar is theirs. 

Walking closer, they hear a rumbling sound—the ground trembles. A creature bursts from a snowbank, a careening, blue blur of death. Something akin to a mingling of worms, centipedes, and dragons, the beast moves in a bewildering serpentine pattern, its dozens of legs hurtling it forward at a speed that belies its immense mass. Wing-like membranes grow along the beast's sides, unfurling, probing for prey or obstructions in the path. With a screech, the worm rises up on its many legs, mandibles clicking in ravenous anticipation.
