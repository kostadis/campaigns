# Chapter 73 - Orsik is stunned
Orsik is stunned, which for him has been a rare occurrence ever since he became super-intelligent. Being stunned happens when you can’t anticipate the other person’s actions. Now, he realizes that you can be stunned in a completely different way. Where the stupidity of someone’s actions stuns you.  
But in this case, he reminds himself, it’s neither. Really, what’s going on is that this spectral Raven has stunned him. And all he can do is ask himself, “Where did it go wrong?” 
He reflects that his decision to become a fighter wasn’t a decision but more a natural outcome of the physical traits he was blessed with. He was strong, he was obstinate, and he wasn’t particularly clever. 
Now that he is intelligent, he sees the limitations of his chosen profession. His job is to go into the breach, smash, kill, and be targeted while other people do the clever stuff. 
“Woot! I killed the creature with my sword by standing next to it! Woot! I am so cool.” 
Orsik remembers when he derived joy from being the most challenging, brutal, and dangerous monster in any room. He couldn’t outthink you, but he could beat your face into a pulp, and when it was beaten, you couldn’t think straight. 
He now understands the meaning of the phrase, “Drag them down to your level and beat them with experience.” 
While Orsik reflects on the nature of his existence, Thistle flits about and kills a specter with his arrows. 
Poor Unla Kee, who is also stunned, is unconcerned, even when a figure detaches itself from the shadows and glides toward him—weightless, formless, undead. Even when he feels the negative energy of the creature sap his life force as the undead creature flows through him. From the corner of his eye, Unla Kee sees that Orsik is also being targeted by this variation of the specter. And then watches as the creature flows through more of the 3HPP. 
And then the Raven emits a soul-chilling cry inducing despair. Thistle, although ordinarily resistant to such evil, isn’t. The cry reminds him of his lost love. Of the despair he has felt over the years looking for her. 
Although terrified and with trembling hands, he manages to strike down the Raven with his bow. 
And the fear, that intense feeling of despair, is gone. At the same time, Thistle redoubles his efforts to find his lost love. 
Orsik notices the flash of a fireball, and the searing hot flames jolt his consciousness free. And he stands around, ready to do what fighters do.
Except they are all dead. 
And the rest of the group is just staring at him smugly as if they are trying to tell him that he’s not really needed. 

Orsik knows better.
With the undead gone, the 3HPP looks around. 
“This was the kitchen,” remarks Unla Kee. 
Thistle notices a note. 
“A recipe?” asks Vaardis.
“No, it’s a bit of a rant. Apparently, Sandesyl was accused of betrayal and treachery. I don’t think that Vampires can create specters…” 
That font of all things arcane, Unla Kee, inserts casually, “A specter is created when the soul of a creature is ripped from it while they still live.” 
“That sounds like what Sandesyl would do.” 
Thistle turns pale as he realizes that Sandesyl isn’t a dilettante vampire obsessed with Romance Novels – “She’s a disciple of Sammaster.” 
Everyone turns to him, “Sammaster was chosen by Mystara and the person who originally figured out how to make Dracoliches. Uncle Joon told me about how some members of the Cult of the Dragon believed that Dragons were just the perfect form of undead but that there had to be other magical processes for creating undead and that there were members of the cult who pursued that magical research.” 
Unla Kee mutters, “Not part of the end game, so not interesting.” 
“What end-game?” 
“THE END GAME.” 
Meanwhile, Vardis finds a magical gem that he hands to the wizard. 
“A gem of elemental summoning,” says Unla Kee as he pockets the item. 
“So we going to take this Sandesyl character down?” asks Vaardis. 
“Not now,” announces Unla Kee.
“What?” 
“Although she isn’t part of the end game prophecy, I have an intuition about bad plans, and this one is screaming bright red – bad idea. So no.” 
Orsik is pleased because he was stunned when Vardis asked Unla Kee. “That would be the kind of stunning thing that happens when you hear something stupid,” he says to himself. 
And just then, Unla Kee rushes from the Kitchen to the unlit barracks. 
And again, Orsik is stunned. 
“From the unlit Kitchen to the Unlit Barracks…” 
“Look, we have money, and we can always get a raise dead spell,” observes Vardis. And then thinks, “Unless you happen to be a cleric banned from his temples. But that’s what money is for.” 
Unla Kee returns sheepishly to the Kitchen, realizing he had already explored the barracks earlier. Overhearing the conversation, he says, “And it’s not like we must go to the ‘good’ clerics.” 
“But will they raise or animate you?” asks Orsik. 
“Great question,” asks Thistle, “I guess you find out after the fact?” 
To the north of the barracks are a series of rooms. One contains some dead stone giants that were obviously the cooks for the giants. 
Another contains food that has since rotten, but Orsik finds a bottle of Blackstaff wine. He can’t resist sharing his recently acquired fine wine expertise, “Blackstaff is a type of wine from Waterdeep. It has a big, bright, and unbalanced flavor. It was said to perfectly capture the spirit of the City of Splendors. The wine was exported to the Western Heartlands, the lands along the River Chionthar, and the city of Baldur’s Gate as of the late 15th century, Dale Reckoning. Explains why we would have found this bottle here.” 
Across the hall from the pantry is a room containing half of the dragon’s remains, wearing the distinctive purple garb of a Wyrmspeaker, the inner circle of the Cult of the Dragon. From the creature’s garb, it’s clear that this was the Black Dragon Wyrmspeaker. 
Thistle remembers stories about this Wyrmspeaker. “She’s Rezmir. Rumor has it that she was in charge of this castle and responsible for moving the hoard that was part of the ritual. The rumors have it that she was killed by the heroes of the War of the Dragons. Others speculated she ran afoul of internal politics.” 
Just then, Orsik finds a piece of paper that bolsters the theory of internal disputes, “Look at this …” 

The note he finds writes – “The idea that I need to be audited beggars’ belief. My operations are sound, and my commitment to the cause cannot be doubted. The auditors who infiltrated the Black Dragon Hatchery were spies. And that our troops would think I needed to be investigated reflects some of our mercenaries’ lack of faith in the cause. The five heads of Tiamat do not attack each other and do not distrust each other. They are one and five at once. Let us not forget that. Any auditing of any operation going forward must involve all of us or be considered a trick by our enemies to sow dissension or disrupt our plans.” 
Orsik scours the room for treasure but finds nothing. 
“So now what?” asks Vardis. 
“I’ve been thinking. This glacier has a lot of ice that is not part of the caves that the Dracolich uses. Sandesyl must have carved her lair into the ice. It explains how she could take control of the castle and shows up wherever she wants.”
He then turns to Unla Kee, “Meanwhile, the mage is right. We need to rest up. Sandesyl isn’t going to let us raid her lair and retreat. We will have to get rid of the Ogre Zombies. But once we do that, then she’s really going to be pissed off.” 
With that, the group arrives back in the castle’s control tower. 
The crystals and runes spin and twist and fly into the shape of a female cloud giant’s head. The mouth of the head, formed from runes, gems, and lights, moves and forms words, although the sounds seem to come from everywhere. 
“I’ve been working on my avatar. Not quite what I want,” declares Escarlotta. She then turns to the 3HPP – “Did you remove the undead infestation?” 
And before they can answer, she remarks, “You do realize that the undead were the products of Sandesyl’s necromantic experiments?” 
At this point, Blagothkus jumps in – “I just want to make it clear that this isn’t my fault.” 
Escarlotta sighs, “Not your fault? We discussed This precisely about taking accountability for your actions and decisions.” 
“After the War of the Dragons, my husband became obsessed with the role of giants. I was crashed into a glacier in Mkoz. My worthless husband could not get the help of the Frost Giant Jarl to free me. The heroes of the War of the Dragons were able to free me. For a while, I was under the control of the Frost Giants, who used me to defeat the dragons at the final climactic battle in the well of the worlds above the summoned temple of Tiamat. The poor fools thought Sandesyl had fled the castle and it was theirs to command. She had not. Instead, once the cult was destroyed, she took time to transform the new occupants into undead. You met the Jarl, I believe. He was a skeletal giant you destroyed?”
She pauses, “Sandesyl discovered she could not control the castle or me. And so she agreed to find my husband, who was stuck on the Mkoz glacier. I assumed that the fool would get over his ludicrous megalomania. Still, instead… Seeing the failures of the Dragons and convinced that he was part of some kind of Giant renaissance, he visited the Oracle. There, the All-Father explained that he was a failure and that our son was the leader the giants needed, and all our son had to do was kill his Father. Our son failed, and in my grief, we ended up stranded where you found us. Blagothkus implored that we move, and I refused. My castle was infested with undead, and my son was dead.” Then dramatically, the runes reform into a floating fist with a finger pointing at the Cloud Giant, “and my husband was a fool.” 
At this point, Thistle is tempted to ask why Escarlotta is obsessed with destroying the Cloud Giant Countess.

“And to top it all off, Countess Sansuri was making a play for the Ordning! Imagine that classless poseur of a magic-wielding cloud giant as the QUEEN of all giants! THIS IS ALL HIS FAULT!”
“He made a deal with the devil,” observes Unla Kee.
“No, a Vampire. Tiamat was the Devil. Severin was the one who made the deal with the Devil.” 
Thistle feels awkward: “It’s not like you want a devil running a play.” 
And Orsik adds, “Sometimes folks make deals with devils and don’t even realize they are making deals with devils. They don’t understand every clause in the contract.” 
Thistle continues, “They audition for the part, and you don’t know.”
“People have to be forgiven for these kinds of things,” says Orsik soothingly. 
The runes stop moving and return to the configuration of the control panel. 
Escarlotta mutters, “Forgiveness is hard. But you are right.” And again, the runes form a fist with a finger, “Are you going to do anything other than sit on your fat ass?” 
Blagothkus stares, “Stand?” 
The couple appears to be in a standoff again when Orsik intervenes and explains to Escarlotta that he’s learned that sometimes, direct action is necessary to deal with the mistakes of the past. Just watching and praying is not enough. 
She agrees and informs the 3HPP that Blagothkus will help them defeat Sandesyl.
