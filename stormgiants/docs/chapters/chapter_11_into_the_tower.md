# Chapter 11 - Into the Tower

The group stands on the island in what was Mama Mia’s bedroom. 
Looking around, they figure out from the crenelated battlements that this must have been the roof of the Wizard’s tower. A tower that has sunk into the swamp.
Taking a closer look at Mama Mia’s bed, they realize it’s a stone table. And across the stone table in the northeast corner is a statue. 
Wallace walks up to the statue. He looks to Orsik for guidance. Orsik waves his hand, and the young lad starts to clean it.
Pretty soon, it’s obviously a gargoyle, and this time, just a statue. 
The young lad from Parnast discovers that it has an arm that can be rotated.
Unla, who always feels lucky, steps forward and moves the arm.
The table tries to move. Thistle examines the stone and realizes Mama Mia’s pendant can be used as a key. 
Unla pulls again, and the table opens, revealing a staircase going down. 
Wallace is excited, “My first tower,” and runs down. 
The stairs end at a landing with a poem and symbols 
*Be not faithless
Magic and Knowledge divine
Not in power or sphere presented
But in names Ascending*
And the symbols are 
Thistle and Unla quickly determine that they must be put in order and that the symbols represent various gods of the realms. The human hand, finger pointing upward, is Azuth. The single-lit candle is Deneir. And Oghma is a simple blank scroll. 
Unfortunately, Vardis doesn’t recognize the star of Mystara, a god of wizards. Neither does Unla Kee, but then again, he has a dim view of the gods as he knows that he is why the world continues. 
Regardless, the failure to recognize Mystara causes an old trap to spring, and a boulder collides with our heroes in front of the wall. 
After gathering themselves up, Thistle takes on the task of guessing again because he can fly away from a boulder. 
But, as they say in the Fey, he picked a dandelion, and the door opened. 
And with that, the doors open, revealing a room 30 feet deep and 25 - feet wide. Archways to the left and right provide a view into the rooms beyond. Immediately facing the entrance, a prismatic-hued will-o’-wisp hovers over a short pillar. Atop the pillar rests an offering bowl, and behind it, a fresco depicting a blue, seven-pointed star. The walls are covered in murals depicting a woman performing feats of magical prowess. 
At this point, the group figures out that this must be Mystara. The others give some sideways glances at Vardis and Unla, but they disregard them. 
Vardis approaches the offering bowl, sees a tourmaline, and doesn’t steal it. Unla leaves it, explaining he’s a gambler, not a thief. 
The group heads through the north passageway. As they walk, they take notice of their surroundings. The areas are well lit with globes of spun glass that glow with bright, magical light. The floors are stone, bear a thick layer of dust, and seem to have formed mounds. The air they breathe is chilly and, oddly, stale and dry for a castle in a swamp. 
Thistle notices that there are no insects or spiders, “weird.” 
The archway north leads to a pillared hall bathed in a dancing cascading rainbow light. 
Thistle examines the arches and then looks up and concludes this place was once open to the elements, but earth and mud have filled the openings. 
He sighs. And then takes in the peculiar light sources. They are walls of scintillating light anchored by pillars in the center of the room. 
And they block his view of the entire room. He’s about to go down the hall when he notices a pair of altars. 
Vardis, Thistle, Unla Kee, and Orsik examine the first altar made of jasper with an offering bowl that resembles a polished geode. 
Vardis reads an engraving, “Grumbar’s oath unchanging and true. Earth remains strong, protecting all.” 
The Fey remembers an old story from his uncle and says, “He’s the boss of elementals. His symbol represents stability and resistance to change and is the guardian of secret treasures. He is said to be fond of offerings of sardonyx and jasper, and often statues of him are made of those with rubies as the eyes.” 
Everyone stares at Thistle.
“My uncle traveled a lot and told me many stories.” 

Thistle shouts, “okay, let’s put some dirt on the altar!” 
Vardis suggests they use their mud mephit. 
And Orsik turns to Wallace, “back away. They are planning to make an offering of dirt. We need to be out of the blast radius.” 
As Thistle drops the dirt in the geode, he mutters, “it is a god of the earth.” 
And suddenly, he feels a blessing course through his bones that makes him resistant to being hurt by the earth. 
Thistle explains that to his colleagues. Orsik hits the ground and thinks, “I guess it’s only the people making an offering.” 
Unla seeing what happened, steps forward to the next altar. It resembles a wave crashing and appears made from quartz crystal with varying shades of green and blue with flecks of white resembling crashing surf. A deep valley between the waves serves as the altar’s offering bowl. 
He looks for an engraving and finds one – 
*Istishia’s triumph to confirm
Water rushes out to cover all*
Without hesitation, he drops some water and knows that water can not harm him. 
Vardis walks past the shimmering walls to the next altar, determined to get the blessing. The altar is shaped like a 4-foot-high tornado; the eye of the funnel cloud serves as the offering bowl. Streaks of lightning that are carved into the stone clouds are engraved with the words:

*Akadi’s healing kiss once more to feel
Air bursts free in no one’s thrall*
The cleric turns to Thistle, who once again remembers his lessons about the Gods of Faerun. 
“Istishia *(Is-TISH-ee-ah) *is more abstract than some of the better-known deities of water, such as Umberlee, Eldath, and Valkur. He represents water, but not any specific formation or body of water. He furnishes a crucial resource but apparently cares not how it is used. He prizes offerings of water-hued fine fabrics that ripple like waves, gemstones that echo the color of water, fine inks or dyes, or books or papers on which are written unsolvable mathematical problems.”
Prompted by Thistle, Vardis burns incense and feels a surge of power. He is blessed too. 

Orsik looks at him and smirks, “isn’t your willingness to offer incense to other gods why you were exiled?” 
“Pragmatic.” 
Orsik pushes everyone aside and mumbles, “I gotta get me some resistance.”
He steps in front of the altar and examines it. The altar is a 4-foot high flame made of crystals of varying shades of red and yellow. A brazier before the altar serves as the altar’s offering bowl. Engraved are the words:

*Kossuth’s renewing purification performed
Fire erupts, dancing death for all*
Vardis wanting to help his friend offers a lit candle. 
Thistle flies out of the room, muttering, “I don’t want to be here for the explosion.” 

But the candle and torch they burn seem to have no effect.
Unla stares at the inscription and says, “maybe I should set myself on fire and put myself in the offering.” 
“Sounds risky,” says Vardis. 
Unla considers it for a moment and decides against it. 
After burning some rope, casting produce flame also fails to produce an effect. Orsik is tempted to smash the altar but reconsiders.
Seeing nothing else to be done, Thistle leads the group through a door to the south. It is a guest room. As he takes in the sights,  he notices the walls are paneled in wood. The room has three large piles of rocks with statues scattered about. The center of the room has a layer of fine gravel. There is a door in the wall opposite. The eyes of the figures open, and a sound like grinding rocks comes forth from the open mouth of the largest statue.
 Unla casts comprehend languages and can translate the grinding words – “What is your business here?” 
Orsik realizes that they can’t speak, so he makes a hand gesture. 
The gargoyle smiles, “Yes, I agree with you. His arrogance bound us to protect his tower.” 
Orsik tries another hand gesture, and the gargoyle stares back. 
On the other hand, Wallace is excited to go through his first secret door and marches into one in the southeast part of the room. 
It’s not an actual secret door, as the floor has a clear sign of wear from the door and the creatures going to and from it. 
The secret door leads to another secret door. 
“Leave it for later,” shouts Orsik.
Orsik then turns to the door going south and tries to open it. 
When he fails, he gestures to the gargoyle, saying, “well, just go through the wall.” 
Orsik stares and realizes that the gargoyle did not need a key to this door. 
Thistle can find and remove a trap, but the door is too tightly locked to be opened without the tools.
Everyone stares at Orsik, who mutters, “I am offended that you think I would have thieves’ tools.” 
So the group heads back the other way through the hall with the shimmering walls and past the shrine of Mystara. 
Thistle once again is in the lead. As he passes the archway, he sees a fresco depicting a human hand, index finger pointed up. A bearded, old human male statue stands on the southern wall, holding a topaz-capped staff lovingly in both hands.
Orsik recognizes him as Azuth and is not entirely happy with this god’s unwillingness to part with his blessings. 
He notices a staff one and half times Azuth’s height topped by a sizeable cabochon-cut topaz.
Vardis shouts, “I strayed from my God’s teachings, but I am not looting.” 
Thistle once again takes the lead into the next room. The walls of what the Fey assumes is a shrine are covered in all types of scripts and glyphs, some from languages long dead. The statue is of a balding man clad in voluminous robes, covered in glyphs, standing beside a desk bearing a quill and a beautiful ink pot. His arms cradle several scrolls.
“Deneir,” shouts Thistle.
“Your uncle?” 
“No, I recognized the symbol from earlier.” 
Vardis once again announces, “no looting of places of worship.” 
And as everyone leaves the room, a distinct sound of something is heard behind them.
Vardis turns and sees a scroll tube. He opens it, and it is a scroll of tongues. 
“I’ll take that,” says Unla. 
Just then, Orsik and Wallace enter the next room. The walls of this room show various ways of preserving knowledge, including carving stone tablets, preparing paper, and binding books. Also shown are people of different races and genders creating knowledge by experimentation or spreading knowledge.

Orsik mumbles, “book people.” He scans for a statue but finds a desk with a blank, open tome, an inkstand with a quill, and a large diamond paperweight.
“Great, just when it’s my turn, I don’t get a statue but a desk. Who needs a desk?” Orsik thinks. 
As he is about to take the diamond paperweight, a voice is heard – “All should surrender knowledge so it may be shared.”
And then the quill rises up next to the book. 
Vardis, who is outside, shouts, “They want us to write some knowledge in the book.” 
Wallace writes – “My name is Wallace.” 
Orsik can’t resist and writes – “Dwarves are awesome.” Although he does wonder if that constitutes knowledge as it is a well-known and established fact. 
Vardis writes – “berries are good.” 
Thistle writes – “spring’s first flower!” 
Unla steps forward and writes for some time. When queried, he says, “I know how the world is going to end.” 
Everyone tries to read his writing, but it is written in a cipher only known to Unla Kee. The cipher is called “Bad Hand Writing.” 
A voice suddenly shouts, “The gift of knowledge freely given is blessed. And yes, Vardis, I do enjoy a good berry. And Unla Kee, that’s not how the world ends.” 
Orsik snickers, “You just destroyed this guy’s whole raison d’etre!” 
“I didn’t say it was all wrong. Unla just got some details wrong. On the,” and the voice whispers something to Unla Kee alone. 
Vardis picks up the scroll that materializes, smiles, and says – “lesser restoration.” 
And with that, the group heads down the stairs through the secret door in the gargoyle room. 

Wallace takes the position of honor and goes down first. He arrives at a landing and a moldy door. He pushes the door open. 
He looks around the open area with a few chairs and shelves that may have had some valuable objects. Still, now the only thing that remains is rusted pieces of metal. Wallace touches one, and it turns to dust. 
Everyone piles in and heads to a scintillating wall of shades of orange and red. The wall radiates heat.
Unla notices an inscription – “Sing praises or be tested.” He clears his throat and is about to sing some old show tunes he loved in his youth. 
Thistle stops him and sings a Fey song of love. 
Orsik scowls, “out of tune screeching. Dwarven music is what we want. That’s not going to work.” 
The ranger is irritated and marches through and is burned. 
Orsik smirks.
Thistle glares and then looks around. He is in a 20’ room that has a brass brazier in each corner and a circle of arcane symbols inscribed in the center of the room. 

On either side of the walls of the passageway he entered are shelves. Thistle examines them and sees some fabrics that he touches. Much like the rusted metal, these too turn to dust. 
The eastern wall is pierced by a wall of white and grey light that smells of incense and ozone and occasionally flashes silver with lightning. 
He looks around for an inscription and finds one – “*Songs of our ally may be treated gently.*”
Unla suggests instead of singing just complimenting, “perhaps the singing is a metaphor?” 
Thistle dismisses that thought. He sees the swirling effects and thinks, “water.” And sings the inscription for the water goddess Istishia from her altar. 
He steps through the wall and is zapped. 
Thistle swallows some berries.
Orsik turns to Vardis, “I really hope there is a way to disable the traps.” 
Vardis says, “Yeah, otherwise we must go through all those damaging walls.” 

Thistle shouts, “maybe the folks who are just watching may want to look for some useful information?” 
Wallace takes a cue and starts looking, and finds an inscription. He turns to Orsik and whispers the information. Orsik shouts, “Look what I found!” He points to a note in the far wall – 

Vardis shouts, “I got it! Gods of Fire and Air are allies as the gods of Earth and Water. The gods of Water and Fire are enemies, as are the gods of Earth and Air.” 
Thistle can get through the walls without getting hurt by singing the fitting praises. Wallace, Orsik, Unla, and Vardis also follow, careful to repeat the songs Thistle got right. After getting through the remaining two rooms and their scintillating walls, they arrive in a room covered in mold. 
Unla Kee notices a foot-long piece of ivory wood. It draws his attention because it is the only thing that has not crumbled into dust. He picks it up and smiles, “a wand of webs! I knew it was magical!” 
Vardis shouts, “we’ll decide which spell caster can use it later!” 
Thistle, who is also a spell caster, agrees. 
Orsik looks at the door and is about to open the door when Thistle says, “wait, let me check for traps.” 
“none.” 
Thistle grumbles because he is sure there would have been a trap. This is such a prominent place to put one. “Probably thought invaders would be dead by now.” 
Unla Kee quickly looks inside the room and says, “a laboratory.” He points to the workbenches and supplies that are, again, too old to be helpful. He then notices the tables are made of stone, “Ah, what a good design choice, and very expensive, furniture made of stone to resist explosions.” 
Not all is destroyed, a set of jewelers’ tools are still in good shape, and he pockets them. 
But the prize, in Unla Kee’s eye, is the spellbook. He rushes to the table and grumbles about the height. He reaches over and grabs all three books. He feels the magic of glyphs being triggered and thinks, “oops.” 
The door closes, is locked, and two magmin appear. 
While that was happening, Wallace found several gems and coins totaling 200 GP. 
The last thing Unla Kee thinks about is – “so who is Bad Fuul? Why does the SEER think there may be books in an ancient tower to help them? And there is no way the SEER is getting this spell book.”
