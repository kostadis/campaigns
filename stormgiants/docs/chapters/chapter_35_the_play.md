# Chapter 35 - The Play
## 5th day of the 2nd tenday of Flamerule 1492

Thistle is annoyed. Not as much as Orsik, mind you. Orsik wonders how his brilliant plan to run a play for profit is turning into a potential disaster. 

As he stands across the road from The Troll Inn, he looks at the theater. 

“Looks nice,” mutters the dwarf. 

Thistle groans, “Apparently, he bought the property?” 

“Bought? Why did he buy the property? Couldn’t he have rented it? We are so going to need to take over this production.” 

Urgala, who had told them about the bad news, looks at Orsik and says, “Let me get this straight: you made a deal with an out-of-work actor to put on a play and trusted him?” 

Orsik taps his Headband of Intellect, “This headband isn’t working as well as I would like.” 

However, for a moment, he wonders if the problem is that he’s been listening to people with worse ideas.

Urgala interrupts his deep thoughts, “The good news is that the play exists, and many people are here. At least Kaelen delivered the play. The buzz you created and the fame of your exploits have made this opening the event of the season.  If you guys can get this play to have a second night, you’ll make the money you need to make. If not, the Zhentarim and Hand of Yartar will get their pound of flesh.” 

She pauses and mutters, “The problem is the dialogue and story are a mess, the actors can’t act, and the set is a disaster.” 

Orsik asks, “Can we delay the opening?” 

Urgala laughs heartily. And then she says, “No.” 

Realizing that they will have to parallelize, Unla Kee will take on the script, Thistle will take on the direction, Orsik will do the acting, and Vaardis will collect the CGI. 

## Unla Kee

Unla Kee marches up to Kaelen, who is pleasantly surprised to see him. 

Kaelen smiles, “What an excellent script! I am so pleased!” 

The diviner looks at him, shakes his head, and says in an authoritative voice, “I have seen the future, and the future says this is going to be a disaster.” 

Kaelen’s confidence on a good day is poor, and this dismissive tone breaks him. He whimpers, “It’s that bad?” 

“Yes.” 

Kaelen grabs the script and rushes into his chambers in the The Talking Troll. A few hours later, he showed up with a new script. 

“Better,” mutters Unla Kee. As the mage reads it, he realizes that Kaelen has decided to use an archaic form of the common tongue that nobody would understand. He turns to the actor/producer/playwright and says, “I am very impressed with the use of this dialect. It’s very authentic, and nobody will understand a word the actors are saying.” 

“But it’s authentic! I was going for authenticity,” he argues. 

Unla Kee looks at Kaelen, and a stroke of genius hits him, “The trick isn’t to speak archaic common. Using just enough words creates the illusion of speaking archaic commonly. So instead of saying ‘You,’ we’ll say ‘Thou’ and ‘Thee.’” 

“BRILLIANT!!!” 

A few hours later, a cleaned-up script appears in Unla Kee’s hands, and he finally begins to see why the play is a disaster. The protagonist's motivations are wrong, and his interactions with the other characters are unmotivated and garbage. The play makes no sense. 

Unla Kee realizes the entire play can’t be rewritten; he must devise a trick to make this work. And then it hits him: “How about a play within a play about a bad play?” 

Kaelen stares at him; Unla Kee creates an awful outer play about the actual play. So, the audience is watching a play about a dreadful play. 

“Brilliant!” Kaelen is ecstatic. “This way, with a few lines of dialogue, this will be perfect, and we will protect the existing investment.”

Unla Kee then mutters, “The ending is terrible.” 

The wizard can’t make heads or tails out of it. Kaelen seems touchy about the bizarre, incomprehensible ending, as if it matters more to him than the entire play. Unla Kee realizes that he will have to awe the playwright with his intellect, so he manipulates fate to figure this out and help him brainstorm what he needs. And it works. 

Kaelen is pleased. 

Unla Kee is relieved. The play is no longer a mishmash of every bad avant-garde idea from off-High Road and has coherence. However, the story itself seems oddly elusive. 

## Thistle 

An unimposing, heavy-set man wearing a multi-colored shirt and a purple scarf over his head, barely hiding a buzz cut and his enormous ears introduces himself, “You are one of the investors! I am Everard Barney and completely committed to Kaelen's vision.” 

Thistle looks at the director and says, “Me too.” 

The director then starts to sob frantically.  “Kaelin, he creeps me out. And I can’t deal with the main actors! I give them directions, but they don’t listen. My entire family are bookkeepers. And I decided to branch out. And if this fails, I will have to return to Waterdeep and be an accountant.” 

Everard is now sobbing uncontrollably. 

Thistle realizes that Everard cannot understand his actor’s motivations, “Have you talked to your actors and asked them what they want?” 

“No?” 

Thistle sighs. He walks around the set, absorbing everyone’s body language and how they are talking. The fairy realizes that the lead actor, Amy, likes to pretend that he’s all fluffy and joyful, but she is status-driven and cares about what everyone thinks. Whenever anyone tries to get him to commit to something or make a definitive statement, it’s always “I don’t know, and I am not sure.” It’s almost as if he is hiding some mysterious past. 

Thistle tells Everard, “I am going to talk to Amy.” 

“How is talking to the actor going to help?” 

Thistle rolls his eyes, “For this lead actor, you must pander to their sense of being a star.” 

Everard listens and mutters, “So that scene where he gets on stage and says this soliloquy.“ He pauses thoughtfully, “I shouldn’t have her sitting on a pile of dung.”

“Correct.” 

“But I am trying to contrast between the heroic message and the crap we have to deal with on a day-to-day basis,” he whines pathetically. 

“What if we had him standing on a hill? And the dung is below him? So there is some separation?”

“Right, so they don’t make an association that I think Amy is shit.” 

“EXACTLY!”

“And that Amy elevates us from the shit?” 

Thistle nods. 

Everard is super excited at this point. “Since you seem to have such insight, perhaps you can help me with these other actors. They aren’t very good, and they keep forgetting their lines.” 

“Why?” 

“Because they are drunk.” 

“What if we make the fact they are drunk part of the play? That way every time they get a line wrong, it’s not a mistake, but part of the play?” 

Everard is tempted and then shakes his head. “Nah. What I am going to do is scream at them louder in the morning.” 

Thistle, who has dealt with Uncle Joon’s alcohol binges for years, agrees, “It will help with their hangovers. But instead of screaming for my drunk uncle, I just pour buckets of water. It works brilliantly, just like yelling.” 

Everard smiles, “My uncle screams at the drunk apprentices. I’ll have to tell him about the water trick.” 

They both smile. 

As the day winds down, Everard sheepishly approaches Thistle, “Any ideas on the finale?” 

Thistle stares at the incomprehensible staging and direction, hand gestures. He feels like something is wrong, but he has no insight. And then it hits him. This ending works great and says, “Change nothing.” 

## Orsik

While Thistle tries to fix the direction, Orsik concludes that the actors are not athletic enough. There is too little emphasis on physicality as if they treat this running race like a stroll. Their lack of energy is hurting the play, sapping its vitality. 

Thistle has told Orsik about Amy’s needs. Orsik considers being nice but mutters, “Terror is always the better option.” 

Casting a spell to disguise himself as a human acting coach, he marches up to Amy. 

“Who are you?” 

Orsik looks at her, “I am here to get your acting back on track. The name is Geoff Mamet.”

“What? I’ve never heard of you. Did Kaelin sign off on this? Is this under budget? Or am I paying this out of my pocket?” 

Geoff-Orsik growls, “You are not here to ask questions.” 

Amy deflates, “You’re the boss. Whatever you want…” 

She then describes how she wanted to do the main lead. Kaelin has been pushing back on his ideas, but she is a very timid, milquetoast kind of person who can’t push their ideas.

Geoff-Orsik realizes that he’s going to have to give her some spine. Unfortunately, his shout, “GROW UP AND STOP ACTING LIKE A WEAKLING!” doesn’t have the desired effect. 

She sighs, “I’ll just do what Kaelin wants.” 

Orsik hopes that Unla Kee and Thistle have fixed the script and direction enough to compensate for her acting. 

Then she starts explaining that she doesn’t like this scene, whining about how it doesn’t work and wanting it done differently. 

Geoff-Orsik stares at her and realizes her problem is a lack of energetic obedience to the director, not the director's direction or the script. 

She then explains that at the end of the play, she’s confused because the lines are complicated—“And I have to deliver these weird lines, and I have to make these over-the-top gestures and...” 

Geoff-Orsik has had enough and just shouts, “ENOUGH! You aren’t the director. You aren’t the screenwriter. Just deliver the lines, put some energy into it, and you’ll be fine.” 

She stares at him and meekly assents.

As Geoff-Orisk watches the rest of the rehearsal, his decision to focus on the energy level of the acting pays off: “What a brilliant idea I had!” 

He walks out of the theater, dispels the illusion, and walks back in. 

Amy sees him and shouts, “Oh, Mr. Orsik. How wonderful that you were willing to pay for Geoff Mamet to come and give me pointers. He’s made all the difference. Would you like to see it?” 

Orsik nods. 

## Vardis

Vardis walks to Urgala’s Inn and asks, “So tell me about this magical device?” 

She smiles, “Chancellor Geoffrey Invitations is what you need.  Chancellor Geoffrey would use the device to create these elaborate invites with sound and animated visuals.” 

“Wouldn’t an illusionist work just as well?” 

“Perhaps, but this won’t cost you anything.” 

“And where is it?”

She shows him a location south of Yartar in the Dessarin Hills - “A tribe of Kobolds has it. They don’t know what it is. They pilfered it off of one of Geoffrey’s messengers. And poor Geoffrey has been looking for it ever since.” 

“How do you know about this?” 

She smiles, “I wasn’t always an innkeeper.” 

A few hours later, Vardis is standing at the entrance of the Kobold Warrens. He pulls out a scroll of disguise and transforms himself into one of the creatures. 

Kobold-Vardis walks up to the entrance and, as he does, notices that there are dozens of Kobold footprints leading to a small three-foot cave. 

The cave entrance leads into a warren of cave tunnels illuminated by giant fire beetles. 

He stares at the ground, “Let’s see where these footprints go.” 

Eventually, the tunnels open into a cave, and another kobold is therein. The creature speaks to Kobold-Vardis, who looks like a Kobold but doesn’t speak the language. 

Kobold-Vardis shrugs, which confuses the other creature. It turns and runs deeper into the caves. The cleric follows. 

The tunnel opens up into a chamber with a few dogs. Kobold-Vardis thinks, “Pets?” 

He moves deeper into the cave complex and arrives at an enormous chamber with a 20-foot-high ceiling. Kobold-sized tables and chairs are scattered throughout the southwestern part of the room. On its northeastern side, a one-foot-high altar stands before a 15-foot-high monolith made of rusting iron.

“Let’s take a look at the altar.” 

The altar has an image of the Kobold god Grumsh strangling Tiamat. As he stares at the carving, he sees that Grumsh is a new addition to the monolith. 

“Focus on the mission.” 

He peers down the northeastern passageway from where he thinks the Kobold originated. 

The tunnel curves away from the cave with the monolith and then opens up into another cave. This one has tripwires stretching from one cavern's end to the other. 

Kobold-Vardis thinks to himself, “I could go back. But that would mean sacrificing our entire fortune. And I am not losing that money.” 

And he fails.

A bell rings, and a few Kobold guards appear. They are surprised to see one of their numbers caught up in the tripwires. 

Kobold-Vardis tries to imitate a confused and unintelligent Kobold who has gotten hopelessly lost. 

And pulls it off. 
One of the Kobold guards starts yammering at the other. Although Kobold-Vardis doesn’t understand anything he’s saying, he gets the intent - that Kobold-Vardis wasted his time and that the Kobold guard will have to spend all day cleaning up this mess. 

Kobold-Vardis successfully deceives the guard with a look of shame. 

The Kobold guard frees him, pushing him deeper into the complex. 

After stumbling ahead, Kobold-Vardis enters what looks like a large cavern, but then he realizes he’s outside. The sky above is starry. The moon is visible. The ground below is that of any valley he’s seen in this part of the north.

Then he realizes it’s all an illusion—an illusion projected by a ball with tiny, mirrored shards across its surface. 

“Gotcha.” 

And then he notices a large ogre sitting beside the ball with mirrors. 

Upon seeing him advance, Kobold-Vardis walks up to the ogre, who shouts something in Kobold. Kobold-Vardis continues to walk up to him. The ogre gets annoyed and starts yelling and waving his club. Kobold-Vardis stops before the ogre, bows, and then grabs the ball. 

The ogre is stunned and swings his club at what, to him, is an impertinent Kobold that needs to have its skull smashed.

Except this Kobold is anything but. Vardis ditches his disguise and uses his warding flare to stun the Ogre. Then Ogre blinded, misses. 

And Vardis starts to run. 

His first stop is the cavern with the tripwires. As he enters, he wonders how he will get through that. He stops just before the first trap and smiles. The Kobold Guard who freed him disables the entire mechanism. Vardis smiles and dashes past him. 

For some reason, he takes a left instead of a right and rushes into a chamber with Kobolds banging on the floor. 

He looks at them confused and then notices swarms of centipedes approaching him. He also can hear a pack of Kobolds rushing up against him. 

“Fireball.” 

A fiery mote flies off his hand and lands on the ground, and a blast of fire explodes in every direction, incinerating all of Vardis’ enemies. 

The fireball stops the on-rushing Kobolds, and Vardis scrambles through the caverns, carrying his mirror ball.

# 6th day of the 2nd tenday of 1492
## Bob Saget

The play was a smashing success the following evening. Vardis, Orsik, Unla Kee, and Thistle chose not to sit in the theater. They spent their night in the Talking Troll, ready to run or fight. 

And so they learn that a new character appeared at the end of the play: a demon-looking creature that introduced itself as Bob Saget. Using the effects of the CGI, it started to crack jokes that made everyone laugh. 

Thistle is confused about the outcome and looks at the original play more carefully. 

“Damn.” 

Thistle shouts, “This was a summoning ritual.” 

Thistle explains that the original play was a summoning ritual. The theatergoers were intended as sacrifices to tempt the demon to enter the gate, which is why the original ending was so confusing and bizarre. 

“What happened?” asks Orsik. 

“Well, we improved the play, so I don’t know?” 

Thistle mutters, “We should go check it out.” 

And there is this demon in purple smiling and laughing. It’s got a purple top hat and a purple formal jacket with purple tassels that seem almost alive. It’s wearing paints that go up to its waist and have embroidery that seems to move the more you stare at it. Its hands are covered in white gloves. But it’s the face that’s most disturbing. Two giant eyes with a purple iris, a mouth with jagged teeth, and purple skin that seems stretched over a skull. It looks every bit the part of a demon of the Abyss. 

The crowd should be terrified. 

The crowd is ecstatic.

Unla Kee approaches it, and the Demon introduces himself in a thick, heavy voice: “I am Bob Saget, my pronouns are he, him, and I am the tormentor of the kings who killed their jesters. I have been tormenting Kings for eternity with my dad jokes. I understand that I have you to thank for arranging my escape from the Abyss. You are the first people to summon me in eons.” 

Orsik steps forward, “So do we fight?” 

“Oh no, Orsik. I love this new job. I get to practice my material on a new audience. The Abyss is so - boring.”

Meanwhile, Urgala tells Vardis, “WE HAVE A HIT! Everyone wants to come tomorrow to see it again.”

The 3HPP is delighted. They have their hit, and they have a new star. Most importantly, the coin will flow. 

Kaelin, however,  is confused; he had planned this play as revenge on the theater-going public that never admired him for his creative genius, and now they are. He planned to summon a demon that would have laid waste to the entire town, consuming the theatergoers in hellfire. 

He pauses and shouts, “I AM GOING TO BE FAMOUS!”
