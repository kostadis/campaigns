# Chapter 74 - The Wand of Ill Fortune
In the morning, the members of the 3HPP find a letter sealed with Orsik's logo. 

Each of them, in turn, looks up at Orsik and sees his smile. 
So they open the message.
*The dracolich has agreed to help us kill the vampire in exchange for us locating and returning his phylactery. This is a Bad Idea. A freed dracolich could wreck untold horrors upon Faerun. I, Orsic, am a Hero (tm), and heroes do not free dracoliches. We do need the help in battle, though. So what should we do?*
*
We have a potential ally—an old friend, you might say—who can help us out of this predicament: Bob Saget.
*
*Now I know what you're going to say. Didn't we fight Bob? Isn't Bob an evil demon? Bob isn't our friend, and he's certainly not an ally. Yes, yes. That's all very well and good. But listen...
We had to fight Bob. He had a zombie army and was going to Waylay Silverymoon. A hero like me, Orsic, doesn't let a town be ransacked and destroyed, not even Silverymoon. I had to act. We had to act. We couldn't let Bob go through with this plan. But the truth is, I kind of liked Bob. He was funny. He made us a LOT of money. I didn't want to fight him and I didn't want to kill him. So I talked Unlakee into sending him to the astral plane instead. I knew Bob would be safe there and we could get rid of the zombies and be Heroes (tm). And we did it. The plan worked perfectly. Bob was safe, and we even sent one of his drow allys with him and four stooges. The astral plane is a dangerous place. I didn't want Bob to go alone. And we let his other drow get away safely. Everything went just like I drew it up.
*
*So now's the time to reunite with Bob. Vardis has a spell: Planar Ally. He can bring Bob to us. I'll explain the whole story to him just like I did to you. And then we ask for his help: fight the vampire alongside us, help us retrieve the phylactery, and the dracolich is yours to keep. Sure we had to destroy your zombie army, but a dracolich is way better than a zombie army anyway.
*
*Do you understand? Do you see the sheer brilliance of this plan? Can you comprehend its awesome, intricate, unvarnished intellectual fortitude? We get the dracolich for the battle. We get Bob for the battle. We don't have a pesky dracolich to deal with at the end of the battle. We remove Bob as an adversary (he's been plotting his revenge against us). Maybe we can even ally with Bob again someday. We would be fools not to follow this plan, my plan. We will all be Heroes (tm) again.*
*As we should be. As we shall be.*
*I await your acquiescence.*
Thistle is impressed and immediately pens a reply and hands it over to the grinning Dwarf. 
*Why wouldn't (1) bob betray us just to see us fail. (2) Bob betray us in hopes of getting the dracolich for free (3) Bob using the dracolich for something even worse (e.g. how is Bob in control of a dracolich not worse than a free dracolich? They are both problems, but being problems together is harder to clean up)*
Orsik tut-tuts the sloppy grammar, poor capitalization, and generally less than excellent prose. He then responds with another missive of his own. 

*1. Bob is plotting against us right now anyway. We can try to get him on our side. Worst case, he continues to plot against us.
2. Get the dracolich for free how? The vampire has to be dead for us or Bob to get the phylactery.
3. A free dracolich is Our Fault. And we will not be Heores if we let it happen. Unless you want to try to kill the dracolich immediately after we've spent all of our spell slots killing a vampire. Bob in control of a dracolich is... unfortunate? But Bob's actions are His Fault.*
Orsik glares. 
Thistle stares. 
At this point, Unla Kee asks, “Perhaps we should examine exactly what the rules for Planar Ally are.” 
- You beseech an otherworldly entity for aid. The being must be known to you: a god, a primordial, a demon prince, or some other being of cosmic power. That entity sends a celestial, an elemental, or a fiend loyal to it to aid you, making the creature appear in an unoccupied space within range. If you know a specific creature’s name, you can speak that name when you cast this spell to request that creature, though you might get a different creature anyway.
- When the creature appears, it is under no compulsion to behave in any particular way. You can ask the creature to perform a service in exchange for payment, but it isn’t obliged. The requested task could range from simple (fly us across the chasm, or help us fight a battle) to complex (spy on our enemies, or protect us during our foray into the dungeon). You must be able to communicate with the creature to bargain for its services.
- Payment can take a variety of forms. A celestial might require a sizable donation of gold or magic items to an allied temple, while a fiend might demand a living sacrifice or a gift of treasure. Some creatures might exchange their service for a quest undertaken by you.
As a rule of thumb, a task that can be measured in minutes requires a payment worth 100 gp per minute. A task measured in hours requires 1,000 gp per hour. And a task measured in days (up to 10 days) requires 10,000 gp per day.
-  If the task is aligned with the creature’s ethos, the payment might be halved or even waived. Nonhazardous tasks typically require only half the suggested payment, while especially dangerous tasks might require a greater gift. Creatures rarely accept tasks that seem suicidal.
- After the creature completes the task, or when the agreed-upon duration of service expires, the creature returns to its home plane after reporting back to you, if appropriate to the task and if possible. If you are unable to agree on a price for the creature’s service, the creature immediately returns to its home plane.

Vardis examines the rules and says, “What prevents him from sending the Dracolich to attack us?” 
Orsik shrugs, “Maybe we can give him something else of value, like a wand of wonder?” 
Unla Kee shakes his head, “No.” 
“And if I am not mistaken,” mumbles Vardis, “Bob goes back to the Abyss, and now he has an emissary on the prime material plane. And that emissary is invulnerable because the phylactery is somewhere in the abyss. I’m not sure given a demon a dracolich is -heroic-.” 
Seeing his friend point out the flaws in his plan would have made the old Orsik angry, but the new Orsik understands that plans get better when people contribute their perspectives and that he, as the most brilliant person in the room, has to identify those good points and incorporate them into the plan.
“Alright, maybe we double cross Bob? We give him the phylactery and then send the Dracolich to get it?” 
“A backstab?” asks Thistle. 
“Not really, because he was never on our side,” observes Vardis. 
“So, a stab?”
Orsik grouses, “The Bob plan was good, but the problem of the Dracolich remains. Let’s table it for now.” 
Orsik looks at the 3HPP, pleased. He knows that sometimes you have to table a plan when the team doesn’t see how right it is and just bring it back when they are ready to accept it. 
Unla Kee is disappointed. The potential for mayhem and chaos was high, and since he knew it wasn’t the end of the world, it was not dangerous. 
Later, when the sun has risen, the heroes climb to the top of the control tower, some 70 feet above the upper courtyard. 
“So we do this with no spells, right?” 
The rest of the 3HPP nods at Orsik’s admonition. 
Thistle fires at the Ogre Zombies, as do Vardis and Orisk. Realizing what is happening, the zombies head to the tower and climb. 
Vardis stares at the zombies – “I wonder why they are doing that?” 
At this point, Orsik asks, “Can we use mold earth on the stone?” 
Unla Kee sighs, “I think I explained this to you. Yes, the arcane lore about the mists of Ravenloft does talk about a dark lord who the Dark Powers seduced with the ability to mold stone as if it were earth. But the cantrip doesn’t work that way, normally.” 
Everyone looks at Unla Kee – “Look, the story of Slerdax is quite interesting. He’s a dark lord trapped in the mists of Ravenloft. He runs a restaurant that periodically opens its doors in some cities and traps the unwary. Slerdax wants to be praised for his brilliant cooking and excellent service but is cursed to doubt. And so whenever someone says nice things, he thinks they are lying and tries to kill them. The killing is done subtly. But if subtlety doesn’t work, the seething rage of an undying dark lord works. To escape, you must either kill the dark lord, and most people don’t show up in full battle dress at a dinner party, or convince him that you are telling the truth.” 
At this point, everyone is staring at Unla Kee – “Look, I told you I went to a fantastic school of magic, where the forbidden lore teacher was interested in teaching us the dangers of Ravenloft. I took the advanced course, which covered his particular area of interest: minor lords with small domains.” 
And with that, Unla Kee uses toll the dead on one of the Zombies. 
Vardis mutters, “Not exactly the best cantrip to use.” 
And then proceeds to use it on the same monster, “but when the monster is injured, it is the best cantrip to use.” 
Unla Kee is irritated. 
And the zombies continue to climb up the tower. 

Blagothkus pauses, looking at the zombies. 
Orsik asks with an affectation of irritation, “Do you have another boulder?” 
“Of course he does,” snarks Vardis. “He only has the best boulders.” 
The cloud giant smiles and nods, “Why, yes. Only the very best.” 
With that, the giant drops it on one of the oncoming Zombies, whose ranks are thinning. 
Thistle fires two arrows at two zombies to make the wizard and cleric’s toll on the dead more effective by giving them more wounded targets. He admires his handiwork until he sees Unla Kee pull out the small boulder they found at the one stone. Thistle groans, the boulder of wonder. 
A puff of green, noxious smoke quickly expands into a cloud. 
“Stinking cloud.” 
The cleric shrugs, “That was almost useful. But they are immune to poison.” 
Orsik is about to start screaming and sighs, “I can’t.” 
“Are you surprised at this point?” asks Vardis. 
Blagothkus, new to the group, says, “I have met better magic users. I know better magic users. I have a whole binder full of better magic users.” 
Orsik looks at the cloud below and thinks, " Great. Not only are they immune, but we can’t target them.” 
Blagothkus mutters, “Binders.” 
“You will dismiss this spell,” he growls at Unla Kee. 
The wizard stares and agrees. 
And then Unla Kee mutters, “Every moment is not about optimizing your actions; it’s about living. And living is about taking risks, going outside the box.”
“Which is why I wanted to bring Bob. The demon of dad jokes.” 
“And give him a real dracolich,” sighs Vardis. 
Meanwhile, somewhere in the Astral Plane, Bob wonders why he hears the faint sounds of his name. 
Vardis stares at the oncoming Zombies and kills one. “I’ll leave the weakened one for Unla Kee.” 
Orsik then turns to Blagothkus, “You’re up. You know you are the second giant friend we’ve had. Our first was Harshnag, but he died when the Oracle fell on his head. We learned about this place from your son, who is a ghost. Your son wanted to kill us. He also told us you weren’t good enough for the All-Father.” 
Blagothkus clenches his fists, thinks about tossing the dwarf down, then remembers that he’s all about personal growth, takes some deep breaths, and tosses a boulder on one of the onrushing zombies. 
While the other members of the 3HPP are enjoying good-natured ribbing and laughing, Thistle is flying off to the side and looking for any oncoming monsters. 
And he notices Glazhael the Undying sneaking towards the tower. 
“The dracolich is coming!” he shouts. 

Orsik has noticed that Thistle’s ability to see things hasn’t been up to par recently, and he is distrustful. 
So, the dwarf decides to end one of the zombies. 
Unla Kee then summons the arcane powers of his artifact, and darkness envelops the zombies!
Orsik cheers, “SEE! I knew this was going to be useful!” 
Vardis mutters, “Except now we can’t use toll the dead!” 

Glazhael advances on the group and uses her dragon fear on the 3HPP from a safe distance. Thistle notices that if she had a heart instead of an empty husk of a chest wall, it wouldn’t be in it. 
Vardis notices how Glazhael is moving and mutters, “Shadowboxing us.” 
Thistle and Thuul are affected, but the rest of the 3HPP are not. Blagothkus, for his part, shouts, “I am not going to wait here. I plan to go downstairs.” 
Orsik shouts – “I thought your wife said you had to participate in this battle?” 
“And I will, once the dragon does its thing. I will come back up and attack. I am not standing here to get killed. You might think of doing the same.” 
Vardis hears what he says, shoots an arrow into the darkness, and kills a zombie. 
Blagothkus turns to Orsik, “Alright, I am going down to get some more rocks.” He pauses and says, “Not to question your plan, but I finally figured out what the zombies were there for. Their job was to keep anyone fighting long enough to give her pet dracolich a chance to kill them.” 
Orsik turns to the other members of the 3HPP, who are now just Vardis, as the dragon's fear has overcome Unla Kee. “I told you we needed Bob.” 
Then he takes his decanter of endless liquids and dumps two gallons of mayonnaise on an approaching Zombie. The zombie covered in mayonnaise loses its footing and falls but is not killed. 
And with that, Orsik heads for cover. 
Thistle, heading Blagothkus’ warning, moves away from the tower and shoots two more Zombies. 

Unla Kee, ever the gambler, picks up the giant artifact and conjures up its wild magic. The tower shimmers green and then is covered in green grass. The wizard thinks as he heads down into the building, “Hmm… while in the darkness, they go slower, but once outside, they will go faster. Not a bad tradeoff.” 
Glazhael advances, and her annoyance is visible as she breathes on the tower, which is covered in ice. As she flies away, she screams, “THEY ARE ALL DEAD!” 
As she flies away, Orsik shouts, “Bob’s still an option!”
