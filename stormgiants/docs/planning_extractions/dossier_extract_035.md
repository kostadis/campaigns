## Kaelen

Kaelen is an out-of-work actor whom Orsik previously made a deal with to produce a play. He purchased the theater property outright rather than renting it, contributing to the financial chaos Orsik and the party face. He serves as actor, producer, and playwright for the production.

When Unla Kee confronts him about the quality of the script, Kaelen's already-poor confidence collapses immediately at the dismissive tone, and he whimpers that the script is that bad before retreating to his chambers to rewrite it. His revised script uses archaic common tongue, which he defends on grounds of authenticity. He is persuaded by Unla Kee's argument that selective archaic words create the illusion of authenticity without sacrificing comprehension, calling the idea brilliant.

Through successive rounds of revision, Kaelen proves oddly protective of the play's ending — more so than any other element — becoming touchy when Unla Kee attempts to address it. Unla Kee must manipulate fate itself to successfully help Kaelen brainstorm changes to the finale.

The true nature of the play is revealed after its successful opening: the original script was a summoning ritual. The theatergoers were intended as sacrifices to lure a demon through a gate. Kaelen had designed the entire production as revenge against the theater-going public that had never recognized his creative genius, planning to summon a demon that would lay waste to the town and consume the audience in hellfire.

Because the party improved the play, the ritual misfired in an unexpected direction, summoning Bob Saget rather than a destructive demon. When the play becomes a hit, Kaelen's revenge plot is entirely derailed by his own vanity. He pauses, apparently abandoning his murderous intentions on the spot, and shouts that he is going to be famous.

Kaelen last appears at the theater following the successful opening night, stunned by the acclaim he had spent years being denied.

## Urgala

Urgala is an innkeeper — implied to be the proprietor of The Troll Inn — who serves as an intermediary and advisor to the party. She is the one who delivers the bad news about Kaelen's mismanagement of the production to Orsik and Thistle before the opening.

She contextualizes the stakes for the party: the play's buzz and the fame of the party's exploits have made the opening the event of the season, but if the production fails to secure a second night, the Zhentarim and Hand of Yartar will collect their pound of flesh. She identifies the specific problems facing the production — the dialogue and story are a mess, the actors cannot act, and the set is a disaster — and confirms that a delay is impossible, laughing heartily at the suggestion.

Urgala also directs Vardis to retrieve the CGI device, describing it as belonging to Chancellor Geoffrey Invitations, who used it to create elaborate animated invitations. She reveals that a tribe of kobolds south of Yartar in the Dessarin Hills unknowingly possesses it, having stolen it from one of Chancellor Geoffrey's messengers, and that Geoffrey has been searching for it since. When Vardis asks how she knows this, she says only that she wasn't always an innkeeper.

Following the successful opening night, Urgala tells Vardis that they have a hit and that everyone wants to return the following evening.

## Everard Barney

Everard Barney is the director of the play — a heavy-set, unimposing man wearing a multi-colored shirt and a purple scarf over his head, barely concealing a buzz cut and large ears. He introduces himself to Thistle as completely committed to Kaelen's vision.

When Thistle encounters him, Everard breaks down sobbing almost immediately. He reveals that Kaelen creeps him out, that he cannot manage his lead actors because they ignore his directions, and that his entire family are bookkeepers. He branched out into theater and fears that failure will force him to return to Waterdeep to work as an accountant. He sobs uncontrollably through much of the conversation.

Everard does not understand his actors' motivations and has never thought to ask them what they want. With Thistle's guidance, he grasps how to handle the lead actor Amy — specifically, that he should not stage her iconic soliloquy atop a pile of dung. He had intended the dung as a thematic contrast between heroic ideals and everyday hardship, but accepts Thistle's suggestion of a hill with the dung below, allowing Amy to appear to elevate the audience above it rather than sit in it. He is enthusiastic about this reframing.

He also consults Thistle about the drunken actors forgetting their lines, initially tempted by the suggestion to incorporate their drunkenness into the play but ultimately rejecting it in favor of screaming at them in the morning. He exchanges a friendly moment with Thistle about managing drunk people, comparing his uncle's method of screaming at apprentices to Thistle's bucket-of-water technique.

At the end of the day, Everard sheepishly approaches Thistle about the finale, accepting Thistle's verdict that the ending should be changed not at all.

## Amy

Amy is the lead actor in the play. Thistle's observation of Amy's body language and behavior reveals that she presents as fluffy and joyful but is fundamentally status-driven and deeply concerned with others' opinions. She avoids definitive statements and commitments, always deflecting with uncertainty, as though concealing a mysterious past.

When Orsik, disguised as acting coach Geoff Mamet, confronts her, Amy immediately defers to his authority — asking whether Kaelen signed off and whether it is in the budget — before deflating entirely under his commanding tone. She describes wanting to play the lead role in a specific way and reveals that Kaelen has been pushing back on her ideas, but she is too timid and milquetoast to advocate for herself.

Orsik's attempt to build her spine by shouting at her to grow up fails. She sighs and agrees to do whatever Kaelen wants. She then complains about specific scenes and the complicated lines and gestures in the play's ending, which Orsik silences by ordering her to deliver the lines with energy and stop trying to direct or write.

She ultimately performs well enough that the play succeeds. When Orsik reappears after dropping his disguise, Amy warmly thanks him for having paid for Geoff Mamet's coaching and credits the coach with making all the difference.

## Bob Saget

Bob Saget does not appear until the end of the play's opening night. He is a demon summoned — apparently accidentally, as a result of the party's improvements to the original summoning ritual — during the finale of the play.

His appearance is striking: purple top hat, purple formal jacket with purple tassels that seem alive, trousers with embroidery that appears to move when stared at, white gloves, and a face with enormous eyes with purple irises, jagged teeth, and purple skin stretched tight over a skull. Despite looking every part the demon of the Abyss, the crowd greets him with ecstasy rather than terror.

He introduces himself to Unla Kee in a thick, heavy voice, stating his pronouns are he/him and describing himself as the tormentor of kings who killed their jesters, tormenting monarchs for eternity with dad jokes. He confirms to the party that they are the first people to summon him in eons and expresses genuine gratitude for his escape from the Abyss, which he found boring.

When Orsik asks whether they should fight, Bob Saget declines, saying he loves his new job — practicing his material on a new audience. He becomes an immediate sensation, cracking jokes that delight the crowd.

His arrival transforms the play into a hit. Urgala confirms that audiences want to return the following night. Bob Saget's last known location is the theater, where he is performing for an adoring crowd.

## Chancellor Geoffrey

Chancellor Geoffrey does not appear in this session. He is referenced by Urgala when she directs Vardis to retrieve the CGI device. She describes him as someone who used the device to create elaborate invitations featuring sound and animated visuals. The device was stolen from one of his messengers by a tribe of kobolds in the Dessarin Hills south of Yartar, and Urgala notes that Chancellor Geoffrey has been searching for it ever since.
