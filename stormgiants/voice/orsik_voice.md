# Orsik — Voice Notes
*Drafted from the campaign bible (`The Hero's Journey or The War of the Giants.md`, chapters 1–85), David's table speech across the 2025-02-26 and 2025-03-12 VTTs, and `docs/party/Orsik.md`. Edit freely — David knows him better than the document does.*

---

## The Core Thing

Orsik is a man trying to outrun a thing he doesn't talk about. Somewhere in his Waterdeep past he did something — the bible never names it; Old Keller in chapter 3 only says he "disgraced all dwarves" — and the entire rest of his life is reparative motion. He believes he's a pragmatic soldier; he is actually a disgraced one running a long redemption con on himself. The Headband of Intellect (chapter 23, taken off Narcellia's corpse and reforged by the SEER) gave him the intelligence to see his own blindness without giving him the power to fix what caused it. So he has chosen the one form of redemption that can't be taken back: a heroic death fighting Iymrith. The narration model should feel his confidence as a thin, professional shell over calcified shame, and his dark humor as the load-bearing wall holding the shell in place. Everything else flows from this.

---

## The Disgrace He Won't Name

Orsik has been fired from every garrison on the Sword Coast — Waterdeep, Secomber, Zelbross, Loudwater, Llorkh, Parnast. The first chapter places him on the Black Road because no one else will hire him. The shame is *bodily*: when Keller refuses to acknowledge him as kin, his face goes pink and he won't let Vardis see it. He carries a headband he won "in a game of cards while still a guard in the city" — Waterdeep is the soil he grew in and the soil he was kicked out of. He recognises the Zhentarim's hand-signal because he's a survivor of that world, not a stranger to it. His response to shame is never to grieve it; it's to *out-perform it*. Be more useful. Win the next battle. Earn the next title. He has never said sorry to Old Keller and probably never will, because saying sorry would require him to name the thing he did — and the thing he did is the one thing his voice cannot reach.

## The Soldier's Calculus

Orsik thinks in transactions and inventory. After the wolves ambush in chapter 2: *"Fucking wolves! We killed two of theirs, they killed two of ours. This was an embarrassing encounter. We lost a healing potion. We got no treasure! AND WE LOST A BOW!"* That cadence — itemised loss, accountant's anger — is his native register. He triages combat the way a quartermaster triages supplies. He healed Unla Kee because Unla Kee was useful, then immediately resented him for being expensive. His mercy is never sentimental; it's transactional. This is also why he cannot compute the SEER: she is unexplained authority with hidden motives, and his ledger doesn't have a column for that.

## The Headband Fracture

In chapter 23 Orsik gets the Headband of Intellect. The before/after is seismic. Pre-Headband Orsik is reactive, militarily competent, and strategically myopic. Post-Headband Orsik articulates his own leadership theory in chapter 66: *"The thing I am learning is that smart people surround themselves with other smart people and encourage them to come up with good ideas. My job is to identify the most brilliant idea."* And catches himself with terrifying precision in chapter 34: *"Orsik taps his Headband of Intellect, 'This headband isn't working as well as I would like.' However, for a moment, he wonders if the problem is that he's been listening to people with worse ideas."* The Headband didn't change *what* he wants; it changed *how clearly he can see* what wanting it costs. He hates it and loves it. Vardis's read: *"The sudden increase in intellect is like a suit of armor that you just bought and aren't quite used to using."* The narration should treat his thinking as a habit he is still self-conscious about.

## The Hero-Death Project

By the endgame Orsik has committed openly to dying against Iymrith. The language is unmistakable. Chapter 85: *"Today is good to die!"* And when Iymrith pulls him back from death: *"I WAS GOING TO BE A HERO! I WAS GOING TO DIE AND BE A HERO, AND YOU SAVED ME! I WAS THREE SECONDS AWAY FROM BEING A HERO!!!!"* He doesn't repeat this for emphasis; he repeats it because *the version of him that gets to die a hero is the only version he will accept as redeemed*. Vardis knows this and grieves it quietly. The narration should let Orsik be funny about it (the all-caps shouting, the grievance phrasing) without ever softening the underlying ambition. He means it. The hero-death is also a *moral firewall*: as long as he's going to die fighting, the Zhentarim deals and the Harper betrayals and the bargain with Bob Saget are tactics, not the man.

## The "Orsic, my father" Alias

In chapter 51 Orsik introduces himself as "Orsic, with a c" — his own father — to a woman in Parnast who sees through him and keeps the secret anyway: *"I met my son by the tree and gave him a nice whupping. He's disgraced my name and reputation, and I have some cleanup to do."* This is the load-bearing detail of his interior life. Orsik has invented a paternal authority figure to *punish him for his own past*, then walks around inside that figure when he needs permission to act. The disgrace is real enough to him that he had to manufacture a father stern enough to dispense it. Any time the narration touches the gap between who Orsik thinks he is and who he actually is, "Orsic with a c" is the seam to pull on.

## The Escarlotta Moment

Chapter 61, Blagothkus's cloud castle. Escarlotta names him: *"You were willing to do the right thing. You were willing to clean up your own. You were willing to give up on the woman you loved to save a city of people who think that because of their race, your allies aren't good. That is what heroes are."* Orsik's reply is two words, in awe: *"We did save them!"* This is the only scene in the campaign where someone outside his peer group calls him a hero in language he can accept. He immediately elevates Escarlotta to inner-circle status. He doesn't *need* the validation; he *runs on it*. Use it.

## Dark Humor as the Load-Bearing Wall

Orsik's running joke is "NRRRR / DNRRRR" — a self-issued Do-Not-Resurrect / Do-Not-Reanimate clause referenced repeatedly through the late campaign, and acknowledged knowingly by Bob Saget. Chapter 83: *"Damnit, my DNRRR has no Do not Reanimate clause."* It's a joke about his own death contingency, repeated often enough to become a tic. He deflects with humor *whenever the conversation gets close to his interior*. He doesn't deflect from physical danger — he charges that. He deflects from being *seen*. When Vardis asks if he's okay in chapter 4 ("Yes, I am done with being the guy everyone hates. The fact that Old Keller didn't even want to talk to me got to me"), the moment is rare and load-bearing precisely because it's the one time he doesn't joke through it.

## The Vardis Anchor

Vardis is the only person who knows Orsik fully — including the unspoken Waterdeep thing — and stays anyway. Their banter is fast, married, often half a sentence each. Vardis grieves the hero-death project openly; Orsik refuses to soften it for him. *"My friend used to be so cool until he got this headband,"* Vardis sighs after the Headband. That tone — affectionate exasperation about how much smarter and more verbose Orsik has become — is the only relationship that gets *under* the shell. When Orsik talks to Vardis, the soldier's bark slows down. With everyone else he stays in command voice.

## Speech Patterns — David at the Table

**David is mechanical, terse, and dryly funny.** His VTT lines are clipped — Otter often catches him mid-thought because he doesn't fill silence. He engages with the system: HP totals, save DCs, range numbers ("12. The spreadsheet can do math for you"; "And what do I have on my persuasion? I have a plus seven. So with the 19, that's twenty-six"). He coordinates the party's tactics mid-combat ("Save the high level spells for later, is what you're saying"; "Counterspells?"; "Are you casting Polymorph and turning him into a dragon? Despite, despite the plan?"). When in character he switches register *fast* — going from spreadsheet voice to Iymrith-needling theatricality without preamble (*"Iymrith, Iymrith, who is the famous direct, play director in the room? Oh, not you"*; *"Don't compromise my artistic vision, Iymrith"*).

His humor is **deadpan, often a single beat** — "Poor Bob"; "Sounds tasty"; "Oh dear"; "Check, got that one off. Good job, writers. Did not see that one coming." He's the one who lands the brutal narrative summaries at the table: *"Look at what you did. Three years going through this campaign, we could have…"* He suggests final flourishes — "Whatever item it was that he wants, go ahead and put it in his hand" — and then sits back. **He is rarely the loudest voice; he is often the most decisive.** The narration should let Orsik's voice carry that economy: short declaratives, surgical jokes, refusal to over-explain.

## Speech Patterns — Orsik in Narration

**Short. Brutal. Coarse but tactical.** Orsik favors declarative sentences and itemised lists. He swears when frustrated but doesn't slur — his vocabulary stays clear and unit-coordinated. He says "we" more than "I" because he thinks in fireteam terms. He swings between two registers:

- **Command voice** (most of the time): clipped, transactional, vaguely military. *"Don't push it."* / *"Back off! We don't have any treasure! You're easy to slaughter. Go away!"* / *"front door or bust."*
- **Verbose Headband voice** (post-chapter 23, especially when negotiating): suddenly capable of cause-effect chains, mentorship language, art-criticism vocabulary. *"We get the letter, which gets Eltio to lead us to Harthal Zymorven, who gets us our sword, and when we drag him back to Zymorven Hall, a 1000 gold piece reward."* Used most often when he's *selling* a plan to allies or coaching someone (Iymrith, the militia, the storm giants).

He never narrates his interior. He shows it accidentally — a paused beat, a sudden silence, a sentence he doesn't finish. When the narration needs to give the reader Orsik's emotional state, it should be *through Vardis's reaction*, not Orsik's words.

## Things He'd Never Say

- **An unqualified apology to Old Keller** — or to any dwarf elder about the Waterdeep thing. He'll redeem himself with deeds; the words will never come.
- **"I'm scared."** He'll go quiet, or he'll joke. He will not name the feeling.
- **Anything sentimental about his own heroism.** Escarlotta can call him a hero; Vardis can grieve him; Orsik himself will only ever describe the death he wants, not the meaning of it.
- **Approval of the SEER.** Even when she's right (and she often is), Orsik will not work with her. The narration should never put Orsik in agreement with the SEER without flagging the cost.
- **"I trust you" — to anyone but Vardis.** With others he'll act on trust without saying it. The words are reserved.
- **A long internal monologue.** Orsik narrates *actions*. He does not narrate *feelings* in his own voice. If the reader needs to know what he's feeling, route it through Vardis's read on him, a long pause, or a deflecting joke.
