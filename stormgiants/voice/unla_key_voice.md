# Unla Key — Voice Notes
*Drafted from the campaign bible (`The Hero's Journey or The War of the Giants.md`, chapters 1–85), Jared's table speech across the 2025-02-26 and 2025-03-12 VTTs, and `docs/party/UnlaKey.md`. Edit freely — Jared knows him better than the document does.*

---

## The Core Thing

Unla Key has built a metaphysical prison where his own survival becomes cosmically mandatory, and he lives inside it cheerfully. He believes he alone has decoded how the multiverse ends, that he alone is currently keeping it from ending, and therefore that any risk he takes is *retroactively justified* by the fact of his continued existence. The bible literally has him think *"if he died, the world would die with him, and the world would keep him alive."* This is the operational engine: he gambles because losing is metaphysically impossible; he drinks because sobriety hurts the brain doing all that load-bearing; he chaos-casts because if the spell works, fate intended it, and if it doesn't, fate will give him another turn. The narration model needs to feel this as *genuine intellectual conviction*, not posturing. He has a real prophecy fragment (the altar in chapter 14 actually corrected him on a detail — "*Unla just got some details wrong*" — which to a normal person is humbling and to Unla Key is *confirmation* that he was 95% right). He is Neutral Evil not because he's malicious but because he is fundamentally solipsistic: the universe is a stage, and either it depends on him to keep running or it doesn't deserve to. He cannot tell the difference between "I am saving the world" and "saving the world is the only rationalisation that lets me keep doing what I want," and at this point he doesn't want to know which one it is. Bonus: late campaign he is *also* found out by Bob Saget that there are infinite worlds — and *smiles*, because nobody ever asked him which world he was saving. He's been hedging the multiverse this whole time. The narration should let him be funny, generous-when-it-suits-him, and operationally terrifying.

## "I Know How the World Ends"

This is the load-bearing belief. The bible plants it from chapter 6-7 and crystallises it at the altars in chapter 14 — Unla Key encodes his knowledge of the world's ending in a cipher only he understands, and the god of the altar *corrects him on a detail*. He doesn't update. He keeps believing. By chapter 20 he has told the party — out loud — that the world will end if he dies; the dwarf later stares at the sky after Unla Key takes a heavy hit and notes drily, *"Huh, I guess the world isn't coming to an end."* The narration should treat the cosmology as **a genuine prophecy fragment he has spun into a totalising worldview**. He has the Prophecies of the Apocalypse book from the SEER. He has been recognised as the Wizard of the Crag. He has inherited three spellbooks from a wizard who died over money. Every external signal that comes in he metabolises as evidence of his cosmic role. When Bob Saget reveals the ISD treaty violation will bring the three empires to claim Toril, Unla Key *smiles*:

> *"He smiles because he has always known about the infinity of planets. And because he knew about the end of the world. And nobody ever asked him if it was 'this' world. If this world ends, he knows he will survive it."* (chapter ~85)

This is the seam. He is not saving *the* world; he is *a* world, and he is hedged across the multiverse. The narration should let his apocalyptic-prophet register feel less like fanaticism and more like *insurance underwriting*. He has run the math.

## "I Take Risks"

The operational corollary. The bible's framing, chapter 6: *"'I take risks,'"* he tells Orsik, *"matter-of-factly, as if this explains everything."* And chapter 6 again: *"I SURVIVED AN ATTACK! I AM INVINCIBLE!"* — said after nearly dying. The risk-taking is not bravado; it is **theological necessity**. From his frame, the multitude of planes are safe *only if he takes risks*. Every wild Fireball, every Wand-of-Wonder discharge, every shouted "Fireball!" into a tight room is — in his head — *required of him by cosmic position*. The narration should treat his recklessness as *purposeful behaviour with a coherent (if delusional) justification*. He's not a careless wizard; he's a careful wizard with a load-bearing delusion that careful and reckless are the same word.

The flip side: he is **constantly being saved by the party**. Vardis raises him. Iymrith's cultists raise him at Ascore (the lightning hit through the blue-dragon/purple-worm hybrids). The Maelstrom assault doesn't go his way; he's killed and brought back. He does not reflect on this. He absorbs each resurrection as evidence the universe wants him to keep going. **The world keeps saving him because the world cannot afford to lose him** — that's his operating reading, even though the actual mechanism is that he has friends.

## The Drink, the Bet, and the Sobriety Trap

Unla Key has a serious problem. Chapter 20: he has been *sober for two weeks* and notes drily, *"the longest."* The first drink in chapter 31 puts him on his heels: *"Now it turns out that Unla Kee had been alcohol-free for two weeks and that sobriety was starting to hurt his brain. The fresh alcohol soothed his nerves."* He snaps, loses focus, gets snide. The drinking is **neural suppressant for the constant cognitive load of being the load-bearing intelligence of the multiverse** — his theology is exhausting, and alcohol is the only thing that turns it off.

Gambling is the same drive in a different shape. Chapter 20: *"we should gamble. I change the fates."* — said as a verdict, not a question. He is a Diviner. He believes he can manipulate probability. So why not put money on it? The drinking and the gambling are not vices; they are *expressions of his theology*. The narration should let them be matter-of-fact about it. Drinking is a tool. Betting is a sacrament.

The Kee and Wing Casino (formerly the Grand Dame in Yartar) is **his vices industrialised into infrastructure**. He owns the building where the addiction logic he runs internally is sold to other people at a markup. The casino is operated by Pow; Unla Key holds naming rights. Blagothkus's cloud castle is being converted into a second venue. This is not a side-quest. This is *the externalisation of his metaphysics*: chance, fate, and risk made into a revenue model. He has even monetised the apocalypse — if the world is ending, you might as well take the action while you can.

## Fireballs Are Fun

In combat, Unla Key fights like a Diviner who has decided that probability is on his side and therefore area-of-effect is the natural way to spend a turn. Wall of Force, Forcecage (he used it on the SEER late in the campaign), Fireball (cast joyfully into rooms with allies in them), Lightning Bolt, Disintegrate, Thunderwave. Chapter 70: he and Vardis exchange a glance and *incinerate an entire tribe of defenders with paired fireballs*. *"They smile smugly. The defenders are dead. The altar is theirs."* Two wizards, two fireballs, moving on.

He also has the **Wand of Wonder, Rock of Wonder, Wand of Web, Gem of Elemental Summoning, mud mephit summoning stone**, and three spellbooks from the Wizard of the Crag. The Wand of Wonder's wild magic results have included stinking cloud, darkness, and grass-on-the-ground. He uses these instruments not strategically but **invocationally** — he reaches for chaos when desperate or bored, trusting fate to deliver something. The narration should let his combat feel *gleeful*. Vardis flagged that they could have avoided the fight; Unla Key's reply, chapter 70: *"But fireballs are fun."* That sentence is the whole combat philosophy.

The signature gesture: **he psychic-lances a creature he isn't sure is psychic-vulnerable** (chapter ~63) and reflects: *"He remembers that he was drunk when that topic was covered, and trusting his gut casts psychic lance."* Then it works. Then: *"it doesn't matter what I thought I knew. I know the future, and the future said the spell worked."* That last line is **the Unla Key thesis**: hindsight is divination, retroactively.

## The Pegasus Wings, the Avatar Con, the Tiny Halfling

Mid-campaign Unla Key acquires twelve-foot magical pegasus wings. The bible plays them as both ridiculous (a *halfling* with twelve-foot wings) and majestic (the Sky Pony tribe sees them and is awed into worship). He **stages a con against the Sky Pony tribe** at the One Stone spirit mound (chapter ~52): he dismisses invisibility, shouts *"I am here. Bow before me,"* and the tribe falls into worship. He then *commands them onto a seven-day vision quest* to buy the party time. Internal debate: *"Should we go for the I am the vengeful avatar or the super-cool avatar that everyone wants to be friends with?"* He picks super-cool. The deception isn't malice — it's **theology with PR strategy**. Even his charity is calculus: *"a benevolent god always attracts more followers than one that does not bestow gifts."*

He also says *"I'm so tiny"* at the table in March 2025 from inside pegasus form on a huge battlemap. The physicality matters: he is **small enough to be overlooked**, with wings that make him **legible from a great distance**. He has chosen visibility. Tiny by birth, larger-than-life by reputation, twelve feet across in flight — and never, ever, attempting to blend in.

## The Headband Crisis

Through chapters 1 through 22, Unla Key is comfortably the smartest person at the table. Then Orsik puts on the Headband of Intellect and starts articulating leadership theory: *"smart people surround themselves with other smart people and encourage them to come up with good ideas. My job is to identify the most brilliant idea."* (Orsik, chapter 66.) Vardis notices: *"you had straightforward plots in your head; now you have very complex ones."*

Unla Key is **rattled by this in a way he does not articulate**. He had built an identity on being the intellectual; now a *dwarf fighter* is outthinking him with a hat. The bible records his irritation (chapter 31 / 40 area): *"Unla Kee is impressed with Orsik's erudite complete paragraph explanation. And is annoyed when Orsik notices that, and the fighter taps his headband."* The narration should let this rivalry stay quiet but present. Unla Key has not *adapted* to it; he has *compensated*. He plays the cosmology card louder, makes the casino-mogul move bigger, gets recognised as the Wizard of the Crag — all to re-establish the hierarchy Orsik's hat collapsed. **It is the only relationship in the party where he feels threatened, and he absolutely refuses to admit it.**

## Thistle, the Quiet Bond

Unla Key's relationship with Thistle is the one place his solipsism softens. Chapter 31: Thistle casually reveals he's been shooting two arrows per round since *before the Hill Giant fight at Parnast* — and didn't mention it because *"Wanted the challenge."* Unla Key's reaction is the bible's most unguarded Unla Key beat:

> *"The mage laughs and thinks, 'I like this guy. I really like this guy.'"*

This is a private thought; he doesn't say it. But from here forward, he and Thistle co-conspire — the Sky Pony con, the Margaster framing, the marsh skirmish where Unla Key explicitly pledges to protect him at personal risk. The narration should treat the Thistle bond as the **only place Unla Key consistently underplays himself**. He is louder around Orsik (defensive), drier around Vardis (peer), grand around NPCs (theological). With Thistle, he is *quiet and amused*. That is the only place the cosmic-savior armor comes off.

## Speech Patterns — Jared at the Table

**Jared is the verbose enthusiast.** He brings food porn (the nine-course pre-fixe ramen at Noodle in a Haystack — "*Wagyu hamburger in this like milk bread with uh, almost like a donut like crust on it. This was out of this world.*"), tech-tool recommendations (Granola.ai for meeting transcription, Docker for his home lab), and academic-curiosity asides (*"What's the origin of a dot out? How did that become the like default output?"*). He is the table's *enthusiasm engine*. He brings new things into the conversation and offers them with affectionate confidence.

In play he **engages mechanics with full vocabulary**: spell slot levels ("*I mean, I have up to like 7th level, so should I cast it at like 3rd level?*"), attack DCs ("*Yeah, I've got a DC 21*"), positioning ("*So when I'm flying, how far can I go?*"), range checks ("*Chain landing at least is 150 feet. I'm guessing that, uh, Fireball's not a good choice.*"). He talks tactics aloud and welcomes correction ("*You can't use the lightning against dragons*" — and he updates).

**Single-word spell declarations** are characteristic: *"Polymorph."* / *"Disintegrate."* / *"Fireball."* He announces the spell, then explains the math.

**Risk-comfortable to the point of betting on himself.** From the March 2025 session, mid-combat: *"Anybody want to take a wager on whether I die, or we run out of time before it's my turn?"* He frames his own potential death as a *prop bet*. The line is the entire Unla Key cosmology rendered in table language.

His **humor is dry, observational, and arrives mid-tactic**: *"the load bearing dragon"* (about Iymrith). *"It's gonna be these little, little fuckers right next to us."* He's not the loudest at the table; he's the one who lands the line that reframes what everyone else is doing.

He **reads aloud the ChatGPT-drafted Iymrith monologue** in the 2025-02-26 session, voicing the dragon for the party. He's comfortable taking on NPC voices when nobody else will. He brings the *production values*.

## Speech Patterns — Unla Key in Narration

**Three distinct registers**, often switched between within a single scene:

- **Academic-arcane** (verbose, slightly nasal, faintly pleased with himself): *"Excellent question. My linguistic research suggests it was done to emphasize the different nature of the ritual and the draconic aspect of the undead creature."* / *"I am very impressed with the use of this dialect. It's very authentic, and nobody will understand a word the actors are saying."* Used when he's flexing knowledge to peers.
- **Apocalyptic-prophet** (matter-of-fact, declarative, slightly amused that you don't get it yet): *"I know how the world is going to end."* / *"I know because I know."* / *"All of your demonic powers haven't given you the power of divination to know what I know."* The narration should treat this voice as **calm rather than fervent**. He is not preaching; he is informing.
- **Drunk-impulsive / gambler** (the chaos voice): *"we should gamble. I change the fates."* / *"This seems like an incredibly bad idea. He pauses and then shouts, 'I'm in.'"* / *"But fireballs are fun."* / *"I'm gonna go fight the mind flayers."* Used when the cosmology argument has produced its inevitable conclusion: *the risky play is the necessary play.*

He **does not narrate his interior emotional life**. The bible occasionally tells the reader he was annoyed (Orsik's headband moment) or charmed (the "I really like this guy" Thistle moment), but Unla Key in dialogue does not say "I feel." He converts feelings into spells, bets, theological pronouncements, or business decisions. When the narration needs the reader to know Unla Key is afraid, the route is *escalation*: a bigger Fireball, a wilder bet, a more grandiose claim about the multiverse. The fear comes out as *expansion*.

His **catchphrase rhythm** is the matter-of-fact verdict followed by the action: *"It's called showbusiness."* (locking Delsephine into residuals) / *"No, we want money."* (rejecting Orsik's blackmail proposal) / *"Problem solved."* (after a quick dismissal). Short verdicts. He doesn't argue; he *closes*.

## Things He'd Never Say

- **"I was wrong about the apocalypse."** This is the one belief he cannot afford to update. If the world isn't ending, or it's ending on a schedule he didn't predict, or it's already ended in branches he doesn't know about, *his entire operating identity collapses*. He has hedged this — see the smiling-at-Bob-Saget multiverse beat — but he will never *say it out loud*.
- **"I have a drinking problem."** The drinking is theology. Even his longest sobriety stretch ("two weeks — the longest") is named with a wry smile. He does not pathologise it.
- **An unqualified compliment to Orsik's new intelligence.** He'll acknowledge Orsik is articulate (chapter 40-ish: *"impressed with Orsik's erudite complete paragraph explanation"*); he'll *never* say "you're smarter than me now." That admission would dismantle the only hierarchy he cares about being on top of.
- **A direct prayer or apology to a deity.** He treats gods as *informational sources* (the altar that corrected his prophecy detail; the Prophecies of the Apocalypse book; the multiverse math). He does not pray. He *consults*. Compare to Vardis, who at least argues with Corellon; Unla Key would consider arguing with a god *embarrassingly emotional*.
- **A heartfelt declaration of love or loyalty.** With Thistle, the bond shows through *action* and *private thought* ("I like this guy. I really like this guy" is interior, never spoken). With the rest of the party, it shows through *spell support*. He does not declaim affection. He casts Healing Word — or Polymorph — instead.
- **"Let's play it safe."** The line literally cannot come out of him in good faith. If the narration needs him to back off, the route is *redirection* — he'll propose a *different reckless thing* that happens to be tactically better. He does not retreat from risk; he *re-routes* it. The narration must never give him a clean cautious line.
