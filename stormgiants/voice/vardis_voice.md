# Vardis — Voice Notes
*Drafted from the campaign bible (`The Hero's Journey or The War of the Giants.md`, chapters 1–85), Wade's table speech across the 2025-02-26 and 2025-03-12 VTTs, and `docs/party/Vardis.md`. Edit freely — Wade knows him better than the document does.*

---

## The Core Thing

Vardis is a moral conscience whose pragmatism keeps moving the line, and he is just self-aware enough to know it. He was excommunicated from every temple of Corellon for offering prayers to other gods (Istishia, Akadi, Kossuth — "pragmatic"). His god responds to prayers when invoked in crisis (he literally turned Eigeron's ghost away by invoking Corellon in chapter 59) but also reportedly covered him in bird droppings as a deity-flavored insult. The SEER offered him reconciliation with the temples. He *refused*. Sandesyl's unapologetic centuries-long planning inspired him to consider founding "an alternative believers' cult of Corellon" — his own splinter church for the rejected. Through the late campaign he laughs about the 200 civilians trapped in Bob's pocket dimension ("I am sure there were good people"), describes the Margaster legal injustice as a reason to *avoid antagonising the rich*, and watches his oldest friend openly court a heroic death without trying to stop him. The narration should feel his voice as the *driest, most knowing* of the four — the one who flags the danger, the one who tells himself he flagged it, and the one who casts the Wall of Fire anyway. He is not a hypocrite. He is a man building a theology that doesn't require him to be one. "Pragmatic" is the load-bearing word — say it out loud and the rest of him follows.

## Orsik — the Bond and the Grief

Vardis and Orsik have been run out of every Sword Coast garrison together. The friendship is the spine of the campaign, and Vardis carries it on his back. He revives Orsik over other downed party members ("don't make me regret my choice to heal you!"), notices when Orsik gets smarter and tells him so (*"you had straightforward plots in your head; now you have very complex ones"*, chapter 28), and watches him commit to dying against Iymrith without ever once trying to stop him. The diagnosis is precise — interior, chapter 70:

> *"Ever since Orsik became smarter, he hasn't liked his chosen career. It's like he was too dumb to be anything but a sellsword, and now that he can be anything, he resents those choices. It's almost as if he has a death wish."*

And at the table, the same beat surfaces in three words mid-combat in March 2025: *"My best friend has a death wish."* That line is the load-bearing emotional fact of Vardis's late-campaign interior. The narration should let it be unsentimental — Vardis names it, then casts Healing Word, then keeps fighting. He grieves the project without intervening in it. He is loving enough to revive Orsik over anyone else and *not* loving enough to refuse the heroic-death frame on its face. He thinks that distinction is wisdom. It is also the seam to pull on.

## The Corellon Problem (and the Cult)

Vardis's god has *trust issues* — Vardis says so himself (chapter 62):

> *"clerics of Corellon Latherian have to deal with a god that has trust issues. Ever since his first consort betrayed him, anything but true loyalty is a source of anxiety and affects the relationship. And for a person like Vardis who is already on the outs, a further demonstration of his lack of loyalty may provoke his god."*

He *knows* prayer works. He turned Eigeron's ghost in chapter 59 with a single invocation. He still gets Guiding Bolt, Wall of Fire, Flamestrike. Corellon's response to his existence is *bird droppings on his shirt* and *a working spell list* — petty and functional at once. The SEER offered him a path back to the temples. He refused.

Then he met Sandesyl. Chapter 70:

> *"'That's a long-term career plan,' observes Vardis. After his unfortunate dismissal from the temples of Corellon, he has been wondering what the future holds. Her bold proposal to reanimate a cult makes him think that perhaps a personal long-term career goal of establishing an alternative believers cult of the Elf god might be an option."*

This is the load-bearing theological seam. Sandesyl wasn't a villain to Vardis in that moment; she was a *role model*. The narration should treat the alt-cult plan as a real, simmering ambition — not a joke, not a wistful thought — and one Vardis has not yet told the rest of the party about. **He is privately building a heresy out of grief and resentment, and calling it career planning.** The fear underneath the refusal of reconciliation isn't pride — it's that going back would require accepting that the rejection was his fault. Easier to build a shadow church.

## Fire as Judgment

Vardis is a Light cleric. He uses Wall of Fire, Fireball, Flamestrike, Guiding Bolt as primary weapons. The theological irony is not lost on him — chapter 68:

> *"He remembers the endless debates in Waterdeep at the Temples before his heretical views on the true nature of the responsibilities of the Clerics of Corellon made him persona non-grata about how Flamestrike used to be more potent in the past."* / *"'Good thing I chose to follow the light, eh Corellon?'"*

He throws fire while making *jokes about following the light*. The temples wanted Corellon-the-poet, Corellon-the-archer, Corellon-the-elegant — and Vardis chose Corellon-the-light, where "light" rapidly became "the burning kind." His combat decisions are unsentimental: Wall of Fire across a hallway to cut off giants; Flamestrike to crit a Frost Giant; Guiding Bolt as judgment. He doesn't pray *before* casting; he diagnoses the tactical problem and resolves it. The narration should treat his fire as a closed argument with the temples that rejected him: *this is what light does in the world, this is what your god permits, this is what 'pragmatic' looks like.*

## Pragmatism as Sliding Justification

The word "pragmatic" appears repeatedly in the bible attached to Vardis. He uses it to describe his theology (offering to other gods), his combat choices (fire is what works), and his moral positions (chapter 29, on Silverymoon Margaster: *"These Margasters are rich and powerful, and we don't want to make enemies of them"*). The narration should track how the word *moves*:

- **Early arc**: pragmatism = doctrinal flexibility (offer water to Istishia if you need water)
- **Middle arc**: pragmatism = political prudence (don't antagonise the powerful)
- **Late arc**: pragmatism = nihilism in priest's clothing (*"Collateral damage is unavoidable in war"*; *"I am sure there were good people"* with a laugh; *"two hundred hundred fewer today"*)

He is the moral conscience of the party. He flags Thistle's executions and Orsik's death wish and the danger of the Bob Saget summoning. **And then he does not stop any of them.** He casts the spells. He stands the watch. He cracks a joke. The narration must hold both facts: he sees clearly *and* he keeps moving. The gap between Vardis-who-warns and Vardis-who-enables is the central tension. He is just self-aware enough to know — chapter 80-ish — that he is *choosing silence* when a sermon would land. *"He's tempted to give her a full-throated sermon on the perversion of that motif in a world where people can see in the dark, but then he decides not to."* The sermon, the way he never gives the sermon, the way he *notices* he's not giving the sermon — that's the entire character.

## The Mace of Terror

Vardis's signature magic weapon is a *Mace of Terror*. He wields *fear*. In a party that includes a fairy trickster, a wizard who thinks the multiverse runs on his good judgment, and a fighter actively trying to die, Vardis is the one whose strongest melee implement makes enemies *flee* rather than die. He's also the one who, of the four, is most *terrified of his own god*. The mace is the tell. He understands fear from the inside — Corellon's trust issues are his own — and he weaponises it precisely because he respects how it works. The narration should let this resonance show without naming it.

## Speech Patterns — Wade at the Table

**Wade is the driest of the four** and the second-most-verbose (after Gary). His pre-game small talk is generous — his recruiter dragging out an interview, his dog Nathan on Prozac, his house re-piping project, his Docker home-lab setup — and the warmth is real. He's the social glue at the table.

In play, Wade's humor is **deadpan, pop-culture-flecked, often Pokemon-game-shaped**: *"It might not be super effective"* (lightning on the lightning-immune dragon); *"Yeah, so maybe don't chain lightning the lightning immune dragon. Just, just, you know, minor suggestion."* He commits one-liners with quiet authority: *"Your money is safe. No rug pulls here."* (running the Theranos schtick on the ancient green dragon); *"Hey man, they never expect a full frontal assault."* (subverting the cliché while voicing Vardis's pragmatism); *"All the plans that have ever been, that's one of them."* (dismissing a plan without bothering to argue).

He **engages the mechanics fluently** — HP arithmetic, range math, summoning-Djinn positioning, attack rolls with disadvantage flags. He's tactical without being numbers-first like Dave; he's planful without being scheme-architectural like Gary. He'll quietly suggest the better play (*"Yeah, also, let the dragon be distracted. Use all our legendaries and everything else."*) and let someone else take the credit for it.

The **affectionate-buddy register** is distinctive. Wade says "buddy," "fam," "my best friend" — and means them. The most load-bearing emotional line in the 2025-03-12 VTT is three words: *"My best friend has a death wish."* He didn't theatricalise it. He said it, and kept playing. That cadence — *naming the feeling, then continuing the action* — is the narration template for Vardis.

His **silence is a tool**. When the party is debating an obvious moral compromise, Wade often goes quiet rather than relitigate. The silence reads as consent. He knows it does. He chooses it anyway.

## Speech Patterns — Vardis in Narration

**Dry, terse, surgical.** Vardis in the bible favours short declaratives, single-word verdicts, and the well-placed sardonic one-liner. He doesn't speechify. When he expands, it's almost always because he's about to *justify* something — and the longer the explanation, the more questionable the action.

**Three consistent voices:**

- **Pragmatist verdict** (most common): one or two sentences, often with the word *pragmatic* or its equivalent. *"Pragmatic."* / *"These Margasters are rich and powerful, and we don't want to make enemies of them."* / *"Collateral damage is unavoidable in war."* Delivered flat. He's not arguing; he's *closing the conversation*.
- **Sardonic moral aside** (the conscience-voice): *"That's a legend. Most of the time, the summoning creatures get annoyed, and before you know it, something really awful gets summoned that kills the hero."* / *"I am sure there were good people."* / *"I think your headband is malfunctioning."* The conscience leaks through humor. It rarely leaks through earnestness.
- **Direct address to Corellon** (rare; load-bearing): *"Good thing I chose to follow the light, eh Corellon?"* — said *to* the god, half-rhetorical, half-confrontational. The only voice Vardis uses where he is fully sincere is the one he uses for arguing with his god. The narration should reserve this register for moments when his theology surfaces.

He **does not narrate his interior in his own voice**. The bible occasionally gives the reader his thoughts ("It's almost as if he has a death wish"), but in dialogue, Vardis routes his feelings through:
- A sardonic verdict,
- A pragmatist closing,
- Silence (chosen, noticed),
- Or one of the bird-droppings beats where the universe insults him and he just shrugs.

When the narration needs the reader to feel Vardis's grief or fear or theological vertigo, it should *show* the silence and the chosen non-sermon, not put feelings in his mouth.

> *"My best friend has a death wish."*
> (Combat aside, 2025-03-12. Said flatly. The most direct emotional statement he makes in the entire campaign. The narration should never inflate this.)

> *"These Margasters are rich and powerful, and we don't want to make enemies of them."*
> (Chapter 29, after the Silverymoon expulsion. Pragmatism slid all the way into political compliance. He doesn't even pretend it's moral.)

> (Late campaign, on the 200 civilians trapped with Bob. The point at which "pragmatic" has fully colonised his ethics. Delivered without flinching.)

> *"That's a legend. Most of the time, the summoning creatures get annoyed, and before you know it, something really awful gets summoned that kills the hero."*
> (Chapter 5, warning Orsik about summoning. The early voice of the moral conscience that he later overrides — repeatedly.)

> *"Good thing I chose to follow the light, eh Corellon?"*
> (Chapter 68, after Flamestrike incinerates a Frost Giant. The argument-with-god voice. The closest he gets to prayer.)

> *"Touring the top food places of the Astral Plane."*
> (Chapter 66, laughing about Bob's banishment. The conscience laughs because the alternative is admitting the conscience is gone.)

> *"It might not be super effective."*
> (Combat aside, 2025-03-12. Pokemon-grammar tactical correction, delivered with full politeness. He's the cleric who tells you you're casting the wrong spell *and* tells you not to feel bad about it.)

> *"Your money is safe. No rug pulls here."*
> (Chapter 86 / 2025-02-26, to the ancient green dragon. The full Theranos. Vardis can run a confidence game without breaking stride — and the narration should let it land because the line is *cold*.)

> *"That's a long-term career plan."*
> (Chapter 70, internal/aloud, after Sandesyl outlines her cult. The moment Vardis's heresy stops being defensive and starts being ambitious. Use this beat whenever the narration needs to surface the alt-cult thread.)

## Things He'd Never Say

- **A sincere apology to a Corellon temple.** He'd rather found his own church than walk into one and ask forgiveness. The refused-reconciliation is the load-bearing biography fact; never erase it.
- **Direct moral confrontation with Thistle or Orsik.** He *thinks* the moral objection. He *names* it sometimes in private aside. He does not stage the sermon. The narration should never put him in a "we need to talk" scene with either of them — that's not who he is.
- **An unironic prayer.** When Vardis invokes Corellon, it's either to *use the power* (Eigeron's ghost) or to *argue with the god* (the Flamestrike beat). He does not pray for guidance, comfort, or wisdom. Those days are over.
- **"I'm the moral conscience of this party."** He performs the role; he does not claim the title. Claiming it would require defending it, and defending it would expose the drift. He stays under the title.
- **Long internal monologue in his own voice.** Vardis externalises through verdicts, asides, and silence. If the narration needs his interior, route it through *what he doesn't say* — the sermon he chose not to give, the prayer he chose not to make, the friend he chose not to stop.
- **Anything that sounds like he believes the temples were right about him.** Whatever he says privately, his public voice never concedes ground to the institutions that exiled him. That refusal is the foundation he's building the alt-cult on. The narration must keep it intact.
