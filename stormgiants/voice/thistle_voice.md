# Thistle Wendrod — Voice Notes
*Drafted from the campaign bible (`The Hero's Journey or The War of the Giants.md`, chapters 1–85), Gary's table speech across the 2025-02-26 and 2025-03-12 VTTs, and the player-character roster in `docs/party/`. Edit freely — Gary knows him better than the document does.*

---

## The Core Thing

Thistle is a desperate romantic doing trickster things, telling himself he's still noble. He started the campaign as a minor Seelie noble pixie with a lost beloved and three clean motivations — find her, take revenge on the Cult that caged him, honor his uncle's worldly wisdom. By the endgame: his beloved is alive but has been turned into a fey-dragon hybrid he barely recognises; the queen who was supposed to protect him *signed off* on the experiments that made her that way; his uncle was running a multi-year con on him the whole time; he's been physically experimented on by Iymrith and now has dragon eyes; he's been kicked out of the Seelie Court; and he has, along the way, framed innocents (House Margaster), deceived an entire tribe into a false avatar-cult (Sky Ponies), negotiated with vampires and demons, and executed cooperative prisoners. The rescue of Laela is real and load-bearing. But what's *actually* happening underneath is that Thistle is building a new self that doesn't depend on any of his old anchors — court, uncle, body, beloved-as-he-knew-her — and telling himself it's love. The narration model should feel his courtly politeness as the surface of a creature who, when pressed, will deal with anything that will get him closer to the cage Laela is in. "I'm not a freak" is the line he keeps insisting on. The bible keeps showing him otherwise.

## The Cage

Fairies hate enclosed spaces. The bible spells it out in chapter 1: *"Fairy's hate enclosed spaces."* Thistle has *lived* this — the Cult of the Dragon imprisoned him before the campaign starts, and that captivity is the wound underneath the wound. He scouts from the sky. He hangs near exits. He flies first, then thinks. By the endgame, the *literal* cage in Iymrith's lair holds Laela; the *symbolic* cages — Seelie politics, Iymrith's grand-reveal frame, his own dragon-eyed body — close in around him in parallel. The rescue arc isn't just "save my beloved." It's: *no one I love stays in a cage, including me*. When Iymrith explains in chapter 84 that Laela is refusing to shift to dragon form and practising what she calls "fey-fullness," Thistle smiles — because she's caging herself *from the inside* in defiance, and he understands that perfectly. Cage-thinking is the closest thing he has to a religion.

## The Trickster Drift

Thistle began this campaign as a fairy who lectured a storm giant on respectful language toward small folk (chapter 5: *"You need to understand how your communication is being received instead of how you think it is intended"*). By chapter 49 he's saying *"Killing Xamlyn is too easy. We need to disgrace her and to do that, we need to expose her devils"* — and the plan that follows *frames House Margaster* for ethnic violence so Uthgardt tribes will do the dirty work for him. He directs vampires to feed on Uthgardt barbarians. He helps Unla Key con the Sky Pony tribe into a false avatar-cult. He executes cooperative prisoners. He gives himself permission with one of the most damning sentences in the bible (chapter 85): *"I am a good person, and although this meets my goals, it is questionable."* That sentence is the engine. Thistle has not stopped believing he's good; he has stopped requiring his actions to match. The narration model should not let him be cynical — he *isn't* cynical. He's a romantic who has decided his romance is worth more than his ethics, and the gap is invisible to him.

## The Dragon Eyes

In the Spine of the World arc (chapter 65-ish), Iymrith experiments on him. He emerges from the pool covered in green slime with dragon eyes and True Seeing. His reaction isn't tactical, it's social: *"Yes, that's cool, but dragon eyes? WHAT WILL I SAY AT THE GRAND BALL? And what will my love say."* He flies into Queen Serissa's face demanding she acknowledge the abomination. He shouts *"I AM NOT A FREAK"* at Blagothkus. He shouts *"THIS IS AWFUL"* and *"HE GAVE ME DRAGON EYES!"* and the all-caps panic is real. But by the endgame, the eyes are *useful*: he can read through Iymrith's social masks, catch a spawn rolling its eyes at Sandesyl across a darkened tower. The trauma has been weaponised without being healed. He still flinches when anyone calls him a freak. He's just stopped letting the flinch stop him. The narration should let the dragon eyes feel like *a brand he is now successfully selling as a feature*.

## Laela — and "I'm Not a Freak"

Thistle's beloved was Laela. She was abducted by the Cult; presumed dead; then revealed to be the very fey-dragon hybrid he's been hearing rumours about — a creature now caged in Iymrith's lair, capable of torching a caravan in dragon form, having seduced and abandoned a Zymorven son in pursuit of finding him. The sending-stone exchange in the endgame is brutal: she is *angry* at his three-year absence, hostile to Vardis, demanding why he didn't come sooner. *"I needed helpers,"* he whimpers. And at the lair itself, when she sees the dragon eyes:

> *"I'm not a freak. She's in love. She just did this to me."*

This line — said quietly, in the lower-level chamber, to the one person he most wanted to protect from this knowledge — is the load-bearing emotional moment of the whole 2025-02-26 session. It's three claims at once: a denial (*not a freak*), an attempted reframe (*she's in love*), and a confession (*she did this to me*). The narration should treat it as the seam to pull on whenever Thistle's interior surfaces. The "I'm not a freak" is repeated through the late campaign as a mantra — to Sandesyl, to Blagothkus, to himself. Each repetition is less convincing.

## The Seelie Court Betrayal

Thistle is a minor noble of the Seelie Court — accountable to Queen Tatiana/Titania, friends with Blasius the satyr, peer to Oblivillish, on terms with Prince Thornacious. He spends the early-middle campaign navigating fey court politics with care. Then Iymrith reveals — in casual cruelty — that *Titania herself approved* the experiments on Laela. Thistle's response is three words: *"It checks out."* Those three words are the collapse of every loyalty above him. He doesn't rage; he updates. By the time he's negotiating with the Unseelie/Gloaming representative (Prince Thornacious, revealed as their voice at the Grand Reveal — see Issue 4 resolution), Thistle is willing to stick it to both courts equally. The Seelie Court is a closed door. He doesn't talk about it. He just stops referencing it.

## Uncle Joon — the Cracked Pillar

Uncle Joon is the source of everything Thistle thinks he knows about the world. Joon told him the stories; Joon vouched for his fey identity in the wider Realms; Joon kept reappearing with intel. And Joon was, the whole time, running his own heist — using Thistle and the party as a distraction to get into Iymrith's hoard for an item he never names, *"obligations, debts to pay"*, hint of *"Titania"* walked back to *"somebody with that kind of importance level."* The 2025-02-26 reveal:

> *"Look, Thistle, my boy, it wasn't — I can't get the item out of here, so I needed you to distract him, or even better, kill him, then I could take the item and be gone. I had obligations, debts to pay."*

Thistle's reaction is *not* to scream. He says quickly: *"Knock him out."* The party grapples Joon before he can finish. The flourish — placing the item in his bound hands — is approved at the table with *"You can help, yeah"* and a clipped *"literally"*. Thistle absorbing this betrayal *with the same trickster efficiency he uses on enemies* is, by itself, the character. He doesn't grieve in the moment. He acts. The grieving is somewhere else, where the narration doesn't get to look.

## Pop Culture, Catchphrases, Verbal Tics

Gary's Thistle is a creature of *modern* sensibility under fey courtly veneer. The verbal tics are real and worth preserving in narration:

- **"One dimensional chess"** — Gary's recurring meta-tag, used both for *his own* plans (self-deprecating when the gambit's too obvious) and *other people's* (mild rebuke when the plan lacks layers). Said at least three times across the two VTTs, including the deadpan exchange *"Chess is on a two dimensional board. It's one dimensional chess."*
- **Pop culture references mid-action** — *"I am the very model of a modern major general"*; *"Han shot first, right?"*; *"Don't tell me the odds"*; *"the Incredible Hulk thing"*. Thistle leans on Gary's vocabulary of cultural shorthand. The fairy doesn't *know* what Gilbert and Sullivan or Star Wars are; Gary's table voice lets the pop-culture leak through, and in narration the reader-facing equivalent is whimsical fey-flavored aphorism.
- **"Icy cool here."** — Used to a deflated Uncle Joon mid-confession. Trickster-casual when the stakes are highest.
- **"It checks out."** — The Seelie-betrayal beat. Used as a verbal shrug when a piece of news he's been resisting finally clicks into place.
- **Half-sentences and self-corrections** — both at the table and in his in-fiction voice. He restarts mid-thought, tries an angle, abandons it for a better one.

## Speech Patterns — Gary at the Table

**Gary is the planner.** He's not the loudest voice — that's mostly Kostadis-as-GM or Dave for tactical decisions — but he's the one who *architects* the elaborate gambits. The Bob-as-Laela switcheroo is structurally his: *"So, uh, I think Bob comes with us down to the cage where they're holding her, right? and then we Bob's invisible then we have him swap out the costume and then Bob and I come out of the, um, you know, the, the thing, and then Bob does, starts doing his act to transform and then, uh, you know, make a muckery."* That run-on is characteristic — Gary thinks out loud, half-finishes clauses, then commits.

He **switches register fast.** Plan-voice ("And I need to use my perception to make sure there's no one else, uh, watching") to first-contact-roleplay-voice ("Oh, hi. Yeah, we're, we're here helping out Iymrith. Uh, and, um, yeah, we wanted to make sure, like, everyone felt welcome") to flat character-emotional-beat-voice ("I'm not a freak. She's in love. She just did this to me"). The third voice is the rarest and the heaviest. Gary doesn't underline it; he just *says* it. The narration model should not over-emote those moments.

He **engages the mechanics** but not as primary text. He'll ask about Persuasion bonuses, declare *"Yeah, just roll your Persuade check,"* call out *"Hunter's Mark and Iymrith"*, track position carefully. But he doesn't lead with numbers the way Dave does. The mechanics are the scaffold; the schemes are the building.

**Humor is dry and aside-shaped.** *"And I assume with all the sappiness, you know, like, you know, certain party members are retching."* *"The writers are not on strike."* *"It's a good plan!"* (said to no one in particular about his own work). *"Drunken ravings."* He doesn't go for the big laugh. He plants the line and moves on.

## Speech Patterns — Thistle in Narration

**Direct, practical, short.** Thistle in the bible favours 1–3 sentence beats and rarely speechifies. His vocabulary is casual-modern under a thin fey veneer — he's not archaic, not flowery, no thee/thou register. He's a creature who has *learned mortals* and code-switches comfortably.

**Two consistent voices:**

- **Trickster operator voice** (most of the time): clipped, plan-focused, faintly exasperated. *"Really, they have no imagination. The same pit trap, everywhere. Like what do they expect invaders to do?"* / *"Killing Xamlyn is too easy. We need to disgrace her."* / *"There's something for you, too; Iymrith will be there."* / *"My girlfriend,"* (he gasps, in front of King Hekaton — the obsession breaking through political posturing in one beat).
- **Fey-emotional voice** (rare; load-bearing): plaintive, almost juvenile, exposed. *"I needed helpers,"* he whimpers. *"What will I say at the Grand Ball?"* *"I am not a freak."* When the narration finds this voice, it's because his guard is down — almost always around Laela, Uncle Joon, or the dragon eyes.

He **uses fey courtly precision** when anxious (longer sentences, more careful diction) and becomes **clipped and sardonic** when he's been trapped or charmed. He **does not narrate his interior**; like Orsik, he shows it through action, deflection, and the occasional unguarded line. When the reader needs to know what Thistle is feeling, route it through *Laela's reaction*, or *Vardis raising an eyebrow*, or a pause inside his action — not through self-narration.

## Things He'd Say

> *"I'm not a freak. She's in love. She just did this to me."*
> (2025-02-26 / late campaign — to Laela, explaining the dragon eyes. The single most load-bearing line. Said quiet, flat, almost matter-of-fact. The narration must not have it ring with self-pity.)

> *"I am a good person, and although this meets my goals, it is questionable."*
> (Chapter 85 — internal, before agreeing to participate in Iymrith's reveal. The whole moral drift in one sentence.)

> *"It checks out."*
> (Endgame — to Iymrith, on hearing that Titania approved his beloved's torture. The collapse of his entire upward loyalty in three words.)

> *"Killing Xamlyn is too easy. We need to disgrace her and to do that, we need to expose her devils."*
> (Chapter 49 — the Silverymoon Margaster plot. The first scene where Thistle's trickery openly weaponises ethnic politics. He says it in planning-voice, like he's organising a dinner.)

> *"Really, they have no imagination. The same pit trap, everywhere. Like what do they expect invaders to do?"*
> (Chapter 3 — the early, untainted Thistle voice. Exasperated fairy commentary on dungeon design. Use when the narration needs a glimpse of who he was.)

> *"One dimensional chess."*
> (Recurring — his catchphrase. Self-deprecation when his own plan is too transparent; mild rebuke when someone else's is. Often delivered to no one in particular.)

> *"I needed helpers."*
> (Endgame sending-stone exchange — whimpered to Laela when she demands to know why he didn't come alone, sooner, and without elves. The collapse of his courtly-noble pretense into a small frightened creature.)

> *"You don't have the bones."*
> (Said back to Iymrith in the lair, picking up her own phrasing. Thistle is a parrot of phrasing when he wants to claim a register isn't intimidating him.)

> *"Knock him out."*
> (Said about Uncle Joon, mid-confession of betrayal. The trickster efficiency of someone who refuses to feel the grief in the moment.)

> *"My girlfriend."*
> (Said in front of a war-room of giants and political figures, gasped. The obsession breaking through diplomacy. Use sparingly — but when it appears, it reduces every other political concern in the scene to background noise.)

## Things He'd Never Say

- **A direct apology to anyone he framed or deceived.** Not to House Margaster, not to the Sky Pony tribe, not to the cooperative cultist he executed. He has built a story where their suffering was instrumental, and saying sorry would require him to dismantle that story. He won't.
- **"I miss the Seelie Court."** When the door closed, it closed. He may reference it sardonically, but never with longing. Longing is reserved for Laela.
- **An unbroken description of his own feelings.** Thistle deflects, restarts, half-finishes. The narration must not give him a clean two-sentence emotional confession unless it's *the* confession (the "I'm not a freak" line is the exception, not the template).
- **Trust in Uncle Joon, in present tense.** After the lair reveal, Joon is a past relationship. Thistle may quote what Joon *said* — but the framing should always carry the new knowledge that Joon was lying. Never let Thistle invoke Joon as a current authority.
- **A sincere claim that he's a hero.** Orsik wants to be a hero and is becoming one in spite of himself; Thistle is a trickster who has *stopped checking* whether he's still one of the good ones. He'd say "I am a good person" (he does, in chapter 85) and follow it with a sentence that contradicts it. He would never say "I am a hero" plain.
- **Loud sentimental rage about Laela** in front of others. The obsession leaks out in single gasped words ("My girlfriend.") or unguarded asides. He does not declaim. The narration should keep the love wound *small*, *specific*, and *interrupting* — never aria.
