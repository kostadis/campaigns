# Chapter 53 Candlekeep's Cold Case: The Grygumite Gambit

## Thistle — Pre-Battle Preparations and Banter

The thing about ancient blue dragons is that they don't get ancient by being careless. I knew that. We all knew that. The knowing didn't help.

We were running through the plan again — which is generous phrasing for what was essentially a group of people talking themselves into believing the plan would work. Orsik had that look he gets when he's done the math and doesn't love the answer but has decided to proceed anyway. He turned to Vardis with the deliberate calm of someone delivering news they'd been sitting on.

"Vardis. I have one thing to say to you."

Vardis, who has spent enough time with Orsik to know that *one thing* almost never means one thing, said: "Keep you alive at all costs."

"If you burn your actions trying to heal me," Orsik said, "we will both end up dead."

A beat.

"But," Vardis said, "by definition of healing you, you wouldn't be dead."

I am choosing not to comment on this exchange. I was a prisoner in an enclosed space for longer than I care to remember, and I have developed a high tolerance for circular logic as a survival mechanism. Orsik just said "breathe into that what you will," which is either very enlightened or very tired, and possibly both.

Vardis wanted to know when Orsik had gotten so emo about it.

"I'm just making a comment about the action economy," Orsik said.

I believed him. He says things like *action economy* the way other people say *we're running low on firewood* — practical, resigned, the words of a man who has stopped pretending war is clean.

Someone raised Hero's Feast. I don't remember who; the suggestion materialized into the conversation the way sensible ideas do, having been obviously correct for several minutes before anyone said it out loud.

Vardis went through his spells. *Oh! Uh, no, I do not have it learned.* He said it like he'd found a sock under the bed — mildly surprised, not catastrophically so. Someone pointed out she could swap one in and drop something else. Vardis considered this.

"Let's drop Blade Barrier. I've never used that, never will. I have used Sunbeam. I'm just gonna keep that all around."

Blade Barrier. Gone. Sunbeam, kept. This is how battle preparation actually works: the spells you have used are real; the spells you have never needed are theoretical, and theory doesn't make it into an ancient dragon's lair. She dropped the thing she'd never cast and picked up the meal. I respect the pragmatism.

The feast went around. Vardis announced the outcome with the modest satisfaction of someone who had done a good thing and knew it.

"Everybody gets sixteen bonus hit points — rather, sixteen to their max. I guess it's not quite bonus. It's a little better."

*A little better* is Vardis's version of enthusiasm. Orsik confirmed he could still stack temporary hit points on top of that, and there was a brief negotiation about the exact benefit — maximum, not temporary, twenty-four hours, immune to poison and frightened, resistant to poison — and Vardis wrapped it up with a warmth that made me unreasonably fond of the whole operation: "I think we all will. I mean, if you want to eat my delicious feast."

I ate the feast. Obviously.

Then the actual news landed.

The dragon has clerics who heal her mid-fight. There are also more minions we haven't spotted yet. This was delivered in the cheerful tone of someone who wants you to know the fire is behind the other door too.

Orsik, undeterred, asked if the Tarrasque was coming.

"Because that's what I really want."

"No," said the air around us.

"What kind of chromatic blue dragon can't even summon a Tarrasque?"

This led to several minutes about a first-level Aarakocra and the 2014 edition rules and some scheme involving reduced cattle and a swallow attack and intestinal explosion, which I will not reproduce in full but which I will say was the most cheerful anyone had been since we arrived. Orsik called it creative. He liked it. Vardis wanted to know why anyone would remove that kind of fun. The answer, presumably, is that fun and balance are different things, and the people who care about balance win eventually.

We came back around to the Cube of Summoning. Air elemental or earth? The dragon is immune to lightning — everyone knew that, which is why the question mattered. But an air elemental doesn't do lightning damage. It does thunder.

"Oh, cool," I said, when the clarification landed. "Sorry. There it is."

Air elemental. It can fly. The dragon can't zap it with the thing it's immune to. This is the kind of plan I understand: not elegant, but structurally sound. One dimensional chess, but the board is on fire and we're already behind.

Orsik offered the conch of teleportation around. He didn't intend to use it. He said this with the calm of someone who has already filed their departure paperwork internally. Vardis had no attuning capacity left. I got the distinct impression the conch was looking for someone who might use it to leave, and I didn't say anything about that either.

And then, at last, Bob.

Bob Saget. The Balor. The being of pure demonic violence we had, in a previous and more creative hour, declined to fight by rolling extraordinarily well in a theatrical context that I'm still quietly proud of. Orsik acknowledged it with the tone of a man conceding a point.

"Well, you finally get to use him in combat after all these years."

Bob rushed the dragon. Combat began.

Somewhere below us, Laela was in a cage. I kept the thought small and specific the way you hold something delicate — not gripped, just carried. There was nothing to do with it yet. The feast sat warm in my stomach. The air elemental was incoming. The dragon had healers, and we had Bob, and we had each other, and that would have to be enough.

## Orsik — The Escape Plan and the Call to Battle

The battle was already happening above us. We could hear it — something massive moving, stone cracking, the particular frequency of chaos that means *this is not a drill.*

I was up top. Thistle was not.

Thistle was below, with the girl he'd come to rescue, and the girl — whatever her name was, I'd been briefed but names run together when you're cataloguing assets — had heard the same sounds and reached the obvious tactical conclusion: there was a side exit, and they should use it.

Reasonable. I would have done the same.

"How long have you been dating this girl?" I asked, mostly to Vardis, when word filtered up about Thistle's persuasion attempt. Three. He'd rolled a three.

Vardis had a theory. "I don't think she trusts him in his new, you know, eyes and other draconic features."

Also reasonable. Thistle had been spending a lot of time looking like something that lived in a dragon's lair. Hard to be a convincing rescuer when you've started blending into the décor.

Below, based on what came up to us in fragments and shouted relays, the conversation had a certain geometric quality. She was screaming at him. *We're gonna go up there where the big battle is going to occur? Is that your plan?* The voice carried. She had range.

Thistle's reply, when it came back to us: "One dimensional chess."

Then a brief pause.

"Chess is on a two dimensional board. It's one dimensional chess."

I chose not to comment on that. A man playing one-dimensional chess with his great love in a dragon's lair, five minutes before the final battle, while a demon charges the dragon above them. No note I could add would improve the picture.

She offered him a counter-plan with the blunt common sense of someone who had been caged for a long time and had no patience left for heroics. *You're gonna go up that way and distract them while I go out the other side and we'll meet at the exits.* Clean. Survivable. Humiliating.

Thistle relented. "Alright, we'll do it your way."

Then — and this is the part that actually impressed me — he thought to read her. Twenty-six on the insight. Sharp, when it mattered.

She meant it. She genuinely believed it was the best plan. What she really wanted was for both of them to flee. But if he was committed to going up and fighting, she wasn't going to follow him into the grinding teeth of it. She'd seen enough. The reasoning was sound: *if you don't show up, they might suspect something.* Thistle going up sold the prank. Thistle not going up created a variable.

She gave him a final appeal. *You've come so far to rescue me. Why don't you go one step further?*

Then, from what I'm told, a tearful farewell. She slipped out through the side exit.

Thistle flew up.

I will say this: it worked. His arrival above sold everything. They'd all convinced themselves the whole affair had been a stunt by Bob — that Thistle had gone below to look for his great love and she hadn't been there, and so clearly it was a prank, and here he was, emerging from below, which confirmed the prank, which is the sort of elegant accident you can't plan for. One-dimensional chess, accidentally played on two dimensions.

Somewhere in all of this someone brought up Uncle Joon. The late Uncle Joon, who had apparently been about to tell Thistle something significant about his lineage, right before he died. The door on that particular corridor had closed permanently. We will never know exactly what he was about to say.

Thistle's assessment: drunken ravings.

I left it there.

Bob rushed the dragon.

That sentence deserves its own line. A demon — our demon, the one we summoned and contracted and pointed at this problem like an artillery piece — rushed the dragon.

Combat began.

*Today is good to die*, I thought, and I meant it, and I moved.

## Unla Key — The Battle of the Ancient Dragons Begins

The initiative order went up on the tracker and Iymrith's number sat at the top like a threat made visible. I had done my calculations. I had, in the privacy of my own extraordinary mind, accounted for every variable. Ancient blue dragon, lair full of chromatic competitors, a demon on loan, five storm giants who may or may not be coordinating, and one halfling wizard whose continued existence is the only thing standing between the multiverse and unraveling entropy.

The math, as usual, was not in my favor.

She moved first. Of course she moved first. The kind of creature who has survived long enough to accumulate a title — *Doom of the Desert*, they call her, and she earned it, which is the worst thing about it — doesn't survive by being slow. She crossed the vast floor of that cavern like something purposeful and terrible, and then she made a decision. Not the lightning breath, though it was right there, humming in her throat. She looked at Hekaton, couldn't line up the shot, and chose instead to simply tear into Bob Saget with her claws.

Three hits. I watched Orsik doing the arithmetic out loud.

"90 minus three is 87."

And then she turned invisible.

I'll say this for Bob Saget: he absorbed it. He's a Balor. That's what Balors are for. But watching something that large take that kind of punishment and *stay standing* told me everything I needed to know about what was coming for the rest of us.

Serissa charged. Her kin peeled off toward the flanks. The cavern, which had already been the size of a small city, now had moving parts in every direction — storm giants closing, gargoyles manning a trebuchet on a balcony somewhere above, dragon priests clustered around Iymrith's last known position, and somewhere in the middle of all of it, a Balor who had been promised a performance and was instead getting systematically dismantled.

Orsik summoned the Air Elemental, because of course he did, and directed it into position with the calm efficiency of a man who has recently upgraded his brain and would like everyone to notice. I noticed. I've been noticing for several sessions now and I find it deeply inconvenient.

The gargoyle trebuchet fired on Bob. Orsik said, "Poor, poor Bob," with what I can only describe as genuine mourning. He wasn't wrong. Twelve seconds of combat, and the demon who had terrorized an entire theater company was taking siege fire from a balcony. There's a lesson in there about hubris. I chose not to extract it.

Hekaton vaporized two gargoyles with a lightning blast, knocked out one trebuchet, advanced forty feet. The dragon priests, undeterred by any of this, huddled around Iymrith's general area and channeled healing into her. Ten points. It was insulting in the way that only deeply sincere acts can be.

Then Iymrith came back.

She moved to Bob — visible again, briefly — and hit him with her tail on top of everything else she'd already done. Another twenty-one points. The Balor aura scorched her for thirty in return, which was something, but the exchange rate was not encouraging. Orsik was already eulogizing: *All that time on the astral plane, and he's just — not gonna make it to a third round.* Twelve seconds of combat.

The storm giants cleared the last of the gargoyles with a kind of offhand thoroughness. The other chromatic dragons were advancing on each other — red, green, white, all of them far too busy with their own ancient grievances to bother with a halfling and three companions. *They don't think you're important enough to kill.*

I have complicated feelings about being underestimated. In general, I find it strategically useful and personally offensive. Right now, in this specific cavern, with Iymrith somewhere invisible and a Balor losing structural integrity two hundred feet away, I was prepared to be gracious about it.

"Here's your moment, Unla Key. What are you going to do?"

I was in Pegasus form. I am always in Pegasus form. Twelve-foot wingspan, ninety feet of fly speed, the ability to be somewhere other than where I started. The Nirany School would have called it imprudent, abandoning humanoid form in a combat scenario with this many variables. The Nirany School also never had to personally prevent the unraveling of the multiverse, so I weight their opinions accordingly.

"So when I'm flying, how far can I go?"

"Whatever Pegasus can do."

"90 feet," Orsik supplied.

I oriented myself on the battlefield. The party was clustering there — Vardis already booking it seventy feet toward the fight, Thistle materializing up from the lower passage and moving to match him, the five red mages advancing sixty feet in their wake like they'd been forgotten and were trying to catch up quietly. Which, it turned out, they had been.

*Oh, we didn't add the wizards.*

I find it profoundly validating, as the group's only wizard, that when the situation called for additional wizards, they were immediately misplaced. It confirms my thesis that arcane talent is both essential and chronically underappreciated.

I banked on my Pegasus wings and repositioned — out of the central danger zone, up, above the fray, finding the angle. Around me the cavern churned: Hekaton throwing a boulder with what his own stat block apparently described as a Ballista, which struck Iymrith for thirty-five points and made sense to no one; the dragon priests channeling another thirty-three points of healing into her in response; Giants Two and Three advancing and throwing rocks, one hitting, one missing; the Air Elemental swinging at Iymrith and missing entirely, which felt like a metaphor for something.

Below me, the battlefield was a disaster in the early stages of becoming a different kind of disaster. The kind you survive.

I am, as I have noted, the reason the multiverse continues. This is not a comfortable thing to be. It involves a great deal of finding vantage points above the fray and calculating angles and watching a demon you contractually summoned take seventy-odd points of claw damage from an ancient dragon who hasn't even gotten to her legendary actions yet.

But the planes were counting on me, and I had ninety feet of fly speed and a grimoire that would make grown wizards weep.

I found my position.

## Vardis — The Siege of Iymrith

The Djinn was already materialising when I said it out loud: "All right. Now I actually do something, and you know, by something I mean summon somebody else."

There was fire in the air. There had been fire in the air for about three rounds.

Let me back up.

Iymrith is not a difficult concept to grasp. She is an ancient blue dragon who kills people for architecture — she builds spires out of the bones of her ambitions — and she has been, until today, essentially untouchable. We had a plan. The plan involved Hekaton, his storm giants, five Silverymoon battle mages, a demon of considerable personal grievance, and us. The plan also involved Bob, but Bob is — was — a discussion I no longer need to have. Not my problem anymore.

Iymrith was not untouchable today. Today she was just very, very difficult to touch.

Unla Key opened from about eighty feet up, Pegasus wings spread, looking approximately as reckless as he always does when he's convinced the multiverse requires him to do something idiotic. The debate before he fired was brief.

"Maybe don't chain lightning the lightning immune dragon," I said. "Just, just, you know, minor suggestion."

He landed a Fireball. She burned one of her legendary resistances to halve it, which told us the shape of the problem we were actually dealing with: not whether we could hurt her, but how many shields she had left, and how fast we could peel them off before she killed us all. Ancient dragons are not final bosses because they hit hard. They're final bosses because they make you spend everything you have on their defenses before you're even allowed to start talking about killing them.

Orsik knew this. The headband has been good for exactly one thing, really — Orsik can now tell the difference between a problem and a problem inside a problem. He said it plainly: "Save the high-level spells for later, is what you're saying. Assuming he's alive to cast them at that point."

There it was. Said it himself, didn't even mean it as a joke. The man who told me once, in the back of a Waterdeep garrison that smelled like wet boots and old decisions, that dying in bed would be the worst thing that could happen to him. He'd meant it as a joke then, too.

My best friend has a death wish. I have been carrying this fact for long enough that it doesn't break over me anymore. It just settles.

I put the Djinn on the far side of the dragon to provide flanking. It was the right call. The Djinn proceeded to miss three times in a row.

"Good job, Jin, but at least you're flanking."

At least you're flanking. The consolation prize of a cleric whose god responds to prayers with bird droppings. The Djinn was present, which is more than Corellon has managed recently. I take what I can get. Pragmatic.

Meanwhile, Bob the Balor was absorbing what seemed like a disproportionate share of the dragon's attention, which was either proof of our tactical brilliance or proof that we had placed a demon in harm's way and were now watching him pay for it. Iymrith hit him for enough damage in a single round to fell a lesser creature outright. Then she moved in close, went briefly invisible, and took thirty points of fire damage from Bob's own aura while she was doing it. There is a neatness to that I appreciate. She tried to hide from him and caught herself on fire. The universe contains small mercies.

Serissa threw a boulder. I felt it connect from thirty feet away. A natural perfect strike — the stone hit Iymrith like a second sentence in an argument that was going extremely well. Eighty points of damage. Best single hit of the engagement. The storm giants, when they commit, commit.

Orsik ran. A hundred feet of full-tilt dwarf in plate armor, directing his Air Elemental into flanking position, screaming at Bob the Balor: "KILL HER! KILL HER!"

I added, from what felt like a responsible distance: "Now's your chance! Pull her hands down! Humiliate her!"

I don't know why I said the second part. I think I wanted the dragon to be embarrassed. I think I wanted something to feel personal.

Bob, for his part, did not need the encouragement. He was enraged far past anything resembling rational volition. He hit her twice. I noted it. Catalogued it. Moved on.

The mages were the turning point. Five of them, advancing in formation, and Orsik counting it out loud: "She can burn all her legendary resistances. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 Fireballs."

There was something almost ceremonial about it. Iymrith has five legendary resistances. Five mages. One-to-one correspondence. The universe likes symmetry; I don't have to.

She saved against two. Burned three to survive the rest. Five fireballs for roughly seventy points of fire damage total, and after Unla Key added one final low-level Fireball to make her spend the last resistance — she was through. No more shields. No more automatic survivals. She was now just an ancient dragon slightly below two hundred hit points, in pain, surrounded by enemies, and out of ways to cheat.

She used her last legendary action to kill Bob.

Twenty-one points. That's all it took. Bob the Balor, demon of considerable size and grievance, who took a bus ride to our plane to perform in a theatrical event that never happened, vanished in a puff of smoke.

"Hey," I said. "Now we don't have to deal with any consequences of lying or, you know, swindling him."

Orsik considered this. "He's not really dead, right? He's just at home. This was his, like, bus ride home?"

That's Orsik. The man standing in an active dragon encounter, blood in the air, finding philosophical comfort in demonic metaphysics. The old Orsik would have just said "good riddance." The new Orsik turns it into a question about the nature of the afterlife for extraplanar entities.

I have feelings about the headband. Some of them are complicated.

And then Iymrith breathed.

She opened up in a line — lightning, because she is a blue dragon and that is the word she knows in every language — and the bolt caught Orsik dead center. One hundred and three points of damage. The DC on the Dexterity save was twenty-three. Orsik's Dexterity is his worst save. He would have needed a perfect roll to halve it.

He didn't get one.

He took it all.

"Excellent," Orsik said. The word came out like a flat stone dropped in deep water. Then: "Alright, doesn't she have like four more attacks?"

I looked at him. Still on his feet. Somehow. The armor took some of it, the stubbornness took the rest, and somewhere in the arithmetic of dwarf constitution and willful refusal there was just enough left to keep him upright. The concentration broke — the Air Elemental, caught in the same bolt, simply stopped existing. Dispersed. Gone.

"Aw, well that was a waste," Orsik said.

"Probably dead now," I said, meaning the Elemental.

"Yeah. Yeah, it's dead." A pause. "Okay, that didn't work out so well."

The thing is, I know what he was thinking. Not the tactical math — not the lost Elemental, not the broken concentration, not the question of how many more hits he could absorb before the answer changed. I know because I have watched this man fight his way toward significance for twenty years, and the calculation he was running was not the same one I was running.

He was thinking: *not yet*.

Not *I survived*. Not *this is bad*. Not even *this hurts*.

Just: not yet. Not the one. Not here.

And Iymrith went invisible.

The rocks can't hit her now. The Djinn is at disadvantage. She has shed her shields and is bleeding and furious and currently nowhere — everywhere — in the air above a desert that used to be something else, and the round ended with that particular stillness that falls between the inhale and the exhale of a fight that isn't finished.

I was still standing. Orsik was still standing.

Not yet.
