Thistle came up through the hole like a man who had just lost an argument with someone considerably more sensible than himself.

I'd been standing at the edge of the amphitheater, watching the opening where the fake Laela had come through — the decoy, the bait, the whole theatrical setup we'd run past Bob Saget's patience. The battle had started above. I could hear it: the crack of storm giant lightning, the bass rumble of something enormous objecting to its situation. I wasn't up there. I was down here, watching a hole in the floor, waiting to see if Thistle would come back up with her or come back up alone.

He came back up alone.

I looked at him. He looked at me. I asked him what happened.

He told me.

Laela had heard the commotion — of course she had, you could hear it two rooms away — and had suggested the side exit. The sensible one. The one that didn't lead directly into a dragon's ongoing diplomatic incident. Thistle, to his credit, had tried to talk her out of it.

"One-dimensional chess," he said, reconstructing the conversation with the specific enthusiasm of a man describing a battle he survived but did not win.

He paused.

"Chess is on a two-dimensional board. It's one-dimensional chess."

I didn't say anything. There was a version of me, the old version, the pre-Headband version, who would have had a lot to say about that. The new version just waited.

So Laela had looked at Thistle's plan — *go up through the main entrance, into the chaos, trust the twig-armed stranger who found you in a dragon's lair* — and delivered her verdict:

"We're gonna go up there where the big battle is going to occur? Is that your plan?"

Thistle had apparently not had a compelling counter-argument. Which was, honestly, tactically correct of her. Laela had then produced her own plan, the way people do when they've decided they're the only adult in the conversation:

"You're gonna go up that way and distract them while I go out the other side and we'll meet at the exits."

Thistle had looked at her. Had looked at the exit. Had presumably done whatever internal calculation a man runs when the person he came to save is calmly organizing her own rescue and only needs him to go somewhere dangerous and draw fire.

"Alright, we'll do it your way."

She'd given him one last chance. I could picture it — the look on her face, something between exasperation and gratitude and genuine grief, because she'd clearly understood that Thistle being here at all meant he had burned something significant to get to her, and she wasn't going to waste it on a bad route.

"You've come so far to rescue me," she'd said. "Why don't you go one step further?"

Then she'd slipped out the side. Thistle had come up through the top.

"It's a good plan! I'll see you on that way," he'd told her, apparently, as a parting reassurance.

I nodded slowly. It *was* a good plan. Laela's plan. And Thistle flying back up through the main entrance — back into the teeth of whatever Bob and Iymrith were currently doing to each other — that sold the whole deception. The enemies above would see him and think: he went down there, checked, found nothing, came back. The love of his life wasn't there. Obviously it was a prank by Bob. Obviously Thistle was just another piece of theater.

Sometimes the best operational outcome is the one someone else makes the call on.

Thistle was watching the hole where she'd been, the way you watch a door after someone's walked through it.

"Maybe she'd like to come with us and turn into a dragon. Oh, wait, no."

He said it almost to himself. Some private thread he'd been pulling on underground, something about Laela and what she might be, what Uncle Joon had been about to say before the situation stopped Uncle Joon from saying anything permanently.

He turned the thought over for a moment. Then:

"Drunken ravings."

I looked at him.

"Unfortunately," I said, "Uncle Joon is dead. So we'll never know exactly what he was about to tell you."

I said it gently, for me. Which isn't very gently. But there it was: the door closed, the secret sealed, the man who knew filed under *irretrievable*. I've had a few of those. You collect them, if you live long enough.

Thistle took it the way he takes most things — with his face doing several calculations at once.

Above us, the battle was still going. The plan was running. Laela was out the side exit, which was the intelligent choice, and we had a dragon to finish killing, which was the only choice we'd been building toward since before I could remember clearly.

I put the thought of Uncle Joon away.

We had work.
