There's a thing you learn, moving in the circles I move in — or used to move in, before the circles started closing — about how to hold dread at a distance. You make jokes. You discuss logistics. You debate the finer points of action economy with the same gravity other people reserve for last rites, and you let the planning feel like control, because it's the closest thing you'll get.

We were hours out from Iymrith's lair. Laela was in a cage somewhere underneath it. I was talking about elementals.

"She didn't survive this long by being your average dragon," came the assessment, delivered with the particular flatness of someone who has read the ledger and wants you to understand the number. "There's a reason everybody's going for the treasure trove."

Right. Yes. The treasure trove. That's definitely what we're here for.

I filed this under *things I already know* and moved on to the part where Orsik delivered his pre-battle philosophy to Vardis with the solemnity of a man reading a will.

"Vardis," he said, in the voice he uses when he's decided something is non-negotiable. "I have one thing to say to you."

"Keep you alive at all costs," Vardis supplied, which was either very good or very wrong.

"If you burn your actions trying to heal me, we will both end up dead."

There was a pause. The kind that happens when someone has said something technically correct and spiritually questionable.

"But — by definition of healing you, you wouldn't be dead."

"Dude, that's really dark, I think."

Vardis, to Orsik: "When did you get all emo?"

"I'm just making a comment about the action economy."

He really was. That's the worst part. Orsik has a Headband of Intellect now and he uses it for things like this — clean, tactical assessments of why saving him would get us all killed. I respect it enormously. I also find it very bleak. He's been planning his own heroic death for months. I've been trying not to think about that, the same way I've been trying not to think about the cage.

Logistics. Keep moving.

Hero's Feast came up. Orsik suggested it with the quiet hopefulness of someone who has learned to take his boons where he can.

"That would be amazing," he said.

"I don't think I have it learned," Vardis said, in the tone of someone already reaching for their spellbook.

"You can just change one of your spells to it."

"Is that — what level is this, fifth?"

"Sixth."

Vardis rustled through her options with the focused energy of someone streamlining before a crossing. "Let's drop — we're not fighting vampires anymore, I don't need—" a pause, a reconsideration— "I'll drop Blade Barrier. I've never used that, never will. I *have* used Sunbeam. I'm just going to keep that all around."

Blade Barrier, never used, never will. I think about the list of things I've used that I probably shouldn't have. It's longer than Vardis's list. It's longer than most people's lists. I am a good person, and — well. You know the rest.

The feast happened. Vardis spread her hands with the mild satisfaction of a host whose dish came out right.

"Everybody gets sixteen bonus hit points — or rather sixteen to their *max*. It's a little better than bonus." A beat. "You're immune to poison and frightened. I don't think either of those will come up, but at least you can't be frightened."

I thought: *good, because I am absolutely going to be frightened.* 

I said nothing. Ate my feast. Quietly revised upward my sense of what my body could absorb before it stopped cooperating.

"These are not temporary hit points," Orsik said, with the reverence of a man offered something real.

"Right. It's an actual maximum for twenty-four hours."

"That's awesome. I'll take them."

"I think we all will," Vardis said warmly, "if you want to eat my delicious feast."

I wanted to eat her delicious feast. I also wanted to be somewhere that was not five hours from a dragon who had my girlfriend in a cage. The two things coexisted.

Then: the warning. Delivered with the unhurried certainty of someone who has already thought about the shape of the problem and wants to make sure you have too.

"The dragon has a bunch of clerics around. They'll be healing her during the fight."

Orsik: "The dragon has *healers*?"

"Yes."

A pause, then: "There are more minions that you haven't yet spotted. I just want everybody to know that there are minions."

Great. Minions. Undiscovered ones, specifically. The variety where you find out about them by surviving them.

Orsik absorbed this and did what Orsik does with unwelcome tactical information: pivoted immediately to something worse.

"Is the Tarrasque coming? Because that's what I really want."

"No."

"What kind of chromatic blue dragon can't even summon a Tarrasque?"

There followed a brief academic interlude on the history of the Tarrasque's design flaws — apparently the 2014 version had no ranged attacks, which meant a first-level flying bird-person could simply hover above it indefinitely and peck it to death, which is the kind of thing that makes you wonder about the people who originally wrote the monster. The 2024 version cleaned this up. So did the popular technique involving reduced cattle enlarged from the inside.

"You could cast Reduce on a whole bunch of cattle," Orsik explained, with the genuine appreciation of someone filing this away for future use, "and the Tarrasque would swallow them, and then they'd re-enlarge in the gut and explode it from the inside."

"Why would you remove that kind of fun?" Vardis said.

I thought: *one dimensional chess, all of it*. You reduce the cattle. The plan is technically sound. Nobody asks whether the cattle had plans of their own.

I am a good person.

Moving on.

Cube of Summoning. Orsik turned it over in his hands, the particular posture of a man who has prepared for this.

"Air, earth, fire, or water?"

"Air. They can fly."

"Is the elemental's damage incompatible with a Blue Dragon?"

Lightning immunity, it turned out, was the dragon's only restriction in that department. The Air Elemental hits with thunder. Different category entirely.

"Oh, cool," I said. "Sorry. There it is."

"Air makes sense. Air it is."

These are the conversations that keep the mind occupied. Elemental damage types. Spell slot levels. Who carries the Conch of Teleportation into a fight with an ancient dragon. Orsik offered it around the table; Vardis was already carrying as much as she could hold. He looked at me.

"I think you should take it. I'm definitely not going to use it."

*I'm definitely not going to use it* from Orsik means: *I intend to be here until I am not, and I have made my peace with that*.

I didn't say that. I took the conch.

And then — finally, the part I'd been quietly dreading in a different way — Bob.

"Since you guys refused to fight Bob when I had him as the hero of the play," came the assessment, with the patience of someone who has been waiting a long time to say this, "and you did that really lame-ass thing—"

"Rolling really well," Orsik supplied. "Rolling a bunch of natural twenties or something."

"Well, you finally get to use him in combat after all these years."

Bob Saget. Demon. Technically our contractor. The creature we acquired, deployed as theatrical cover, and then declined to let die in a blaze of glory because we needed him to complete a performance he would have preferred to skip. We owed him a fight. Apparently we were delivering.

"Alright," came the word, carrying the weight of something that had been building for a while. "Bob rushes the dragon. Combat begins."

I was holding a Conch of Teleportation I had no intention of using, standing inside a body with dragon eyes that I kept telling people was fine, eating the magical feast of a cleric whose god had recently covered her in bird droppings, about to fight an ancient blue dragon with healing support and undiscovered minions, in order to rescue the woman I loved from a cage somewhere below.

One dimensional chess.

It's a good plan.
