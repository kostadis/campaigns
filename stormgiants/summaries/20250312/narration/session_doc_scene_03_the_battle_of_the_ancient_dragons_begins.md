The initiative order went up on the tracker and Iymrith's number sat at the top like a threat made visible. I had done my calculations. I had, in the privacy of my own extraordinary mind, accounted for every variable. Ancient blue dragon, lair full of chromatic competitors, a demon on loan, five storm giants who may or may not be coordinating, and one halfling wizard whose continued existence is the only thing standing between the multiverse and unraveling entropy.

The math, as usual, was not in my favor.

She moved first. Of course she moved first. The kind of creature who has survived long enough to accumulate a title — *Doom of the Desert*, they call her, and she earned it, which is the worst thing about it — doesn't survive by being slow. She crossed the vast floor of that cavern like something purposeful and terrible, and then she made a decision. Not the lightning breath, though it was right there, humming in her throat. She looked at Hekaton, couldn't line up the shot, and chose instead to simply tear into Bob Saget with her claws.

Three hits. I watched Orsik doing the arithmetic out loud.

"90 minus three is 87."

And then she turned invisible.

I'll say this for Bob Saget: he absorbed it. He's a Balor. That's what Balors are for. But watching something that large take that kind of punishment and *stay standing* told me everything I needed to know about what was coming for the rest of us.

Serissa charged. Her kin peeled off toward the flanks. The cavern, which had already been the size of a small city, now had moving parts in every direction — storm giants closing, gargoyles manning a trebuchet on a balcony somewhere above, dragon priests clustered around Iymrith's last known position, and somewhere in the middle of all of it, a Balor who had been promised a performance and was instead getting systematically dismantled.

Orsik summoned the Air Elemental, because of course he did, and directed it into position with the calm efficiency of a man who has recently upgraded his brain and would like everyone to notice. I noticed. I've been noticing for several sessions now and I find it deeply inconvenient.

The gargoyle trebuchet fired on Bob. Orsik said, "Poor, poor Bob," with what I can only describe as genuine mourning. He wasn't wrong. Twelve seconds of combat, and the demon who had terrorized an entire theater company was taking siege fire from a balcony. There's a lesson in there about hubris. I chose not to extract it.

Hekaton vaporized two gargoyles with a lightning blast, knocked out one trebuchet, advanced forty feet. The dragon priests, undeterred by any of this, huddled around Iymrith's general area and channeled healing into her. Ten points. It was insulting in the way that only deeply sincere acts can be.

Then Iymrith came back.

She moved to Bob — visible again, briefly — and hit him with her tail on top of everything else she'd already done. Another twenty-one points. The Balor aura scorched her for thirty in return, which was something, but the exchange rate was not encouraging. Orsik was already eulogizing: *All that time on the astral plane, and he's just — not gonna make it to a third round.* Twelve seconds of combat.

The storm giants cleared the last of the gargoyles with a kind of offhand thoroughness. The other chromatic dragons were advancing on each other — red, green, white, all of them far too busy with their own ancient grievances to bother with a halfling and three companions. *They don't think you're important enough to kill.*

I have complicated feelings about being underestimated. In general, I find it strategically useful and personally offensive. Right now, in this specific cavern, with Iymrith somewhere invisible and a Balor losing structural integrity two hundred feet away, I was prepared to be gracious about it.

"Here's your moment, Unla Key. What are you going to do?"

I was in Pegasus form. I am always in Pegasus form. Twelve-foot wingspan, ninety feet of fly speed, the ability to be somewhere other than where I started. The Nirany School would have called it imprudent, abandoning humanoid form in a combat scenario with this many variables. The Nirany School also never had to personally prevent the unraveling of the multiverse, so I weight their opinions accordingly.

"So when I'm flying, how far can I go?"

"Whatever Pegasus can do."

"90 feet," Orsik supplied.

I oriented myself on the battlefield. The party was clustering there — Vardis already booking it seventy feet toward the fight, Thistle materializing up from the lower passage and moving to match him, the five red mages advancing sixty feet in their wake like they'd been forgotten and were trying to catch up quietly. Which, it turned out, they had been.

*Oh, we didn't add the wizards.*

I find it profoundly validating, as the group's only wizard, that when the situation called for additional wizards, they were immediately misplaced. It confirms my thesis that arcane talent is both essential and chronically underappreciated.

I banked on my Pegasus wings and repositioned — out of the central danger zone, up, above the fray, finding the angle. Around me the cavern churned: Hekaton throwing a boulder with what his own stat block apparently described as a Ballista, which struck Iymrith for thirty-five points and made sense to no one; the dragon priests channeling another thirty-three points of healing into her in response; Giants Two and Three advancing and throwing rocks, one hitting, one missing; the Air Elemental swinging at Iymrith and missing entirely, which felt like a metaphor for something.

Below me, the battlefield was a disaster in the early stages of becoming a different kind of disaster. The kind you survive.

I am, as I have noted, the reason the multiverse continues. This is not a comfortable thing to be. It involves a great deal of finding vantage points above the fray and calculating angles and watching a demon you contractually summoned take seventy-odd points of claw damage from an ancient dragon who hasn't even gotten to her legendary actions yet.

But the planes were counting on me, and I had ninety feet of fly speed and a grimoire that would make grown wizards weep.

I found my position.
