## Vardis — Arrival At Iymrith S Lair

The lair smelled like ozone and old bone. That particular combination — the blue-white crackle of a storm dragon's presence pressed into every stone, undercut by something older and charred — was the kind of air that tells you, before your eyes have adjusted, that you are somewhere you should not be. I noticed it immediately. I am a Light cleric. I have walked into burning buildings with less concern. I noticed it.

Iymrith was waiting for us.

She was the size of a bad decision that has had several centuries to compound interest. She looked at us the way a collector looks at pieces she has already paid for and intends not to damage before the auction. "So, you've arrived," she said, and the words had the warmth of a verdict.

Thistle confirmed that yes, we were here to fulfill our bargain, which was technically true in every way except the important one. I stood slightly behind the others and ran a professional eye over the architecture. Exits: limited. Stone ceiling: high enough for a dragon to maneuver. Sightlines: very much in her favor. Expected. I kept my face pleasant.

She told us the guest list was impressive, that it was really important this go off without a hitch. She used the word *important* like a threat dressed in manners, which is a skill I respect even when it is aimed at me. I was already cataloguing the space — the upper chamber, the lower passages, the sightlines from the entrance — doing the arithmetic that has kept me alive long enough to be excommunicated from every major institution that once employed me.

Pragmatic.

"Will your high school bully be here?" I asked.

It seemed like a reasonable deflection. Get her talking, get her comfortable, find the edge of the ego and apply gentle pressure. Standard method. What I did not account for was the sincerity.

"No. No, no, no, I ate her."

I let a beat pass. "Don't want to show her what a success you've become?"

"No, I enjoyed chewing her bones and spitting out her flesh. Why? Is there something else?"

I said something about rubbing it in — showing off the success — which I will admit was a gamble on whether ancient blue dragons understand the concept of *social humiliation as a motivational framework*. They do not, apparently. They have a different framework.

"Every time I want to think about what a success I am, I just fantasize about that meal."

She said it without a flinch. She said it like it was obvious. She looked at me with those enormous, terrible eyes and said *is there something else I should have done?* with the tone of someone genuinely puzzled that there might be another metric.

I had nothing. I, Vardis, exiled cleric of Corellon, veteran of more campaigns than I can count on fingers and toes combined, man who once talked down a Frost Giant by explaining the theology of hospitality at considerable length — I had nothing.

Thistle, bless the fairy, stepped in. "You don't have the bones," Thistle said.

Iymrith went very still. "No," she said, mournfully. "No, I do not have the bones."

She collected herself almost immediately — *of course it wasn't quite high school* — and moved on, but something had passed between us in that moment, some shared understanding that the bones were the real subject of this entire enterprise and nobody in this room was going to say it plainly. Useful. I tucked it away.

Then came the deadline. She needed the performance tomorrow. If it didn't happen, someone was going to be burned to a crisp. She delivered this with the apologetic air of a hostess explaining a dietary restriction.

Orsik — because Orsik has a death wish and at some point I stopped being surprised by it — said, "Can it be me?"

Three words. My best friend has a death wish, and he will use the most dangerous creature on the Sword Coast as the delivery mechanism for his own darkest jokes. I have been carrying this fact for weeks now like a stone in my boot that I have decided, for complex theological reasons, not to remove.

Iymrith turned to look at him with an expression I can only describe as *considered menace*. She had heard about his goals in life, she said. He was not dying. In fact, she would personally ensure he never died from a dragon, even if she had to protect him from dragons herself. *The real punishment.*

I do not know what the SEER offered Orsik that he refused, but Iymrith had just accidentally reinvented it from the other direction and Orsik's face went through several interesting stages. The irony was not wasted on me. The man who wants to die heroically is now promised immortality by the creature he intends to die fighting. All the plans that have ever been, that's one of them.

We were asked about rehearsal time. Thistle said the show was improv. Unla Key said the show was improv. I said, flat as I could manage, "It'll have to be," which was as close to a prayer as I was willing to offer in that room.

Iymrith was visibly uncomfortable with this answer.

I tried the method acting approach. I needed to understand her theology, I explained, in order to be properly in character for my role. This was not entirely a lie — I did want to understand her theology, primarily to find the load-bearing assumptions and pull on them. She shut it down without ceremony. *I don't have time for that.* We pressed. *We have time. Performance is tomorrow.* She moved on. *This is—* She was already walking.

We followed, because that is what you do in the lair of a creature that can reduce you to ash with weather.

She led us through chambers that explained things without being asked. The scale of the place. The stone carved to accommodate something vast. Here and there: evidence of the Grand Reveal in progress — arrangements, preparations, the infrastructure of a scheme centuries in the making. I looked at all of it the way a doctor looks at a wound: noting what I see, calculating what it means, deciding what can still be fixed.

Then we passed the Yuan-ti.

A group of them, arranged before a statue of Iymrith, prostrated in worship. She told us to pay no attention — they were just part of the group that worships her. Orsik, who was wearing his Headband of Intellect and occasionally forgets that the headband does not include tact, pointed out that they were worshipping a statue of her rather than her directly.

She said she liked to redirect. Didn't want all the glory going to her. The theological inconsistency of this position — a god encouraging proxy worship by proxy object — is something I would normally have eight minutes of opinions about. I looked at the Yuan-ti, looked at the statue, thought briefly about the alternative believers' cult I have been building in the back of my mind since Sandesyl showed me what long-term career planning looks like, and said nothing.

She took us lower.

Laela was in a magical contraption she could not escape. I looked at it, looked at Thistle's face when Thistle saw it, and updated the order of priorities. Getting Laela out of that device was going to be whatever we did next.

But first, Iymrith wanted her monologue.

She tapped her claws against the stone — a sound like a bridge deciding whether to hold — and asked when the performance would begin. She wanted to see the script. She had been promised many lines. She wanted a monologue. This was her moment of triumph, she said. She wanted to show all of those who had doubted her and all of her investors that she had achieved the impossible.

I watched her say it and had an unexpected, uncomfortable thought: *I understand this.* Not the centuries of manipulation, not the eaten bully, not the Yuan-ti and the scheme and the assembled guest list. Just that specific hunger — the one that says *I will show you what I've built out of what you threw away.* The temples exiled me and I walked out and I have been building something in the space they left. Different scales. Roughly the same fuel.

I did not say this aloud. Some sermons you decide not to give.

Thistle redirected elegantly: why not go practice the monologue with Orsik as director, in the stadium, while Thistle worked with Laela here to get her ready? Iymrith asked who the director was and then immediately answered herself. "Are you the director? Yes. Orsik is the director."

"Definitely," said Orsik, which had the particular flavor of a man who has just been handed a job that will allow him to be alone with the ancient dragon he intends to die fighting.

Before they could leave, Orsik noted — with an expression of artistic sincerity that I have never once seen him deploy in genuine situations — that Laela probably couldn't rehearse from inside the magical contraption. "Wasn't that right, Thistle?"

Iymrith began to object. Orsik escalated to artistic outrage. *Call it off. Forget it. My artistic vision is being compromised.* Thistle appealed to her investment in the show. The whole thing was the most committed piece of improvised theater I have watched anyone perform, and I watched it with the part of my brain that is always taking notes.

Iymrith folded. The monologue would go down in the epics of monologues. Orsik was an artist. Laela could be out. She could be out.

And just like that, Laela was free of the contraption, the costumes were confirmed, and the dress rehearsal was underway.

I looked around the lair — its scale, its depth, the exits I'd catalogued, the stone ceiling, the Yuan-ti, the scale of Iymrith's long investment in her own mythology — and made the silent inventory of everything that needed to go right tomorrow. It was a long list. The column of things that could go wrong was longer, and some of the entries had my best friend's name next to them.

Pragmatic, I told myself.

I have been telling myself that for a very long time.

## Thistle — The Rescue Plan And Rehearsal

My uncle used to say that a good plan has one moving part. He said it while running three simultaneous cons, so perhaps take that as the cautionary version.

The plan had Bob.

Bob was a demon. Bob was, by application of a Polymorph scroll acquired through methods I am not going to document here, now wearing Laela's face. Bob was going to stand in a cage in front of the assembled chromatic dragon families, the fire giants, the cloud giants, the Red Wizards of Thay, and apparently Prince Thornacious — who I had last seen at a perfectly pleasant fey gathering and who had since become, as it turned out, the Gloaming Court's representative at a dragon's grand political theater piece — and Bob was going to transform, magnificently, while Laela walked out a side passage and I pretended the whole thing had been my idea all along.

I checked the corridors first. Old habit. Fairies hate enclosed spaces; we hate them more when there might be something listening in them.

"And I need to use my perception to make sure there's no one else, uh, watching," I said.

Vardis, to his credit, said: "You are the perceptive one among us."

The corridors were clear.

So I told her.

"Alright. The plan is: we hired a body double. Okay. And this body double is going to jump out, pretend to be you, transform, and meanwhile, be Skidaddle."

She looked at me for a long moment.

Then Laela said, very carefully, as though testing whether the words would fall apart if she said them too fast: *"So how do you intend to escape when all of the dragons will have their eyes on the body double?"*

"We just go out that way."

*"So while they've got the eyes on the body double, we go out that way. That way."*

"Where they're not looking."

She didn't know whether to laugh or cry. Honestly, looking at her face, I'm not sure she knew either. She stood in that cage looking at me the way you look at someone who has brought you something you desperately needed in completely the wrong container, and you are trying to decide if it's still good.

Vardis was not present for this exchange. I learned later that he had observed, to no one in particular: "Of all the plans that have ever been, that's one of them."

He's not wrong. It was definitely a plan. It had sequential steps and everything.

Then came the complication she'd been holding.

*"So, she pauses and says — could you just explain to me what you promised Iymrith?"*

"Well, there's one thing Iymrith wanted," I said.

*"Yes."*

"Which was — persuade you to do this Incredible Hulk thing."

*"Turning into a dragon is what it was."*

*"She goes, I can't do that."*

"Doesn't matter, you don't need to. Body double will."

She accepted this. I moved on before she could think about it too hard.

Then she looked at my eyes.

It's the dragon eyes. They find them. Everyone finds them eventually. The True Seeing is useful; the vertical pupils are harder to explain at parties. I'd told myself I'd gotten used to people's reactions. Blagothkus calling me a freak. Queen Serissa's barely-concealed wince. I'd gotten used to the flinch.

What I hadn't gotten used to was the idea of Laela flinching.

*"Thistle, what are you?"*

"I'm not a freak." The words came out fast. "Iymrith did this to me."

*"What has she done to you?"*

Her voice when she said it. That's the part I'm not describing in detail.

*"I want her dead."*

"I want that too. But like, I'd really rather survive this."

She steadied. Something settled behind her eyes — the look I remembered, the one that meant she was setting a thing aside for later without letting it wreck the present.

*"Yes. Well, you know, there's something to be said for survival."*

It's an ugly little sentence. I've been saying a version of it to myself for a while. Good to hear it back.

We moved to logistics, which helped.

The guest list was alarming enough to be almost funny. The five chromatic dragon families. Fire giants. Cloud giants. Frost giants. Storm giants — yes, she confirmed, storm giants too. And:

*"Iymrith also said that, you know, later today I would get to meet a representative of the Fae."*

"Oh, you mean me, right?"

*"No, no, it was — who was that twit that we met at that party?"*

"You mean Prince Thornacious?"

*"Yes, Prince Thornacious. I was really hoping he would come to do something for us. Do you think he will?"*

I kept my expression very pleasant. "We've seen him around. He does stuff sometimes."

What I did not say: I know exactly what Prince Thornacious is doing here, and it is not helping us, and if Laela is still counting on Seelie Court protection, someone needs to have that conversation with her and it is not going to be tonight.

*"And then he said there would be some surprise guests, some new alliance that he's made—"*

"Oh, the Red Wizards of Thay?"

*"Oh, yes, they would be there too."*

One dimensional chess. The whole thing. Iymrith had been playing it for years and calling it a grand scheme.

The inspect-before-the-show wrinkle required some actual planning. Laela would be present for the detect-magic pass — she had to be; pulling her early would trigger exactly the kind of scrutiny we couldn't afford. Then the inspectors would clear out, the show would begin, Iymrith would take the stage to deliver her monologue, and in that window we'd make the swap.

"When do we swap Bob out?" I asked.

Orsik had clearly run this geometry already. "After they leave, at the beginning of the — as the show is starting, right? Iymrith's up there as MC, whatever, and you're down here with Bob, and there you go. She'll be fine."

"But they need to check out Laela."

"Yeah."

"They check out Laela before the show."

"They check out Laela before the show," Orsik agreed, with the patience of someone who has explained something twice and is now explaining it a third time by simply repeating the words in the same order, "then they all go up. Then you do the switch, after they've left, right?"

Right.

The rehearsal I was not present for — or rather, present for in the way a very small winged creature can be present for something happening in a large room, which is to say: at a distance, watching the dwarf attempt to be a theatrical director.

"Iymrith, why don't you give this monologue a shot? Try it out. Try it out on me."

She did. The first pass was exactly what you'd expect from an ancient dragon who has spent several thousand years being unilaterally terrifying and has never once required an audience's sympathy. Every word landed like a siege weapon. The cadence was wrong in the way that only something technically correct can be wrong.

"Okay, that was good. Now, lower your voice, quieter, and read it faster."

"More natural," Vardis added.

"Quicker? No, no, no. Faster."

Iymrith ran it again.

And — this is the part that bothers me, in a low-grade way I haven't fully examined — it was *better*. Noticeably. The second pass had what the first one lacked. Vulnerability, almost — like a creature that had wanted something and was finally getting to say so out loud.

"Much better! You got it. Just keep working on that."

*"She goes, you're a genius."*

"It's going to be amazing tomorrow."

*"This is going to be awesome."*

"It is going to be awesome."

The thing about Iymrith is that she genuinely meant all of it. That is, in its own way, the most unsettling part of her.

Earlier — before the rehearsal, while we were still setting up — she had said, with what I can only describe as genuine wistfulness: *"I'm really going to be disappointed if I have to kill you. But it will have been a good monologue. Just one last monologue."*

One last monologue. She was moved by her own material. I suppose that's something.

Orsik, who had apparently established something called *artistic freedom* in a previous conversation I'm choosing to believe went exactly as absurdly as this one, pressed the point again.

"Iymrith, let me explain to you how it works, right? Like, you hired us to do you a service, to put on a show for you. And I need the artistic freedom in order to, you know, make this happen for you. I don't want you putting any sort of constraints on me or compromising that vision — sometimes I need to do things and I can't always explain to you why. It's just the way it is. We had this discussion yesterday and you, you can't, you understood and you came around. Do we need to have the same discussion again?"

*"No, no, no. All right."*

"Thank you."

*"All right."*

"This is gonna be great. You're doing really well on your monologue."

She permitted us to meet the arriving guests. Not all of them — she drew a line at *all* of them — but the dragons first, Orsik announced, because they were the most powerful, and then the giants, and Vardis offered to cover other ground, and I stood in that lair and let the planning wash around me while my mind ran the other track.

The inspection would happen. The swap would happen. And somewhere below us, in a cage that smelled like stone and old sorcery, Laela was waiting with dragon eyes of her own and a three-year list of things to say to me.

The show hadn't started yet.

## Unla Key — Schmoozing With The Chromatic Dragons

The young red dragon who intercepted Orsik looked down at him the way you look at something that crawled under a door — with mild disgust and the faint hope it would reverse course on its own. I hung back and watched, which is where I do my best thinking.

The relay came back crisp: *I was promised something special and all I smell is a tiny, sniveling dwarf.*

Flume. Right. I'd had about thirty seconds to form an opinion of Flume and already she was my favorite person in the room — which is a low bar when the room contains Iymrith, but credit where it's due. The contempt was architectural. She'd built a whole room in her head for people who didn't clear her threshold, and Orsik had just been handed the key.

Orsik, to his credit and occasional my sincere alarm, walked straight through.

"I'm just going to clue you in on everything that's going to happen tomorrow ahead of schedule," he said, with the particular warmth of a man who has decided he is charming. "Give you all the information before, you know—" a gesture that implied *the riffraff*.

A pause from Flume's end. Then: *Oh, you are going to give me — you're going to tell me about this show, this spectacle?*

"That's right."

*Iymrith said you were some kind of spectacular director.*

"Yeah, have you ever been to Silverymoon? I can neither confirm nor deny those rumors."

*I can confirm nor deny,* Flume agreed, which is not a sentence, technically, but I understood it perfectly — the agreement of someone who has lived long enough that the laws of grammar have stopped applying to her through sheer accumulated precedent.

"Right, exactly," said Orsik. And then, without any perceptible shame: "You're awesome, you're amazing, thank you for coming. You're getting the special treatment nobody else gets, we love you, you're, you know."

I watched an ancient red dragon receive this like it was her due. Because it was. That's the thing about flattery — it only embarrasses you if you think you might not deserve it. Flume clearly had no such uncertainty.

*Flume,* she said. *That's my name. My name's Flume. I have a much longer name, but it's Flume.* A pause, philosophic. *Once you reach a certain age, and a certain power, and a certain respect, you don't need fancy names. A simple name is all they need.*

"It's genius," said Orsik.

*You have all these other red dragons with these names, like the red beater, or the — something that's twenty-eight syllables that nobody can remember. But when you're, when you're — when you're me — you need something simple, something so that people can remember it.*

Useful. I made a note. Flume wasn't vain in the usual sense — she wasn't preening. She'd simply arrived at the conclusion that she was the standard against which other things were measured, and she was explaining the world to someone who hadn't caught up yet. That's a very specific psychology. I know it because I share it. We got along fine.

Iymrith made her remarks before the crowd settled properly. There would be no sampling of her creations, she said, and then she looked specifically at Flume: *Last time I did one of my exposés, there was hearts everywhere. It was a mess.*

Flume waved a claw. The claw said: *I maintain my right to sample and also to be offended you brought it up.*

---

Vardis had drifted to the green dragon, which I noticed the way you notice a colleague taking the bad meeting so you don't have to. Ancient green dragons are, as a category, exhausting — too intelligent for their own good, too paranoid to enjoy anything, constitutionally incapable of accepting reassurance. This one arrived early specifically to check for tricks, which is either diligence or neurosis and in this case probably both.

*I wanted to make sure there weren't any tricks going to be played,* she told Vardis. *I had to check out the merchandise myself.*

"What sort of tricks might be played that you're worried about?"

*Well — maybe the fairy will be an illusion or a phantasm. Or maybe the creature that is a fairy is actually a dragon right now, and she'll cast a spell while we're not looking, and voila, we'll have a dragon.*

Which is, I have to say, exactly the trick Iymrith is running — in reverse, through a demon, with a polymorph scroll — so the green dragon wasn't wrong to be suspicious, just wrong about the direction. I respected the analytical instinct even as I appreciated the error.

Vardis managed her beautifully. *Pretty sure that's the real deal here,* he said, and the green dragon exhaled something that was not quite reassurance.

*Maybe she is, and maybe she isn't. As far as I'm concerned — I have been waiting for her to do this for too long. She has taken too much of my money to conduct these experiments, and as far as I'm concerned, if she does not pay out this time, there will be hell to pay.*

Vardis turned just slightly — not enough to be rude — and said, "Interesting. This one doesn't need much persuasion."

She didn't. She'd already persuaded herself. She wanted to be angry at Iymrith; she'd been saving that anger for years like a dragon saves gold; she just needed the performance to fail and she'd detonate entirely. All Vardis had to do was keep the expectations high enough that failure became inevitable.

"You're not familiar with the Hulk, are you?" he said, which — genuinely — I could not have predicted from him. "Iymrith has a creature that can transform into unbelievable power. And we'll do so for you on stage. That's the claim. I'm sure you won't be disappointed. Get your hopes as high as possible."

*Well,* said the green dragon, with the dignity of someone who has decided to be cautiously optimistic against her better nature. *If it's everything that Iymrith has promised, it will have been worth the investment. Finally, I will have a weapon I can use.*

"Your money is safe," Vardis told her. "No rug pulls here."

Orsik, somewhere behind me, murmured something appreciative about Theranos. He wasn't wrong.

Which left the black dragon. Obsidianth. I'd been watching him from across the room, doing the initial read — big, dark, smug in the way of someone who thinks they've outplayed everyone in a game the other players don't know they're losing. The kind of person who confuses complexity for power. I had a theory about him.

I relayed, helpfully, that the red and green dragons had been talking about him. Not warmly.

"Well no," I said, "it's more that the red and the green dragon were ragging on you."

*They're just jealous that I have the biggest operation,* Obsidian announced. *They don't understand the myth. I have spent centuries outmaneuvering them, and now my clan controls most of the trade.*

"Trade?" said Orsik. A beat. "Really? Trade? I mean, murder, like — rampage, bloodletting — that's cool, but trade? Like, really? Isn't that what the Zhentarim do?"

*I'm not the head of the Zhentarim,* Obsidian said, with the precise tone of someone who has practiced this distinction. *But I get paid to work for them.*

"Oh!" Orsik lit up. "You're like a capo. You're like — you're like a—"

I saw the conversation going sideways and tried a different angle. "Do you think one day you'll be as powerful as the giants?"

*I don't want to talk to you,* said Obsidian.

And then he left.

Vardis said, with admirable evenhandedness: "That one might need some later persuasion."

He might. Or he might just be the kind of mark who needs to feel ignored before he comes back around. I've seen it at card tables — the player who folds on principle and then can't stop watching the hand. You leave the seat open and you wait.

## Orsik — The Fire Giant Confrontation

The fire giants.

Of all the factions milling around Iymrith's Grand Reveal — cloud giants, frost giants, stone giants, whatever Thornacious was representing — I picked the fire giants.

"These are the ones that we stole the thing from," I said, as if stating it out loud might make someone talk me out of it.

It didn't. Of course it didn't.

"Like it's literally them." I looked at the four henchmen flanking Duke Zalto and ran a quick inventory of the situation. Same species. Same general geographic origin. We had walked into their fortress, caused what I would describe as a spirited amount of mayhem, looted their private quarters, and left without formally introducing ourselves.

On the other hand, fire giants are not exactly known for their record-keeping.

"We just caused mayhem in their lair and ran out and they don't know who hit them," I said. This was probably true. Probably.

The plan, such as it was, arrived the way my better plans usually arrive now — not in a flash of inspiration but in the slow assembly of available parts. We had infiltrated Ironslag. We had been in their bedchambers. And fire giant bedchambers, it turned out, were not without character.

"Did we find, like, questionable things in their bedchambers?" I asked.

Thistle confirmed we had.

"We could bring that up," I said. "That would be awkward."

Thistle is faster than me when it comes to the architecture of a con. "Wait — we found this thing in Iymrith's hoard, like, somehow tie Iymrith to it?"

That was it. That was the thing.

"That would be pretty persuasive." I nodded once. "Thistle, this one's you, man."

This is the other thing the Headband has done for me, besides making me uncomfortably aware of my own thinking: it's made me better at identifying who in the room has the right tools for the job. Thistle is a professional liar. I am a professional blunt instrument. The correct allocation was obvious.

Duke Zalto was sitting with his four henchmen, occupying the kind of space that very large things occupy — the room arranged around him by necessity rather than design. He looked at us the way fire giants always look at things: like he was deciding whether to eat it or step on it.

"So that's your cue," I told Thistle.

Thistle launched in. Something about being Iymrith's assistants, making sure everyone felt welcome, very particular tastes, Iymrith had preserved something especially for him —

"What is it that you hand to him?" I asked, because I had genuinely lost track.

Thistle was consulting some internal list. I stepped in.

"You could just, like, give them some whips or something. Like, Iymrith brought these, she had these for you, she knew you would like them. Something like that."

"Yeah," said Thistle. "There we go."

And then Duke Zalto, ruler of the fire giants, commander of Ironslag, one of the most powerful beings in the ordning — looked at the whips, and his expression shifted into something I can only describe as fond.

"Iymrith, she always knows what I like."

I'm going to note for the record that this was not the intended outcome.

He was practically glowing. Not literally — well, actually also literally, fire giant — but emotionally. The man was moved. Iymrith, Iymrith, Iymrith. Practically composing a sonnet about her. The deception had landed and the target was now more devoted to the mark than before we started.

"So you're, you're out then," I said, trying to find a handhold on this.

Zalto confirmed that yes, he was, as he put it, totally into the whips and chains. Apparently it was, quote, all the rage.

Right.

I pivoted. The Headband earns its keep in moments like these, when the obvious play closes and you need the next one fast.

"I — Iymrith implied it was some sort of secret thing that you didn't want to share. Keep it quiet."

"That's because Iymrith understands," he said, "but others don't."

There it was. The seam.

"Iymrith's a true friend, huh?" I let that sit. Then: "So are you worried about others finding out, or are you cool with it?"

He was cool with it. He said so. Confident. Unbothered.

"You're cool," I said. "But it's Iymrith that's mistaken."

He was processing that. Good. I pressed.

"She's ashamed of you."

The question that came back was not what I expected. His voice dropped, and what had been confidence a moment ago bent into something rawer, more private. Something that sounded like a man who had wondered this before.

"Is this why she gave me a small whip?"

Thistle confirmed it. I piled on — something about cloud giants, something about whipping, I was working fast and not entirely tracking my own mouth — and then Zalto broke.

Not quietly. He started cursing. His guards moved to hold him back. The room had changed registers entirely; we had gone from diplomatic maneuvering to the edge of a genuine giant-shaped incident in about forty-five seconds.

"Oh," I said. "Oh. Did we say the wrong thing? We should go."

We should absolutely go.

"Well, that was our bad. Whoops a—"

"—doodles," Thistle finished. "We'll tell Iymrith not to do that plan."

"Yeah, yeah, yeah, yeah," I said, already moving toward the exit. "No cloud giants whipping the Lord Fire Giant. Yeah, okay. Maybe we should go tell them right now that the plan is off. Let's go do that."

We left.

Behind us, Zalto was still fuming, his guards still holding him at arm's length, his relationship with Iymrith cracked right down the middle where we'd hit it.

Inventory: one fire giant duke successfully turned against his patron. One brief, alarming moment where the entire plan nearly became a love letter. Zero treasure. No bow lost this time, which I'm choosing to count as a win.

## Unla Key — The Investors And The Betrayal Of Uncle Joon

The mind flayers arrived the way mind flayers always arrive — wrong. Not dramatically, not with fanfare, just *wrong*, in the way a bad smell is wrong before you've identified what's producing it. They drifted into the gathering like oil moving through water, and nobody moved toward them. Not the dragons. Not the giants. Not the assorted power-adjacent entities who had spent the last several hours eagerly networking their way toward violence.

Vardis said, drily, "Their money's green too, apparently."

Iymrith called them investors and declined to elaborate. Orsik, who had lately developed opinions about due diligence, pressed the point.

"Was it the mind flayers that were behind the Kraken Society? Are they — can we ask them, Aretha? Are they the Kraken Society mind flayers?"

Something tightened in Iymrith's bearing that I would describe as *nervousness* if I were talking about a creature capable of nervousness, and not a dragon who had spent centuries orchestrating the slow decay of giant civilization. But there it was. She was nervous.

"They are investors," she said, and that was the end of the conversation.

Orsik tried one more angle — "We're trying to make sure that you're not being taken for a ride, Iymrith. We're looking out for your interests" — which was, I had to admit, an impressive piece of theater from a dwarf who had recently decided to die heroically. Iymrith said something about getting the creature to fly and everyone being fine, and I noted privately that a dragon who has spent decades pulling strings at the highest levels of power is *nervous* about these particular investors, and everyone else at the event had reached the same conclusion independently.

"I mean," I said, "that's just good."

Orsik agreed. Self-preservation instinct. Elemental, really.

I considered going to fight the mind flayers. I am not entirely sure what the thinking was there — something about the cosmological necessity of me specifically taking risks, which is a framework I find more compelling when I am not currently nursing the accumulated damage of the previous several hours. Orsik suggested we charm them instead. The question went unresolved, which is probably a favorable outcome for everyone involved.

---

Uncle Joon stepped out of the shadows the way a man steps out of the shadows when he has been rehearsing his entrance for several weeks and has finally given up on waiting for a better moment.

"I've been waiting for you to show up here," he said to Thistle, with the comfortable familiarity of someone who considers himself in good standing.

I have run a version of this con. Not identical — my version involved a warehouse in Yartar and a fake antiquities dispute and a fence named Pellrin who cried convincingly about a dead grandmother — but the architecture is the same. You need a capable party who will do the difficult thing. You arrange the conditions so that the difficult thing is also *their* thing, something they would have done anyway. You make sure you're positioned to collect your fee from the resulting chaos. You don't tell them about the fee.

The part where the capable party figures out the architecture — that's the part you don't rehearse enough.

Thistle said, sweetly, that perhaps Uncle Joon might like to come a bit further into the lair. There was something to see. Joon, who clearly had some instinct for self-preservation that had not quite kicked in fast enough, asked if they were cool, and Thistle said, "We're icy cool here," and even I felt a small chill that had nothing to do with the desert night.

Then Joon, to his credit, did not make it complicated.

"I staged the whole thing."

He laid it out with the weary efficiency of a man who has been holding a secret for too long and is quietly relieved to be done with it. He needed something from the hoard. He had debts — obligations — to someone of considerable importance, who may or may not have been Titania, he was very clear that it was probably not Titania, but whoever it was operated at that altitude. He needed the party to distract Iymrith, or kill her, while he extracted the item. He had made sure they had the tools to show up. He had been waiting in the lair for *weeks*.

"You weren't supposed to show up at this big conference," he said, confused and slightly aggrieved, as if the party had been late to a planned appointment. "You were supposed to show up when you could."

Orsik, somewhere behind me, said, "Fredo, how are things?"

I've run this con. I know the part he hadn't counted on was Thistle's specific gravity — the fact that Thistle collects people who matter to him, and when those people turn out to have been using him, the accounting gets done with a thoroughness that would impress a Waterdhavian magistrate.

Vardis said something quiet about ultimate betrayals. Game of Thrones level, he said, which felt accurate and also slightly formal for the occasion.

---

Orsik grappled Uncle Joon with the cheerful confidence of a man who has a plus ten to the relevant number and knows it is a foregone conclusion. Joon, mid-restraint, said something about how this wasn't personal, which is what people say when the thing is extremely personal.

Thistle said: gag him. Tie him up. Leave him in the lair.

Orsik said: wearing nothing but a sock.

I want to note that the sock was Orsik's contribution and not something I would have thought of, but I thought it was good.

Then — and this is the part that I keep returning to, the part that makes me genuinely appreciate the elegance of it — Orsik said: put the item in his hand. Whatever item Joon had been waiting weeks for, had sold out his nephew to obtain, had constructed an entire elaborate misdirection to acquire. Put it in his bound hands so he could hold it.

He wouldn't be able to do anything with it. He would just have it.

Joon said, "Guys, I can help." And Orsik said he could help, yes, absolutely. And then Orsik observed that we had apparently been capable of speeding directly to the end this entire time, if only we had known which uncle to follow.

"And that's the end of Uncle Joon. Well, that was a nice plot point we tied up."

"Literally," said Thistle.

I have run versions of this con. But I have never been on the receiving end of an exit this tidy. Joon had weeks to prepare, had positioned himself perfectly, had the item within reach — and was now lying bound in a dragon's lair holding it in both hands, unable to move.

The con within the con. The only thing you never see coming is the one you built yourself.

## Vardis — Iymrith S Grand Opening

The roar hit first — not as sound exactly, but as something that reorganized the air inside your chest and kept going. Then the lightning, a jagged white line punched straight up into the desert sky. And then the dragons answered. Fire in one direction, acid in another, a column of hissing green from somewhere in the back ranks. The giants drove their fists into the stone in a rhythm that I felt through the soles of my boots. The mind flayers stood at the edges of all of it and did absolutely nothing.

That last part bothered me more than the rest.

Every other faction jostled and postured and performed. The mind flayers just hovered, evenly spaced, radiating the specific indifference of creatures who don't need to impress anyone because they are not here to be impressed. Every other creature in that amphitheater gave them a wide berth. Even the dragons.

Problems for later. I returned my attention to the stage.

Iymrith descended into housekeeping with the air of a bureaucrat who genuinely enjoys bureaucracy. "I know you are all here to see my greatest creation," she announced, and I caught the slight shift of weight across the assembled crowd — anticipation, greed, hunger, the whole taxonomy of wanting. "I just want to get some minor bureaucratic things out of the way. There will be no sampling of any of my creations."

Orsik, predictably: "No *what*?"

"Sampling," said Unla Key, from the side of his mouth.

"Sampling," Orsik repeated, sticking it in his head somewhere for later.

I said: "Like remixing?"

I don't know why I said that. It came out before I caught it. The context clarified itself without me: chewing, not music. Iymrith had turned to face the red dragon Flume directly, and the heat coming off that particular lizard was not metaphorical. "I especially mean you," she said. "Last time I did one of my exposés, there were parts everywhere. It was a mess."

Orsik said it sounded tasty. Flume waved a claw with the elaborate disinterest of someone who has been in enough rooms like this to know she'd get away with it. Iymrith moved on.

I scanned the crowd. Giants — hill, stone, fire, frost, cloud — representing whatever remains of the ordning's wreckage. A delegation from the Unseelie Court, which was its own information. The mind flayers, still hovering, still silent. Somewhere in the mass of bodies, Laela was caged, and Thistle was six feet away from me doing the math on how to get her out. And somewhere in the wings, Bob Saget was waiting.

This was a problem Vardis-of-a-year-ago would have had something to say about. He flagged it. He named it. He cast the Wall of Fire.

Iymrith's voice rose. It was a good voice for a dragon, and she'd obviously rehearsed.

"I remember the whispers, the doubts, the laughter, the shaking hands in candlelit halls, the murmurs and shadowed corners. Iymrith is too ambitious, you said. No dragon can bend the laws of magic itself. No creature, scaled, mortal, or divine, can wield such power without being consumed by it. And yet here I stand."

She spread her wings, and the air around her did something that I can only describe as bending. Not theatrically. Actually bending, the way reality sometimes does when very large amounts of power are being pointed in the same direction. The crowd went quiet — not impressed-quiet, but the other kind, the kind where everyone is calculating at once.

"You cried madness when I sought to harness the storms, to command the weave of magic as one commands a cowering servant. You believed the heavens would strike me down for my audacity—"

Unla Key murmured the next line to her, barely audible, the prompter behind the curtain.

"—but tell me, dear friends, where is your storm now?"

She pointed directly at a storm giant in the front ranks. The storm giant said nothing. Probably the correct call.

"You invested not in my vision, but in your own fears. You placed your trust in the laws of the world that I was meant to rewrite. You failed to see what I have always known. That impossible is a word spoken only by those too weak to claim what is theirs."

I counted heads while she spoke. Or tried to. There were too many. Giants, at minimum a few dozen. Dragons, seven that I'd confirmed, probably more. The mind flayers I'd stopped trying to count because the number wasn't the point — the point was that they were here, and invited, and whatever Iymrith had promised them was presumably the kind of thing mind flayers find worth showing up for.

How many of them were going to be standing when this was over?

Pragmatic. The word moves around a lot, I've noticed. It started as *doctrinal flexibility* and now it means *I am standing in a room full of chromatic dragons and I am not going to think too carefully about the audience casualty projections.* Collateral damage is unavoidable in war. I am sure there were good people.

"And now," Iymrith said, pausing for precisely the right duration, "I present to you the perfect assassin. The fairy dragon."

I didn't look at Thistle. I already knew what Thistle's face was doing.

## Thistle — The Great Switcheroo

Below the stage, the air tasted like stone and old magic. I didn't linger.

The plan was simple, the way any plan looks simple when you've compressed three weeks of catastrophic contingencies into a single sentence: *Bob goes up, I go down, Laela comes out*. That's all it was. One dimensional chess, but played in a dragon's living room, with a demon holding the pieces.

"I'll have to go back down to get the real Laela and then prepare for battle," I said, and moved before anyone could find a reason to argue.

Behind me, I caught Orsik's voice: "Is she not coming up invisible?"

"Uh, I was gonna wait until after there was a distraction."

"Oh, oh okay. So you just go, you just fly back down? Alright."

And then, somewhere above me, not quite under his breath: *"Yeah, except for Thistle, who apparently went back down to his girlfriend."*

The word hit differently down here. *Girlfriend.* Said with that particular Orsik flatness that might have been affection, or might have been the closest a dwarf soldier gets to tenderness. I didn't think about it. I kept flying.

---

Up above, Bob was taking the stage.

I wasn't there to see it. I was descending through Iymrith's lair, following a route I'd memorized the way you memorize escape paths — obsessively, quietly, every detail noted so that when the moment comes you don't have to think, you just *move*. The plan required that I not be in the room when it worked. That's the thing about misdirection: the misdirector has to leave. You can't watch your own trick.

But I could hear it. Or imagine it. I've had worse nights.

The command came first. Even from below, Iymrith's voice carried the way old stone carries cold — through the walls, through the floor, through the soles of your feet.

*"Turn into a dragon!"*

And then — because Bob is Bob, and Bob has been waiting centuries for exactly this — I heard laughter.

Specifically, the particular kind of laughter that happens when someone tells a bad joke and everyone laughs anyway because the badness *is the point.* "I just found out that I'm colorblind. The news came out in polymorph." Above me, the party was playing it beautifully. Orsik's voice, loud and theatrical: *"That was good. That was good, Laela. That was good."*

I found the cage.

I won't write about what it was like to see her. I've been composing that scene in my head for three years and every version is wrong. What I'll write instead is that I gave her my cloak and told her which way to go, and she went, and I watched her until she was around the corner and moving fast and there were no guards between her and the exit. That's what I did. The rest of it — what her face looked like, what she said, what I didn't — that's somewhere I don't have words for yet.

---

Above me, Iymrith was apparently running out of patience.

*"If I don't see a dragon in five seconds—"*

I could hear the crowd. I could hear the party debating whether Unla Key should cast Polymorph and actually produce a dragon, which would have been a completely reasonable idea to suggest *before* we were inside Iymrith's lair with Laela ten feet from the exit, but timing has never been Unla Key's strongest quality. 

What I know — what I pieced together later — is that Bob did not become a dragon. Bob became Bob. The disguise dissolved. The demon stood next to Iymrith in front of every powerful figure she'd assembled to witness her triumph, and announced, in the voice of something that has been practicing this monologue for longer than most kingdoms have existed:

*"THE JOKE'S ON YOU, DOOM OF THE DESERT! YOU THOUGHT YOU HAD LAELA — YOU HAD A DEMON!"*

And then, because Bob is thorough, he explained it. At length. The whole plan, the whole joke, the years of waiting, the humiliation of Iymrith rendered into performance for her own audience. The party told me Iymrith's face did something that didn't have a name.

The crowd, to their credit, understood immediately which way the wind was blowing.

"Boo! Boo!" — Orsik, who has always known how to work a room when the work involves destruction.

"Boo!" — Unla Key, committed.

"Everybody boo. Everybody boo. Turn everyone on 'em. Come on."

I wasn't there. I was below, watching Laela's borrowed cloak disappear around a corner, listening to an ancient dragon get publicly humiliated in her own house, and thinking: *it's a good plan.*

It was, actually. It was a very good plan.

## Orsik — Chaos At The Amphitheater

The chromatic dragons saw the hole before anyone else did.

One moment Obsidianth was circling the amphitheater with the regal menace she'd spent the whole performance cultivating, and the next she was pointing — actually pointing, one claw extended like a banker identifying a defaulted property — and her black dragons broke formation and dove for it. The red followed half a second later. Then the green. All that theatrical grandeur, gone the instant someone found the treasure room.

I almost respected it.

They're not suicidal. That was the thing to hold onto. Every chromatic in the air had run the same calculation: Iymrith is cornered and angry and not worth dying over, but the hoard she's been sitting on for decades is very much worth dying over — or at least worth racing someone else for. Red dragons especially. Red dragons *care about the money.*

Obsidianth was already directing her brood toward the entrance in the rock. The green followed. What had been a performance venue sixty seconds ago was now a traffic problem.

Then the storm giants arrived.

I felt them before I saw them — the ground settling differently, the way it does when something very large is moving toward you with intention. They came in from the east, Hekaton's people, and every other giant faction in the amphitheater read the room in exactly the way giant factions always do: old grudges, immediate chaos, everyone with someone to fight. Fire giants squared off against storm giants. Cloud giants did what cloud giants do, which is stand at the back and calculate whether winning or losing suits their interests better. Sensible, in a cold-blooded way I couldn't fully dismiss.

The Red Wizards of Thay were already gone. Smart enough to know when to write off a bad investment. They'd find another angle; they always did.

And then — the mind flayers. Standing back. Watching. Everyone giving them a wide berth without discussing it, the way you give a deep drop a wide berth without discussing it. Nobody was walking toward them.

Unla Key looked at them with the expression he gets when he's about to suggest something catastrophically stupid.

"I'm gonna go fight the mind flayers."

I said it before I could weigh it: "Maybe we could get them on our side. Why don't you cast some, like, charm spells on them, Unla Key?"

It was a deflection, but it was also correct. The mind flayers were a problem to manage, not a fight to pick — not when there were giants and dragons doing our work for us if we could just point them in the right direction.

Thistle had the right instinct first: "So, once the dragons go in, can we Wall of Force to seal it off?"

I'd been thinking the same thing. "Is there a way to collapse that hole so that like, all the dragons are trapped inside?"

Interesting on paper.  We had Mold Earth, we had a wizard, we had options. But the thing I kept coming back to was: we didn't need to win every engagement in this amphitheater. We needed to win one.

Vardis leaned toward me with the quiet urgency he uses when he's about to be right about something. "Think it's time for that rousing speech using Unla Key's ability to twist fate."

He was not wrong.

I looked at the storm giants. Hekaton's people. We'd freed their king from a crab the size of a building. They knew who we were. And they were angry — the right kind of angry, the directed kind, the kind that moves — but anger needs a target or it burns everything.

Unla Key's portent was already in his hand and it was powerful. He can do that sometimes, the little halfling — see the thread that needs pulling and pull it exactly right, before anyone else has the vocabulary for what they're looking at.

I stepped forward.

"Iymrith is to blame," I said, making sure the storm giants heard the whole sentence, "and once Iymrith is dead, there will be gold for everyone!"

Simple. Accurate. The kind of thing that sounds obvious after you've said it but needed someone to say it.

It landed, better than I could have ever hoped.

The chromatic dragons weren't interested in Iymrith — why would they be? The hoard was right there, unminded. Let them have it. Let them race each other into the ground. The storm giants, though, had something to prove, and we'd just given them a flag to prove it under. They committed. They turned toward the dragon.

Bob was still out there, somewhere on the far flank, keeping Iymrith occupied with whatever demons find funny. Probably still making jokes. Iymrith was dealing with Bob's chaos on one side and storm giant thunder on the other, which meant she was dealing with neither particularly well.

The battlefield was a disaster. Dragon breath arcing across open ground, giant footfalls that you had to track by pressure rather than sound. Moving through it meant absorbing whatever came — a bad gamble at any moment. The red circle, as I thought of it in my head, was everywhere. You didn't walk through it without knowing you might catch something aimed at someone else.

Fine. That's what armor is for.

Then it clicked, the thing Vardis had been building toward without quite naming.

"Wait a second," I said. "I think what you're saying is, if we kill Iymrith, and all these people get the Horde, they'll see us as the heroes."

Not heroes the way Escarlotta meant it. Not heroes the way Vardis means it when he's being careful with the word. More like Tony Soprano being grateful. Michael Corleone being grateful. Every ruthless, greedy, centuries-old faction in this amphitheater, owing us the particular debt that evil things owe when you've done them a useful turn.

I could work with that.

The chromatic dragons were already fighting, over the hole to Iymrith's legendary collection — gold, artifacts, the accumulated hoarding of a dragon who'd been operating under a fake identity for so long she'd probably forgotten what any of it was for. Somewhere down in the dark, Uncle Joon was bound and holding the very thing he'd sold out his nephew to get his hands on. I let myself enjoy that for exactly one second.

"They should go see the one that Uncle Joon's holding."

Nobody had seen that coming. He'd known the whole time.

I hadn't.

But I knew what came next. Iymrith was cornered, preoccupied, and running out of angles. The storm giants were moving. Bob was still talking, bless him. Laela was free.

The fight I'd been promised myself was here.
