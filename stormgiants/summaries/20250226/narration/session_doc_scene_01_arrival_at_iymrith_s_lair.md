The lair smelled like ozone and old bone. That particular combination — the blue-white crackle of a storm dragon's presence pressed into every stone, undercut by something older and charred — was the kind of air that tells you, before your eyes have adjusted, that you are somewhere you should not be. I noticed it immediately. I am a Light cleric. I have walked into burning buildings with less concern. I noticed it.

Iymrith was waiting for us.

She was the size of a bad decision that has had several centuries to compound interest. She looked at us the way a collector looks at pieces she has already paid for and intends not to damage before the auction. "So, you've arrived," she said, and the words had the warmth of a verdict.

Thistle confirmed that yes, we were here to fulfill our bargain, which was technically true in every way except the important one. I stood slightly behind the others and ran a professional eye over the architecture. Exits: limited. Stone ceiling: high enough for a dragon to maneuver. Sightlines: very much in her favor. I filed this under *expected* and kept my face pleasant.

She told us the guest list was impressive, that it was really important this go off without a hitch. She used the word *important* like a threat dressed in manners, which is a skill I respect even when it is aimed at me. I was already cataloguing the space — the upper chamber, the lower passages, the sightlines from the entrance — doing the arithmetic that has kept me alive long enough to be excommunicated from every major institution that once employed me.

Pragmatic.

"Will your high school bully be here?" I asked.

It seemed like a reasonable deflection. Get her talking, get her comfortable, find the edge of the ego and apply gentle pressure. Standard method. What I did not account for was the sincerity.

"No. No, no, no, I ate her."

I let a beat pass. "Don't want to show her what a success you've become?"

"No, I enjoyed chewing her bones and spitting out her flesh. Why? Is there something else?"

I said something about rubbing it in — showing off the success — which I will admit was a gamble on whether ancient blue dragons understand the concept of *social humiliation as a motivational framework*. They do not, apparently. They have a different framework.

"Every time I want to think about what a success I am, I just fantasize about that meal."

She said it without a flinch. She said it like it was obvious. She looked at me with those enormous, terrible eyes and said *is there something else I should have done?* with the tone of someone genuinely puzzled that there might be another metric.

I had nothing. I, Vardis, exiled cleric of Corellon, veteran of more campaigns than I can count on fingers and toes combined, man who once talked down a Frost Giant by explaining the theology of hospitality at considerable length — I had nothing.

Thistle, bless the fairy, stepped in. "You don't have the bones," Thistle said.

Iymrith went very still. "No," she said, mournfully. "No, I do not have the bones."

She collected herself almost immediately — *of course it wasn't quite high school* — and moved on, but something had passed between us in that moment, some shared understanding that the bones were the real subject of this entire enterprise and nobody in this room was going to say it plainly. I catalogued this too, under *useful*.

Then came the deadline. She needed the performance tomorrow. If it didn't happen, someone was going to be burned to a crisp. She delivered this with the apologetic air of a hostess explaining a dietary restriction.

Orsik — because Orsik has a death wish and at some point I stopped being surprised by it — said, "Can it be me?"

Three words. My best friend has a death wish, and he will use the most dangerous creature on the Sword Coast as the delivery mechanism for his own darkest jokes. I have been carrying this fact for weeks now like a stone in my boot that I have decided, for complex theological reasons, not to remove.

Iymrith turned to look at him with an expression I can only describe as *considered menace*. She had heard about his goals in life, she said. He was not dying. In fact, she would personally ensure he never died from a dragon, even if she had to protect him from dragons herself. *The real punishment.*

I do not know what the SEER offered Orsik that he refused, but Iymrith had just accidentally reinvented it from the other direction and Orsik's face went through several interesting stages. The irony was not wasted on me. The man who wants to die heroically is now promised immortality by the creature he intends to die fighting. All the plans that have ever been, that's one of them.

We were asked about rehearsal time. Thistle said the show was improv. Unla Key said the show was improv. I said, flat as I could manage, "It'll have to be," which was as close to a prayer as I was willing to offer in that room.

Iymrith was visibly uncomfortable with this answer.

I tried the method acting approach. I needed to understand her theology, I explained, in order to be properly in character for my role. This was not entirely a lie — I did want to understand her theology, primarily to find the load-bearing assumptions and pull on them. She shut it down without ceremony. *I don't have time for that.* We pressed. *We have time. Performance is tomorrow.* She moved on. *This is—* She was already walking.

We followed, because that is what you do in the lair of a creature that can reduce you to ash with weather.

She led us through chambers that explained things without being asked. The scale of the place. The stone carved to accommodate something vast. Here and there: evidence of the Grand Reveal in progress — arrangements, preparations, the infrastructure of a scheme centuries in the making. I looked at all of it the way a doctor looks at a wound: noting what I see, calculating what it means, deciding what can still be fixed.

Then we passed the Yuan-ti.

A group of them, arranged before a statue of Iymrith, prostrated in worship. She told us to pay no attention — they were just part of the group that worships her. Orsik, who was wearing his Headband of Intellect and occasionally forgets that the headband does not include tact, pointed out that they were worshipping a statue of her rather than her directly.

She said she liked to redirect. Didn't want all the glory going to her. The theological inconsistency of this position — a god encouraging proxy worship by proxy object — is something I would normally have eight minutes of opinions about. I looked at the Yuan-ti, looked at the statue, thought briefly about the alternative believers' cult I have been building in the back of my mind since Sandesyl showed me what long-term career planning looks like, and said nothing.

She took us lower.

Laela was in a magical contraption she could not escape. I looked at it, looked at Thistle's face when Thistle saw it, and updated the order of priorities. Getting Laela out of that device was going to be whatever we did next.

But first, Iymrith wanted her monologue.

She tapped her claws against the stone — a sound like a bridge deciding whether to hold — and asked when the performance would begin. She wanted to see the script. She had been promised many lines. She wanted a monologue. This was her moment of triumph, she said. She wanted to show all of those who had doubted her and all of her investors that she had achieved the impossible.

I watched her say it and had an unexpected, uncomfortable thought: *I understand this.* Not the centuries of manipulation, not the eaten bully, not the Yuan-ti and the scheme and the assembled guest list. Just that specific hunger — the one that says *I will show you what I've built out of what you threw away.* The temples exiled me and I walked out and I have been building something in the space they left. Different scales. Roughly the same fuel.

I did not say this aloud. Some sermons you decide not to give.

Thistle redirected elegantly: why not go practice the monologue with Orsik as director, in the stadium, while Thistle worked with Laela here to get her ready? Iymrith asked who the director was and then immediately answered herself. "Are you the director? Yes. Orsik is the director."

"Definitely," said Orsik, which had the particular flavor of a man who has just been handed a job that will allow him to be alone with the ancient dragon he intends to die fighting.

Before they could leave, Orsik noted — with an expression of artistic sincerity that I have never once seen him deploy in genuine situations — that Laela probably couldn't rehearse from inside the magical contraption. Wasn't that right, Thistle?

Iymrith began to object. Orsik escalated to artistic outrage. *Call it off. Forget it. My artistic vision is being compromised.* Thistle appealed to her investment in the show. The whole thing was the most committed piece of improvised theater I have watched anyone perform, and I watched it with the part of my brain that is always taking notes.

Iymrith folded. The monologue would go down in the epics of monologues. Orsik was an artist. Laela could be out. She could be out.

And just like that, Laela was free of the contraption, the costumes were confirmed, and the dress rehearsal was underway.

I looked around the lair — its scale, its depth, the exits I'd catalogued, the stone ceiling, the Yuan-ti, the magical architecture of Iymrith's long investment in her own mythology — and made the silent inventory of everything that needed to go right tomorrow. It was a long list. The column of things that could go wrong was longer, and some of the entries had my best friend's name next to them.

Pragmatic, I told myself.

I have been telling myself that for a very long time.
