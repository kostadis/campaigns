The mind flayers arrived the way mind flayers always arrive — wrong. Not dramatically, not with fanfare, just *wrong*, in the way a bad smell is wrong before you've identified what's producing it. They drifted into the gathering like oil moving through water, and nobody moved toward them. Not the dragons. Not the giants. Not the assorted power-adjacent entities who had spent the last several hours eagerly networking their way toward violence.

Vardis said, drily, "Their money's green too, apparently."

Iymrith called them investors and declined to elaborate. Orsik, who had lately developed opinions about due diligence, pressed the point.

"Was it the mind flayers that were behind the Kraken Society? Are they — can we ask them, Aretha? Are they the Kraken Society mind flayers?"

Something tightened in Iymrith's bearing that I would describe as *nervousness* if I were talking about a creature capable of nervousness, and not a dragon who had spent centuries orchestrating the slow decay of giant civilization. But there it was. She was nervous.

"They are investors," she said, and that was the end of the conversation.

Orsik tried one more angle — "We're trying to make sure that you're not being taken for a ride, Iymrith. We're looking out for your interests" — which was, I had to admit, an impressive piece of theater from a dwarf who had recently decided to die heroically. Iymrith said something about getting the creature to fly and everyone being fine, and I noted privately that a dragon who has spent decades pulling strings at the highest levels of power is *nervous* about these particular investors, and everyone else at the event had reached the same conclusion independently.

"I mean," I said, "that's just good."

Orsik agreed. Self-preservation instinct. Elemental, really.

I considered going to fight the mind flayers. I am not entirely sure what the thinking was there — something about the cosmological necessity of me specifically taking risks, which is a framework I find more compelling when I am not currently nursing the accumulated damage of the previous several hours. Orsik suggested we charm them instead. The question went unresolved, which is probably a favorable outcome for everyone involved.

Uncle Joon stepped out of the shadows the way a man steps out of the shadows when he has been rehearsing his entrance for several weeks and has finally given up on waiting for a better moment.

"I've been waiting for you to show up here," he said to Thistle, with the comfortable familiarity of someone who considers himself in good standing.

I have run a version of this con. Not identical — my version involved a warehouse in Yartar and a fake antiquities dispute and a fence named Pellrin who cried convincingly about a dead grandmother — but the architecture is the same. You need a capable party who will do the difficult thing. You arrange the conditions so that the difficult thing is also *their* thing, something they would have done anyway. You make sure you're positioned to collect your fee from the resulting chaos. You don't tell them about the fee.

The part where the capable party figures out the architecture — that's the part you don't rehearse enough.

Thistle said, sweetly, that perhaps Uncle Joon might like to come a bit further into the lair. There was something to see. Joon, who clearly had some instinct for self-preservation that had not quite kicked in fast enough, asked if they were cool, and Thistle said, "We're icy cool here," and even I felt a small chill that had nothing to do with the desert night.

Then Joon, to his credit, did not make it complicated.

"I staged the whole thing."

He laid it out with the weary efficiency of a man who has been holding a secret for too long and is quietly relieved to be done with it. He needed something from the hoard. He had debts — obligations — to someone of considerable importance, who may or may not have been Titania, he was very clear that it was probably not Titania, but whoever it was operated at that altitude. He needed the party to distract Iymrith, or kill her, while he extracted the item. He had made sure they had the tools to show up. He had been waiting in the lair for *weeks*.

"You weren't supposed to show up at this big conference," he said, confused and slightly aggrieved, as if the party had been late to a planned appointment. "You were supposed to show up when you could."

Orsik, somewhere behind me, said, "Fredo, how are things?"

I've run this con. I know the part he hadn't counted on was Thistle's specific gravity — the fact that Thistle collects people who matter to him, and when those people turn out to have been using him, the accounting gets done with a thoroughness that would impress a Waterdhavian magistrate.

Vardis said something quiet about ultimate betrayals. Game of Thrones level, he said, which felt accurate and also slightly formal for the occasion.

Orsik grappled Uncle Joon with the cheerful confidence of a man who has a plus ten to the relevant number and knows it is a foregone conclusion. Joon, mid-restraint, said something about how this wasn't personal, which is what people say when the thing is extremely personal.

Thistle said: gag him. Tie him up. Leave him in the lair.

Orsik said: wearing nothing but a sock.

I want to note that the sock was Orsik's contribution and not something I would have thought of, but I thought it was good.

Then — and this is the part that I keep returning to, the part that makes me genuinely appreciate the elegance of it — Orsik said: put the item in his hand. Whatever item Joon had been waiting weeks for, had sold out his nephew to obtain, had constructed an entire elaborate misdirection to acquire. Put it in his bound hands so he could hold it.

He wouldn't be able to do anything with it. He would just have it.

Joon said, "Guys, I can help." And Orsik said he could help, yes, absolutely. And then Orsik observed that we had apparently been capable of speeding directly to the end this entire time, if only we had known which uncle to follow.

"And that's the end of Uncle Joon. Well, that was a nice plot point we tied up."

"Literally," said Thistle.

I have run versions of this con. But I have never been on the receiving end of an exit this tidy. Joon had weeks to prepare, had positioned himself perfectly, had the item within reach — and was now lying bound in a dragon's lair holding it in both hands, unable to move.

The con within the con. The only thing you never see coming is the one you built yourself.
