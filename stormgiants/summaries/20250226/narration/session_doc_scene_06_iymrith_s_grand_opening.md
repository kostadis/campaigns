The roar hit first — not as sound exactly, but as something that reorganized the air inside your chest and kept going. Then the lightning, a jagged white line punched straight up into the desert sky. And then the dragons answered. Fire in one direction, acid in another, a column of hissing green from somewhere in the back ranks. The giants drove their fists into the stone in a rhythm that I felt through the soles of my boots. The mind flayers stood at the edges of all of it and did absolutely nothing.

That last part bothered me more than the rest.

Every other faction jostled and postured and performed. The mind flayers just hovered, evenly spaced, radiating the specific indifference of creatures who don't need to impress anyone because they are not here to be impressed. Every other creature in that amphitheater gave them a wide berth. Even the dragons.

I filed that away under *problems for later* and returned my attention to the stage.

Iymrith descended into housekeeping with the air of a bureaucrat who genuinely enjoys bureaucracy. "I know you are all here to see my greatest creation," she announced, and I caught the slight shift of weight across the assembled crowd — anticipation, greed, hunger, the whole taxonomy of wanting. "I just want to get some minor bureaucratic things out of the way. There will be no sampling of any of my creations."

Orsik, predictably: "No *what*?"

"Sampling," said Unla Key, from the side of his mouth.

"Sampling," Orsik repeated, apparently filing it.

I said: "Like remixing?"

I don't know why I said that. It came out before I caught it. The context clarified itself without me: chewing, not music. Iymrith had turned to face the red dragon Flume directly, and the heat coming off that particular lizard was not metaphorical. "I especially mean you," she said. "Last time I did one of my exposés, there were parts everywhere. It was a mess."

Orsik said it sounded tasty. Flume waved a claw with the elaborate disinterest of someone who has been in enough rooms like this to know she'd get away with it. Iymrith moved on.

I scanned the crowd. Giants — hill, stone, fire, frost, cloud — representing whatever remains of the ordning's wreckage. A delegation from the Unseelie Court, which was its own information. The mind flayers, still hovering, still silent. Somewhere in the mass of bodies, Laela was caged, and Thistle was six feet away from me doing the math on how to get her out. And somewhere in the wings, Bob Saget was waiting.

This was a problem Vardis-of-a-year-ago would have had something to say about. He flagged it. He named it. He cast the Wall of Fire.

Iymrith's voice rose. It was a good voice for a dragon, and she'd obviously rehearsed.

"I remember the whispers, the doubts, the laughter, the shaking hands in candlelit halls, the murmurs and shadowed corners. Iymrith is too ambitious, you said. No dragon can bend the laws of magic itself. No creature, scaled, mortal, or divine, can wield such power without being consumed by it. And yet here I stand."

She spread her wings, and the air around her did something that I can only describe as bending. Not theatrically. Actually bending, the way reality sometimes does when very large amounts of power are being pointed in the same direction. The crowd went quiet — not impressed-quiet, but the other kind, the kind where everyone is calculating at once.

"You cried madness when I sought to harness the storms, to command the weave of magic as one commands a cowering servant. You believed the heavens would strike me down for my audacity—"

Unla Key murmured the next line to her, barely audible, the prompter behind the curtain.

"—but tell me, dear friends, where is your storm now?"

She pointed directly at a storm giant in the front ranks. The storm giant said nothing. Probably the correct call.

"You invested not in my vision, but in your own fears. You placed your trust in the laws of the world that I was meant to rewrite. You failed to see what I have always known. That impossible is a word spoken only by those too weak to claim what is theirs."

I counted heads while she spoke. Or tried to. There were too many. Giants, at minimum a few dozen. Dragons, seven that I'd confirmed, probably more. The mind flayers I'd stopped trying to count because the number wasn't the point — the point was that they were here, and invited, and whatever Iymrith had promised them was presumably the kind of thing mind flayers find worth showing up for.

How many of them were going to be standing when this was over?

Pragmatic. The word moves around a lot, I've noticed. It started as *doctrinal flexibility* and now it means *I am standing in a room full of chromatic dragons and I am not going to think too carefully about the audience casualty projections.* Collateral damage is unavoidable in war. I am sure there were good people.

"And now," Iymrith said, pausing for precisely the right duration, "I present to you the perfect assassin. The fairy dragon."

I didn't look at Thistle. I already knew what Thistle's face was doing.
