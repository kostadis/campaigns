# Naturalism as Moral Framework

A philosophical system that rejects moral categories such as “good,” “evil,” “monster,” “barbarian,” or “corruption” as human constructs that impose artificial hierarchy on a fundamentally neutral and diverse cosmos. All entities — whether treants, drow, planar entities, or cultists — are seen as natural expressions of universal balance, each fulfilling a role in the ecosystem of existence.

Adabra Gwynn embodies this framework, arguing that labeling a creature as “evil” is not a moral judgment but an act of violence — a refusal to accept its place in the natural order. She reframes wandering peoples as “nomads,” demons as “natural phenomena,” and the Cult of Talos as “the necessary element of distraction in the cosmic balance.” This view directly challenges the party’s reliance on moral binaries to justify action.

This framework is not relativism — it is a form of cosmic determinism. It does not excuse harm; it recontextualizes it as inevitable. The moral imperative becomes not to stop evil, but to avoid disrupting the flow of existence. This leads to profound ethical tensions: if a treant dies because of intervention, is the intervention the cause — or merely the trigger? If a drow is “natural,” does that absolve their actions?

It extends the wiki’s treatment of [[hybridization-experiments]] and [[ambiguous-sentience]] by denying the ontological distinction between “natural” and “aberrant.” It also undermines [[soul-manipulation]] as a moral category — if souls are part of the natural order, then their manipulation is not inherently wrong, merely disruptive.

Naturalism as a moral framework transforms the campaign from a battle of good versus evil into a clash of epistemologies: one side sees a world that needs saving; the other sees a world that needs to be left alone.
