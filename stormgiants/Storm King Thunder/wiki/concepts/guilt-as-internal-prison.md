# Guilt-as-internal-prison

A psychological condition in which unprocessed guilt becomes a self-sustaining, inescapable mental structure. Blagothkus’s entire behavior — his deflection, passivity, and emotional volatility — stems from his refusal to accept responsibility for killing Eigeron. He did not flee physically; he fled inward.

His guilt did not manifest as remorse, but as paralysis. He could not lead, speak, or act without external pressure because to do so would force him to confront the truth. His conversations with Escarlotta were not reconciliations — they were attempts to delay the inevitable.

This concept parallels [[spiritual-abandonment]] but is internalized: instead of losing a god, Blagothkus lost himself. His prison was not stone or iron — it was his own mind. His death was the only escape.

This model explains why Blagothkus could not be redeemed by repentance — his guilt was not a sin to be forgiven, but a structure to be destroyed. Only death could break it.
