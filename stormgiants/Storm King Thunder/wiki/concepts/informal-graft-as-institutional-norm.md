# Informal Graft as Institutional Norm

A system of corruption in which off-the-books transactions, bribes, and personal enrichment are not hidden anomalies, but normalized, unspoken rules of operation — accepted as “how things are done.”

Harbin Wester exemplifies this: he charges a private 50 gp fee to process temple paperwork, frames it as a “community donation” when caught, and describes such practices as standard in the Overbright. He arranges supply deliveries for his brother’s logging operation under the guise of civic duty. He avoids cash payouts by offering free lodging — a transaction disguised as charity.

This is not rogue behavior — it is institutional. The system does not punish graft; it absorbs it. Harbin’s authority is not undermined by corruption — it is sustained by it. His role as Townmaster is a [[cover-identity-as-administrator]], masking personal gain under civic legitimacy.

This concept extends [[cover-identity-as-merchant]] and [[layered-command-structure]] into municipal governance: power operates through invisible channels, where the public face (order, law, procedure) conceals the private reality (bribery, favoritism, exploitation). The absence of scandal is not proof of integrity — it is proof of normalization.

In this system, the most dangerous corruption is the kind no one questions.
