# Planar-phenomena-as-commodity

Planar-phenomena-as-commodity is the economic framework in which objects, materials, or artifacts derive their value not from their physical properties or magical potency, but from their origin in, exposure to, or resonance with planar instability — such as rifts, temporal echoes, or the lingering effects of divine collapse.

This concept is exemplified by Linene Graywind’s willingness to pay double the market price for orc gear and pelts recovered from areas affected by planar disruption, such as the Neverwinter Wood. These items are not inherently magical — they are *planar-touched*. Their value lies in their provenance, not their function. This transforms mundane goods into collectibles within a specialized, institutional market.

Unlike [[magical-resonance-with-artifacts]], where objects trigger physiological or emotional reactions in bonded beings (e.g., Blooddrinker’s convulsions near the stone chest), planar-phenomena-as-commodity treats resonance as a market signal — a certification of origin. The “bosses” of Lionshield Coster are not interested in the artifact’s power; they are interested in its *history*.

This economic logic extends beyond mere rarity. It implies a structured demand from unseen actors who use planar residues as components, records, or even spiritual currency. The defunct interplanar shipping device referenced by Valphine suggests this is not an isolated practice, but part of a broader, institutionalized infrastructure for harvesting and transporting planar matter.

The War of the Giants disrupted this market not by destroying goods, but by severing the supply chains that delivered them — introducing “tariffs” that may be metaphysical, bureaucratic, or divine in nature. This links planar-phenomena-as-commodity directly to [[supply-chain-disruption-by-planar-conflict]] and [[shattering-of-the-ordning]], showing that cosmological collapse has tangible, economic consequences.

Planar-phenomena-as-commodity reframes the campaign’s magic system: the universe is not just alive with spirits and gods — it is a marketplace. And in a world where gods are silent, the most valuable goods are those that carry the echo of their absence.
