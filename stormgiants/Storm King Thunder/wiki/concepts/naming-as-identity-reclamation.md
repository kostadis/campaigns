# Naming as Identity Reclamation

The deliberate act of adopting a new name to signify acceptance of a transformed identity — particularly after death, resurrection, or radical change. The name is not imposed by others, but embraced by the individual as an act of self-definition and psychological integration.

Boney’s transition from “Barney” to “Boney” is a canonical example. The party suggested the change to reflect his skeletal form. He accepted it happily, signaling his full embrace of his undead state. The old name, “Barney,” was tied to his mortal life — a life that ended violently. “Boney” is not a label of horror, but a badge of continuity: he is still *him*, just changed.

This concept parallels [[cover-identity-as-merchant]] and [[disguised-identity-as-race]] in that names are tools of perception and power — but here, the subject is the agent of change. Naming becomes an act of agency in the face of existential dislocation.

In a world where identities are manipulated, hidden, or stolen, Boney’s choice to rename himself is quietly revolutionary: he defines himself on his own terms, even in death.
