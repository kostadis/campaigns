# Moral Ambiguity of Inaction (Undead)

The ethical dilemma posed when non-intervention — whether by undead, bystanders, or institutional actors — results in death or suffering, yet no direct violence is committed. The moral weight of inaction is weighed against the perceived necessity of restraint, neutrality, or strategic delay.

Originally applied to boney, the sentient undead horse who witnesses but does not intervene in moral events, this concept is extended to living agents. Boney was present when Sister Kaella killed Corbin. He did not act. When Soma later questioned his inaction, she immediately retracted the comment, recognizing its unfairness. This moment crystallizes the core tension: Boney is not a moral agent in the human sense. He is not bound by human codes of justice, nor does he possess the capacity or context to interpret Corbin’s death as a moral violation. His loyalty is to Lady Alagondar — not to the party’s ethics.

Yet the ambiguity deepens when we consider the living. Corbin’s death is not caused by Kaella alone — it is caused by the party’s decision to leave him alone. They did not pull the trigger, but they created the conditions for his death. Their inaction was not passive observation — it was active neglect, a calculated choice to prioritize strategy over compassion. Adabra Gwynn argues this is the true sin of interventionism: not the violence, but the willingness to sacrifice the vulnerable for a greater goal.

The ambiguity lies in whether inaction is passive or complicit. Is it helplessness? Irrelevance? Or moral cowardice? This concept challenges the assumption that all sentient beings must be judged by human moral frameworks — and further, that morality requires action. Sometimes, the most destructive force is not the blade — but the decision not to hold it.

Boney’s silence haunts not because he feels guilt, but because others project it onto him. It echoes [[guilt-as-internal-prison]] but flips it — here, the guilt is externalized onto a being who does not feel it. In a world where gods are silent and morality is fractured, Boney’s inaction becomes a mirror: if even a loyal, sentient creature can stand by and do nothing, what does that say about the moral clarity of those who act — and those who choose not to?
