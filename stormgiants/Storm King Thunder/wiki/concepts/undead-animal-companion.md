# Undead Animal Companion

An undead creature, typically an animal or beast, that retains its personality, emotional bonds, and non-hostile intent after death — functioning as a loyal, sentient companion rather than a mindless horror. Unlike traditional undead tropes, these entities are not controlled by necromancy, driven by hunger, or corrupted by malevolent forces. Instead, their existence is sustained by residual emotional energy, devotion, or unexplained metaphysical continuity.

Boney is the canonical example: a skeletal horse who remembers his master, mourns his lost form, and transfers loyalty seamlessly to new companions. His humor, acceptance, and moral ambiguity (e.g., standing by during Corbin’s death) demonstrate that undead sentience need not imply evil. This concept extends [[animal-companion-bonding]] into the realm of death, suggesting emotional bonds can persist beyond biological life.

Undead animal companions challenge assumptions about the soul, agency, and the moral status of non-human entities. They often serve narrative functions of emotional grounding, thematic contrast, and quiet subversion — turning horror into pathos, and monstrosity into devotion.

This archetype is rare in fantasy settings, making Boney a pivotal case study in narrative design and moral complexity.
