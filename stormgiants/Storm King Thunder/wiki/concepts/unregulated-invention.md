# Unregulated invention

**Unregulated invention** refers to technological experimentation that operates outside formal safety, ethical, or consent-based constraints — whether by explicit rejection of oversight or by co-opting institutional frameworks to mask moral abandonment.

Traditionally, this term has been applied to figures like **Facktore**, whose chaotic, isolated experiments — such as his automated crossbow platform designed to collect data through non-consensual human testing — exemplify reckless, unmonitored innovation. His actions reveal how even within epistemic communities that value empirical inquiry, the absence of oversight transforms innovation into predation. Gnomengarde’s failure to monitor him underscores a critical vulnerability: institutional silence or bureaucratic complacency enables dangerous outliers.

However, **Fibblestib redefines the concept entirely**: he is not chaotic, but calculated. He operates within Gnomengarde’s institutional framework, follows protocols, and even seeks approval — yet systematically ignores moral boundaries. His **Sanity Ray** disregards the nature of madness; his **harvesting of mimic entrails** treats sentient life as raw material; his advocacy for a **bicameral legislature with unitary executive** seeks to centralize power under technocratic control. This is **regulated but amoral innovation**: invention that is technically lawful but ethically bankrupt.

This form of innovation is more dangerous than Facktore’s because it is invisible — it wears the mask of legitimacy. Fibblestib does not break rules; he redefines them to serve his vision. His existence exposes a deeper failure: institutional systems are not inherently self-correcting or ethically grounded. The greatest threats are not those who operate outside the system, but those who use it to normalize moral erosion.

Unregulated invention, therefore, is not merely reckless — it is *institutionally enabled*. Whether through neglect (Facktore) or complicity (Fibblestib), the failure lies in the system’s inability to distinguish between technical compliance and ethical integrity. This concept challenges the assumption that rationality, structure, or procedural adherence guarantee responsible innovation. It demands a reevaluation of what constitutes ethical oversight: not just rules, but the moral courage to enforce them.

> **Key insight**: Unregulated invention thrives not only in the shadows of absence, but in the glare of institutional legitimacy.
