# Narrative Economy in Personal Trauma

The deliberate withholding of explicit detail about a character’s deepest personal losses to preserve emotional weight, mystery, and psychological realism. [[falcon-the-hunter]]’s story is saturated with unspoken grief: the identity of “his girlfriend” who died in the lodge’s destruction, and the “dearly beloved” referenced on the wooden elven ears, are never named, never explained.

This omission is not an oversight — it is narrative discipline. By refusing to define these losses, the source allows them to remain universal: they are not just Falcon’s grief, but the grief of anyone who has lost someone without closure. The wooden ears and labyrinth square become vessels for all unspoken sorrow.

This technique, [[narrative-economy]], transforms trauma from exposition into atmosphere. It avoids melodrama by refusing to name the wound — and in doing so, makes it more real. The audience is not told what to feel — they are invited to feel it themselves, in the silence between the words.
