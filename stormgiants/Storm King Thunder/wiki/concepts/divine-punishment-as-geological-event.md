# Divine-punishment-as-geological-event

Divine-punishment-as-geological-event is a folk-religious belief system in which natural disasters — earthquakes, avalanches, landslides — are interpreted not as random acts of nature, but as direct, moral retribution from a deity for ritual failure or spiritual neglect. This concept is distinct from the structured, constrained truth-telling of the [[divine-oracle]] and instead reflects localized, oral traditions passed among communities.

Dazlyn Grayshard describes the destruction of the ancient dwarven settlement as the result of Abbathor’s wrath, triggered by inadequate sacrifices. This explanation is not presented as dogma, but as cultural memory — a way for the dwarves to make sense of catastrophe. It is not a mechanism of divine control, but a framework for grief and meaning-making.

This belief system operates outside institutional religion. It does not require priests, oracles, or temples — only memory and fear. It is the emotional counterpart to [[divine-silence]]: where the gods no longer speak, the people invent explanations in the language of the earth.

This concept enriches the portrayal of [[temple-of-abbathor]] beyond cults and divine agents, grounding the deity’s influence in the lived experience of ordinary dwarves. It also provides narrative texture to ruins and disasters — not as mere plot devices, but as sacred wounds in the landscape.
