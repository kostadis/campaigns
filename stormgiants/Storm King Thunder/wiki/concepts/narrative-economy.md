# Narrative Economy

Narrative economy is the deliberate omission of exposition, backstory, or detail — particularly in NPCs, locations, or events — to preserve focus on plot-critical elements, avoid narrative clutter, and enhance emotional resonance. It is not negligence or laziness, but a precision design principle of efficiency: only what serves the story is rendered. Depth is conveyed not through exposition, but through behavior, implication, sensory detail, and the power of what is left unsaid.

In this campaign, narrative economy manifests in two primary, contrasting forms:

1. **Intentional voids** — Characters like [[backes_dunfield]] are presented with zero depth, signaling to players that no investment is required. Their emptiness is a message: *this is not important*. This form reinforces world realism by refusing to generate illusionary depth where none is needed.

2. **Strategic ambiguity** — Characters like [[azan]], [[baash-a-kweelani]], corbin, [[brewbarry]], cooragh-struckt, and facktore have details withheld not because they are irrelevant, but because revealing them would diminish their narrative function. Their mystery sustains tension, preserves symbolic weight, and invites emotional projection without demanding explanation.

The distinction is critical. [[information-asymmetry-npc-encounters]] deals with hidden truths — secrets waiting to be uncovered. Narrative economy deals with *non-existent* or *unspoken* truths — silences that discourage investigation not by obscuring, but by refusing to generate the illusion of depth where none exists.

Corbin’s entire arc is a masterclass in narrative economy. His past is never narrated — it is whispered in confession to Soma. His trauma is not explained — it is shown in his trembling hands and refusal to confront Carver’s gang. His redemption is not declared — it is evidenced in his restraint, his questions, his sacraments. Similarly, Brewbarry’s unspoken exile and Cooragh Struckt’s silent cooking resonate because they are never explained.

Facktore exemplifies strategic ambiguity at its most potent. His backstory is not omitted because it is unimportant — it is omitted because it is dangerous. To define him fully would reduce him from a symbol of institutional failure to a mere villain. His power lies in what is *not* said: his origins, his motivations beyond testing, his possible allies, his future plans. The mystery of his whereabouts, his inventions, and his potential return sustains tension without requiring exposition.

The most resonant moments in this world are not the ones shouted — they are the ones left unsaid. The existence of Backes Dunfield proves that silence — even in character design — can be a powerful tool. Narrative economy is the art of knowing what not to say, and trusting the audience to feel what is missing.
