# Cynical-alliance-as-strategy

A political and moral framework in which alliances are formed not for mutual benefit, but to weaken third parties — or to sacrifice individuals — so that the actor may rise in their absence. This strategy operates on cold, utilitarian calculus, where human value is measured solely by utility to a larger goal. Morality is suspended not out of inherent evil, but out of perceived necessity: the belief that certain losses are justified for abstract, long-term gain.

Blagothkus allied with the Cult of the Dragon and Sandesyl Morgia not to achieve peace or direct power, but to ensure the destruction of “small peoples” — mortals, dwarves, and other non-giants — so that giants could inherit a weakened world. His actions exemplify the strategy’s core: betrayal is not hidden, but celebrated as wisdom. He weaponized [[divine-knowledge-as-strategic-tool]], not to guide justice, but to sanctify atrocity.

This same logic manifests in the party’s decision to leave Corbin alone with Sister Kaella. They did not intend to kill him — they intended to win. His life was weighed against the mission to Icespire Hold, and found wanting. This is not villainy, but the logic of power: the expendability of the individual framed as strategic pragmatism. It mirrors Azam’s use of Unla Kee’s integrity as a tool, and echoes the broader pattern of [[sacrificial-decoy-tactics]] and [[moral-ambiguity-of-inaction-undead]].

Unlike [[cover-identity-as-merchant]], where deception conceals operations, cynical alliance openly embraces betrayal as policy. It is not hidden — it is rationalized as necessity. Yet as Blagothkus learned, and as the party now faces, such strategies breed isolation, resentment, and inevitable collapse. His downfall was not caused by enemies — it was caused by the moral vacuum his strategy created. The tragedy is not that the party chose to sacrifice Corbin — it is that they believed they had a right to choose.
