# Castle-as-character

The flying castle at Parnast is not merely a location — it is an extension of Blagothkus’s psyche and Escarlotta’s will. Its systems, corridors, and control interfaces reflect their internal states: isolation, guilt, control, and spectral imprisonment.

The castle’s sentience is not magical in the traditional sense, but psychological: it responds to emotional states, traps its occupants in loops of memory, and enforces moral accountability. Blagothkus’s imprisonment in the control room mirrors his emotional confinement; Escarlotta’s control over its systems mirrors her dominance over his conscience.

This concept extends [[operational-anxiety]] and [[organizational-secrecy-via-bosses]] into architecture: power is not held by people, but by spaces. The castle is a character with desires — to punish, to reveal, to release. It does not speak, but it acts.

Its survival after Blagothkus’s death suggests it is not bound to him alone — it may be older, older than giants, and its true purpose remains unknown. Is it a prison? A temple? A memory made stone?
