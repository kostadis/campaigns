# Music as Metaphor for Cosmic Order

A unique philosophical device used by Adabra Gwynn to articulate her worldview: she frames the multiverse as a symphony, where every entity — from treants to demons — plays a note in the grand composition. Interventionists, she argues, are those who reject certain “music” — planar energies, outer-planar beings, or cultic rituals — as dissonant, and seek to silence them.

This metaphor is not poetic flourish — it is ideological architecture. It transforms abstract cosmic balance into an audible, emotional experience. When she tells Vukradin that interventionists “reject the music,” she is not speaking metaphorically — she is invoking a sensory, spiritual truth she believes he once understood.

Vukradin, a man who once played music for its own sake, is uniquely vulnerable to this metaphor. His later reflection — “Did I only do it for the music, or for the audience?” — reveals how deeply Adabra’s framing penetrated his identity. The metaphor is so potent because it bypasses logic and speaks to emotion, memory, and belonging.

This concept elevates philosophy from doctrine to art. It shows how ideology can be transmitted not through argument, but through resonance — a harmony of belief that, once broken, leaves only silence.

It is the most beautiful and dangerous weapon in Adabra’s arsenal.
