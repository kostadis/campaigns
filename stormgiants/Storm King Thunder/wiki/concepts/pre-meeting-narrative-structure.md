# Pre-meeting narrative structure

A storytelling technique in which a character or location is introduced as a destination, objective, or rumored figure — referenced, described, or alluded to — before the party ever encounters them directly. The entity remains undefined: its traits, motives, affiliations, and true nature are withheld to generate narrative tension, anticipation, and player speculation.

This structure delays exposition, preserves plot flexibility, and heightens the impact of the first encounter. By avoiding premature revelation, it allows the GM to adapt the character’s role based on evolving party actions, ensuring the narrative remains responsive and organic. The mystery encourages players to speculate, invest emotionally, and anticipate resolution.

**Examples:**
- [[azan]] — Introduced as the target of a meeting at the Oasis of Vuerthyl; identity and motives entirely unknown; the journey to him *is* the active plot.
- [[baash-a-kweelani]] — Mentioned by Unla Kee as a known merchant; no direct contact yet, but her appearance and profession suggest high narrative stakes and hidden depth.

This approach contrasts with direct exposition and relies on the player’s investment in uncovering truth. It is closely related to *information asymmetry in NPC encounters* and often supports archetypes like *cover-identity-as-merchant*, reinforcing the theme that not all truths are meant to be known until the moment of revelation. It is a foundational element of deliberate campaign architecture, enhancing pacing and suspense through controlled revelation.
