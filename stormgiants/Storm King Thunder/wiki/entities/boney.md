# Boney

**Boney** (formerly known as **Barney**) is a sentient, non-hostile undead skeletal horse who serves as a loyal companion, mount, and emotional anchor to the adventuring party. His existence challenges the assumption that undead must be evil or soulless, embodying loyalty that transcends death and demonstrating [[undead-sentience-without-corruption]].

## Identity & Personality

Boney retains a cheerful, good-natured disposition and a penchant for self-deprecating humor regarding his skeletal state. He accepts his undead form with grace, even embracing the renaming from “Barney” to “Boney” as an act of identity reclamation. Though he mourns the loss of his mane and physical features, he does not dwell on it, instead focusing on service and companionship.

His personality is marked by emotional warmth and quiet dignity. He is not corrupted, controlled, or malevolent — his sentience is genuine and uncorrupted, making him a rare example of a benevolent undead creature. He does not speak, but his demeanor — cheerful, self-deprecating, and unwavering — reflects a soul uncorrupted by death.

## History

- **Original Life:** Boney was once a living horse named Barney, serving the noble [[lady-alagondar]].
- **Death:** He was killed by malevolent [[will-o-wisps]] while guarding a secret within the [[dragonbarrow]] tunnels, before he could complete his duties.
- **Reanimation:** He was reanimated not by necromancy, but by the force of his devotion to Lady Alagondar. Upon the party’s arrival — specifically [[soma]]’s approach — he mistook her for the reincarnation of his former master. The party successfully convinced him of this identity, securing his immediate and unwavering loyalty.
- **Loyalty Transference:** His devotion shifted seamlessly from his deceased master to the party, demonstrating a profound bond of loyalty that persists beyond death. This transfer is central to his narrative function and embodies [[loyalty-transference]].
- **Narrative Significance:** Boney was left in the Dragonbarrow to guard the Dragon Slayer Sword — a task he fulfilled faithfully until the party recovered it. Upon its retrieval, Lady Alagondar’s ghost appeared, confirming his loyalty and the significance of the artifact. His story is one of quiet redemption: a creature of death who chooses service, companionship, and grace over vengeance or despair.
- **Emotional Resonance:** Lady Alagondar never spoke to him in life, yet he carries her purpose beyond death — a powerful example of [[emotional-resonance-through-absence]].

## Key Events & Relationships

- **Dragon Slayer Sword:** Boney provided the critical directional intelligence that led the party to the Dragon Slayer Sword, proving invaluable to their quest. His guardianship of the artifact was the defining purpose of his afterlife.
- **Bond with Valphine:** After the sword’s discovery, he formed a deep emotional bond with [[valphine]], further cementing his role as a cherished companion.
- **Corbin’s Death:** Boney was present during the moment [[sister-kaella]] killed corbin. His lack of intervention during this event has sparked ongoing moral debate, particularly with [[soma]], who questions whether human ethical standards apply to non-human entities — especially undead ones. This moment highlights the ambiguity of moral agency in non-human companions.

## Combat Status & Utility

- **Role:** Active party companion and mount. He is actively protected and managed in combat by [[soma]], who uses him as a mobile asset to reposition and maintain tactical advantages.
- **Fragility:** Boney is highly fragile, possessing only **13 HP**. He is not designed for frontline combat and relies on his party for protection.
- **Narrative Over Tactical Value:** While he can be used tactically, his true utility lies in his emotional and narrative impact. He is not a weapon — he is a symbol.

## Moral & Philosophical Dimensions

Boney’s existence raises profound questions:
- Can loyalty and sentience exist without a soul?
- Do ethical obligations apply to non-human, undead companions?
- Is inaction in the face of violence a moral failing — or a limitation of being?

[[soma]] has noted his “questionable” moral standing, not due to malice, but due to his passive nature during violent events. Yet his kindness, humor, and devotion suggest a moral compass that operates outside human norms — not broken, but different.

His story is one of quiet redemption: a creature of death who chooses service, companionship, and grace over vengeance or despair.
