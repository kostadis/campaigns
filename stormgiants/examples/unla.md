## From Scene 03 (Schmoozing with the Chromatic Dragons): the intellectual outpaced

The Grand Reveal was still hours away, and the guest list was turning out to be significantly more fire-hazard than anticipated.

Three ancient chromatic dragons — Flume, whose name apparently needed no elaboration; a green dragon whose financial grievances predated most civilizations; and something called Obsidianth who controlled *trade*, apparently — had arrived early, circling each other at a territorial remove that suggested centuries of accumulated contempt. I watched them from a distance that I privately considered optimal for continued existence, and I thought: these are the most dangerous beings in this courtyard, and Orsik is about to walk up and flatter them.

The thing about Orsik with the Headband on is that he is, frustratingly, good at this. The new Orsik calculates. Schemes. Uses words like tools rather than projectiles. I was the intellectual of this group — I have the cosmological proof to demonstrate it — but watching him stride toward Flume's entourage with that particular dwarf confidence, I felt the familiar small thorn of knowing he might actually pull this off better than I would.

The young dragon blocking his path didn't last long. I couldn't hear the full exchange from where I was standing, but whatever Orsik offered — a private preview, special access, the unambiguous suggestion of being treated as *different* from the other guests — it was enough. Within minutes he was standing before Flume herself.

Flume lowered her enormous head. The heat rolling off her was a physical presence, a wall of it, and I had the sudden conviction that she could have toasted Orsik to a cinder before he finished his first sentence if she'd felt like it.

"What do you have to offer?" she said. "I was promised something special and all I smell is a tiny, sniveling dwarf."

Orsik didn't flinch. I have to give him that. "I'm just going to clue you in on everything that's going to happen tomorrow ahead of schedule," he said. "Give you all the information."

The great red head tilted. "Oh — you're going to tell me about this show? This spectacle?"

"That's right."

"Iymrith said you were some kind of spectacular director."

Orsik didn't miss a beat. "Have you ever been to Silverymoon? I can neither confirm nor deny those rumors."

Something shifted in her expression that might have been amusement. "I can confirm nor deny," she said, which somehow made sense coming from a creature who had accumulated enough power to find grammar optional.

Then Orsik started laying it on — *schmoozing*, he would call it later, without any apparent embarrassment — you're awesome, you're amazing, thank you for coming, you're getting treatment no other guest is receiving. The particular alchemy of flattery and exclusivity that humans and dwarves use on each other in taverns and throne rooms, deployed now on an ancient apex predator, and somehow working.

She told him her name was Flume. A much longer name existed, but this was what she went by. The simplicity, she explained, was the point.

"Once you reach a certain age, and a certain power, and a certain respect, you don't need fancy names. A simple name is all they need."

"It's genius," Orsik said.

"You have all these other red dragons with these names," she continued, "like the red beater, or something that's twenty-eight syllables that nobody can remember. But when you're me — you need something simple. Something so that people can remember it."

I stood at my watching distance and quietly absorbed this. An ancient red dragon explaining personal branding. The conviction she delivered it with was absolute. She had been right about everything for so long that the possibility of being wrong had simply ceased to exist as a concept for her. I recognized that feeling. I lived inside it myself on most days.

The difference, I noted, was that she was genuinely ancient and terrifying, and I was a three-foot halfling who had recently won an argument with a mud mephit.

## From Scene 03 (Schmoozing with the Chromatic Dragons): the mantra, the helping somewhat less

Then we approached Obsidianth.

I should clarify that I had a plan. The plan was to destabilize him — ancient predators have egos, and egos have pressure points, and if you find the right one and push, you can redirect a great deal of attention and goodwill. The other dragons had given us nothing to work with, but the right taunt, aimed correctly, would give us leverage.

My contribution was: "Those guys said you were the weakest dragon."

In my defense, the sentiment was sound. The attribution was the problem.

"That's what Iymrith said," Orsik added immediately.

"That's what Iymrith said," Thistle confirmed, without missing a beat.

Obsidianth — and the name had a self-important ring to it that I appreciated in the abstract — did not seem troubled by the insult. He waved it off with the contempt of someone who had processed and dismissed much larger threats.

"They're just jealous that I have the biggest operation," he said. "I have spent centuries outmaneuvering them, and now my clan controls most of the trade."

Orsik stared at him. "Trade?"

"Murder," Orsik continued, "rampage, bloodletting — that's cool. But trade? Isn't that what the Zhentarim do? Are you the head of the Zhentarim? Is that what you do now?"

Obsidianth began to reply, and Orsik was already moving: "You get paid to work for them? You're like a capo — you're like a —"

I tried to redirect. "Do you think one day you'll be as powerful as the giants?"

Obsidianth looked at me. He looked at Orsik. He appeared to conduct a very brief internal assessment of whether this conversation had anything to offer him.

"I don't want to talk to you," he said, and flew off.

Vardis watched him go. "That one," he said, pleasantly, "might need some later persuasion."

Obsidianth, we were informed by the empty air where he'd been, was unimpressed.

I stood there and considered what had just happened. We had successfully charmed one ancient dragon, financially reassured a second, and been dismissed with contempt by a third who considered himself a trade magnate. I had contributed a gambit that required immediate correction by two of my companions. The dwarf with the intelligence headband had outperformed me in the diplomacy event.

The multitude of planes are safe only if I take risks.

I repeated this to myself, which usually helped.

It helped somewhat less than usual.

## From Scene 05 (The Investors and the Betrayal of Uncle Joon): the cleared space in the crowd

The mind flayers arrived without announcement, which is the only way something like a mind flayer *can* arrive — any other approach would be anticlimactic. One moment there was the general press of assembled dignitaries and monstrosities filling Iymrith's lair for her Grand Reveal, and the next there was a pocket of absolute stillness in the crowd, a clearing that nobody had decided to make but that everybody had quietly agreed on anyway.

I noticed it immediately. Hard not to.

"Ooh, that'll be useful. Useful? What?"

I said it before I finished thinking it, which is a habit I've never successfully broken. The observation was genuine — there is something cosmically useful about creatures that every other creature in a room instinctively avoids. I filed it away.

Orsik, being Orsik, went directly to the source. He leaned toward Iymrith — our enormous, ancient, technically-our-employer dragon — and asked, with the easy conversational manner of a dwarf who has survived this long entirely through audacity: "Can we ask Iymrith? Iymrith, what's up with the mind flayers?"

She said: "Investors."

The word landed flat, delivered with the practiced neutrality of someone who does not wish to discuss a thing and hopes that naming it will end the inquiry.

"Really?" I said.

It did not end the inquiry. Orsik, newly equipped with a headband that had upgraded his intellect to something genuinely threatening, was not going to let an answer like that stand. He pressed her — concerned about the mind flayers' financing, ostensibly looking out for Iymrith's interests, which was the most transparent piece of political theatre I had heard in recent memory and also, I have to admit, delivered with reasonable conviction.

"We're trying to make sure that you're not being taken for a ride, Iymrith. We're looking out for your interests."

The dragon was not moved. "Just get the creature to fly and we will all be okay."

She was nervous. An ancient blue dragon, the architect of a scheme that had destabilized the entire giant lordship, nervous about her *investors*. I glanced at the cleared space in the crowd where the mind flayers stood in their awful, self-possessed quiet.

Nobody was approaching them. Every other faction in the room — fire giants, frost giants, a dragon or two, assembled guild representatives, a Gloaming Court envoy I was trying not to think too hard about — all of them had potential adversaries they were eyeing, sizing up, quietly calculating. But nobody was calculating the mind flayers. Nobody was even looking in their direction if they could help it.

"I mean," I said, to no one in particular, "that's just good."

## From Scene 05 (The Investors and the Betrayal of Uncle Joon): the technique, the sloppy execution

He said Titania required things of him. Then, as if he heard how that sounded, he walked it back immediately: "Maybe it wasn't Titania. I don't want you to think that it was Titania. But it was somebody with that kind of importance level, you know."

I appreciated, in a technical sense, the efficiency of that reversal. Name a power that makes the debt sound cosmically inevitable, then quietly rescind the name before anyone can check, leaving only the *feeling* of cosmic inevitability behind. It's good technique. Sloppy execution — he let the uncertainty show — but the underlying structure was sound.

He was also, he mentioned, confused about the timing. He had been waiting in the lair for weeks. The party wasn't supposed to show up during the Grand Summit. *Why did you show up now?*

Orsik said, quietly: "Fredo, how are things?"

I didn't know the reference. The tone was enough.

Joon kept going — debts, obligations, the waiting, the timeline that hadn't matched his expectations — and somewhere in the middle of the third or fourth justification, Thistle made a decision.

"Han shot first," Thistle said. "Kimberly — I'd like to suppress him."

## From Scene 05 (The Investors and the Betrayal of Uncle Joon): the sense of occasion

And then Orsik, once the matter was settled, had what I can only describe as an idea that was genuinely inspired in the way that only slightly petty ideas can be.

"Whatever item it was that he wanted — go ahead and put it in his hand so he can hold it in his last few moments."

The lair accepted this proposal without comment. Joon, bound and gagged in the shadows beneath an ancient dragon's hoard, would spend the chaos of the Grand Reveal day holding the very thing he had traded his nephew's safety to reach — and be unable to do a single thing about it.

I found myself, for perhaps the fourth or fifth time since joining this party, mildly impressed by Orsik's sense of occasion.
