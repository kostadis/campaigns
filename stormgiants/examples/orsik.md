## From Scene 02 (The Rescue Plan and Rehearsal): coaching the dragon

The plan was coming together. That's the thought I kept returning to as I stood in the amphitheater above and listened to a dragon rehearse her own eulogy.

Below us, Thistle had swept the area before speaking — a careful check, thorough enough that I didn't need to second-guess it. Twenty-three, he confirmed, and nobody was watching. Good instincts, for a man currently wearing his captor's eyes in his skull. I don't know what Laela made of that detail when she saw it — the dragon-slit pupils in the face of the person she loved — but I heard the eruption from the amphitheater and I didn't need to be down there to understand it. *I want him dead.* Then she calmed down. That's what captivity does to a person's emotional range. You learn to triage.

I was focused on other business.

"Iymrith," I said, "why don't you give this monologue a shot? Try it out on me."

She did. Slow. Ponderous. Each word placed like a stone in a wall.

*I remember the whispers, the doubts. The shaking hands in candlelit halls —*

"Okay," I said. "That was good. Now, lower your voice, quieter, and read it faster."

She looked at me with the expression a dragon gives you when it is deciding whether to be insulted. Then she tried again.

"Faster."

A pause.

"Faster."

The second attempt was different. She found something in it — the rhythm of the piece, the architecture of the grievance. It moved. *No creature, scaled, mortal, or divine, can wield such power without being consumed by it. And yet here I stand —*

"Much better!" I told her. "Much better! You got it. Just keep working on that."

Iymrith straightened. Something genuinely moved behind those ancient eyes.

"You're a genius," she said.

I didn't argue. "It is going to be awesome."

"This is going to be awesome," she agreed, and I believe she meant it. There's a particular species of vanity in dragons — the need to be witnessed, to be *recognized* — and I had just handed her the precise tool she wanted for that purpose. She was grateful in the way powerful things are grateful when they get what they need without having to reach for it.

## From Scene 02 (The Rescue Plan and Rehearsal): artistic freedom, ledger absurd

"Well," I said to Iymrith, once the rehearsal warmth had settled between us, "if I meet the guests, then that should be easier for me to persuade them at the right time."

Iymrith considered this with the caution of someone who enjoys being the center of attention and does not love competition for it. "Well, I mean, Laela is the star, but I suppose — we could have you present. We could have you meet some of the guests of honor. I don't want you to take too much of my thunder."

I looked at her levelly.

"Iymrith, let me explain to you how it works. You hired us to do you a service. To put on a show for you."

"Yes."

"And I need the artistic freedom in order to make this happen for you. I don't want you putting any sort of constraints on me or compromising that vision. Sometimes I need to do things and I can't always explain to you why. It's just the way it is. We had this discussion yesterday and you came around. Do we need to have the same discussion again?"

A pause. The kind of pause that in a smaller creature might indicate wounded pride. In a dragon it just looks like weather.

"No, no, no. All right."

"Thank you."

Artistic freedom. I had given a speech about artistic freedom to a dragon who was planning to install herself as the supreme authority of the known world, in the last hours before I intended to help kill her. The soldier's ledger would have found this absurd. The headband just noted it as effective.

The plan was set. The guests would come. The show would begin. Laela would get out. I would be in the room with five dragon lords, the Red Wizards of Thay, giant representatives from half the families in Faerûn, a Fae traitor, and one very sincere, very moved dragon who currently believed I was a genius.

It was, as Iymrith herself had said, going to be awesome.

## From Scene 07 (The Great Switcheroo): one-dimensional chess

The plan had looked better on paper. Everything does.

But here we were, and Bob—Bob, disguised as Laela, standing in front of a crowd of assembled dragons and giants and whoever else Iymrith had managed to impress into attending her Grand Reveal—was telling dad jokes.

Thistle had gone back down. That was the part I had to hold onto while the rest of it unraveled at whatever pace it was unraveling. One piece at a time. Thistle down, real Laela up, Bob does his thing, crowd turns. That was the sequence. One-dimensional chess, Thistle had called it.

"Alright. Wow."

I said it to no one in particular. Maybe to myself. Maybe to the universe, which at this moment appeared to be operating exactly as it always does—taking my careful plan and improvising freely within it.

"Alright, Bob, that's your cue."

Then, because Bob was standing there as Laela and not, technically, as Bob: "Uh, I mean, Laela."

Thistle was still sorting out the logistics—*Alright, Laela, you're up. What? Uh, Laela's up, or is it, uh*—which was the kind of sentence that meant nobody was entirely certain who was supposed to be where. I tried to simplify it.

"Laela, Bob, the switcheroo, it's time."

We sorted the sequence out loud. Laela first, then she had to go prep, Thistle explained. Real Laela still below. The crowd had already inspected the fake one. The inspections were done, which meant the clock was moving whether we were ready or not.

"We will stay here," I said, "while you bring Laela to the surface."

Thistle confirmed he'd have to go back down to get her. I thought about that for half a second.

"Why can't she—is she not coming up invisible?"

Thistle's answer was the kind that made complete tactical sense once you heard it: he'd been waiting until after there was a distraction. He'd fly back down, then come back up under cover of whatever chaos we generated above.

"Oh, okay," I said. "So you just go—you just fly back down? Alright."

"One dimensional chess," Thistle said.

"Yeah," I said. "Except for Thistle, who apparently went back down to his girlfriend."

It wasn't a great joke. But the alternative was standing in silence while Bob-as-Laela faced down a crowd expecting something spectacular, and silence felt worse.

## From Scene 07 (The Great Switcheroo): the plan landing, "good enough today"

Iymrith—the Doom of the Desert, architect of a conspiracy spanning decades, patron of giants and collector of leverage—was currently standing before her Grand Reveal audience, surrounded by booing and jeering, completely preoccupied by the demon she thought was her prisoner's human face.

By Bob.

"By Bob?" I confirmed.

By Bob.

I don't have a word for what that felt like. The Headband gives me vocabulary for a lot of things. It doesn't have a column for this—for the specific sensation of watching a plan not just work, but work in exactly the way you hoped it might, which is a different thing entirely. The plan working is transactional. This was something else.

The crowd was turning. The boos were spreading. Iymrith was sputtering.

I'd been running the soldier's calculus on this operation for sessions. Itemised the losses, tracked the assets, managed the timing. And now the thing I'd built—the ridiculous, overcomplicated, perfectly-constructed thing—was landing exactly where I'd aimed it.

Good enough today.
