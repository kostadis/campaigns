## From Scene 04 (The Fire Giant Confrontation): the threshold, the "Awwww"

The fire giants' corridor stank of ash and old metal, the kind of heat that doesn't cool overnight. I'd grown accustomed to warmth — the Wall of Fire was my argument, Flamestrike my verdict — but this was different. This was the ambient temperature of something enormous just being alive on the other side of a very short hallway.

"The fire giants," Orsik said, with the tone of a man confirming an unpleasant medical diagnosis. "These are the ones that we stole the thing from."

They were. All five of them. Duke Zalto and his guards, exactly where they'd been when we'd raided his quarters, ransacked his bedchambers, and fled into the dark before anyone could ask uncomfortable questions. The pragmatic read was that they'd never actually seen our faces. The less pragmatic read was that we were about to hand a fire giant lord a gift he'd recognize from his own bedroom.

Orsik had the idea, as he often does when an idea will either be brilliant or catastrophic. "Did we find, like, questionable things in their bedchambers?"

Thistle, without missing a beat: "Yes, yes, you did actually."

"We could just, like, give them, like, some whips or something, like, Iymrith brought these, she had these for you, she knew you would like them."

"Yeah. There we go."

Thistle. To her credit. I watched Zalto's face — as much as you can read the face of a creature the size of a collapsed building — and I made the calculation I always make: how far does this go before it stops being deception and starts being the last mistake. The threshold keeps moving. I have noticed this about myself. I note it now, and move on.

Zalto looked at us. "Who are you?"

Orsik, ever helpful: "So that's your cue."

Thistle stepped forward with the easy warmth of someone who has never met a giant she couldn't charm, presenting the item with the confidence of a legitimate courier. Iymrith's personal assistants. Everyone felt welcome. Very particular tastes. Preserved here for you, just so.

The Duke's expression shifted. The guards didn't move. And then:

"Iymrith. She always knows what I like."

"Awwww."

I said that. I am aware that I said that. The word arrived before the assessment did, which is not characteristic of me, but there it was: a warm, involuntary noise of recognition at the precise moment a fire giant lord confirmed he was enthusiastically into the contents of his own stolen bedside table.

Orsik recovered faster than I did. "Okay, I guess that was effective."

Effective. The word for when a plan that shouldn't work works in a direction you weren't prepared for. Zalto was not suspicious. Zalto was *delighted*. "I'm totally into the, you know, whips and chains. It's, it's, it's all the rage."

## From Scene 04 (The Fire Giant Confrontation): the door, pragmatic

There it was. The fracture. Not embarrassment — something closer to loneliness, the particular loneliness of a giant warlord who believes his closest ally sees him clearly when no one else does. I know something about that. Corellon sees clearly too, allegedly. It doesn't stop the bird droppings.

"Iymrith's a true friend, huh?" Orsik probed. "So are you worried about others finding out, or you're cool with it?"

"I'm cool with it."

"You're cool. But it's Iymrith that's mistaken."

And then Zalto went quiet in the specific way that giants go quiet before something structural changes. "Is this why she gave me a small whip?"

Thistle: "That's right."

The confirmation landed clean. I watched it land. This is the part I notice — not the lie itself, but the moment after, when it settles and takes hold, and the person being deceived rearranges themselves around it without knowing they're rearranging.

Orsik added the cloud giants. He couldn't help it. The man has complex plots now where he used to have simple ones; sometimes the complexity runs ahead of the objective. The cloud giants laughing at Zalto's private tastes, Iymrith having shared the secret, the small whip as a kind of coded mockery —

Zalto started cursing. The kind of cursing that doesn't stop because it finds new targets. His guards moved to contain him, which told you something about how often this happened.

"Oh," Orsik said. "Oh, did we say the wrong thing?"

The self-assessment arrived about four seconds after the obvious answer. I have seen him do this my entire adult life — charge forward, then check. The Headband hasn't fully corrected the sequencing.

"Oh, we should go."

"Yes, you should."

"Well, that was our bad. Whoops a—"

Thistle was already reframing, walking backward with the practiced ease of someone who has redirected worse: "We'll tell Iymrith not to do that plan."

"Yeah, yeah, yeah, yeah, no cloud giants whipping—"

"The Lord Fire Giant. Yeah, okay. Maybe we should go tell them right now that the plan is off. Let's go do that."

We left. Zalto was still being held back by his own retinue, still cursing Iymrith's name, still furious on behalf of a betrayal that hadn't happened — that we had assembled from stolen property and a deception roll and Orsik's instinct for the pressure point. The fracture was deeper now. One more crack in a coalition we needed to be rubble before we were ready.

Pragmatic.

The word closed the door behind us. It always does.

## From Scene 08 (Chaos at the Amphitheater): threat-math, surrounded by professionals

The red dragon screamed first.

Not words — just that tearing, metallic shriek that means a creature the size of a house has decided the situation has changed. I tracked it the way you track anything that could kill you at distance: fast, eyes moving, threat-assessing before the sound finished echoing off the amphitheater stone. The red dragon's neck swung toward the hole in the floor. The black dragon swung toward the hole in the floor. And the chromatic dragons' little ones — the wyrmlings and the half-grown ones who'd been arranged so carefully for Iymrith's reveal — started climbing over each other to get there first.

They didn't care about Iymrith.

They never had. These were dragons. They cared about the gold. Whatever loyalty they'd shown was contractual at best, and the contract had just been voided in spectacular fashion, and now there was a hoard at the bottom of that hole and nobody was guarding it. Pragmatic. Even the chromatic dragons were pragmatic.

The problem was the hole.

"That happens to be the entrance into the Horde," I heard, and then — Thistle's voice, flat with the particular calm of someone processing terrible information in real time:

*"That's where Laela and I are coming up from."*

I felt the calculation happen somewhere behind my sternum, the kind of threat-math that doesn't wait for your conscious mind to catch up. Thistle and Laela climbing up through a passage. Three chromatic dragons climbing down through the same passage. The geometry was not good. I kept my feet where they were and looked for the other problems, because there were always other problems, and I would not be useful to anyone if I stopped counting.

The storm giants materialized in the upper terraces of the amphitheater like weather arriving — vast, armored, their presence reorganizing the air pressure of the space itself. And the other giants looked at King Hekaton the way men look at money they've been trying to steal for years. One-time opportunity. Every frost lord and fire count and hill thane doing the arithmetic at once, and the arithmetic said: the storm king is surrounded by enemies and distracted, and that never happens twice.

I filed that problem under *Orsik's job* and kept moving.

The Red Wizards of Thay were already walking away. Quietly, unhurried, the way very powerful people exit situations that no longer serve them. Someone said they were writing off their investment. The reading was too charitable.

"No," I would have said, if anyone had asked me. "They're filing it under *unfinished business*. Wizards of Thay don't lose interest. They just go find a longer lever."

Nobody asked. The mind flayers simply stepped back from the edge of the crowd and waited, and the crowd parted around them the way water parts around something cold, and nobody moved toward them because that was what survival instinct looked like in this company. The cloud giants sat on their terraces like judges at a tournament, calculating which outcome served them best.

Everybody here was pragmatic. I was surrounded by professionals.

## From Scene 08 (Chaos at the Amphitheater): follow the light

Somewhere on the far side of the amphitheater, Bob Saget was doing what Bob Saget did — something enormous and terrible and, at this particular moment, extremely useful — and Iymrith was occupied, hemmed in, preoccupied by the demon on one side and the storm giants on the other. She was still a god in scale. She was still the thing at the end of all of this. But she was not, for this one moment, looking at us.

We had storm giants. We had Bob. We had each other and a battlefield full of enemies who would accidentally help us if we managed this right.

My best friend has a death wish.

I cast my eye across the chaos, measured the distance to Orsik's flank, counted what I had left in me. Enough. For now, enough.

Good thing I chose to follow the light.
