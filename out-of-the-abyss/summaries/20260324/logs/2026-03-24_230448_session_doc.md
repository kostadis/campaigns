## Section — Thorin — Tactical Preparations

The spell-slot conversation had the shape of a budget meeting, except the budget was four fourth-level slots and the consequences of overspending were dying in a drow outpost.

I listened. That's what I do in these conversations, mostly. Grygum and Daz were running the numbers — Glyph of Warding, Dust of Suleiman, Phantasmal Killer, Fireball — one each, maybe, if everything lined up. Zalthir was talking about teleporting directly to Ilvara, or grabbing her and dropping her somewhere she wouldn't enjoy. The plan had that particular quality that good plans have: it was aggressive, it was detailed, and it depended on everything going exactly right in the first three seconds.

I have thoughts about plans like that. I usually keep them to myself, because Thorin saying "what if it doesn't" every five minutes does not make anyone fight better.

What I noticed was this: every move on the board was a spell. Every pivot, every contingency — it came back to the slots. Grygum had them. Daz had them. I had Dawnbringer and the crossbow I'd just borrowed from Grygum, which he'd handed over without ceremony because we both understood the arithmetic. A fighter with no ranged option is a fighter who stands still and waits to be solved. Grygum didn't make a thing of it. I didn't either. It was just a crossbow changing hands on the way into a fight.

The Glyph was Grygum's idea, and it was a good one. He moved to the bridge on Jorlan's side and started inscribing — careful, focused, the kind of work that looks slow until you understand what it costs to get it wrong. I watched him work and thought about the bridge. About a hostile creature stepping onto it and the world deciding not to let that stand. There's something satisfying about a trap you can see being built. It doesn't feel like deception. It feels like math.

The GM pulled Grygum and me aside before the planning got too far.

Don't touch the mushroom artifact.

We'd both met Zuggtmoy before. The warning wasn't abstract — it had texture, it had memory behind it, it had the specific weight of something we had already survived once and did not want to survive again. I noted it. Filed it in the part of my mind that holds the non-negotiable rules. That list is short. It's short because long lists of rules are how you end up hesitating when you should be moving. But "do not touch the demon queen's artifact" earns a spot without argument.

Asha's rebuke hit before any of that, actually. Grygum's player had floated something — blowing her up, the shape of the idea — and Asha turned that particular brand of drow contempt on the group like she was adjusting the angle of a blade.

*"I thought you had strong allies, who had a tactical awareness and understanding how to use their magic for destructive purposes."*

Nobody in particular. Everybody exactly.

I stood there and absorbed it. There's a version of me that argues with that — that points out we're improvising a plan in real time against a drow outpost commander and a potentially demon-touched situation with limited information and four fourth-level slots between two casters. There's a version of me that says something direct and probably unhelpful.

I didn't say it. Because she wasn't wrong, exactly. She was just expressing it like someone who has never needed to be patient with the people she was insulting.

Her terms, when they came, were: she'd wait outside until we initiated, then hold the perimeter. Not "I will fight beside you." Not a guarantee. A flank that might not collapse immediately, bought by Daz doing something theological and improvised that I didn't entirely follow but that apparently worked.

I can work with "might not collapse immediately." That's better than nothing. Giants don't promise more than that either, and they mean it completely when they do.

So. Glyph on the bridge. Crossbow in my hand. Four slots mapped against four targets. Asha outside, holding something that I'll believe is held when I see it held.

We knew he was there — the scout, Jorlan's man, whoever he was. He didn't know we knew. That was the only real asset we had that wasn't a spell slot, and I kept coming back to it.

*But he doesn't know that we know that he's there.*

One guy. One angle of approach we'd already solved. The rest of it was going to move faster than planning, and everybody in the room understood that, even if nobody said it out loud.

I didn't say it out loud. Thorin picking a direction and shouting East only works when you know which way East is.

I was still working on East.
