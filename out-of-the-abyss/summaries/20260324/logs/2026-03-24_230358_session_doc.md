## Section — Daz — The Deception Completed

She had looked at the Dancing Lights and asked the question I was least prepared for.

"You're a wizard. A wizard… a wizard who prays to Lolth?"

Not quite an accusation. Not quite curiosity. The space in between those two things, which is where the dangerous questions live. I ran a quick inventory: she was a cleric, which meant she understood divine power from the inside, which meant she knew what it looked and felt like, which meant my performance had been evaluated by someone who actually knew the criteria. Suboptimal.

The possession angle came to me in the half-second before she expected an answer. Not Daz prays to Lolth — no, obviously that doesn't hold, she's already rejected it. But *Lolth speaking through Daz.* Male flesh as an unprecedented vessel. An anomaly so strange it couldn't be planned, and therefore couldn't be faked.

"I am… you are not speaking to the wizard of this body. I… he… this body has communed with Lolth. And I… I am speaking through this body."

I let the hesitation sit in the right places. A genuine divine possession would be disorienting, grammar-fracturing. I gave her that.

She stared at me for a long moment.

"Aha," she said. "You are trying to test me, to see if I was one of those fake followers of Lolth, who are willing to believe every charlatan magic user that is somehow being possessed by the power of Lolth."

There are two kinds of wrong. There's the kind where the mark sees through you. And there's the kind where the mark builds her own false structure and walks inside it. What Asha had done was the second kind — she hadn't disbelieved the con, she'd replaced it with a different story that she found more flattering to herself. She thought she was the one being tested.

I could work with that. I filed it away and kept moving.

"There are many imitations," I said, "but as you saw, I came to you in my smaller form, and led you here. You should know the truth."

The spider. The familiar I'd dismissed before the lights. If she'd been watching — and a survivor watches — she'd seen a spider lead her to this moment. That's not nothing. That's physical evidence, and physical evidence outlasts argument.

But she'd already moved past it.

"Oh, dear goodness," she said, and I heard the shift in her voice — less challenge, more diagnosis. "Have you two been infected by the spores, possessed by the crazed magic of Zuggtmoy? Convinced of the bride's power? Is this another trick of Elvara's?"

The divine claim had collapsed. That was clear. But here's the thing about a collapsing structure: sometimes it falls onto something more useful than what you'd built. Asha had landed on *deluded but sincere.* A madman who genuinely believed he'd been touched by Lolth. That was, in several practical ways, better than a wizard making fraudulent theological claims.

A madman can still kill someone. A madman with conviction is harder to destabilize than a man running a con. And Asha, I was starting to understand, did not need divinity. She needed a weapon she could aim.

"Perhaps," she said slowly, as though settling something in herself, "my prayers have been answered through this… confused vessel."

There it was.

I didn't push. When someone talks themselves into something, you don't celebrate, you don't confirm, you just let them finish the sentence and move on.

"I really do believe that I have," I said. Meaning: communed with Lolth, touched something real, whatever she needed that to mean. "Perhaps there are some spores within me, but I… it feels… I feel it. And by the way, I've convinced others to join me."

I brought them forward then. The party. Not as evidence of divine favor — that argument was behind us now — but as evidence of influence. A deluded man who had nonetheless assembled a crew was still a man with operational capacity. That was the sell. That was what she was actually buying.

She told us about Ilvara. I won't reproduce all of it here, but I want to note that the hatred in it was real. Whatever Asha was concealing, whatever angles she was running that I hadn't fully mapped yet, the loathing she had for what Ilvara had become was entirely genuine.

"Elvara, who was once a high priestess of Lolth, has become seduced by this abomination, this idea that Lolth wishes to be married. There is something foul and stinking about her, and then all of those allies she seems to covet have turned into fungal creatures that are mindless. We need to kill her. We need to butcher her! We need to send her to an unearthly grave! I fear that her disease will spread."

Noted. Sincere. Usable.

The obvious question presented itself.

"How do you propose to kill her if you're not gonna get within 20 feet of her?"

"I was going to kill her," Asha said. A pause. "That is what you… where you come in."

Clean. Two lines. The alliance crystallized into something I could hold: she had the information and the motive, we had the proximity she couldn't afford. Simple enough division of labor.

"If I and the crew that is with me were able to take care of this small problem for you," I said, and I let the word *small* do the work it was designed to do, "what would be in it for us?"

"The gratitude of Lolth."

"I already have the gratitude of Lolth. I have that."

I didn't flinch on it. The character I was playing — the sincerely infected, genuinely confused vessel of divine will — would have said exactly that. The confidence was the point. A man who doubts his divine mandate doesn't say *I already have that,* he says *surely, yes, more of that would be welcome.* I didn't hedge. I just told her what the character believed.

She didn't like it.

"That is not the kind of conviction a follower of Lolth shows."

"It's the kind of conviction this follower of Lolth shows."

No apology. No course correction. She'd decided I was a confused vessel, and a confused vessel has its own internal logic that doesn't need to conform to standard doctrine. She couldn't call me wrong — she'd already written the explanation that made my wrongness make sense.

This was also the moment Glabbagool decided to be a rock.

I say *decided.* He opened his eyes and let them drift left to right, which is not a feature of rocks that I am aware of. I give him credit for attempting concealment. I do not give him credit for the execution.

"Glabbagool," I said, "I'm taking care of this, it's fine."

And then, because there was nothing else to be done with an ooze who had very publicly failed to be geological, I introduced him as my familiar.

Asha was impressed. Somehow, the gelatinous cube landed where the Lolth possession had not. I don't fully understand this. I have filed it under: Underdark theology is strange, and a wizard with an ooze companion is apparently more credible than a wizard claiming divine visitation. Perhaps it's more verifiable. You can see the ooze.

She looked at me with something approaching clarity.

"You will deal with her in the appropriate way? You will kill her and destroy her magical artifact and make sure that her disease does not spread outside of this cavern?"

Kill Ilvara. Destroy the Big Heart Fungus. Contain the spores. She named it explicitly, and the weight behind it told me how she'd been surviving: twenty feet of distance at all times, separate food, Jorlan the only other one who'd managed it. She'd been meticulous. She wanted that meticulous caution to mean something. She wanted to know it had been worth it.

And then Grygum said something tactically inadvisable about our approach — I won't render it in full, but the effect on Asha was immediate and unpleasant.

"I thought you had strong allies," she said, "who had a tactical awareness and understanding how to use their magic for destructive purposes."

The opening was narrow. I stepped into it.

"We are not completely familiar with the spores and the mushrooms and all of that," I said, "and so it makes tactical sense for—"
