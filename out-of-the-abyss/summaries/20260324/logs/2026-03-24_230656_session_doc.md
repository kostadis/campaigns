## Section — Thorin — The Glyph Detonates

The glyph went off like a door slamming in a stone hall — sudden, total, final.

I'd watched Grygum work Jorlan from across the cavern, taunting him forward with all the subtlety of a boulder rolling downhill. "Come get it, idiot." That was the whole invitation. And Jorlan, because Jorlan was running on rage and corrupted instinct and whatever Ilvara's influence had made of him, took it. Ran straight at the warded bridge like he'd forgotten what a trap was. Maybe he had. Maybe the spores had taken that part of him too. I'd been watching the threshold, watching his feet, and when he crossed it I felt the air change — a half-second of pressure, the kind you get before a rockfall — and then the world went white and loud.

Force and energy, erupting outward. One spore servant just gone. Jorlan and two others caught in the sphere, and Jorlan — Jorlan didn't pull clear. Twenty-one damage. The bridge was still smoking when Grygum's voice cut through: "Kaboom! I didn't expect it to work."

I found that funnier than I should have. That's the thing about Grygum — the honesty in it. Not *I knew this would work*, not *as I planned*. Just: kaboom. I didn't expect it. Valid. Neither did I, fully. You plan a thing, you set a thing, and then reality has to cooperate, and reality underground has its own opinions.

But it worked. That's what mattered.

Jorlan was staggered. The bridge was hot. I moved.

Dawnbringer found the mark on the first swing — I felt it go clean through, the blade doing what it knows how to do, finding the opening in a man who's already been rocked and hasn't quite found his feet again. Fourteen damage. The second swing went wide, momentum carrying past him, but I wasn't thinking about the miss. I was already pressing in, already reading where his weight was, where his next move had to come from.

The Sap is something you learn when you've watched giants hit things wrong. A giant connects with everything — they're enormous, they don't need to be precise, they can afford bad form. But if you're Thorin, and you're not enormous, and you're standing across from someone who is still, even staggered, capable of putting you down — you don't just hurt him. You make sure what comes back at you is worse than it should have been. Disadvantage on his next attack. Not just damage. Disruption. Make him swing at a thing he can't quite see straight.

That's the calculation. Hurt him and blunt him. Both.

Then one of the surviving servants swung at me on Ilvara's action, because of course it did. First blow went wide — a wild, spinning miss, completely harmless. Second one connected. Five damage. Small, but noted. These things used to be people. They had names, probably. They were standing guard in this place when they had choices, before the spores got into them and Ilvara pointed them at us.

I don't forget that. It doesn't slow me down, but I don't forget it.
