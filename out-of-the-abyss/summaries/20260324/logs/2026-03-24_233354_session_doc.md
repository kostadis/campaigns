## Section — Daz — The Deception Completed

The bat was already back on my wrist before Asha had finished watching the light die. That part had gone well — the Dancing Lights threading down like a column of divine attention, absorbed into the familiar, absorbed into me, blinking out. Clean. Considered. I'd been genuinely pleased with myself.

Then she spoke.

"You're a wizard. A wizard… a wizard who prays to Lolth?"

Not wonder. Appraisal. The flat, practiced appraisal of someone who has lived her entire life in a society that treats information as currency and lying as a primary language, and who has therefore developed, out of sheer professional necessity, an excellent ear for both. I filed the tone away. She wasn't convinced. She wasn't unconvinced. She was running the numbers.

So I leaned in.

"I am… you are not speaking to the wizard of this body. I… he… this body has communed with Lolth. And I… I am speaking through this body."

I shifted registers. I let the sentence stumble, then recover. I was trying to sell her something a drow cleric would recognize — the specific texture of divine intrusion, the grammar of a body that is briefly not its own. I've read enough. I've observed enough. Being a drow, you pick some stuff up.

It didn't work.

"Aha," she said. "You are trying to test me, to see if I was one of those fake followers of Lolth, who are willing to believe every charlatan magic user that is somehow being possessed by the power of Lolth."

She hadn't disbelieved me because the illusion was poor. She'd disbelieved me because she had seen this exact con before — probably multiple times, probably from people more practiced than I am, probably in considerably higher-stakes circumstances. I felt something I will not call embarrassment. I will call it a recalibration event.

The divine possession angle was closed. I pivoted.

"There are many imitations," I said, "but as you saw, I came to you in my smaller form, and led you here. You should know the truth."

I was threading a needle — the familiar's earlier appearance as the spider, reframed. Not fraud, but mystery. Not possession, but presence. If she couldn't accept what I was claiming, perhaps she could accept that something strange was happening, and that strange things sometimes arrive in wizard-shaped containers.

She was quiet for a moment. And then she began building her own frame.

"Oh, dear goodness, have you two been infected by the spores, possessed by the crazed magic of Zuggtmoy? Convinced of the bride's power? Is this another trick of Elvara's?"

I want to be precise about what happened in that moment: she had discarded my lie and replaced it with a different explanation that she found more coherent. The divine claim was dead. But Asha Vandree was not walking away. She was doing what intelligent people do when confronted with something that doesn't fit — she was finding a frame that let her keep the useful parts and discard the implausible ones.

A deluded magic user was more believable to her than a divinely-touched wizard. Fine.

I could work with deluded.

"Perhaps my prayers have been answered through this… confused vessel," she said — almost to herself, the way people do when they're granting themselves permission to proceed.

She'd seen through me and chosen to keep me anyway. In drow terms, that is more or less a letter of reference.

"I really do believe that I have," I said. Leaning into it now. "Perhaps there are some spores within me, but I… it feels… I feel it. And by the way, I've convinced others to join me."

I brought the party forward. Madness with followers is not simply pathetic — it is tactically interesting. It suggests either genuine power or a sufficiently compelling delusion, and from Asha's position, the practical difference between those two things was probably small.

It was at this point that Glabbagool attempted to pass for a rock.

The eyes gave him away. They drifted left. They drifted right. They did this with the unhurried curiosity of a creature that has never needed to pretend to be geological before and doesn't fully understand why it's being asked to start now. I looked at him. He looked at me. Or at least, several of his eyes moved in my general direction.

"Glabbagool," I said, with what I hope was a tone of patient authority rather than suppressed desperation, "I'm taking care of this. It's fine."

No deception manual I have ever studied contains a chapter on managing an ooze during active negotiations. I am adding one mentally, in real time.

Asha, to her credit, did not run. She assessed. She accepted Glabbagool as a bound familiar — the grey-ooze creature, strange and drifting and thoroughly uncategorizable, fitting quite neatly into the profile of a deluded mage who nonetheless had genuine and unusual power. It may have helped more than the Dancing Lights. I will not dwell on this.

Then she began to talk about Ilvara, and the information was worth everything the con had cost.

The hatred in it was real. Not political calculation. Not tactical positioning. Something that had been building for a long time in close quarters with a corrupted superior, and was now coming out in full.

"Ilvara, who was once a high priestess of Lolth, has become seduced by this abomination, this idea that Lolth wishes to be married. There is something foul and stinking about her, and then all of those allies she seems to covet have turned into fungal creatures that are mindless. We need to kill her. We need to butcher her! We need to send her to an unearthly grave! I fear that her disease will spread."

I listened. Only she and Jorlan remained uninfected — Asha through devotion, Jorlan through hatred, which is its own kind of immunological theory. Twenty feet of maintained distance. Separate food. The mushroom artifact from Neverlight Grove was the mechanism of corruption. The Big Heart Fungus was the primary weapon, would need sustained damage, would not die quickly or easily.

Then, because I am who I am:

"How do you propose to kill her if you're not gonna to get within 20 feet of her?"

A fair question. Asha had a ready answer.

"I was going to kill her. That is what you… where you come in."

The plan, such as it was, had always required someone else to do the lethal part. She had survived long enough to find useful lunatics willing to close that distance for her. I could not fault the logic.

"If I and the crew that is with me were able to take care of this small problem for you," I said, "what would be in it for us?"

"The gratitude of Lolth."

"I already feel the gratitude of Lolth. I have that."

I delivered it without a beat's pause. The dryness of it, I think, may have done more for the deluded-believer angle than anything else I'd attempted — it had the specific texture of a man who genuinely believes his spiritual compensation is already sufficient and is asking a practical follow-up question. Which, I realized afterward, was the most accurate version of the character I had played all evening.

She pushed back.

"That is not the kind of conviction a follower of Lolth shows."

"It's the kind of conviction this follower of Lolth shows."

I didn't flinch. Didn't qualify. Didn't offer an explanation. Steadiness, in my experience, does more work than eloquence — eloquence suggests you've been thinking about what to say, and steadiness suggests you've never needed to. The negotiation stayed alive.

She offered Ilvara's magical items as additional payment, with the caveat that they might be contaminated. I noted that caveat. I filed it.

The intelligence was solid. The alliance was provisional but real. Asha Vandree had seen through my lie, built her own explanation out of the wreckage, found it workable, and decided that a spore-maddened wizard with followers and a gelatinous familiar was, on balance, the best available instrument for killing someone she needed dead.

She wasn't wrong.
