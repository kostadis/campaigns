## Section — Grygum — The Heart Fungus Retaliates

The cloud didn't drift. That was the first thing I noticed. Normal smoke drifts. Normal gas disperses. This hung in the cavern air like it had decided to stay — twenty feet of spore-thick poison, sitting in the middle of the battlefield as if it belonged there, as if it had always belonged there, as if *we* were the interlopers in its home.

Which, to be fair, we were.

I'd been watching the Big Heart Fungus since we entered this chamber. That's not a metaphor. I took notes. It's what I do. And what I had written in my head about that particular organism was: *not scenery*. The moment it detonated that cloud across the cavern floor, every instinct I had confirmed the notation.

Zalthir took the full brunt of it. I watched the dragonborn's body register the toxin — not a dramatic collapse, nothing so clean as that — just a subtle wrongness in how he moved, a falter in the stride of someone who had been, until thirty seconds ago, pressing a meaningful advantage over Ilvara. The poisoned condition settling over a person looks different depending on who they are. On Zalthir it looked like frustration wearing a physical costume.

He disengaged and went straight up. Ten feet. Clear of the worst of the cloud, hovering in that particular way of someone who has made the tactically correct decision and is not happy about it.

"I'm out of actions and bonus actions," he said, from somewhere above the spore line, "so I guess I'll just kind of look on and be confused."

The dry resignation in his voice said everything. I didn't laugh, but I noted it. The battlefield had fragmented faster than any of us had planned for, allies pulled in three different directions by pressure we hadn't fully mapped before we walked in. That's how these things go. You plan for the drow commander. You don't always plan for the floor itself to become an enemy.

And then Ilvara called them up.

Four shapes. Rising from the cavern floor with the patient, purposeless movement of things that are no longer choosing to move — they are simply directed. Former Velkynvelve guards. I recognized the posture of soldiers whose bodies had been repurposed, the fungal colonization of Zuggtmoy's influence made visible through Ilvara's particular corruption. These had been drow with names and duties and probably opinions about their assignments. Now they were just pointed at us.

Zalthir said, from his aerial vantage point, "Those poor guards. They've been through quite a lot."

Then he reflected on Valen. Not out loud, really — it was more in the quality of the silence that followed. I felt the thought land in the room anyway. Valen, who had been one of them. Who could have been one of them, differently categorized. These bodies on the cavern floor had once been exactly what Valen was, and somewhere between then and now, they had lost the thread entirely.

Soldiers. Now weapons. The difference between the two is sometimes just time and circumstance, and I filed that thought away under *things not to dwell on mid-combat*.

Jorlan was moving, and I saw the angle.

The Glyph of Warding on the bridge was still live. We'd placed it with exactly this kind of chaos in mind, and Jorlan was close enough, distracted enough, *angry* enough that he might not be tracking the floor carefully. I calculated this in approximately one second, which is about as long as tactical decisions get in a room that is actively trying to kill you.

"Come get it, idiot," I called across the chaos.

He ran toward the trap.

The glyph detonated. One spore servant ceased to be a problem. The others staggered in a way that suggested they had not enjoyed the experience. Jorlan himself was not having his best moment.

"Kaboom," I said, because it seemed warranted. Then: "I didn't expect it to work."

That last part was honest. The tactic had been a gamble — you call a man an idiot and invite him toward an explosive ward and you are making a bet on ego overriding tactical awareness, which is sometimes a safe bet and sometimes gets you killed. This time it paid. I took note of the conditions that made it pay, in case they became relevant again.

With the servants disrupted and Jorlan compromised, I turned my attention back to the thing that had started all of this. The Big Heart Fungus. Still standing. Still anchoring the far end of the battlefield. Still the reason Zalthir was hovering above the fight instead of in it.

I committed the Dust of Suleiman and cast it directly onto the fungus. This was not a small resource. I made the decision anyway, because the creature had already demonstrated what it was willing to do to us and I had no interest in finding out what it would do next if left unaddressed.

Then Daz's shaped Fireball hit — Ilvara and the Zuggtmoy icon both, simultaneously — and the two effects overlapped in a way that exceeded what either spell would have done alone. The cavern went briefly and magnificently wrong in all the right ways.

"That worked better," I said, surveying the damage — servants staggered, Jorlan reeling, Ilvara absorbing punishment she hadn't budgeted for, fire and spore and disruption filling a space that had, ten minutes ago, been merely dangerous — "than I had any right to expect."

The fungus was still standing. The fight was far from over. But the battlefield had shifted in our favor, and I noted the specific combination of factors that had produced that outcome: bait the ego, trust the glyph, commit the resource, let Daz do what Daz does.

Bahamut may or may not have been watching. I took notes either way.
