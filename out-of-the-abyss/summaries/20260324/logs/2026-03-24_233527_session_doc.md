## Section — Grygum — The Heart Fungus Retaliates

The cloud went up fast — faster than I expected from something that had just been sitting there like furniture. One moment the Heart Fungus was a fixed point on my mental map of the cavern, an obstacle to route around. The next it was a 20-foot radius of bad decisions, hanging in the air with a kind of patient malice that made it very clear it had always been a combatant. I'd taken notes on it. I'd noted *wrong*.

Zalthir was already in the thick of it when the spores detonated. I watched the dragonborn's body stagger — not dramatically, not the way a man does when he's performing being hurt, but the smaller, uglier way a body does when the poison has already started its work and the muscles are the last to get the message that conditions have changed. He'd been pressing his advantage on Ilvara. That advantage evaporated in about three seconds. The toxin settled over him like a decision that had been made on his behalf.

He went up. Ten feet, straight vertical, disengaging from the cloud with the economy of someone who has very quickly run the numbers and arrived at an answer he doesn't like. I tracked the movement, filed it: *Zalthir: above the worst of it, out of the fight for the moment*. The battlefield was fragmenting. It does that. Plans do not survive contact with living mushrooms that have strong opinions about personal space.

"I'm out of actions and bonus actions," Zalthir said, from his new altitude. "So I guess I'll just kind of look on and be confused."

The dry resignation in his voice had a texture I recognized. It wasn't despair. It was the sound of a man who had briefly been the most dangerous thing in the room and was now watching someone else's problem become his problem at a range he couldn't address. *I've been there*, I thought. *I've been there more than once.*

Then the floor moved.

Four shapes. Former Velkynvelve guards, or what remained of them — drow who had been in this place long enough that Zuggtmoy's influence, working through Ilvara's particular brand of corruption, had finished the renovation. They moved with the blank obedience of things that no longer contain opinions. I looked at them and took a note I did not want to take: *these were soldiers, once*. They had names. They had probably had complaints about the food and strong preferences about shift rotations and small private jokes they told in the barracks.

"Those poor guards," someone said. I think it was Gabe, from somewhere in my peripheral awareness. "They've been through quite a lot."

"Yeah," the GM said. "That was Valen's fate."

The thought landed and stayed. Valen. The name connected to a face I'd filed away, a drow who had been close enough to the edge of this thing that we'd watched the edge claim him. These four were further along the same road. The weapons pointed at us had been people, and the sentence had apparently already been served by the time they were pointed.

I moved on. You have to. The ledger of what's already happened doesn't stop filling, but the fight is still in front of you.

Jorlan was close to the bridge. And the bridge had a Glyph of Warding on it — our own preparation, still live, waiting for exactly the kind of moment where someone would run across it without thinking. I looked at Jorlan. I looked at the bridge. I did the math, and the math was *say something*.

"Come get it, idiot."

I will not pretend that was strategic communication. It was the shortest possible message that conveyed the relevant information, which was: *I am over here, you should come toward me*. Jorlan, to his credit as a creature of uncomplicated aggression, did exactly what I said.

He ran toward the trap.

The glyph detonated.

One spore servant ceased to be a coherent problem. The others staggered, which is as much grace as you can reasonably ask from an explosion. Jorlan absorbed the remainder of the blast in a way that I found professionally satisfying.

"Kaboom," I said. And then, because honesty is one of my better qualities: "I didn't expect it to work."

I genuinely didn't. It had been a gamble dressed up as a plan, and the dressing had held, and the gamble had paid, and I made a note: *sometimes the obvious bait works because you are facing a creature that does not question obvious bait*. File that one under *B for Bahamut provides, probably*.

But the Heart Fungus was still standing. Still anchoring its end of the cavern. Still responsible for Zalthir's current altitude and occupation-of-watching-in-confusion. I had a resource committed to exactly this kind of situation. I cast Dust of Suleiman directly onto it and then watched Daz's shaped Fireball find Ilvara and the Zuggtmoy icon at the same moment, and the two effects met in the middle of the cavern in a way that produced results neither spell would have managed separately.

The cavern was burning. Ilvara had taken significant punishment. The servants were damaged, Jorlan was compromised, the bridge had already spent its surprise.

"That worked better," I said, watching the overlap settle into something I hadn't drawn up or predicted, "than I had any right to expect."

The fungus was still standing. The fight was far from over. I took a note.
