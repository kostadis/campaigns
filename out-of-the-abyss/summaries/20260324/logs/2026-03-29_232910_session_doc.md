## Section — Daz — The Spider's Blessing

The plan had three moving parts.

First: my familiar. A small spider, real enough, convincing enough if you don't look too hard at the absence of divine ichor. Second: Dancing Lights, shaped not into floating globes but into something grander — a silhouette, towering and feminine and terrible, the suggestion of eight eyes and infinite patience. Third: my nerve.

I've run more complicated cons with worse materials.

The spider scurried back across the fungal floor toward me on cue, and I was already weaving the lights around it — not frantically, not with the showy flick of wrists that amateur illusionists love, but with the quiet precision of someone who has thought this through to the fourth step. The luminescence built. Condensed. The shape of the Spider Queen herself, reduced and reduced and *reduced* until it shrank into that small skittering body and seemed to pass directly into mine. Then: darkness. The performance was seamless.

I know what I'm doing.

Behind me, somewhere in the fungal shadows, Glabbagool was doing what Glabbagool does. Which is to say: failing at the very simple task of being a rock.

The GM had given him clear instructions. Motionless. Silent. A feature of the cavern. And he had, apparently, decided that rocks sometimes needed to narrate their own invisibility, because he started *talking*. I heard the reminder that invisible rocks do not speak. I heard — I am fairly certain I heard — the small wet sound of him opening his eyes, which then began tracking left and right with the particular energy of someone who absolutely cannot help himself.

"Glabbagool," I said, without turning around, without breaking the posture of a man recently vacated by a goddess, "I'm taking care of this. It's fine."

It was fine. It had to be fine.

Asha Vandry was watching me with those wide, fractured eyes — the look of someone who has spent so long in paranoid devotion that suspicion has become its own kind of faith. The spectacle had landed. I could see that. What I could not yet determine was exactly *which* way it had landed.

She spoke.

"You're a wizard. A wizard… a wizard who prays to Lolth?"

Not awe. Suspicion, clean and immediate. The question of someone who has been deceived before and has filed the memory carefully. I felt, for a fraction of a second, the specific sensation of a gamble that could go either way.

I leaned in. Not physically — just into the role, into the space the performance had opened.

"I am… you are not speaking to the wizard of this body. I… he… this body has communed with Lolth. And I… I am speaking through this body."

Conviction carries what evidence cannot, if you commit to it fully enough. I committed.

Then she surprised me.

"Aha," Asha said, and her voice had the bright, brittle quality of someone who has just solved a puzzle, "you are trying to test me, to see if I was one of those fake followers of Lolth, who are willing to believe every charlatan magic user that is somehow being possessed by the power of Lolth."

I took a moment to process that.

She had not seen through the deception. She had reframed it entirely — had taken my con and reclassified it as a *divine test of her discernment*, which she believed she had now passed. The paranoia hadn't broken my gambit. It had built her a completely different story in which she was the clever one.

There are players who don't know they're at the table. There are players who think they're winning. And then there is Asha Vandry, who had somehow managed to be both at once.

I recovered. This is what you do. You don't argue with the story someone has already decided to believe — you find the thread that connects their version to yours and you pull it.

"There are many imitations," I said, and let the words settle, "but as you saw, I came to you in my smaller form, and led you here. You should know the truth."

The spider. Ground it in the spider, in the thing she had actually witnessed with her own eyes before any question of wizards or possession had entered her mind. A deft redirect — I was pleased with it — anchoring the claim in observable fact rather than asking her to simply take my word for a goddess.

And then the floor dropped out a second time.

"Oh, dear goodness," Asha breathed, and I watched her face change again — not into belief, not into suspicion, but into a third thing entirely, "have you two been infected by the spores, possessed by the crazed magic of Zuggtmoy? convinced of the bride's power? Is this another trick of Elvara's?"

She was not calling me a liar. She was asking whether I was a victim. Whether the lights and the spider and the quiet reverence were not deception or divinity but *infection* — the particular madness of a fungal god's influence playing tricks on a compromised mind.

I stood in the dark of the Underdark with a gelatinous cube behind me failing to be a rock, and I considered the specific difficulty of selling a lie to someone whose paranoia keeps generating better explanations than the truth.
