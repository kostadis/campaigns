# Chapter 53 Candlekeep's Cold Case: The Grygumite Gambit

Date: May 18th, 2026

## Summary
Brother Vareth — whose narrower specialty in half-orc converts to Bahamut had drawn him to Grygum in the first place — led Grygum through the West Inner Ward of Candlekeep to the Immortal Chambers, a striking chamber where each floor tile bore the symbol of a different deity. Along the way, an ancient turtle librarian shuffled past at a glacial pace and pressed into Grygum's hands what turned out to be one of his own dense academic research papers — a syncretic analysis of religions across the Sword Coast. Once settled in Vareth's side office, the old scholar laid out the three great schools of thought consuming the intellectual world: the Stroudite school, which held that mortal choice was sovereign above all; the Bahamutian school, which argued that divine providence arranged all things; and the Draconic school, which claimed that ancient agency set the frame within which mortals merely operated.

Grygum listened carefully and then proposed something none of the existing schools had quite assembled — a theory of fixed points: moments in time where choice became constrained by the shape of the universe itself, while the vast space between those points remained wide open and free. The effect on Vareth was disproportionate. The Question of the Age — mortal choice against divine providence — was as old as theology itself, an argument fought for centuries in footnotes and quiet polemics. Vareth had spent his career in one of those quiet corners, working on a question almost nobody else found worth asking: why would a half-orc want to be a cleric of Bahamut? Orcs and dragons were traditional enemies; they occupied similar ecological niches; and a sufficient mass of orcs, history showed, could actually bring a dragon down. The conversion ran against species, against survival, against the whole arc of recorded combat. It was the kind of subject that produced one careful paper every fifteen years and a smattering of baffled marginal notes from the peers who bothered to read it. Then, three years ago, a dwarf named Stroud — whom almost nobody outside the Coast had heard of — had stood in front of Protanther, the ancient dragon who had been studying a half-finished chess game for four hundred years, ever since Stroud's ancestor Ortho had walked away from the board mid-move. Stroud made a single move of his own. Stroud did not know how to play chess. But the move, somehow, was a move Protanther had not seen in four centuries of contemplation, and the ancient dragon honoured the play. Stroud accepted the honour, walked out of the dragon's hall, and proceeded to tell anyone within earshot, at length and with relish, that the result proved, finally and concretely, that mortal choice was sovereign over the providence of gods — and over the patience of dragons besides. "Pulling a Stroud" was now taproom slang from Waterdeep to Triboar. The Question of the Age had not changed; it had simply acquired a smirking dwarven exhibit, and Vareth's quiet corner of the discipline had been dragged into the middle of every theological dispute on the Sword Coast. Suddenly everyone wanted to talk to him; the trouble was, they wanted originality, and Vareth had been hunting for an original angle of his own and not finding one. Grygum's fixed-points theory was the angle. Building on the earlier "Stroud's Gambit" exchange that had already marked Grygum as someone Vareth wanted to think with, the old scholar electrified — labeling Grygum a "weak Stroudian and strong Bahamutian around fixed points," declaring the idea a potential fourth school of thought and a philosophical quadrangle that could reshape decades of debate. He pressed a collection of Stroudite notes and papers into Grygum's hands, barely able to contain himself as he rushed off to begin his own research into what he was already calling the "strong-weak hypothesis" — and what the wider world would soon know as the Grygumite School. He was no longer the obscure scholar in a forgotten corner of the debate. He had a new angle on the question at the centre of the age, and the question now had his name on the back of it.

That evening, the party gathered for dinner at the Refectory, where the eight Great Readers sat at the high table. The atmosphere was uneasy — one reader was visibly drunk, another looked pale and unwell, and two were locked in hushed, intense conversation in a corner. As the meal wound down, a hooded member of the Avowed hurried past with something tucked beneath their robes, and from the high gallery above, the recited prophecies of Alaundo drifted down through the candlelit hall. One line caught the air like a cold draft: "On the longest night of winter's deepest cold, the keeper falls, the heart is sold."

The party retired to the House of Rest, but their sleep was cut short before dawn by frantic banging at the door. A breathless, boyish acolyte named Fembris stood outside, wide-eyed and urgent, shouting that the Keeper of Tomes was dead and that the First Reader wanted them through the Emerald Door before the rest of the Avowed awoke. Glabbagool, the party's gelatinous companion, tucked himself into the bag of holding with eyeballs peering out as the group set off into the snow-blanketed dark of Candlekeep. Fembris led them through the grounds, pointing out the Astronomicon's curious blue stack of globes, the gleaming Whispering Dome of the Hall of Momentous Deeds, and the Drakonoikos — a building constructed from the scales and bones of an ancient red dragon, home to lore on fire-breathing creatures and a dragon breath simulation chamber that had already claimed one life among the Avowed.

They arrived at the Chapter House inside the Exaltation bastion to find the Council of Twelve in full uproar. Council members shouted over one another — one demanding an immediate vote to restore leadership, another insisting they first determine whether the Keeper had named a successor, and a third crying that the murderer had to be found before anything else. Grygum, seizing the moment, introduced himself as the founder of the Grygumite School, briefly derailing the entire crisis into an academic tangent before the First Reader, a formidable green dragonborn known as Bookwyrm, restored order with a sharp tap of their fingers on the table. A sour-faced council member named Daral dismissed the party as mere sell-swords, but the party defended their credentials by recounting their survival in the Underdark and their discovery of the ancient sentient blade Dawnbringer, which silenced most of the skeptics.

Bookwyrm appointed the party as independent investigators, citing the need for impartiality given the suspicious relationships among the council members themselves. When Grygum immediately asked about remuneration, the council was struck into stunned silence — it had not occurred to them. After some negotiation, Bookwyrm agreed that upon a successful resolution, each member of the party would receive a copy of any book in the library, made at the House of the Binder. The Gatewarden, Kalan Strongbranch — a man with a neatly trimmed gray-white beard and piercing blue eyes — was then publicly stripped of the investigation in front of the full council, owing to his troubled relationship with the victim. Janussi, the Keeper of Tomes, had asked for Kalan's resignation just the day before the murder, citing concerns about his mental faculties. Kalan bowed curtly and stormed off, though the watcher guarding the crime scene would later note that he had looked strangely more determined after his own private inspection of the chamber.

Bookwyrm escorted the party up a spiraling staircase through the tower, passing a shrine of Oghma and a large apartment before arriving at the Keeper's door. On the way, a gray cat sat motionless in the middle of the stairs, yellow eyes open and daring anyone to disturb her. Even Bookwyrm stepped carefully around the animal, and when pressed, admitted that no one had ever been able to prove whether the cat was simply a cat or something far more significant — and so it was left to sit wherever it wished. Outside the chamber stood Tadric, a 23-year-old watcher with a bowl-cut haircut, who provided the party with a careful timeline: the body had been discovered at 7:30 in the morning by Great Reader A'lai Aivenmore and a servant named Miss Hollypocket; the Gatewarden had arrived with Tadric around 8 a.m.; a distraught woman named Kazryn Nyantani had appeared weeping shortly after; a scholar named Fheminor Scrivenbark had arrived at 8:30 wanting to inspect the room but was turned away; Bookwyrm had arrived from the baths at 9; and the Gatewarden had returned at 11 to conduct his own inspection before leaving to report to the council.

The party entered the Keeper's chambers to find a spacious circular room hazy with the lingering smell of tobacco, its dozen lancet windows filling the space with pale morning light. Janussi — a human male of around seventy years — lay near the foot of an elegant mahogany desk, his white robes soaked in blood, his rib cage caved in, and a gaping hollow where his heart had been. The room bore signs of a struggle: a chair was toppled near the desk, a marble statue and its pedestal lay shattered on the floor, and the candles of the candelabra had burned entirely to their stubs — something Tadric confirmed Miss Hollypocket would never have permitted, suggesting the murder had occurred many hours before the body was found. Near a safe on the far side of the room, blue gemstones and empty pouches were scattered across the stone floor, as though the thief had found them worthless compared to whatever they had actually come for. Fembris took one look at the body, sprinted to the nearest window, flung it open, and vomited outside the tower.

The party examined the desk carefully, finding a spellbook called The Discoveries lying open at its center, with notes written in Celestial on a sheaf of papyrus beside it — musings on unfamiliar spells within the book. A quill lay on the floor nearby, its nib wet with midnight blue ink, and a damp linen ink carrier sat in a wicker basket close by, the vessel for the spilled pot. Atop a large pile of notes at the back of the desk, more annotations in the same midnight blue ink had been made on a book called The Golden Ass, while beneath those lay older notes on The Discoveries written in a now-dry purple ink — suggesting Janussi had switched inks recently, perhaps within the last day. The blood around the wound was surprisingly localized, far less than one would expect from such a violent injury, raising the grim possibility that Janussi had already been dead before his chest was opened. Grygum immediately understood the theological implication: the heart had not been taken as a trophy — it had been taken to ensure that no power in the world could ever bring Janussi back to speak of what he knew. The party began to wonder whether someone had already attempted to commune with the dead before their arrival, and whether the Gatewarden's determined expression as he left the scene meant he had found something — or done something — that the party had yet to uncover.

## Locations
### Immortal Chambers

It houses the office of Brother Vareth, where he keeps his collection of polemics and research on the Question of the Age.

### The Refectory
The dining hall of Candlekeep where the Great Readers and the Avowed gather for meals. The party observed the council members here and heard the recited prophecies of Alaundo floating down from the high gallery.

### House of Rest
The guest quarters within Candlekeep where the party stayed during their visit.

### Astronomicon
A curious blue stack of globes within Candlekeep containing all the library's knowledge of the stars.

### Whispering Dome
Part of the Hall of Momentous Deeds within Candlekeep, where the words of past heroes can be heard.

### Drakonoikos
A building within Candlekeep constructed from the scales and bones of an ancient red dragon (Fyrentennimar), housing lore on dragons and other fire-breathing creatures. It contains a dragon breath simulation chamber that has proven fatal to at least one member of the Avowed.

The executive meeting room of the Exaltation bastion, featuring a dodecagonal table for the Council of Twelve and a distinctive rose window that lets in natural light. The party was officially appointed as investigators here.

A facility within Candlekeep where books are copied and bound, referenced as the source of the party's promised reward.

A spacious circular chamber within a tower of Candlekeep, featuring a dozen lancet windows, a mahogany desk, and a wooden staircase leading to a second floor. The site of the murder, heavy with the smell of tobacco and the aftermath of a violent struggle. Located up a spiraling staircase that passes a shrine of Oghma.

## NPCs
### Brother Vareth
An elderly scholar who has kept a corner of the low library at Candlekeep for 41 years. His narrow specialty is the puzzle of why a half-orc would convert to Bahamut, given that orcs and dragons are traditional enemies who occupy similar ecological niches and that orcs, in sufficient numbers, can mathematically kill a dragon. Stroud's chess victory three years ago dragged this niche question into the centre of the Question of the Age. He is thrilled by Grygum's new 'strong-weak hypothesis,' seeing it as a potential fourth school of thought that could define his career.

### Turtle Librarian
An ancient, incredibly slow-moving librarian at Candlekeep who distributes academic tracts and research papers to those passing through the library. He handed Grygum one of his own research papers — a syncretic analysis of different religions across the Sword Coast.
