The West Inner Ward smells like old paper and a kind of ceremonial solemnity that I have mostly associated with spaces where people have decided collectively to take themselves very seriously. Brother Vareth moves through it with the practiced confidence of a man who has been navigating institutional gravity for forty-one years and knows which way to lean into it.

I pay attention to his feet.

The Immortal Chambers have a tile floor with one deity symbol per tile, and the problem is immediately apparent. You have to choose which deity you want to step on. I try to watch where Vareth steps and follow in the same path, which is the correct policy, and also exactly the kind of procedural compromise that a strong Stroudite would say proves the whole Bahamutian theory.

I file that thought for later.

We are briefly interrupted by Pizwog, who is an ancient tortle and moves at the velocity of a very confident glacier. He distributes what appears to be a religious tract, which turns out to be one of his own research papers — a syncretic analysis of different religions across the Sword Coast, deeply theoretical, internally inconsistent in ways I can tell are meaningful without yet being able to specify how, and written in the style of a man who has strong views about what the word *however* is for.

"Is it on the merits of doing things slowly, or…" I ask.

It is not.

"Academics," I say, and tuck it into my satchel. I tell Pizwog I will read it in due time. This is sincere. Vareth, behind me, has the expression of a man exercising tremendous institutional restraint across approximately five minutes of tortle handoff. He remains respectful throughout. I note this. It costs him something.

---

His side office is the corner of a man who has been winning his argument with the furniture for four decades. He has been keeping this corner of the library for forty-one years, he says, and he knows what a fashion looks like.

This is not a fashion.

He has prepared this speech. I can tell because he launches it without looking at his notes.

Libraries are arguing. Taverns are arguing. Chapter houses are arguing. The courts of two cities have asked for written opinions. Three duels have been fought at Candlekeep over which polemic shelf gets the larger window. *Three*, in a library where nobody has dueled in two hundred years. Sister Yvenne threatens to confiscate everyone's bookplates. She has not yet, but he expects it.

"Yes," I say, settling into the chair. "The question of our age, of course."

He tells me what it actually is, underneath the chess game and the dwarf and the partisan colors. What is being asked, he says, is this: what kind of universe do we live in?

He lays out three schools.

The Stroudite school says mortal choice is sovereign. The gods exist. The dragons exist. Providence may or may not exist. But the choosing belongs to the chooser, and is not borrowed, and is not loaned from any throne above or below. Tiamat was denied her power because a dwarf chose. The chain of choices that followed was a chain of free acts. Strike out anyone, and the chain falls.

"Yes," I say. "The choice belongs to the chooser. This is a principle I can get behind."

He nods, with the patience of a man who expected exactly this, and continues.

The Bahamutian school says divine providence prevails. Bahamut moved his pieces beforehand. The mortals served as instruments. Choice was real, but not sovereign. Stroud chose what he was fitted to choose. The chain was an arranged chain.

Then — remembering that I travel with a dragonborn and apparently blanking on the name for a moment — he gets to the third. The Draconic school says ancient agency rules. Protanther honored Stroud's move because Protanther is honorable, not because Bahamut compelled him. The metallic dragons followed Protanther because they trust their elders. The choosing is real, but the scope of the choosing is what the dragons permit.

Three answers, he says. Three universes. They cannot all be right at the same time.

I am sitting with this — genuinely sitting with it, which I do not always do when someone presents me with a structured argument, because usually the structure is doing the work of hiding something — when I notice that none of them quite fit what I actually think. Which is inconvenient, because I had not previously been aware that I actually thought anything about this.

"But… but what if there's a fourth?"

He stops.

"What if there are certain fixed points around which our actions must revolve? And the closer we come to one of those fixed points, the more constrained we are, but between them, perhaps we have a great deal of freedom of action."

The moment of utter panic that crosses his face is brief and genuine.

"A *fourth* school of thought?"

"Hi," I say. "Have you considered the other possibilities?"

What happens next is difficult to describe with total scholarly precision. Vareth has a very specific kind of excitement that I associate with men who have been tending a fire alone in a room for years and have just heard someone knock. He dismisses the Bahamutians. He waves off the dragons. He says things about quadrangles and the center of circles described by three points, and then — this is the part that lands — he pauses and says that a god who wished to control the outcome of mortal lives would not need to compel a single hand. She would only need to shape the rules of the evidence. The form of polemic. The architecture of a chapter house.

I think about Ilvara's prayer book. The Zuggtmoy marginalia. The theological document from Asha Vandree's chain mail — the Lolth/Zuggtmoy equivalence, written in a hand that wanted very much to seem incidental. The quiet. The architecture.

"Yes," I say. "The universe can be silent and indifferent outside of the fixed framework, and then as we approach it, yes, it gets silent and it gets *conspiratorial* to compel us toward the invariance."

He is articulating the theory back to me now — someone had to arrive at a particular point, and at that point their choices were constrained, but who would arrive there was unconstrained — and I am nodding because yes, that is exactly it.

"Aye! It could have been Stroud, our great philosopher. It could have been, say, another party of adventurers."

I think about Stroud making the decisive move — the perfect throw, the unrepeatable instant — against a dragon who had spent four hundred years studying that exact position. The universe, silent and conspiratorial. Someone had to sit down across from Protanther. The chess game didn't care who.

Vareth calls it a compromise of the Bahamutian school. He calls me a weak Stroudite — a strong Stroudite holds there are no fixed points at all — and a strong Bahamutian around fixed points, and a weak Bahamutian outside of them. He names this the strong-weak hypothesis with the satisfaction of a man who has just filed something correctly.

Then he hands me a stack of Stroudite papers, barely able to contain himself, and rushes off to begin his own research.

"I'll give you a marked-up version when I'm done," I say.

He is already half out the door.

I sit in the office with his papers and Pizwog's research tract and the thought that I just proposed a school of theological thought by accident, and that an aging scholar with forty-one years in this corner of the library is now treating it as the question of the age.

"I feel like I handled that competently," I say, to no one in particular.
