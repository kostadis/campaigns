The snow was unexpected. Not alarming — just a texture I had no frame of reference for after months below stone. Candlekeep's grounds were blanketed in it, white and silent, the kind of silence that had nothing hunting inside it. Fembris picked his way up the stairs carefully, testing each step. I followed without testing. Snow was new. Stairs were not.

He swept an arm toward the courtyard with the pride of someone who had memorised the gesture along with the words.

"Behold, the great library."

I beheld. Exits: three visible archways, one service passage, one guarded door to the north. Foot traffic: light, scholars moving in pairs, nobody moving fast. No elevated positions. No obvious coverage points. The exaltation bastion rose above us and I filed it as the dominant structure, tallest fixed point, and moved on.

The Astronomicon caught my eye first — a stack of blue globes, ascending in scale, pale against the white sky. Fembris gestured at it with the enthusiasm of someone who had given this tour many times and still meant it.

"You see that curious blue stack of globes? That's the Astronomicon. Everything we know about the stars can be found there."

I noted it. Surface navigation, if we needed that. Everything the Underdark doesn't have.

The Whispering Dome sat further along, gold under the snow — striking, even by surface standards. Fembris pointed it out as part of the Hall of Momentous Deeds, explained that voices from the past lingered there, that you could hear them if you stood in the right place. He said *inspired* the way people say it when they want you to be.

Then the Drakonoikos.

I noticed it before he named it. The structure was built from scales — actual scales, enormous, overlapping — and bones worked into the walls where another building would have used stone. Fyrentennimar, Fembris finally produced the name after a pause and a visible effort, dragon of some age and significance, reduced now to architecture. In the early light the scales caught the sun and held it.

"Those scales really gleam in the sunrise," Grygum said. "That's amazing."

He wasn't wrong. I had been thinking the same thing and had not said it. There was a monastic logic to the building: the thing that was dangerous becomes the thing that contains the knowledge of how dangerous it was. I wasn't sure the monks at the Garden Shadow would have approved of the metaphor, but Brother Heslin — or possibly Brother Arrath, I was never certain which of them ran the theory sessions on repurposed threat — had said something along those lines once. I had been watching the window.

Fembris warned us about the Dragon Breath Simulation Chamber inside. One of the Avowed had died in it, he said, which was why the Keeper's permission was now required. His tone suggested we should find this discouraging.

"That sounds like a great game to play," Grygum said. "Later."

"I've got another level or two on me," he added, more quietly.

I said nothing. The chamber existed, the danger was real, and one of us had already decided it was theoretical. This was the same register in which Thorin had once described demon lord proximity as *ominous sounding fun*. I filed it and kept walking.

The interior of the bastion was a different kind of assessment. High ceilings — good. Multiple corridors branching at irregular intervals — less good. Oil paintings on every staircase, stern-faced sages in various states of disapproval, busts on pedestals at each landing. The tapestries were heavy and could conceal anything or nothing. I checked as we passed.

Fembris climbed with increasing effort. The rest of us were not experiencing any comparable difficulty. The Underdark does not offer gentle grades; what it offers is drop-offs, uneven rubble, tunnels that require you to crawl for twenty minutes before opening into a space large enough to stand, ledges over nothing where the fall is long and the landing is not the concern because something will have found you by the time you get there. A few staircases in a library was not a test of anything.

We arrived at the Chapter House. A purple-robed watcher stood at the entrance, assessed us briefly, and stepped aside. No challenge. They had been told to expect us.

Fembris's legs were done. Mine were not, and I was aware of the gap — not with pride, just as a data point. The surface had weather and stairs and buildings made of dead dragons. The Underdark had other things. After months of training under conditions the Garden Shadow had never specified in their curriculum, a few flights was nothing.

I noted the Chapter House entrance: single guard, single visible point of entry, no secondary exit apparent from the doorway. Someone had positioned us here deliberately.

I stepped inside.
