The tiles were the first problem.

The Immortal Chambers had one deity symbol per tile — the whole floor of it, from wall to wall, green-roofed and hushed — and the moment I saw it I understood the issue. Every step was a theological statement. You weren't walking across a room; you were publishing a position. I watched Vareth's feet and tried to place my own in exactly the same squares he used. He had forty-one years of navigating this floor and presumably hadn't been smited yet. I took notes on his route.

Pizwog found us before we made it through.

Ancient is a word that loses meaning in a library — everything old enough gets called ancient — but Pizwog the tortle was ancient in the original sense, the kind of ancient that makes the word feel specific again. He shuffled toward us at a speed I can only describe as geological, and distributed what appeared to be a religious tract. I took it. Vareth accepted his copy with an expression of careful, labored respect — the face of a man who has been receiving Pizwog's papers for forty-one years and has developed a policy about it.

"Is it on the, like, the merits of doing things slowly, or…"

It was not. It was a syncretic analysis of different religions across the Sword Coast: dense, theoretical, lacking in structural coherence in the way that only a very confident academic's work can be. The kind of paper where you can tell a tremendous amount of thinking went into it and the thinking is mostly happening in languages inaccessible to the reader.

"Academics," I said.

I tucked it into my satchel and told him I would read it in due time. This is what you say to Pizwog, I assumed. You say it with warmth. You file the paper. You continue.

Vareth's side office had the quality of a room that had not changed in forty-one years because there had been no reason for it to change. Papers exactly where they should be. A window positioned for reading. The posture of a man who had been rehearsing this conversation in his head and was pleased to finally be having it.

"Brother Grygum," he said — you could feel the prepared nature of what was coming — "we are now confronted with the problem of our age."

"Yes," I said. "The question of our age. Of course."

I meant it dryly. I didn't know yet how much I meant it straight.

What Vareth described was a universe in argument. Not a philosophical debate, not an academic fashion — an actual argument, spreading outward from the story of Stroud and the chess game into the libraries and the taverns and the chapter houses and the courts of two cities. Three duels at Candlekeep over which polemic shelf gets the larger window. *Three.* In a library where nobody had dueled in two hundred years. Sister Yvenne was threatening confiscation of bookplates. She had not yet. He expected it.

I thought about Stroud and the chess game, which I think about sometimes when something feels too large and too old to move. Four hundred years of preparation, and Protanther still had a blind spot. Still.

Vareth laid out the three schools.

The Stroudite school: mortal choice is sovereign. Tiamat was denied her power because a dwarf chose. The chain of choices that followed was a chain of free acts. Strike out any link, and it falls.

"Yes," I said. "The choice belongs to the chooser. This is — this is the principle I can get behind."

I do believe this. I have believed it since before the Underdark, and the Underdark did nothing to change it. What happened in Gracklstugh happened because of what we chose. What happened at the bridge happened because of what I chose, with full knowledge of what I was inscribing and who it would kill. The chain of choices belongs to the choosers.

The Bahamutian school: divine providence prevails. Stroud chose what he was fitted to choose. The chain was an arranged chain.

I am a cleric of Bahamut. I take notes and file them as if Bahamut is reading the same set. I have been doing this for some time. I try to be honest with myself about whether this is faith or whether it is a method for living, and the answer is that the distinction has mostly stopped seeming important.

The Draconic school — and here Vareth paused to remember Zalthir's name, which he had momentarily lost — said quietly that ancient agency rules. Protanther honored Stroud's move because Protanther is honorable. Not because Bahamut compelled him. The metallic dragons followed because they trust their elders. The choosing is real; the scope of the choosing is what the dragons permit.

Three schools. Three universes. They cannot all be right at the same time.

Vareth said this, and I thought: well. Yes. But.

"But — but what if there's a fourth?"

He looked at me.

"What if there are certain fixed points around which our actions must revolve? And the closer we come to one of those fixed points, the more constrained we are — but between them, perhaps we have a great deal of freedom of action."

The expression on his face was something I want to record accurately. It was panic followed very quickly by something else. The face of a man who has been orbiting a question for forty-one years and has just had someone hand him a fourth point.

"A fourth school of thought?"

"Hi," I said. "Have you considered the other possibilities?"

What followed was Vareth being ecstatic. I want to be precise about this: not pleased, not interested — *ecstatic*, in the way that only a scholar of forty-one years can be ecstatic, which is very rapidly and in complete sentences. A quadrangle, not a triangle. Or perhaps the center of a circle described by the three existing points. And then, quieter, something that sounded less like philosophy and more like a warning: a god who wished to control the outcome of mortal lives would not need to compel a single hand. She would only need to shape the rules of the evidence. The form of polemic. The architecture of a chapter house.

"Yes," I said. "The universe can be silent and indifferent outside of the fixed framework — and then as we approach it, yes, it gets silent and it gets conspiratorial. To compel us toward the invariance."

Vareth worked it back: if someone were to arrive at a particular point, their choices were constrained at that point in time. But who would arrive there was unconstrained.

"Aye," I said. "It could have been Stroud, our great philosopher. It could have been, say, another party of adventurers."

I was thinking of us, in the Underdark, navigating tunnels that put enemies in front of you that were behind you an hour ago. The fixed points we stumbled into anyway. Whether or not Bahamut was reading the notes.

Vareth classified me. Weak Stroudite — because a strong Stroudite holds that there are no fixed points at all — and a strong Bahamutian around fixed points, and a weak Bahamutian outside of them. He named it the strong-weak hypothesis. He said this was a compromise closer to the Bahamutian school than the Stroudite school, and then, before I could respond, he was already pulling papers together to go do research, handing me the Stroudite collection with the confident air of a man who expected me to find it all nonsense.

"I'll give you a marked-up version," I said, "when I'm done."

He went. I sat with the papers in my hands, in an office that had not changed in forty-one years.

"I feel like I handled that competently," I said to nobody in particular.

"Yes," I thought. "The Grygum school of thought."

I took notes. It's what I do.
