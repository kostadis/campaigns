The road to Candlekeep had given me a lot of time to think. Maybe too much.

I'd spent most of it turning the same questions over in my head like stones I kept expecting to find something under. What does Bahamut actually want from me? Not the general want — the "be just, protect the innocent, oppose Tiamat" want that I could recite in my sleep — but the specific want. The personal assignment. Because somewhere in the theological wreckage of the last few months, between the demon lords and the dead gods and Jimjar-who-was-apparently-a-celestial-the-whole-time, I had accumulated a very significant stack of things I did not understand, and I was hoping Candlekeep might help me start working through it.

The place was exactly what you'd expect from the world's most famous library: enormous, self-important, and surrounded by the kind of architecture that said *we have been here longer than your entire civilization and we have the receipts.* I felt like I should have brought a better book.

As it turned out, we'd brought five.

Daz handled the initial approach — he usually does, being constitutionally suited to the kind of careful, measured diplomacy required when dealing with institutions that have rules posted in multiple languages. The gatekeeper explained the terms with the particular energy of someone who has explained the same terms several thousand times and has made peace with that.

"The rules say that you must provide one book per group. But if you have come to ask more than one question and wish to intersect with many of the avowed — who are sort of the monks of Candlekeep, the sages, the professors — providing more books will get you more FaceTime."

I did a quick count. One question per book. Five books. Four of us, plus the Glabbagool consideration, plus the accumulated mysteries of the last several months.

"Okay, well, we have a few books," I said, trying to organize the triage out loud. "And really the question we want to get answered is, who's — who's following Daz, right?"

The gatekeeper, helpful soul, ran through the rest of the list. Zalthir wanted to know about Jimjar. Thorin wanted help with Dawnbringer. Which prompted Thorin himself to note, with the absolute conviction of someone who has thought about this:

"Okay, but I mean, like, I'm looking for a therapist. This is not a one-question type of thing. This is — there are no shortcuts when it comes to therapy."

"It's a process," I said, "not a product."

He nodded gravely. We understood each other.

Then he proposed giving Glabbagool his own question, which I thought was extremely fair. Glabbagool, for his part, was reportedly very confused about the concept of a library with effectively infinite capacity, and didn't understand why we weren't donating every book we'd ever encountered so we could ask questions until the end of time. This is what I mean when I say Glabbagool's perspective on the world is bracing. He's not wrong. He's just operating on a scale we haven't caught up to yet.

We gave all five books.

The First Reader received them with the barely-contained delight of someone who has been waiting for exactly this delivery. She lingered on the Velkynvelve Prisoner Journal with particular reverence. "This journal of the prisoners is a masterpiece of interesting value. I can't wait to study it carefully. It will go great with my other editions in drow prison journals."

I couldn't help myself. "Oh, there's a whole shelf of those, isn't there?"

There apparently was.

Thorin, ever practical, pointed out that we were the primary sources — she could just interview us and save herself the reading. The First Reader seemed genuinely charmed by this. And then Thorin mentioned, with the casual energy of someone producing a rabbit from a hat, that we also had a drow. Specifically, we had a drow who was currently very small, very angry, and in our possession.

The spy's voice came out approximately at the volume of an extremely annoyed insect. "This is highly irregular, and House T'sarran will have its vengeance!"

Daz didn't even look up. "Tiny, tiny little vengeance."

Thorin said: "Right."

I assigned Glabbagool to keep her under control, which felt like the appropriate use of available resources. "We'll have a goal," I said. "Keep her under control."

What followed was one of those moments that reminds you Glabbagool contains multitudes. He told the spy he could poke her eyes out, then immediately clarified — with the earnestness of someone who genuinely wants to be understood — that the googly eyes were just affectations. "I can actually see different ways. This is how ooze work. But I discovered that if you have these cute eyes, people take you more friendly." And then, to demonstrate his actual authority, he started producing eyes. Many eyes. Proliferating eyes.

It got very creepy very quickly.

The spy regrouped and demanded to know about the prayer beads. Which: bold choice, given the circumstances.

Thorin's response was immediate and merciless. "You don't get a question. You did not donate a book."

I mentally filed that under "correct."

The First Reader assigned scholars. Zalthir got the Jimjar thread — which apparently dovetailed neatly with an avowed named Khell-Vire who wanted to learn about something specific. The First Reader clarified: not about the party generally, but about *me* in particular.

Daz observed that there were apparently two famous people in the party.

I tried to look modest about this and mostly failed.

Thorin and Dawnbringer got directed to someone named Philemon — described as "an avowed monk specializing in psychological preservation and historical cataloging of ancient sentient artifacts, with a tremendous track record of success in working through the interpersonal conflict issues of the sentient artifact." Thorin received this information like a man who has been hoping someone would say exactly these words.

"I'm all up for couples counseling with Dawnbringer."

Good. They needed it. I said nothing.

What I did say, looking at the assembled scholars being pointed at us like a hospitality service we hadn't paid for: "I almost feel like they should be giving us books."

And then the room changed.

A half-elf — elderly, frantic, moving at a speed that his body seemed to object to — came through the door at something between a brisk walk and a controlled fall, directed his outrage squarely at the First Reader, and said: "I cannot believe you did not tell me about the arrival of a cleric of Bahamut."

I looked at him. He looked at me. His eyes had the expression of a man who has found a very rare specimen in the wild.

"I'm feeling," I said, to no one in particular, "in demand here."

His name was Vareth. He had, he explained breathlessly, a decades-long specialty in half-orc Bahamut converts. Which had, until recently, been a quiet, small area of study. A niche. Something he'd pursued with quiet dedication while the rest of the academic world found more fashionable problems.

And then Stroud beat Protanther at chess.

I know this story. I know it the way you know a story that someone told you when you were young and uncertain, when you needed proof that the impossible was occasionally available as an option. A dwarf who couldn't actually play chess, sitting across from an ancient gold dragon who had been analyzing the same position for four hundred years. Protanther had mapped every possible move. He was certain. And then Stroud rolled — and it landed perfectly. Definitively. The kind of thing that doesn't just work, it *announces* itself. Protanther was so stunned he joined the alliance. The world shifted because someone who didn't know what they were doing did the exact right thing at the exact right moment.

I have always found this deeply comforting. Four hundred years of preparation still has a blind spot.

Vareth was still talking. Stroud's victory had turned his quiet scholarly backwater into a flood. The controversy had erupted throughout the realms — what did the sudden half-orc interest in Bahamut mean for the orc pantheon? Was it part of Bahamut's plan? And now people wanted to talk to Vareth. They were inviting him to conferences. Funding was arriving. A living half-orc cleric of Bahamut had walked through his door.

He grabbed my hand and practically dragged me.

I let him. I was curious, and also I had questions of my own.

First, though, I wanted to take the measure of him. "You have to understand," I said, with the measured cadence I use when I'm testing someone, "that it is traditional for clerics of Bahamut to want to try to take over the world. But I haven't moved into that phase yet."

Vareth barely paused. "This is all fascinating! I have so many theories I wish to test out." He looked up. "And I do have something I can share with you?"

All right. We were going to get along.

He circled back to Stroud, to the chess game, to the conversion phenomenon that had restructured his entire career. He was trying to find the shape of it, the causality. I felt it coming together and finished the thought for him:

"It truly was legendary."

And then, because I couldn't help it, and because the phrase had been sitting in the back of my mind since we started this journey and I'd never had the right audience for it:

"Stroud's Gambit."

Vareth stopped. His expression went through several stages very quickly — surprise, recognition, something that looked almost like delight at receiving a gift he hadn't known he wanted.

"*Oh.* Oh! Well, that's not how — this is all novel news! I can't wait to take advantage of it! The field of Bahamut half-orc conversions will benefit tremendously from your interview."

"Oh," I said, as warmly as I could manage, "it's all part of the plan."

"This is all fascinating!"

Somewhere in the background, the rest of the party had begun debating Daz's habit of citing Malfire at every available opportunity. I heard Grygum's name pulled into the discussion — *Grygum*, not me, which is how I know the sentence was reconstructed after the fact — but the content was accurate. We had been through enough together that the citations had become a kind of ambient weather condition, something you adapted to rather than fought.

"Yeah," I said, when someone turned to me for confirmation, "it's like we're kind of sick of each other, but somehow still together, yeah."

And then, because the opportunity was simply too good: "As Malfire said in Volume 3."

Nobody laughed. This was the correct response.

Vareth was still watching me with the expression of someone who has found the answer to a question they've been asking for decades and is now immediately generating seventeen follow-up questions. His research proposals were finally getting funded. He had a living data point. He had "Stroud's Gambit" — a term he was clearly already planning to deploy in the first conference he attended.

I let him hold onto that for a moment.

Somewhere in the library, there were answers waiting. About Bahamut's intentions, about what my conversion meant in the broader theological landscape, about why Vareth's previously obscure field had become a flood. I didn't know what I was going to find. I had notes — the prayer book, the theological documents, the accumulated evidence of months in the Underdark — and I had questions, and I had the particular comfort of knowing that sometimes the person who doesn't know what they're doing walks into the right room and everything clicks.

I took notes. It's what I do.
