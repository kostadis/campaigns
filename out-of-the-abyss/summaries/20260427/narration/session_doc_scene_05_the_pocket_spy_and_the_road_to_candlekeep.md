The mushroom went down her throat fast. One moment she was a full-sized spy — annoyed, calculating, already looking for angles — and the next she was *very squeaky and indignant*, which is a phrase that does not lose anything in translation. She shrank to about the size of a child's toy, her voice pitched up into something that was still clearly fury but now delivered from below knee-height. Thorin watched this with the expression of a man receiving exactly what he ordered.

"Nickname her Polly Pocket," he said. Then, with the flat authority of someone who knows what he's doing: "It's very demeaning."

It was. I filed that away.

The practical questions followed in order, which is how Thorin approaches everything — you solve the immediate problem, then you solve the next one. Do we have a mushroom to grow her back? Apparently yes. Can she slip through a net? She can now. Daz, with the particular tone he uses when the obvious solution is also the inelegant one, offered: "We can put her in the bag of holding with Glabbagool, and he can just hold onto her."

There was a brief detour into the geography of a bag of holding's interior, which I did not contribute to, and then the ventilation question arose. Poke holes. Let her breathe. *Like a hamster*, Thorin said, and added that he hoped the ridicule was getting to her.

It was. She was starting to wonder whether there were other fates.

I understood that. Weeks in the Underdark with us will do something to a person's sense of available options. She was small enough now that Glabbagool could watch her with a single eye without breaking his stride. That was what I noticed — not the spy's indignation, not Thorin's satisfaction, but Glabbagool, walking with one eye locked permanently on the prisoner. He had been assigned a job. He was doing the job. The care in it — the steadiness of that unblinking attention — was the most earnest thing I'd seen in weeks.

He'd been in the dark of that bag for days. No sun, no space, no way to say whether any of it was strange or expected because he had no reference for what was normal above ground. I'd told him I'd protect him from corrupting forces. I hadn't thought about what it would be like to be carried in a bag with a furious, newly miniaturized assassin as your only company. I should have thought about that.

Thorin, not without a certain gentleness, offered: "At least the bag of holding is shade, you know, so she's not gonna be exposed to full sun."

"There you go," I said.

"This is mercy," Thorin confirmed.

It was the most honest description of our mercy I could have asked for. She was small, surveilled by an ooze, breathing through holes we'd poked in the bag, and being shielded from sunlight as a kindness. The drow woman inside had probably survived worse. But probably had never survived anything quite like this.

On the road, Thorin made sure she overheard him: "Those prayer beads are at full power, even though they're shrunken, right?"

Grygum picked it up without missing a beat: "Yes, yes, in fact — I believe they may be more deadly."

"They're more potent. Okay. Good to know."

I watched him say it with the mild interest I reserve for things that are technically his problem and not mine. The prisoner was in the bag. The bag was moving. The misinformation was circulating. All of it was fine.

---

Candlekeep required a book. This was not a problem for us. The problem, as Daz identified it, was which book — because we had, after months in the Underdark acquiring things from people who no longer needed them, accumulated more books than most surface libraries see in a decade. The list, when assembled: Princess Ebonmire's spellbook. Ilvara's prayer book with its Zuggtmoy marginalia, those careful notes documenting the corruption timeline she'd watched unfolding and apparently found worth recording. Asha Vandree's theological argument about Lolth and Zuggtmoy as equivalents — the document I'd watched her press into Grygum's hands. The Velkynvelve prisoner journal. Pliinki's journal from the Whirlstone Caverns, the one where a man had written carefully about helping birth a demonic monster and apparently thought this was worth documenting.

I thought about Asha Vandree, briefly. The way she had looked through me the entire time we were in that room. I had been furniture; I had noticed the quality of her erasure without taking it personally, because it wasn't personal — it was structural, the way the Underdark is cold. You don't take the cold personally. The theological document she'd written would now unlock questions in one of the greatest libraries in the world. She would have hated that.

More books, more questions, more access — the monks of Candlekeep would find all of it interesting, which was the point. One book gets you in the door. Five books gets you *facetime*, which I gathered was the currency that actually mattered there.

Then Thorin said: "Does Glabbagool have anything that he wants to ask also?"

The ooze, apparently, was deeply confused by the concept of a library with finite questions attached to it. He wanted to know why we weren't simply donating every book we'd ever seen and asking questions until the end of time. This was the most logical position I'd heard all week. Glabbagool's confusion was not stupidity; it was a creature without the particular social conditioning that makes you accept "there are rules about how much knowledge you're allowed to receive at once" as a normal thing.

Thorin pressed it anyway: "We give all five books, and we say — Glabbagool, you get one question. Make it count. And I'm really interested to see what he asks."

Grygum said he was curious too.

Five books. Four of us and one ooze who had been in the dark of a bag for most of his conscious existence, watching a shrunken spy with one eye and trusting that the world above ground was worth seeing.

I was also curious what he'd ask. I didn't say so, but I thought about it for the better part of the road to Candlekeep — what a creature with no past and no allegiances and a singular earnest interest in understanding the world would choose to know, given a single question and access to everything.

I didn't have an answer. But I kept watching the bag, the way it moved, the slight weight of it. Somewhere in there, one eye was open.
