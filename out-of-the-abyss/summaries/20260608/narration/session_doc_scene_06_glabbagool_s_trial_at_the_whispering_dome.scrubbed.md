The Whispering Dome was the kind of place I would have liked under different circumstances. Quiet. Low light pooling under the curve of the ceiling. No one talking unless they had a reason. I have spent most of my life looking for rooms like this, and most of my life finding them already occupied by people who would not stop making noise.

Glabbagool was summoned for what they called his little quest. They told me I keep him — that he was, the word they used, permanently attached. I had no objection to log. He moseyed across the floor toward the Dome, an oozing thing moving at his own pace, and I followed. Of course I followed. That was never a question I was going to be asked twice.

He entered and flattened himself against the stone. The Dome is where supplicants go to learn something about themselves. I had not known that when I walked in. I learned it the way I learn most things — by watching what happened next.

A monk posed the question. *What is the art of movement.*

Glabbagool went still.

Not paused. Still. The difference matters. Paused costs something; still costs nothing. The better part of a minute passed. I have done that drill. I have held that exact silence in a shuttered room while some brother I cannot name walked the line correcting our breathing. I held it badly. Glabbagool held it like it was the easiest thing he had ever been asked to do.

Then he answered. *"The body that mimics nothing is already shaped by what it watches."*

The monk smiled. Nodded. Accepted it as wisdom, because it was.

I did not understand it. I want to be precise about that. I heard the words, I registered that they landed, I watched the monk take them as truth — and I did not understand them. There was a class for this. I think it was Brother Heslin, the one who always smelled of incense, or possibly the quiet one with the ink-stained hands. Silent discussion. Communication without sound. I remember the light through the shutters. I do not remember the lesson.

Glabbagool came out of his stillness and said, *"I've been waiting to do this for a very long time."*

And he flowed up onto my arm. *Glabbagool and Zalthir, together now,* he said, settling along my forearm like he had measured it in advance.

"Yes? Can I, like, punch something, or—"

I could. I would be given the particulars. What I felt, watching him arrange himself over my scales, was something I did not have a name for. "This is exciting. We formed Voltron or something."

The particulars arrived. He could teleport in darkness — my native ground, the one curriculum I actually attended. He could throw a flurry of strikes. He could force his acidic shadow key into a wound. And a corrosive embrace, which costs him something, and only works on a target I have already bound. "Hmm. The last one will need some explanation, but this sounds exciting."

"Interesting. Okay, so it will actually ride on my arm, and—"

*We ride,* Glabbagool said.

"I liked it." Then he added that it must be quiet, and I liked that more. The corrosive embrace, when he showed me what it would do to something held still — it was not a wound. It was dissolution. A target restrained, nothing left to hold by the end of it. "This is exciting."

Then the eyes. "Does this also mean I have, like, two eyes on my arm? Like, just kind of looking around?" Four, as it turned out, blinking up from my own scales. "Well, good! So I just admire him now." I suggested he might not need them — that he could blend into me rather than read as a separate creature riding along. "Right, she doesn't need the eyes, right?" He thought people expected him to have them, large and obvious. "Okay, so maybe he can just look more like my normal — scales, whatever I have." He paused over that. Considered it.

Before he settled in, he reminded me the drow in the bag of holding still needed watching. Of course he did. "Well, either way, as long as you check in on the drow, we're all good." He had survived Blingdenstone and the ooze crisis and however long in the dark, and his instinct, at the moment of his own transformation, was housekeeping. Good instinct.

The former shadow monk had been watching the whole exchange. He looked at me and said that if I had paid more attention to the classes on silent communication, I would know exactly what Glabbagool's answer meant. But that, apparently, had been the day I was sleeping.

"Too much gardening research."

There are, by now, a great many days I was sleeping. The student on my arm had mastered the one lesson I most thoroughly missed, and he had done it in under a minute, by going still.

"Right. There's a lot of them at this point. Was I ever there?"
