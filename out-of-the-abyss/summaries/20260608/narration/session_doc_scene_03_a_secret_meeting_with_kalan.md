The key went to Grygum. I marked that before anything else — not into Daz's hand, not into Thorin's, not into the open air for whoever was closest. Kalan stepped out of a doorway we hadn't clocked, swept the corridor left, then right, and pressed a small brass key specifically into the cleric's palm. He chose the orc. I don't think it was chance. The man who carries the weight is the man someone has decided to trust.

"Whatever Brookworm is telling you, listen for what she isn't," he said, low. "The High Tower has two keys. Janussi wore one, I held the other. I have given it now to someone who is not me. If I and I do not see you tomorrow, do not look for me at my post."

"You're worried that you'll be killed for the key?" Grygum asked.

Kalan looked left. Looked right. "It is best that if I am killed, the key not be found."

Thorin pushed at what the key actually guarded. The High Tower, Kalan told us, was the entrance to all of the super magical artifacts that every evil entity in the entire world would love. Thorin wanted to know if it had been compromised — whether Kalan himself might have used the key, walked in, walked out.

You don't need the key for that, came the answer. The tower is watched. Always. People stand at it whether the key is in someone's pocket or not. Entry would be seen.

"So that seems to clear him," Thorin said. And the gatekeeper turned and walked away.

I went after him. The rest of them had their questions, but most of those would dissolve into noise. I had a few that wouldn't.

"You need both," I said, when Grygum circled back to why a second key mattered. "Power with two." Thorin called it a war-game swapper, two officers turning keys to launch the missile. The metaphor was loud and it was also correct.

"Before you go," I said. "Did you find anything? Any idea who's behind this?"

He performed the moment — *an interview with Kalan Strongbranch* — and reminded us, twice, that he had been wanted off the case. Then he turned it on me. Our motives. Why we were here. Why he should trust us at all.

"Purely accidental," I told him. "We have no particular involvement here. Someone put us up to this. We are happy to not get involved." True enough. Filed under things that are also true.

He decided the nature of fate had brought disinterested outsiders to exactly this place, and that this meant something. I offered the simple version: we had the job, he could simply come with us. No. Bookwyrm had made it official that he was to be nowhere near it. "Officially," I said. "See, there you go." Grygum asked whether we could rely on him unofficially, and the ego did the rest of the work for us.

He had picked up a few things. He liked to juxtapose mundane observation with lofty rumination on order, chaos, fate, and futility — and in that rumination he had noticed the poison in the left hand, the saliva. "The black stuff, yes, we saw that." He noticed the heart hacked out — after death, to spoil resurrection. "Yeah, we got that." He noticed the murderer was a magic user who could circumvent arcane and mundane locks. Knock spells of the highest order.

That one I hadn't built. "How do you know that?"

Then he tangled himself. He assumed the murderer was the one who opened the safe. I stopped him. The key was in the locket. The locket was around the keeper's neck. The locket was gone. So why would the killer go through the safe at all? Two hands had searched that apartment — one that knew exactly where to look, one that didn't.

Thorin asked the right small question: how did we know the key had ever been in the locket? Kalan pulled out his own locket to demonstrate the method of carry.

"Which is now empty," I said, "and therefore doesn't prove anything." So we asked him to open it and show us. He did. Because the thing he was holding up as proof could just as easily be Janussi's key, handed off by a man building a clean story. The locket was missing. The key was in the missing locket. None of it forced his conclusion.

"So why do you think the murderer is the same person who rifled the safe? There were, like, four different people in and out of that apartment."

He paused. He looked at me. "I don't know. What I thought — but now that you bring it up, perhaps I was a little bit too hasty. Perhaps it was two different people."

I gave him the *okay* and let it sit.

What he suspected, he said, was that one of the Great Readers was the murderer, and that either Bookwyrm was protecting them or someone had convinced her to fire him to protect themselves. Then the cold part: "I believe you're only being employed so you're likely to bungle the case, or else be silent if you do happen upon the right scent. You don't matter. You're outsiders."

Thorin, to his credit, didn't argue the insult. "I don't doubt that maybe they hired us because they thought we were gonna screw it up."

I had one card and I played it plainly. "Just to point out the obvious — we've already learned more than you knew and corrected one of your assumptions. Would it not be in our interest to collaborate?"

He thought. Then he proposed it in the most extraordinary terms — offline chat, private work streams, a meeting to examine everything one by one, the four of us and him, five streams, merge it all in a few hours.

"I hope you actually used the word sync up," I said. "That makes me happy."

Before he went, I closed the gaps that mattered. He could cast Speak with Dead. Did he have anything in the line of truth detection, location, that genre? Yes — if such a spell should become necessary. So if we suspected someone was lying, he could sort it out? "Of course I could." Wonderful. Something I'd want when the room was full of people I didn't know.

He gave us his timeline — the eight o'clock meeting where he'd pressed Janussi for more watchers and been refused, the quarter-past-ten visit to Sylvira Savikas over some cosmological question, the return to the Chamber of Lost Lore for private research. Then the morning of the murder: the inspection just after eight, the cordon, the posted watcher, the state of emergency, the doubled guard at the High Tower Library. The account of a man who wanted it on record where he had been.

Then I gave him the thing he didn't have. "Is he aware that someone was pretending to be Sylvira Savikas the night of the murder?"

He went still. "No. That is new information." A pause. "You are very good at this."

"Well, we're trying."

Whoever set this up, he said — naming him to the wrong people, perhaps — would be very disappointed in him. As close to a compliment as the man had in him.

"That's the hope, I guess."

"Given that you're at risk of being killed over a key you don't have anymore, watch your back," I told him. "Keep in mind there are people who can change their form. Shapeshift. Whatever you want to call it."

He looked at me the way a man looks at someone explaining how he came to run the gate of Candlekeep.

"I'm just saying," I said, and let him go to his research.
