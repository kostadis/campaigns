The Burning Wizard Inn took its name from a death. That was the first thing I catalogued.

A wizard, burned alive, dragon cult, decades prior. Festroon — the gnome innkeeper, old enough to have heard the story from the people who told it first — pointed to the south wall without prompting, the way a man points at something he has pointed at ten thousand times before. "That is where he fell. We scrub the floor every season. We do not paint over the wall. My grandfather said the wall remembers."

I noted the exits. Front door, obvious. Side passage near the kitchen, partially obscured by a stack of barrels. Stairs to the upper rooms. The window behind the performer's corner, narrow but workable in an emergency. The gnome himself, watching the room with the comfortable authority of someone who had survived thirty years of other people's bad decisions under his roof.

The city would have called this paranoia. The city would have been right. It never made the habit less useful.

There was a bard — a traveling one, by the look of her kit, set up in the performer's corner with a drum and a competent audience. She had what every working bard has at a waystation like this: a closing number she'd been doing all season, calibrated to the room's energy by the second pint and tightened by the third. This one was called "Black Hood." The whole inn came in on the chorus:

*"Oh, the Kenku could not fly, but he flew in his black hood, in his black hood, in his black hood. The raven thief who learned to steal no more, save what was needed, said the small, wise man at his side."*

Grygum, who has an irritating habit of noticing things I would have preferred to notice first, observed drily that the song's principle — steal what was needed — mapped rather well onto Whistler. The Kenku thief of some earlier age who, presented with the complete hoard of Tiamat, had apparently taken only what was necessary for legitimate expenses. Operating goals. Day-to-day funds. Per diems.

I found that, I mean, genuinely impressive. The restraint of it. Most people, faced with the complete hoard of Tiamat, would either take everything or panic and take nothing. Whistler had apparently made a line-item budget.

After her set, the bard invited requests. Grygum leaned forward — he has a scholar's interest in Stroud that he performs slightly harder than the interest actually requires — and offered her a silver piece for something about Stroud's famous chess game. She looked at his holy symbol and asked whether he was Bahamutian or Stroudite, and he explained that he was Bahamutian, with sentimentality for the other. She took the coin and proceeded to sing a love song. Involving flowers. And drinking. The chess game appeared as a metaphor for romantic longing, the grand theological stakes filed down into something a farmhand could hum on the way home.

Grygum received this with the expression of a man who had asked for tea and received warm water. "The only love I remember during the previous campaign," he said, "involved giants, and it wasn't Stroud's." He paused. "These things have to be done with a grain of salt."

I had been cataloguing the room during all of this, which is not something I do consciously anymore. It is just what happens when I sit somewhere new: faces, patterns, the small deviations from baseline behavior. There was a young human scholar in the far corner — not a human scholar, a *young* human scholar, because those are meaningfully different things and I did not want to get the category wrong — who was reciting something under his breath. A chant of some kind. Rhythmic, almost liturgical.

I will not call what I felt next a headache. I will call it the specific cognitive friction of almost-recognition — something pressing at the edge of attention that wants to be resolved.

I had been attributing the sensation to sunlight. I had, in fact, been attributing it to sunlight for most of the day. It has been a while since I have been outside in the sun this long.

I watched the scholar. His breathing was almost right. The pauses fell almost correctly. The pattern was almost the one it was supposed to be — and wrong by a hair in a way that took actual knowledge of the correct version to notice. I know that chant. I know it well enough to know what he was doing wrong, and the specific wrongness of it — the way he syncopated his breath against the syllables, or tried to — was not a beginner's error. It was a very particular kind of degradation.

I have seen it before. Twice. Eldred had it, earlier in the journey — the man who went insane. The Field Ward Preacher had it too.

The same pattern. The same wrongness by a hair.

I wrote it down. The exact cadence, the placement of the false syncopation, the point in the verse where the chant bent away from the original. Candlekeep would have records. Someone there would know whether this pattern belonged to a group, a practice, a doctrine — or whether it was something else being made to look like one.

"Yeah, sure," I said, and closed my journal.

Thorin spent the evening outside with Dawnbringer, standing at what I understand was his first real encounter with the sea. Dawnbringer, in characteristic fashion, lit up everything around them with the particular enthusiasm of a sword that has strong opinions about darkness, which made it impossible to see anything. Thorin apparently found this peaceful rather than counterproductive, which I note without judgment. He came back in with the settled look of a man who had had a thought that satisfied him. "Life takes you in weird directions and weird places," he said. "You just gotta go with it."

I have been thinking about that sentence since he said it. I have not yet decided whether it is wisdom or resignation. Possibly there is not much difference.

Zalthir climbed to the roof to meditate. He came back down — later, considerably more settled — with the particular expression of someone who has been surprised by the value of a thing they previously dismissed. He said "Hmm, true," about something, in the tone of a man completing an internal argument rather than beginning an external one.

I did not ask what the argument was. Some accounts balance themselves.

I sat with the inn a little longer before going up. Festroon wiped the bar. The soot mark on the south wall sat where it always sat. The floor, scrubbed every season.

The wall remembers.

I find I understand that better than I would like to.
