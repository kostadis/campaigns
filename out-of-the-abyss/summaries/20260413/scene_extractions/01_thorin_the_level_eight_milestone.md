- Thorin considers whether to address his lack of ranged capabilities or double down on his current specializations.

<!-- Asking Zalthir about character-building philosophy -->
Thorin: "Is your strategy that you just build a super lopsided character and just play with that? Actually, do you try and create some… because, like, right now, my character has, like, zero range ability, and I'm wondering, do I try and fix that, or do I just double down with the shit that I can't, you know?"

<!-- Explaining his optimization philosophy -->
Zalthir: "I find it more fun to double down with some sort of obscure specialization and then try to break the universe with it."

<!-- Complimenting Zalthir's approach -->
Thorin: "I mean, you're really good at it."

- The characters begin discussing the importance of specialization at higher levels, noting that damage output must scale significantly to keep pace with increasingly powerful foes.


<!-- Explaining the game design philosophy around specialization vs. generalization -->
Thorin's inner monologue: "So, in general, the game rewards deep specialization over general purpose. Because as you gain levels, the monsters start to need more and more damage per hit point. Right? And so you're, like, at the first level, 10 points of damage is a lot of damage. At the 8th level, 20 points is a lot of damage. At the 15th level, 40 points is a lot of damage. And at the 20th level, you better be doing 70 points of damage per round."

<!-- Warning about the lack of ranged capability -->
Thorin's inner monologue: "So it encourages the group to have thoughtful balance. Now, a couple of important points, you are in Under the Dark, so, you know, it's not like there are going to be people flying all over the place, all of the time. But because this is Dungeons & Dragons, and none of you have ranged weapons, of course there will be people."

<!-- Realizing Dawnbringer's damage type matters tactically -->
Thorin: "So question, so Dawnbringer is not slashing, right? It's actually… Radiant damage."

<!-- Confirming -->
Thorin's inner monologue: "Yep."

<!-- Wrestling with the strength cap and feat selection -->
Thorin: "So part of my build is that my strength is currently 18."

<!-- Correcting himself -->
Thorin: "It's 19."

<!-- Advising on the obvious mechanical choice -->
Thorin's inner monologue: "In general, increasing your strength to 20 is, like, the most obvious mechanical thing to do. But 18 is pretty close to the maximum, so…"

<!-- Frustrated by the stat cap -->
Thorin: "That's kind of where I'm at right now, and some of these things are like, increase your strength by 1 to a maximum of 20, and I'm like, well, if I can't go over 20, what even is the point?"

<!-- Explaining the alternative approach -->
Thorin's inner monologue: "Because you can do it later. And then if you pursue the Zalthir theory of micro-optimization, what you will do is you will not do the buffing… by the way, the game theory of micro-optimization has been used in my current run of Baldur's Gate. Where I discovered that I didn't need to increase my strength to 20, because I just get potions of giant strength."

- The group discusses the lack of ranged weapons in the party and how it might be a disadvantage in future encounters.


<!-- Admitting his hoarding tendency with consumables -->

<!-- Acknowledging the bad habit -->
