The desk again. We came back to it the way you come back to a knot you've half-untied — not because anything was new, but because we hadn't yet looked at it in the order it had happened.

In the middle of the desk sat a spellbook. *The Discoveries.* Beside it, blue ink, fresh enough that it had a sequence to it. There was more than one color on those pages, and I wanted the newest one isolated before anything else. "There were multiple… colors of ink," I said. "There's… the blue one is the newer one. He was writing something, but what was the blue text?"

Musings on unfamiliar spells. Notes on how they might be cast — a wizard cataloguing things he'd seen others do and never managed himself. The sort of thing a careful man writes when he is trying to understand something larger than he is. I have a passing respect for that. I let very few classes at the monastery reach me, but the ones I let in, I let in completely.

What mattered was the timing. He'd been struck in the face, returned here, and written. Then he'd died. "Well, no, but he got punched in the face, came back here, was making notes, and then someone killed him," I said. "I'm curious… trying to understand what he was writing at the time of death." The room reads better when you ask what a man was doing in his last unguarded minute than when you ask who he was.

The blue ink on *The Discoveries* had been laid down after the blue ink on another book. *The Golden Ass.* "So that's the last thing he was writing?" Confirmation. The spell research was the final thing under his hand.

"The product placement notes," I said. A homage to some other adventure, a book you could go and buy if the mood took you.

"Oh, the mystery," Grygum said. "You have to go buy the other adventure." He found this funnier than I did. Most things, he does.

Then the second book complicated itself. *The Golden Ass* was a comedy. Janussi had been writing down jokes he found funny — the man had a sense of humor he'd evidently kept private. "No, wait," I said. "The blue ink captures both notes on obscure spells and jokes?" The same hand, the same fresh ink, two registers entirely. Jokes first. The ink on those had dried. Then he'd flopped over into the spell research. "So he was noting down jokes, and then flopped over to spell research." Yes. And somewhere in the seam between the two, the comedy book had walked out of the room.

That was the absence. The notes proved the book had been here — you do not annotate a thing you do not hold. And now it was gone, and it was the only thing gone. A room tells you most by what it is missing. The Garden Shadow had a saying about that — or possibly Brother Tharusk did, the tall one who always smelled of incense; I wasn't listening to either of them closely. The point survived anyway: count what should be present before you count what is.

Thorin had been turning the black residue over. "What color was, like, on his fingers and mouth, was it black or red?" Black. Definitively not the blue ink — Grygum had already settled that, checked it twice and certain. So the black on the man's mouth came from somewhere the blue ink did not.

A gift tag surfaced from the wastebasket as we were leaving — almost behind the desk, in a cramped, sour hand. "Wait, is that describing the type of writing?" Thorin asked. It was. "It's, like, grumpy?" I said. Grumpy. Cramped. The script of someone who resented the act of writing it. *To Janussi, read it and weep, you old snob, a proper Dead Winter gift, your loving rival.* Thorin lit up. "Okay, so that supports my theory. Some sort of rival gave him a gift, but it was sort of like a bitter thing."

There was one thing I wanted closed before we left it. The smoke. "Just to double check, do we know where the tobacco smoke was coming from?" Janussi had loved the stuff — kept his chamber thick with it, medicated his failing lungs with the smell of it, lived inside a haze on purpose. A man who fills his own room with smoke as a habit. Which means a man who would not notice if, for one night, the smoke was working for someone else.

"The one thing that tobacco smoke would do is hide the smell of poison." It wasn't a leap. It was the absence again, read forward. You don't fill a room with smoke to kill a man — you fill a room with smoke to keep him from smelling what's already there.

"Okay, so yeah, there's definitely some sort of poison going on," Thorin said, "and there's no way for us to figure out definitively, we've already sort of blown our chances." Probably. The black on the pages of a book that was no longer in the room. The smoke that hid the smell of it.

"If it really is a comedy book, there's probably not arcane knowledge or anything else there," Thorin went on. "There's something written in the book that wasn't part of the original book — or maybe he's using the book and the notes to do some sort of cipher. Some sort of replacement cipher, and there's, like, some sort of code that the book ends up being the key to. Am I reaching too deep here?"

"I like the way you're thinking," Daz said.

I said nothing to that. The poison and the cipher could both be true. The missing book carried both. We had read the room down to what it was willing to give. The rest of it lived somewhere else, and we'd have to go find it.
