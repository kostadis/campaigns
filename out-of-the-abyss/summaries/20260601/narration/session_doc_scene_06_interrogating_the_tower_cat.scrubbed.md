The interview began the way most interviews do — with someone explaining why this couldn't possibly work, and me proving otherwise.

Zalthir wanted a way to talk to the cat. Sound enough. The animal had been in the chamber; the dead don't volunteer alibis, but a five-year-old housecat with opinions might. Grygum admitted he couldn't afford a potion — clerics of Bahamut, it turns out, run on a tighter margin than their theatrics suggest. So I filed the obvious solution where it belonged: in my own column.

"I think I can talk to the cat," I said.

"Oh, can you?" Zalthir, surprised. He is rarely surprised. I keep a small, private tally of the times.

"Talking to beasts, yeah. It's part of what the stone speaker crystal grants me." A useful thing, the Stonespeaker. I had been carrying it as a curiosity. Now it was a witness chair.

"Well, that saves me money on potions," said Grygum, relieved in the way of a man who has just had an expense removed from his account by someone else's labor. At least we have that.

The cat introduced herself, after a fashion. Queenie — five years old, sleet-grey, and entirely uninterested in being disturbed. "I did not order any more ticklers!" she announced, stretching. "Any good ticklers are the only good… and good ticklers are… tickles are only the good reason for disturbing my beauty sleep."

A negotiation, then. I know the shape of those. Establish what the other party wants and provide exactly enough of it to keep the conversation yours. "I'll give Queenie a little tickle," I said, and tickled. Information is currency. So, evidently, is scratching behind the ears.

She warmed. They always do, when you find the lever.

What she gave us, once warmed, was worth more than the price. She had settled into Janussi's armchair for a nap, she said, "only to be rudely awakened some thirty minutes later by our Sylvira Savikas, this is in the morning, blasting the armchamber with a lance of unerring force light." Thorin dug in — *what was going on with those bolts of force, do we know why* — and the answer arrived dressed as gossip: Sylvira the tiefling, and Janussi, an on-and-off-again relationship. A lover's quarrel, then, opened with a flung working of force. I have known worse opening arguments. I have made worse opening arguments.

She woke from her afternoon nap around eight, after supper, and watched the evening's guests arrive in order — first Fheminor, *the short-legged one*, and later Daral, *the stinky breath one*. There is a precision to how cats categorize people that I find I respect. No titles. No politics. Just the relevant data: gait, smell.

Then Daral's part. Too much shouting, she said, so she retreated to the corridor — "when I later saw him run downstairs with a hypnotic block."

A hypnotic block.

Something in the phrasing snagged. I have spent my life learning to notice when a description is doing work the describer doesn't understand. The detail sat wrong, and the wrongness was the point — *the first rule is to read the witness who doesn't know what they're a witness to.* I pressed.

"What about this hypnotic block?"

It clicked the way a lock gives when your fingers find the tumblers before your mind does. Not luck. The right question, at the right weight, landing exactly where I aimed it.

"Every time they open that block," Queenie said, "they just sit there and don't tickle!"

A book. Not a device, not an enchantment — a book, dull enough to hold a person motionless and uninterested in scratching a cat. *The Golden Ass*, the missing volume, walking out of the room under Daral's arm.

"Oh, it's a TV!" said Zalthir, which was not quite the deduction but arrived at the same destination.

"But the victim was still alive when Daral left," Thorin noted, and the timeline held. Yes. Daral exited with the book, and the dead man was not yet dead.

She wasn't finished. She'd prowled the nighttime cycle and seen *the torn demon lady* coming down the steps — Sylvira's shape — "But she didn't smell like the demon lady, though." A figure wearing Sylvira and none of her scent. The con never stops; I only ever recognize someone else running mine.

"Oh, so we've got, like, a shapeshifter situation going on here," said Thorin.

"That explains the candle," said Zalthir, and the picture began to assemble itself.

Zalthir pushed her for more — blood, organs, a heart, anything from the wrong-smelling figure. Queenie was done being generous. "There have been too little Tickles and too many questions." I tickled. I tickled some more. After a stretch of purring she delivered her assessment of two-leggers entire: "How would I know? I try to hold my breath whenever a two-legger passes. It's clear that you hardly ever lick yourselves at all. I've met cleaner dogs in my time."

I have been insulted by archpriests. This one landed cleaner.

We'd nearly let the locket go unasked — the thing missing from Janussi's neck. Grygum caught it. "Daz, why don't you ask the cat anyway? It's gonna be a weird description from a cat, but…" It was. "The locket itself is intimate. He carried it all the time. He never took it off." What was it, I asked. "I don't know what it was." A cat will tell you a man's habits and not his secrets.

Then Fembris, *by golly*, supplied the column I was missing: Janussi held one of the two keys to the Hightower Library.

I let the dots connect themselves out loud. "So could that be the key to the library that was around there? That might explain why that stuff got deleted off that book that no one should have touched. Someone snuck in." The other key, I learned, rested with Kalan, the gate warden. I filed both.

"More tickles, more padding," I offered, because the account wasn't quite settled.

It was, as far as Queenie was concerned. "You may now leave," she said, "and please give yourself a proper licking before returning to my tower."

And she walked off.
