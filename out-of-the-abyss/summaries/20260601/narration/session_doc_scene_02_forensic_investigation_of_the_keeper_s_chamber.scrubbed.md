Daz worked the body over with the careful attention of a man reading the last page of someone else's diary. He had the eye for it, and the recent education — Milo's murder mystery, fresh in his head — and when his focus snapped clean on the corpse he started naming things faster than I could write them down.

The keeper's left forefinger was blackened. So was the center of his lower lip, and the tip of his tongue.

"Like, Dumbledore style?" Zalthir asked. "Or, like…"

It wasn't a tattoo, it wasn't a wound. The man had been reading a book, smudging his fingers on the ink, and licking the smudges off his lip. A reader to the end. I noted that and felt something I didn't examine too closely — a clerk dying mid-sentence in his own library. There are worse exits.

Then the tooth. A blackening on the tip of the tongue and a chip in one front tooth, jagged, recent. Thorin turned it over out loud — either part of the murder, immediate, or something we needed to chase. Zalthir asked, half-joking, if anyone could find the missing thing by magic, and the chip turned up a stride from the keeper's face. Which told its own story: he fell forward, hit hard enough to break the tooth, and then someone came in afterward and rolled him over to take his heart. Died on his face. Got turned over by his killer. The sequence had a horrible tidiness to it.

That was where I came in, because Daz's reading and Zalthir's logic only got us to the moment of death. I had the part before it.

"And also broke his nose," I said.

I looked closer. This is the kind of thing I know — what a body does when the heart stops, and what it can't do. The bruising around the broken nose held its color, dark and settled, the way bruising only sits when blood was still moving when it formed. My focus narrowed and held, and the picture came clear and certain: this happened before he died. Not the fall. Earlier.

"Okay, so his nose was broken, he was killed, smashed on the face."

Zalthir caught the implication a half-step behind me. "Grygum, you've got the healer's eye, right? How does bruising work if he's dead? Like, I would assume you don't really."

"You don't — you don't bruise after you're dead."

The dead don't mark. The body has to be defending itself to make a bruise, and a corpse defends nothing. So the bruise wasn't from the killing blow. Somewhere between dinner and the moment his heart was carved out, someone had struck this man in the face hard enough to break the bone. A struggle. An assault. An hour of his evening I could suddenly see the shape of, even if I couldn't see the face that did it. I filed it. A bruised nose into a timeline — it's what I do.

The neck told us more. The impression of a chain on the back and sides, and beneath it two broken lead links. Something had hung there, and someone had torn it off him. A locket, a necklace — gone, snapped at the chain. I have catalogued a great many sacred spaces in my time, and there is a particular grief in an empty setting where something belonged. The chain remembered the weight even though the man no longer could.

The room itself kept volunteering details. A silver pedestal by the desk, an ebony pipe finely carved, cherry tobacco. Pretty, until Zalthir's instinct landed it — tobacco smoke would hide the smell of poison. Of course it would.

Then the safe. A box of metal as wide as a man's reach, sunk into the tower wall, its door standing open.

"Oh, is anything inside?"

Empty pouches scattered in front of it, sapphires loose and dazzling, scraps of parchment naming spells. Daz's mind clicked again — material components, every pouch a spell — and he murmured, "a little bit of this, a little bit of that," pleased with himself. But one sapphire was missing. One parchment. "Somebody is trying to build a sapphire spell," he said, "a spell that requires a sapphire."

And here is where the room stopped making sense as a robbery. Because still inside the safe were four sealed pouches, each with its sapphire — and beside them a figurine of a raven, a potion of supreme healing, another of water breathing, five diamonds each worth a fortune, a heap of platinum and gold, a small treasury left untouched.

"In other words, score what they left them."

A small fortune, untouched. Whoever did this came in, took one sapphire, and walked past a king's ransom.

"I mean, well, that can eliminate some former adventurers," I said. The greedy don't leave the gold. This was a man who needed exactly one thing.

Zalthir checked the lock. Open, but undamaged. No forced entry. The killer had the combination, or had no need of one.

Daz drifted to the armchair next, and read the wounds in it the way he'd read the wounds in the man. Three circular impacts, horsehair stuffing scattered, two holes punched clean through the chair back. He studied it and pronounced it: darts of pure force. Somebody had been furious. But not at the keeper — those darts never miss their mark, Thorin pointed out, and the man bore no such marks. Somebody had aimed three bolts of force at a chair.

"Why would you loose three darts of force at a chair?" Daz asked. Nobody had an answer. I wrote it down anyway.

Last, the statue. Three feet of marble, a hooded bearded old man holding a candle aloft, toppled on the floor near the armchairs and the tea table.

"Shake the candle," Thorin said.

Tadric, who'd been hovering and guarding the scene, gave him a look.

"I don't want it to wake up and attack us," Thorin explained. "I've had, you know, had bad experiences."

It worked on poor Tadric exactly as you'd fear. He tried not to look at the candle and then could not stop looking at the candle, and finally turned to Thorin. "Are you sure this — is it true about candles?"

"I mean, you just never know when a statue's gonna wake up," Thorin said, kindly, and I do believe he meant it kindly. "When there's magic with the statue, who knows what's going on with the candle. But, I mean, chances are we're gonna be okay. Just stay around us and we'll protect you."

The statue had simply fallen, it turned out. The Great Reader A'lai Aivenmore had knocked it over in shock when he found the body at dawn.

"How heavy is it?" I asked. "Is it something that you could bump into and knock over?" Three feet of marble. Yes — a man could stagger into that and bring it down, if he'd just walked into a room and seen what we were standing in. I believed it. A scholar discovers a murdered keeper with his heart cut out and reaches blindly for the wall and the wall is a statue. That, at least, was the one honest accident in the room.

Everything else had been deliberate. I closed the file in my head, for now. Someone had struck him, broken his nose, waited or returned, killed him, rolled him over, taken his heart and one sapphire and a locket from his neck — and left the gold.

I took notes either way.
