There is a particular comfort in a corpse. It does not lie. It does not run a counter-play. Everything it knows is written on it, and all I have to do is read.

Janussi, Keeper of the Tome, was laid out before us, and the early findings were already on the table. The heart was gone — torn out, not for any wound it would cause, but as a guard against any working that calls the dead back to answer. Whoever had done this had not wanted Janussi answering questions on the other side of the veil. The ribcage was smashed, something axe-like, and Grygum was the one who pressed on whether the cut was jagged or clean. *Is the wound jagged? Does it seem like it was cut out, or is it magical.* A fair question. The answer was the interesting part.

The blows had been precise. Too precise, too close together, for a man fighting back. No spray across the room — no blood pressure left to throw it. Janussi had been dead before his chest was opened. Whoever did this had not been in a rush. He had not been trying to kill. He had been desecrating a corpse at his leisure, with the unhurried hands of someone who had nowhere to be. I filed that. *The first thing to read is the tempo of the killer.* This one had time.

And then the nose. A whack to the nose, off-center, the kind of thing that happens to a face that meets the floor.

"Anyone want to take a closer look at the body?"

Grygum went first and announced his findings the way you announce bad weather. "I can barely tell which end is which." Thorin muttered something about having the second-worst eye for detail in the room. A low bar, apparently, and they were both under it.

So I stepped up. "Let me look."

I crouched beside the Keeper, and because the moment seemed to ask for it, I gave it the full performance. Steepled fingers. A long, considering pause over the body, the kind a man does when he already knows the answer and wants you to watch him arrive at it.

"Hmm. It would appear…" I let it hang. "Body is dead."

I did say it was a performance. The good part came under it.

Clotted blood under the nose. Smeared bloodstains on the right sleeve of the tunic — the *right* sleeve, the working hand. And blotches of midnight blue across the cuff. I knew that blue.

"Wasn't the blue ink — the ink they used in the books?"

It was. Janussi had been taking notes when this happened. Writing hand, fresh stains. A man interrupted at work.

But there was something else on him, and it was the something else that mattered. The tip of his left forefinger was blackened. Not blue. Darker than blue. And the same dark smudge sat dead-center on his lower lip.

"Like, Dumbledore style?" Zalthir asked. "Or, like—"

"With ink?"

No. Not ink. That was the entire point of the long look — not that the finger was stained, but that the stain was *wrong*. Darker than anything in his inkwell. Zalthir, ever the auditor of other people's competence, pushed on it — *our great detective took his long, careful look and still can't tell us if it's ink?* No. That was exactly what I could tell. The skill wasn't in identifying the substance. It was in knowing, with certainty, what it was *not*.

And the gesture wrote itself across the body. Finger to a page, finger to the lip — turning pages, wetting the fingertip the way readers do without thinking, a hundred times an hour, never once considering that the page might be the thing reading *them*.

"Do we think this black stuff might be poisonous?" Zalthir asked.

I thought it was the likeliest column to balance to. But thinking it and proving it are different ledgers, and we had no one who could read the substance — until Zalthir, of all people, found the angle. "Wouldn't Glabbagool have ingested, like, all sorts of different kinds of materials and poisons and goo in the Underdark?"

Well played. An ooze is, if nothing else, a lifetime of taste-tests. We gave him the substance. He gave it his best. It wasn't enough — a thin first effort, then a second pass with help, and still nothing he could name. But even his failure carried a verdict: there was *something* about the black, something that mattered. When even a creature that has eaten the Underdark can't place a poison, you've learned the poison is worth being afraid of.

More surfaced. The tongue, blackened at the tip — ingestion confirmed twice over now. And a chip in one of his front teeth, the edge jagged. Recent. Not an old break worn smooth, but a fresh wound.

Thorin worked it like a problem. "So either that was related to him being murdered, immediately as part of the murder, or it was something we need to investigate — how did he chip the tooth recently?" Fair logic, but it carried its own answer: if the black smear was poison, why would a tooth chip belong to the murder at all? Two different stories were trying to live in one body.

Zalthir asked if anyone could use a working to find the chip. We didn't need magic. It was right there — a few feet from his face, sitting on the floor where it had landed when the man went down. He had fallen forward. Face-first. Chipped the tooth on the way down. And then someone had come in, after, and rolled him onto his back to take the heart.

"And also broke his nose," Grygum said.

That was the loose thread, and Grygum pulled it. His read of the wound came clean — sharp work this time — and the nose had broken *before* death. Bruising doesn't form on a corpse; there's no living tissue to swell. Zalthir worked the same logic from the other end, confirming it with Grygum twice over: *you don't bruise after you're dead.* So the broken nose belonged to the living man, not the desecrated one. Within the evening — Janussi had eaten dinner with his face intact and his teeth whole. Whatever happened, happened in the narrow window between that meal and his death.

I sat with the shape of it. A man at his books, wetting his finger to turn a poisoned page. The black climbing through him while he read on, suspecting nothing. A fall, forward, hard — a broken nose, a chipped tooth on the stone. And then the patient hands arriving after, in no hurry at all, rolling the body and opening the chest to make certain he stayed gone.

The body had told us everything it knew. It always does. The only question left was who had been certain enough to want him silent on both sides of death.
