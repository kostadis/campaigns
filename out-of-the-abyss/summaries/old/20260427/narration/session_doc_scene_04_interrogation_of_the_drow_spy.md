The stun wore off and she was already calculating. I could see it in the set of her shoulders — that particular Drow recalibration, the moment when violence becomes negotiation and the question shifts from *survive the next five seconds* to *survive the next five minutes.* She was small — Grygum's *Reduce* spell had seen to that — but she held herself with the trained dignity of someone who understood that posture was its own kind of armor.

Zalthir was first, the way he usually is in these moments: unhurried, precise.

"Who are you? Why are you following us? Are you a friend or foe?"

She looked at him. Then at the rest of us. The arithmetic was happening behind her eyes — I recognized it because I had run the same numbers at Velkynvelve, the first morning, when a wrong answer meant a crossbow bolt. She decided she didn't have enough information yet to give anything away.

"And why should I tell you anything? You will kill me on the spot and leave nothing. What do I have to gain from talking to you?"

"I have no particular desire to kill you," Zalthir said, "unless you mean us harm."

Interesting offer. She weighed it. Then, with the careful neutrality of someone who has been trained to give the minimum required to survive: "I am of House T'sarran. I will not give my name. Take my piwafwi if you want a trophy. My matron will know one of hers fell out here, and that is enough."

House T'sarran. The name landed in my chest like a dropped coin — a small, clear note I tried not to react to. I kept my face still and let the others carry the conversation forward.

Thorin tried intimidation. He is, genuinely, a large and battle-tested dwarf who has survived things that would have killed most creatures twice over, so the raw material is there. What he has not spent five years in Menzoberranzan is the instinct for which lever actually moves a Drow. He walked up and told her we could simply let her go — report her failure to her matron, "let them know how pathetic you are." He said this with the confidence of someone who thought he was offering a fate worse than death.

He was not wrong that it was, for someone like her.

She stared at him blankly. The stun was still doing its work; I'm not sure she'd fully processed the comment.

He tried again. She laughed in his face.

I found I didn't feel superior about this. Thorin's instincts have saved us too many times for me to feel superior. What he lacks in Drow politics he makes up for in a kind of irreducible physical honesty that people cannot quite dismiss, even when they're laughing at him. She was laughing, but she was also still talking to us, which is the more important variable.

Grygum, meanwhile, had been watching her the way he watches things — not calculating exactly, more like listening for something the rest of us weren't hearing. There is a quality to his attention I've never fully resolved. He looked at her and I think he saw the whole picture at once: the surface posting, the miserable exposure to the overbright, the desperate bravado of someone who'd drawn a terrible assignment and was trying to make it look like a choice.

"Hey," he said, with the casual tone of someone considering a minor administrative matter, "are you looking for a new job? We could use another helper."

Something shifted in her face. A tiny, involuntary fracture in the composure. *Wait. I might live?*

It is remarkable what that realization does to someone. She had walked into this interrogation with the set expression of a soldier prepared to die with her intelligence intact, and now she looked — for just a moment — like a person who had not yet decided how much she wanted to live. The Menzoberranzan training is very thorough about creating a certain relationship to death: make it seem like a preference, not a threat. But she was young enough, or tired enough, or perhaps just unhappy enough with her assignment, that the offer of survival caught her genuinely off guard.

Then Grygum held out the prayer beads.

He didn't say anything particularly ominous. He just handed them to her and told her to put them on. No threat in his voice. No explanation of what they did.

She looked at a half-orc cleric holding out holy symbols of a deity she knew nothing about, in the overbright that was already making her eyes ache, after having been stunned and grappled and shrunk and laughed at by a dwarf, and something in her assessment of the situation entirely reorganized itself. The beads were the thing that actually broke through the professional armor. A credible threat she understood — extraction, summary execution, reporting her failure up the chain — these were surfaces she had trained to hold against. Unknown religious ritual from an alien faith, administered by a half-orc? That slipped right past every prepared defense.

She looked, in the GM's precise summary, defeated, frustrated, annoyed.

Then Grygum rolled persuasion, and she talked.

"I was just assigned to track you, not to engage. My orders were to report your surface destination, your contact, who approaches you." A pause. Then: "My matron was waiting for you to surface. She sent a couple of us up to the overbright to find where you were. I got lucky, and I spotted you."

"Probably not feeling so lucky right now," Thorin said.

She ignored this with some dignity.

I had been listening. I had been watching. And something had been bothering me since she opened her mouth, a subtle wrongness I couldn't quite name until I let myself actually look at her — properly look, the way you read a room in Menzoberranzan when the words people are saying bear only incidental relationship to what they mean.

She wasn't watching the party.

She was watching me. And working very, very hard to make it look like she wasn't.

Being a drow, you pick some things up. The particular discipline of *not looking at the thing you are most interested in* is one of the first social skills anyone learns in the city. You don't stare at the matron. You don't watch the door where your rival just entered. You keep your gaze distributed, generalized, apparently casual — and the effort of that maintenance, that deliberate control, is itself legible to anyone who knows what they're looking at. I knew what I was looking at.

I let the interrogation continue for one more breath, then:

"So we may be able to figure something out where you live, but first you need to answer some questions. Why am I so important that you're trying hard not to make it look like you're following me, but you're following me?"

A pause. She decided.

"The Matron Mother was interested in you."

"Who's your matron mother?"

She looked at me with an expression that was almost — not quite, but almost — contemptuous. "You have not heard? Are you not drow?"

I said something genuinely stupid at this point, which I will attribute to the fact that I am not, technically speaking, accustomed to conducting interrogations in full sunlight while running on whatever sleep we'd managed in Blingdenstone. "I'm drow, but I've been underground for a while." 

She stared at me.

"You realize how absurd that sounds?"

She was right that it was absurd. I corrected myself: "Let me just put it this way. Let me correct that. I've been away from home for a while."

She told us her matron had ruled House T'sarran for two hundred years. The length of tenure alone told me something — not a house currently in crisis, not a young matron making aggressive plays to consolidate position. Old power. Patient power. The kind that waits.

I thought of something a training instructor had said once, after a particularly messy exercise in leverage: *The first rule of the House: know who holds the debt before you decide whether to collect it.* I hadn't understood it then as well as I understood it now, standing in a field on the surface world, realizing that someone in a house I'd crossed had been watching me since before I knew I was being watched.

The full picture came out under continued pressure — Dawnbringer's light brought close, the beads still around her neck, Thorin floating the suggestion of leaving her out in the full sun in her current reduced state. Not torture. Providing motivation.

"My matron believes that the one who paid — " and here she pointed at me, with the resigned precision of someone who has run out of misdirection — "for him, will reveal themselves once he reaches the surface. We are looking for that person, not for you."

I turned it over once. Twice.

"So basically, you're treating me as bait. You're watching and observing so that when something happens to me, you can go report back what happened to me."

"Yes. Because we are looking for the one who paid to have you protected."

I asked why they cared.

"The matron mother cares, and therefore I care."

Which is the complete answer, in Menzoberranzan. No ambiguity. No further explanation required. The matron's interest is the reason; the matron's interest is sufficient.

I stood there in the sun — which I was, admittedly, still adjusting to — and I understood what had just been said about me. Not Daz. Not even the wizard. The *indicator.* The piece on the board that you move to see who else moves in response. The bait.

I am an eighth-level wizard. I have kept four people alive through the Underdark, through demon lords, through a dragon, through House politics I was running on improvised expertise and nerve. I have apparently been valuable this entire time primarily because someone with resources thinks *watching what happens to me* might reveal a person they are actually interested in.

Milo Goodbarrel would have found a way to feel good about this. Something about being significant. I thought about it for a moment and decided I'd give myself until we reached Candlekeep to develop the appropriate perspective.

She proposed a deal — mutual information sharing, she tells us what she finds, we tell her what we find, everyone wants to know who's protecting me. Thorin was skeptical in the direct way that Thorin is skeptical of things: "I don't buy it. The minute she has the information she needs, she's off, and we get jack shit."

He is not wrong. The deal has no enforcement mechanism. She has no incentive to honor it beyond her own interest, and the moment her interests and ours diverge — which is to say, the moment she has what she came for — the deal dissolves. Drow contracts without consequences attached aren't contracts. They're intentions.

Then Grygum said: "There's only one solution. We need to kill Daz."

Zalthir, entirely deadpan: "It's on the list. The maybe list."

"And then we'll find out who's protecting him," Grygum continued, with the calm of someone describing a routine experimental procedure.

Thorin: "Do it, but make sure we can bring them back."

"Oh, I have a Revivify spell." Grygum, reassuring.

"So that's just a temporary death."

"Just a death light."

Thorin turned to me. "Is Daz uncomfortable with this conversation?"

I am categorically on record as being the only person in this party who treats information as a finite and valuable resource, and yet here I was, the subject of active theoretical murder planning while a restrained enemy watched us debate it. I kept my expression neutral. I have found purpose. I am the feared mage of the Ember Vanguard. This is fine.

She warned us, between our negotiations, that killing her wouldn't stop House T'sarran from sending more agents.

Grygum held up the prayer beads. "I'm not relying on killing you. I'm relying on the prayer beads."

She eventually proposed being escorted to Candlekeep and handed to the guards — and she said it with the confidence of someone who already knew the second half of that plan didn't involve staying in Candlekeep for very long.

"So they know how to escape that prison," I observed. "Is what they're telling us."

Thorin: "She's so confident she can escape, I don't think we want to be taking her into the stronghold."

Accurate. We don't.

Grygum and Thorin exchanged a look. Then, very loudly, making no apparent attempt to lower their voices:

"Those prayer beads are at full power, even though they're shrunken, right?"

Grygum, with great ceremony: "Yes — in fact, I believe they may be more deadly."

"More potent. Good to know."

She looked at the beads. She looked at Grygum. She began, visibly, to reconsider the available options.
