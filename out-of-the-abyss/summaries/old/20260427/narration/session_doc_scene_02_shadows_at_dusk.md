The third day of open sky and I still hadn't stopped scanning.

That's the thing about months underground: the habits don't switch off just because the ceiling lifts. I'd spent every waking hour in the Underdark reading distance, reading approach vectors, reading the quality of dark in every tunnel junction. Up here, with the wind and the light changing by the hour, I had too much information and not enough of the right kind. It was disorienting in a way I didn't have language for. Not dangerous. Just — loud. Everything up here was loud, even when it was silent.

Dawnbringer, at least, had no such ambivalence.

I'd been aware of the sword's mood for days. It radiated warmth the way a hearth does — not directed at anything in particular, just present. Thorin carried it, but the feeling belonged to all of us. I didn't blame her. She'd endured months of deliberate darkness and come out the other side into this: open sky, a sun that actually moved, stars that weren't being competed with by bioluminescent fungus. On the second evening I'd watched the light die on the horizon and found myself calculating threat vectors by reflex before remembering there were no walls to press against.

By the third day I'd almost relaxed.

Almost.

Dusk came in from the west, that particular quality of fading light that smears the edges of things. I noticed it because the edges of things are exactly what I watch. We were on the road, moving, the conversation turning easy around me the way it does when people have been walking together long enough that the silence between words stops feeling like something that needs filling. Somewhere behind me Thorin was saying something to Dawnbringer about making a decent team.

Then something in my chest went quiet.

Not quiet like peace. Quiet like the moment before.

There was a class for this. I'm reasonably certain it was Brother Orveth — or possibly Brother Quellin, the one who kept gesturing at the tree line when he talked — something about reading environmental stillness, the specific shape that a forest makes when something inside it is trying not to be. I remember the light in that room. I remember thinking I could be training instead. I don't remember what he said.

What I know now, without having been taught it properly, is that the trees north of us were wrong. Not visibly wrong. The wrongness was in the negative space — the way no birds had moved through that section for some time, the way the undergrowth hadn't shifted with the wind in quite the pattern it should. Something was in there, and whatever it was had been in there long enough to develop the habit of staying still.

Close. Uncomfortably close.

Grygum rolled his dice with the confidence of a man who already knows the answer. I watched him from the corner of my eye. He came up satisfied. "You conclude," came the word, "that what is following you is a deer."

I didn't say anything. Grygum nodded with serene certainty and indicated he could use a nap. Reasonable enough. If you're certain it's a deer, sleep makes sense.

Thorin's number was clean. Mine was solid. Daz, a step to my left, had come to the same place I had. The three of us looked at each other in the way that the Ember Vanguard has developed as a private language: no words, just a shared acknowledgment that the thing in the trees is not a deer.

"The three of you quickly realize," — and this landed with the weight of verification, not revelation — "after many months of being out in the dark, that somebody's following you."

I looked toward the northern treeline.

"The deer," I said.

A beat.

"Oh, no. It's not a deer. It's not a deer."

"Oh, dear," said Grygum, somewhere behind me, apparently now awake.

Twenty to forty feet, Thorin's read suggested. I adjusted my estimate of the gap. Closer to twenty. Whatever was in those trees had been moving with us, shifting position laterally to stay in the blind spots, using the terrain the way someone uses it when they know what they're doing. Not panicked. Not rushing. Patient. The kind of following that isn't trailing — it's pacing.

"Is it night, or what's the time of day?" I asked.

Dusk, we were told. Which meant we'd reached the worst possible window: too dark for fine detail, too bright for shadow work. Whoever was out there had timed this, or had simply been following us long enough to arrive at dusk by coincidence.

Neither option was reassuring.

Thorin raised the idea of Tremor Sense with the optimism of someone who'd forgotten which world we were currently standing on. He ran through the mechanics of it with genuine belief, right up until the point where it became clear that the forest floor was dirt and the forest road was dirt and none of it was stone. Then he looked at the trees, looked at the road, and came to the conclusion that the universe had personally wronged him.

"I picked it because we were gonna be fucking underground all the time," he said, with the conviction of a man delivering a formal complaint to the cosmos. "And I figured there would always be some stone. And then the one time I need it. We're not underground, and we're not surrounded by fucking stone."

"You've got anger issues," said Dawnbringer.

Thorin said something about driving issues. I said nothing. He wasn't wrong about the stone. And his frustration was legitimate — he'd made a reasonable preparation for a reasonable environment, and we'd somehow ended up standing in the one terrain where it didn't apply. Months underground, and the threat finds us on a dirt road.

The tracker had been moving sides. That was the detail that settled it from observation into certainty. They weren't following a fixed line — they were adjusting, reading our formation, keeping themselves occluded. That requires knowing what they're tracking. That requires intent.

Thorin's suggestion was to fireball the trees.

I considered this briefly, which is about as much time as it deserves.

Daz was already thinking past it. I could see it in the way he'd gone still — not my kind of still, but a wizard's still, the kind where the mind is moving very fast and the body has just stopped participating. "I want to get some intel out of this," he said, the way a man says a thing when he's already decided what he's going to do.

Someone speculated it might be Jimjar, coming to drag us back underground.

I did not rule it out.

"I'm going to misty step up into the tree," Daz announced.

The dusk settled around us. North of the road, something that was not a deer held its position and waited.

So did I.
