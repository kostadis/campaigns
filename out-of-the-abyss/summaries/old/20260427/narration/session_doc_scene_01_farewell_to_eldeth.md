The sun.

I keep coming back to that first moment of it — the way it hit the stone around the tunnel mouth and turned everything amber and gold, and I just stood there like an idiot with my mouth open. Months underground. Months of bioluminescence and faerzress and Dawnbringer's steady glow being the warmest light any of us had seen. And then this.

Dawnbringer, to her credit, said nothing. She'd been waiting longer than any of us.

The others were finding their own ways into it. Grygum turned his face up like he was receiving a blessing, which I suppose he was. Daz squinted and pushed his spectacles up and looked like a man calculating the precise angle of the light source. Zalthir went still in the particular way he goes still when something has moved him and he doesn't want anyone to notice.

And Glabbagool — Glabbagool just looked up.

I don't know how an ooze looks at something. Technically. But those googly eyes caught the sun and he was absolutely, undeniably looking at it, and there was something in the stillness of him that wasn't his usual stillness. He wasn't being a floor. He was witnessing. Zalthir stood nearby and I watched Zalthir watch him, and I thought: that's the face of someone who did a thing worth doing.

"He's got the stillness working for him," someone said — noting Glabbagool's chief qualification for the monk training Zalthir had apparently, in some sense, offered.

Zalthir's expression made clear that "offered" was doing significant work in that sentence.

"I mean, offered is a strong word."

I considered this. Glabbagool was, by most measurements, extremely stationary. He moved the way continental shelves move. Whatever curriculum Zalthir had in mind — the run, run, slap, slap, slap that Glabbagool apparently had very strong feelings about — it would require some foundational work first.

"Yeah, like, literally taking a step might be a good start."

That seemed fair. I thought about it a moment longer, turning it over the way you do when you're looking for the angle that makes something worth saying.

"But he's unpredictable," I said, "because there are no fixed appendages."

This was true. A fighter with no fixed appendages was genuinely threatening from a tactical standpoint. The form diagrams fell apart entirely.

"And it's easy to do, like, twelve hits in a round when you have twelve arms."

Zalthir looked at Glabbagool with the expression of a man reassessing a problem he thought he understood. I filed the idea away. Twelve simultaneous strikes from an opponent with no readable stance, no telegraphed motion, and a body that was essentially a liquid — that was actually a serious problem for anyone trying to defend against it.

Then Zalthir asked whether he could become an ooze himself.

The answer was no. The answer involved acid damage and what it would do to the grapple rules and I agreed with all of it, though I was curious for about three seconds. Then he pivoted to whether Glabbagool could be equipped as a weapon.

Also no.

"It's not a weapon," I said. "Weapons don't have agency. Except mine."

Dawnbringer made a small sound that I interpreted as acknowledgment. She'd been called worse things than agency. Earlier, in fact, someone had described her as a really cool sword who was kind of annoying sometimes, which she had received approximately the way a master sculptor receives being called "pretty good with rocks."

"I'm annoying also, Dawnbringer," I said. "Look, you know, we got each other."

The hilt warmed slightly. We understood each other on that point.

Eldeth found me a few minutes later, while the others were still adjusting to the light. She had that particular set to her shoulders she gets when she's made a decision and is walking toward it rather than away from it.

"Thorin," she said. "Giant friend."

She uses that. Not as mockery — she stopped doing that somewhere in the deep dark, somewhere around the time we stopped having the energy to argue about it and started just surviving together. She says it like it's part of my name now.

"I have to say that here our journey ends. I must go to Mithril Hall. I must inform the dwarves of what is going on in the Underdark. I hope to see you at some point again. If not, it was well met, and thank you for getting me out of the Underdark."

I stood with that for a moment. Not processing, exactly. I'd known it was coming. Eldeth had obligations that didn't include us, that were older than us, that had been pulling at her since Velkynvelve. A king to brief. A clan to return to. The surface world's ignorance to correct before demon lords became something a surface king heard about secondhand from a merchant who'd heard it from a deep gnome who'd heard it wrong.

"I mean," I said, "I'm a dwarf. I'm not invited along."

She explained. Clan business. Which I understood. Which I'd known even when I asked.

The thing is, I had spent most of my life not being the right kind of dwarf for any given room. Too giant-raised for dwarven spaces, too dwarven for the hills. There's a saying the old ones used — I'm translating badly — something like: a rock in the wrong place is just a rock. It doesn't stop being stone. It just doesn't fit the wall yet.

"Is there any way that I can petition to become part of your clan? A pledge, perhaps? Is there a hazing ritual involved?"

She looked at me. Something shifted in her expression — not softening exactly, more like the thing that had been braced against something could stop bracing.

"She goes, giant friend." And then she said it — all of it — the fighting together, the bleeding together, the facing of abyssal creatures together, the whole catalog of what we'd been through in the dark, laid out plainly, the way dwarves will do when they mean something past the point of argument.

I waited for the "but."

"You're not gonna say but there."

"No," she said. "No, that's where it ends."

And then she made the promise. The whole of it — Mithril Hall, Gauntlgrym, the lords who needed to be told about what was underneath them, the briefing that needed to happen without a giant-raised hill dwarf in the room making everyone curious about the wrong things. She said it and she meant it.

"After I have spoken and explained to the lords of Gauntlgrym and Mithril Hall the dangers of the Underdark, I am certain they will want to speak to you, and I will make it a statement of purpose that you shall be embraced by our clan."

And then: "I swear."

Something settled in me at that. The kind of settling that happens when a thing you've been carrying turns out to have been lighter than you thought, or heavier, or just — right. I'm not sure I have the word for it. The giants would have let it sit in the air for three days and known exactly what it was by the end of it. I had maybe four seconds before I had to say something.

"Alright," I said. "There we go."

Not the grandest response to a sworn oath. But it was what I had, and it was honest.

"Just know," I said, "I do want to learn about my dwarven roots. I want to become an integrated person — both my giant upbringing, but also my cultural roots, so, you know."

She nodded. I could see her doing the calculation — she'd already done it, probably, was the reason she'd looked toward Zalthir a moment earlier with that particular expression. A drow in a hood and a dwarf who'd been raised by hill giants, walking into Mithril Hall to brief the king about demon lords in the deep. I knew what she was thinking. She was right. The story needed to be told before the characters told it.

I was taking this seriously, and I needed her to know that.

"I'm taking this seriously," I said. "I don't think you're just being socially nice here. I think you really do want me to."

She extended her hand and I took it, and she held it for a moment the way that means something more than a handshake. Then she let go.

Spiderbait was having a complicated moment with the concept of goblins on the surface.

"I haven't been out here in my whole life," he announced to anyone who would listen, vibrating with the particular energy of someone who is absolutely about to make a terrible decision. "I'm just gonna go explore. It's totally safe for goblins, right? Nobody's gonna attack a goblin for being a goblin. We're totally integrated into the society."

"I don't think so," I said.

Daz agreed, in his careful way. Grygum was more specific.

"The state of goblin equality and inclusion is rough at the moment."

Spiderbait absorbed this. Recalibrated. Pivoted.

"Well, you know, I'm just a small goblin, and you're four big, powerful people. Do you mind if I just sort of hang out with you guys? Maybe find a nice place? This Candlekeep place seems like the kind of place someone like me could find a job doing something innocuous."

Grygum endorsed the plan. He endorses most plans that keep small things alive.

I looked back at the tunnel mouth — the last dark edge of where we'd been, the rock framing the light we'd come out into. Eldeth was already gathering herself, the set of her shoulders that meant moving, that meant west to Mithril Hall and the obligations that waited there.

Glabbagool was still looking at the sun.

West, she said. Head west and you'll hit Candlekeep. Simple as that, the way she says most things that aren't simple at all.

I watched her go.
