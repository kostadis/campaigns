# Chapter 52 Candlekeep: Trials, Truths, and Therapy

Date: May 11th, 2026

## Summary
The party arrived in Daggerford during their journey south toward Candlekeep, and the town greeted them with an unsettling quiet. The townsfolk moved carefully through the streets, offering no explanation for their wariness, and the group settled in for the night at the River Shining Tavern. Before they did, they passed through the village square and found a bronze cast of a severed forearm and hand mounted atop a stone pillar — a monument to Travis Ulayen, a paladin of Bahamut who had died fighting the Cult of the Dragon. A Dwarven inscription on the base read "The move is yours," mirroring a phrase tied to the legendary figure Stroud back in Waterdeep, and a smaller inscription on the back noted the monument had been witnessed and recorded by Milo Goodbarrel of the Order of the Gauntlet. The local village priest arrived soon after, selling small pewter reproductions of Travis's hand cast to raise donations, and explained that the monument had been funded by the Metalworkers Guild — possibly with Stroud-school sponsorship — though he himself declared proudly that he was a "pure Bahamutian."

That evening at the River Shining Tavern, a weaver named Meredith of the Ford approached the party with desperation in her eyes. Her seven-year-old daughter Elan had been silent for six days, drawing the same shape on every flat surface she could find, and speaking only in her sleep — saying that it was only the shape that held. The party attempted to examine the girl, and Dawnbringer, Thorin's sentient sun-sword, pompously declared she would bring the light to bear before performing a magical restoration. The cure was only partial: Elan spoke briefly, uttering the haunting words "The wells are not wells anymore," before falling silent once more. The party concluded that this was no ordinary disease — something was twisting the girl's mind from within, causing auditory hallucinations that no conventional healing could reach, and the madness of the Underdark appeared to be seeping into the surface world.

The road south brought the party to Beregost, the last meaningful stop before Candlekeep, where they took shelter at the Burning Wizard Inn. The gnome innkeeper, Festrun, recounted the inn's grim namesake — a wizard burned alive during a dragon cult uprising decades past — and pointed to a permanent soot mark on the south wall, refusing to paint over it and saying simply that the wall remembers. A traveling bard performed that evening, singing a four-verse drinking song about a Kenku thief who had learned to steal no more, and after Thorin pressed a silver piece into her hand, she sang a winding love song about Stroud and the famous game of chess that had shaped the world. Meanwhile, Daz noticed something deeply unsettling about a young human scholar nearby: the man was reciting a chant, and while the words were almost right, his breathing and cadence were wrong by a hair — the same eerie, syncopated pattern Daz had observed in a madman and a fiend war creature earlier in their journey. He quietly took note of the chant, intending to bring it to the scholars of Candlekeep. Thorin sat near the sea that night, contemplating how strangely life moved, while Zalthir climbed to the inn's roof to meditate and found himself curiously disappointed that nothing interrupted the peace.

The party arrived at Candlekeep the following morning, traveling along the Way of the Lion as the great fortress-library came into view from miles away along the cliffs above the Sea of Swords. As they drew closer, Daz noticed that the pressure behind his eyes had been growing steadily worse since leaving Beregost, intensifying with each step toward the keep. Inside, they were received by Bookwyrm, the First Reader — a dragonborn sage who had clearly heard every joke about her name and was entirely unbothered by it. She assigned each member of the party to a specific mentor within the library, but before she dismissed them, the Gate Warden Kowan Strongbench was summoned to address the matter of the shrunken House T'sarran spy currently residing in the party's bag of holding. Kowan explained that Candlekeep did not keep prisoners — they would hold her briefly in an anti-magic cell and then exile her at the gate with a warning — and left the decision of what to do with her entirely to the party.

The miniature drow spy was furious, demanding to be restored to her proper size and handed over to the Candlekeep guards. Glabbagool, the sentient gelatinous cube who had been serving as her jailer, cheerfully explained that she kept trying to kill him, so he would simply expand to trap her whenever she ran, and then attempt to teach her meditation — and that they had, in his estimation, become great friends. Zalthir confirmed that watching the prisoner was part of Glabbagool's pre-monk training, and the party ultimately decided to leave her in Glabbagool's many-eyed care rather than hand her over to the library's custody.

Zalthir was taken to the Athenaeum's history library, where he met Brother Khell-Vire, a 200-year-old half-elf who had left the Garden of Shadow eighty years ago after refusing an order and now wore a faded shadow monk robe beneath the avowed gray of Candlekeep. On Khell-Vire's desk lay a cryptic note reading "Zalthir, the underdark, until the Bone King sings," the meaning of which neither of them could explain. Khell-Vire sent Zalthir to the Trial of the Broken Mirror, where a blind half-elf monk named Master Kenshi waited to test him. Kenshi immediately criticized Zalthir's shadow stepping — noting that he skipped the third breath and let his energy bleed into his fingertips like a poison — but then paused and admitted that Zalthir had arrived at his destination three seconds faster than the grandmaster. Zalthir proceeded to fail every formal demonstration of skill that followed, stumbling through footwork and form until Kenshi turned to walk away in disappointment — at which point Zalthir landed a devastating strike on the departing monk that left Kenshi genuinely stunned, admitting that perhaps not all was lost.

Kenshi set one final challenge: Zalthir would shadow step to intercept and smash a thrown brick mid-air. Zalthir succeeded, shattering the brick completely, and Kenshi declared that while his forms were wrong in every scroll, his combination of shadow stepping and striking was something special — something that redeemed his lack of traditional skill in every other area. A sage of xenobiology wandered over to ask whether a gelatinous cube could truly achieve enlightenment, and Zalthir defended Glabbagool's intelligence and progress with conviction, noting that he was already walking and already tormenting the pocket drow. The party received a vial of resonant slime from the curious sage. Brother Khell-Vire ultimately acknowledged Zalthir's unconventional mastery and granted him access to a specialized shadow technique as a reward for passing the trial in his own unorthodox way.

Thorin was brought to Master Archivist Philemon, a clinical specialist in sentient artifacts who immediately began assessing Dawnbringer with the detached enthusiasm of a scholar examining a rare specimen. He identified her as a late Netherese-era artifact of sun worship origin that had suffered profound arcane stress from millennia of darkness, and noted with some surprise that the bond between Thorin and the blade was stronger than he had feared. When Philemon asked Dawnbringer about the wielder she had been found with, the sword faltered and went silent. Philemon gently reframed what the blade needed: not polishing, but the chance to remember what daylight felt like without needing to fight — and then, when she was ready, to grieve. He outlined a two-phase recovery process, beginning with the work of light, and Thorin found himself unexpectedly signed up for something that felt far more like therapy than archival research.

Daz was taken to the Hall of Divination, where spectral mage hands drifted through the air fetching tomes for researchers, and where Sister Yvenne — a meticulous and authoritative keeper of the public record — waited for him. He told her everything: the amnesia, the jail, the Underdark, the mercenaries trying to protect him and the ones trying to kill him, and the headache that had been growing worse since he arrived. Yvenne explained the brutal politics of Drow noble houses — the matron mothers, the rule of intrigue, the law that if even one member of a targeted house survived, the attacking house was forfeit and destroyed. She read him a list of houses struck from the record, and Daz noticed that the word "struck" was strange — it implied censorship, not elimination. When he pressed further, Yvenne admitted there was one house she could not speak of in that room, not because she refused, but because the public record was sealed and she was the public record. She told him that if he wanted what lay behind the seals, he would have to find other doors.

Digging deeper into a 200-year-old heraldry book, Daz discovered that an entire appendix had been mechanically removed with a razor at least fifty years ago — a deliberate and expensive erasure. A surviving footnote referenced the name "Dazan'in," which Yvenne explained was a softening name that mothers used for sons they hoped would survive, and that the houses which had used it most had not lasted. As Daz turned the implications over in his mind — the sealed records, the razored appendix, the mercenaries, the name — he began to sense that he was piecing together something far more dangerous than a forgotten past. Yvenne's careful silences and precise body language told him he was close to something she could not say aloud. The truth of who he was, and why someone had gone to such lengths to erase it, was waiting somewhere behind a door that Candlekeep itself could not open for him.
