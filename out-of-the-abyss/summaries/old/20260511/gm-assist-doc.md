# Chapter 51 Candlekeep: Trials, Truths, and Therapy 

## Thorin — Arrival in Daggerford

Daggerford was the first place we'd walked into in weeks where the silence didn't make sense.

We'd been on the road south out of the Silver Marches long enough that I'd stopped counting days and started counting how much my boots hurt. The surface had given us back the sky — I keep noting this, and I keep meaning it — but the sky in the Marches had been wide and cold and mostly empty of people. Daggerford had people. Daggerford had walls, and a market square, and smoke from cook fires, and the smell of horses. Normal things. Surface things.

And yet.

The hill giants I grew up with — they had a word for a silence that wasn't natural. Not the silence of snowfall or empty stone, but the silence of things not being said. The whole village had that. Townfolk walked around something none of them would point at. Nobody explained why. I watched a woman cross the road to avoid a conversation and then pretend she hadn't. Grygum noticed it too, I could tell, though he didn't say anything. Neither did I.

We found the River Shining Tavern and noted it for later. But we didn't go straight in, because Daz had already stopped in the square.

There was a monument there — a bronze-cast forearm, severed at the elbow, mounted on a stone pillar. Not holding anything. Just a hand. I stared at it for longer than I should have.

"Wait," I said. "Is it holding a weapon?"

It was not.

Zalthir looked at it the way he looks at most things: like he was deciding whether it constituted a threat. "This is art?" he said. "Like…"

He let the question sit where he'd put it. Fair question, honestly. It was a severed arm on a stick. The Underdark had less unsettling décor, and that's saying something.

Daz was already reading the inscription. The primary text told us this was Tarvis Ulain, paladin, dead three years. Died here, in Daggerford itself, breaking up a Cult of the Dragon recruitment operation. Grygum made a sound when he got to the part confirming Tarvis had served Bahamut — not a word, just the noise a person makes when something clicks into place. The war had touched everywhere in the North. I knew that the way you know a fact you haven't had to live through. Tarvis Ulain's bronze arm made it concrete.

Then Daz found the secondary inscription. Dwarven, on the base.

*The move is yours.*

"The move…" Daz said, working through it. "Oh, it's like, the chess… a reference to the chess thing."

Same line as the Castle Ward statue in Waterdeep. The one connected to Stroud. I read it twice because Dwarven is, you know, actually my language, or one of them, and I wanted to make sure I hadn't misread. I hadn't. Somebody had put a Stroudite inscription on a monument to a Bahamutian paladin in a town I'd barely heard of, and whoever commissioned it had either not noticed or not cared enough to remove it.

There was a smaller inscription on the back. Common. *Witnessed and recorded for the Gauntlet.* Signed by Milo Goodbarrel, apparently head of some publisher, apparently Order of the Gauntlet. Milo had personally signed off on this. The whole thing was funded two years after the war by a Metalworkers Guild. Grygum was quiet. Not the usual quiet. The kind where he's adding something to the list.

We made it to the inn eventually. The River Shining. A village priest caught us in the doorway — walking in very pleased with himself and carrying a tray of small pewter reproductions of Tarvis's hand cast, miniature things, charm-sized. He was selling them for donations to the Order of the Gauntlet, and he was clearly delighted with his own enterprise.

"Anybody want to buy a Tarvis hand cast in pewter?"

Grygum looked at them. "Are they full size?"

They were not.

The priest's eye landed on Grygum and lit up — another cleric of Bahamut, imagine that — and then he turned to take in the rest of us, and his expression did something complicated. A half-orc cleric of Bahamut. A Dragonborn. A dwarf. A drow. All walking in together.

"Kind of like the opening scene in a joke," Grygum said helpfully.

"And they all walk into the inn," he added.

The priest recovered fast. Probably has to, in his line of work. He pivoted and addressed the general group with renewed energy.

"Will any of you fair sirs be willing to buy one of these pewter hand casts?"

Grygum dropped a gold piece in for his. The priest beamed. Then Grygum asked him to tell him more — he'd already paid, so he had the leverage — and the priest was very willing.

The statue had been funded by the Metalworkers Guild, two years post-war. The guild may have had Stroudite members, the priest conceded, looking left, looking right, like the Stroudites might be listening. He himself was, he wanted us to know, *a pure Bahamutian*. He did not believe in the heresy.

"As am I," Grygum said. "But the inscription shares language with Stroud. Why is that?"

The priest considered this. "It was a gift," he said finally. "And since it was a gift, we decided the inscription could remain. After all — wasn't Tarvis trying to save us all, regardless of what actually happened in that game of chess?"

I didn't have an answer to that. I'm not sure anyone did. Grygum went back to examine the statue more carefully anyway, looking for anything else unusual. There wasn't much. Just a fairly normal monument, as monuments to severed arms go. Just one more marker of a war that had touched everything north of here in some way — dragons, cult raids, fire, pillage. Daggerford had been far enough south to miss the Uthgardt barbarian hordes that had swept through after the giant war. They'd only gotten the cult. Only lost Tarvis.

The priest moved on to find other customers. I stood in the doorway of the River Shining and thought about how many towns north of here had a story like this, and just hadn't put up a bronze arm about it yet.

## Grygum — The Silent Child of Maerith

Maerith of the Ford had the look of a woman who had already been turned away. You develop an eye for it, in this work — the specific exhaustion of someone who has walked into every room that was supposed to help and left each one carrying the same weight back out. She spotted my holy symbol during dinner, which is the usual trigger. People see the dragon-and-scale emblem and they think: well, at least this one won't say no before I finish asking.

She didn't say no to anything, in her own telling. She said yes. Yes, she'd watched Elin for six days. Yes, she'd noted the drawings — a six-pointed star, over and over, every flat surface in the house. Yes, she'd listened to her daughter talk in her sleep, because what else do you do. And yes, she'd been to the temples, plural, and the temples had no answer.

"My daughter Elin is 7 years old and has been silent for 6 days. She draws the same shape on every flat surface. She talks in her sleep. She says it is only the shape that holds. Please, we have asked the temples. The temples have no answer. Will you look at her?"

I thought about Stool. I couldn't help it. A creature that communicated through spores and rapport rather than words, who nonetheless had more to say than most creatures I'd met with full use of their tongues. Silence isn't absence. Seven years old and drawing the same thing on every flat surface is not nothing — it's insistence. She was trying to say something. The problem was we didn't have the vocabulary for it yet.

"Yes," I said. "I… I will, I will examine her."

Daz came along, which I appreciated. He has a precise mind and a habit of noticing the things that other people decide not to notice. Between the two of us, we ought to have been able to produce something useful.

We did not produce something useful.

Medicine examinations go badly in a particular way. Not dramatically. Just the quiet professional failure of looking at a patient and not seeing what you need to see. Daz's assistance helps, as a rule. The two minds catch more than one.

We still came up with essentially nothing.

"Yeah, I still only got a 10," I heard myself say. "Oops."

Which summarizes it. Elin was — and this was the clinical conclusion — not well. I could have told Maerith that before I sat down. Something was wrong with her daughter. The daughter was drawing the same pattern on every flat surface and had not spoken in six days. We had established that something was wrong. We had failed to establish what.

I was still turning this over when Thorin mentioned Dawnbringer could help. Dawnbringer did not wait for a second invitation. The sword has opinions about when to make an entrance, and this was apparently an occasion.

"Let me bring the light to you."

Very pompous. Even for Dawnbringer. Lesser restoration settled in.

And then she spoke.

"The wells are not wells anymore."

Then silence.

I wrote it down. It is what I do. But I held it for a moment first. Something in the voice was doing work the words weren't. The restoration hadn't fixed that part. The wells are not wells anymore. It was the kind of sentence that means more than it says, and doesn't tell you what the extra meaning is.

The restoration had cleared enough for her to say one thing. The thing she chose to say was that.

We worked through it. Not a disease — that was the first solid conclusion. Something was disrupting how the world reached her. Not an infection, not a conventional illness, not something with a clear Underdark vector. I asked about fungal involvement specifically, because I still think about Zuggtmoy, and will probably continue thinking about Zuggtmoy for the rest of my life.

"Is there any evidence of any sort of fungal infection? No. No. Okay. So it's not limited to fungus."

Which was both a relief and the opposite of a relief, because fungus I understand. Fungus has a mechanism. This was something else.

"Oh," I said, as something slid into place. "There's all the people — hearing voices?"

The answer was yes. The madness from the Underdark — the deep, structural wrongness we'd felt building around the demon lords, the way Neverlight Grove had been singing and the Pudding King's court had been dissolving and everything below a certain depth had been quietly coming apart — was not, apparently, staying below. It was reaching up. It was reaching Elin.

Zalthir articulated the mechanism: the input, the audio, the way the world was arriving at her rather than the world itself. And that was right. It wasn't a disease in the conventional sense. It was more like the world had started speaking a language she couldn't process, and the gap between what was arriving and what she could understand was where the drawings and the six days of silence lived.

"She's having hallucinations. Audio."

"And the hallucinations are not magic," came the reply. "They're something more profound than a lesser restoration can fix."

Right. Filed. That's the ceiling on what we can do tonight. Whatever was happening inside Elin was interior in a way that made it untouchable through conventional means. Not an exterior effect. I said it aloud, completing the thought someone else started.

"Not something that can be slapped."

"Or grappled."

Zalthir produced an alarm from somewhere, which I took as professional commentary.

Then Daz mentioned the pressure in his own head — had been mentioning it, in various indirect ways, for a while now. I looked at him. I looked at Elin. I thought about what we'd just established: that something from below was bleeding up, that it was affecting how people processed what reached them.

I sat with that question long enough to actually examine it properly.

"Does it seem related at all to how the other person was hearing voices?"

Twenty-three. My best of the evening, arriving too late to be medically useful and just in time to be philosophically concerning.

There was something there. Not the same thing, not a direct causal chain I could map and annotate and hand to Vareth as a neat theological puzzle. More like two things that had the same obscure author — a tenuous connection, the kind that suggests rather than demonstrates.

"There's some kind of tenuous connection there?"

"Yes. There's some kind of tenuous connection there."

I took notes. Elin couldn't speak and Daz had a headache and somewhere below us the demon lords were apparently still broadcasting, still finding new listeners, still rewriting the language of wells and shapes and held things into something the surface world hadn't been built to receive.

Bahamut may or may not have had a contingency plan for this. I filed it under open questions and kept writing.

## Daz — The Burning Wizard Inn

The Burning Wizard Inn took its name from a death. That was the first thing I catalogued.

A wizard, burned alive, dragon cult, decades prior. Festrum — the gnome innkeeper, old enough to have heard the story from the people who told it first — pointed to the south wall without prompting, the way a man points at something he has pointed at ten thousand times before. "That is where he fell. We scrub the floor every season. We do not paint over the wall. My grandfather said the wall remembers."

I noted the exits. Front door, obvious. Side passage near the kitchen, partially obscured by a stack of barrels. Stairs to the upper rooms. The window behind the performer's corner, narrow but workable in an emergency. The gnome himself, watching the room with the comfortable authority of someone who had survived thirty years of other people's bad decisions under his roof.

The city would have called this paranoia. The city would have been right. It never made the habit less useful.

There was a bard — a traveling one, by the look of her kit, set up in the performer's corner with a drum and a competent audience. She had a closing number she'd been doing all season. This one was called "The Kenku Could Not Fly." The whole inn came in on the chorus:

*"Oh, the Kenku could not fly, but he flew in his black hood, in his black hood, in his black hood. The raven thief who learned to steal no more, save what was needed, said the small, wise man at his side."*

Grygum, who has an irritating habit of noticing things I would have preferred to notice first, observed drily that the song's principle — steal what was needed — mapped rather well onto Whistler. The Kenku thief of some earlier age who, presented with the complete hoard of Tiamat, had apparently taken only what was necessary for legitimate expenses. Operating goals. Day-to-day funds. Per diems.

I found that, I mean, genuinely impressive. The restraint of it. Most people, faced with the complete hoard of Tiamat, would either take everything or panic and take nothing. Whistler had apparently made a line-item budget.

After her set, the bard invited requests. Grygum leaned forward — he has a scholar's interest in Stroud that he performs slightly harder than the interest actually requires — and offered her a silver piece for something about Stroud's famous chess game. She looked at his holy symbol and asked whether he was Bahamutian or Stroudite, and he explained that he was Bahamutian, with sentimentality for the other. She took the coin and proceeded to sing a love song. Involving flowers. And drinking. The chess game appeared as a metaphor for romantic longing, the grand theological stakes filed down into something a farmhand could hum on the way home.

Grygum received this in the way he receives most things downgraded by translation: politely, and with the file already updated. "The only love I remember during the previous campaign," he said, "involved giants, and it wasn't Stroud's." He paused. "These things have to be done with a grain of salt."

I had been cataloguing the room during all of this, which is not something I do consciously anymore. It is just what happens when I sit somewhere new: faces, patterns, the small deviations from baseline behavior. There was a young human scholar in the far corner — not a human scholar, a *young* human scholar, because those are meaningfully different things and I did not want to get the category wrong — who was reciting something under his breath. A chant of some kind. Rhythmic, almost liturgical.

I will not call what I felt next a headache. I will call it the specific cognitive friction of almost-recognition — something pressing at the edge of attention that wants to be resolved.

I had been attributing the sensation to sunlight. I had, in fact, been attributing it to sunlight for most of the day. It has been a while since I have been outside in the sun this long.

I watched the scholar. His breathing was almost right. The pauses fell almost correctly. The pattern was almost the one it was supposed to be — and wrong by a hair in a way that took actual knowledge of the correct version to notice. I know that chant. I know it well enough to know what he was doing wrong, and the specific wrongness of it — the way he syncopated his breath against the syllables, or tried to — was not a beginner's error. It was a very particular kind of degradation.

I have seen it before. Twice. Eldred had it, earlier in the journey — the man who went insane. The Field Ward Preacher had it too.

The same pattern. The same wrongness by a hair.

I wrote it down. The exact cadence, the placement of the false syncopation, the point in the verse where the chant bent away from the original. Candlekeep would have records. Someone there would know whether this pattern belonged to a group, a practice, a doctrine — or whether it was something else being made to look like one.

"Yeah, sure," I said, and closed my journal.

Thorin spent the evening outside with Dawnbringer, standing at what I understand was his first real encounter with the sea. Dawnbringer, predictably, lit up everything around them, which defeated the purpose of going outside to look at the sea. Thorin apparently found this peaceful rather than counterproductive, which I note without judgment. He came back in with the settled look of a man who had had a thought that satisfied him. "Life takes you in weird directions and weird places," he said. "You just gotta go with it."

I have been thinking about that sentence since he said it. I have not yet decided whether it is wisdom or resignation. Possibly there is not much difference.

Zalthir climbed to the roof to meditate. He came back down later, settled. The expression of someone closing an internal argument, not opening an external one. He said "Hmm, true," about something, in the tone of a man completing an internal argument rather than beginning an external one.

I did not ask what the argument was. Some accounts balance themselves.

I sat with the inn a little longer before going up. Festrum wiped the bar. The soot mark on the south wall sat where it always sat. The floor, scrubbed every season.

The wall remembers.

I find I understand that better than I would like to.

## Zalthir — Arrival at Candlekeep and the Prisoner Question

The Way of the Lion runs along cliff edges above the Sea of Swords, and Candlekeep is visible from miles out — a dark stack of towers against the sky, impossible to mistake. I'd seen it described in documents we'd carried out of the Underdark. Seeing it in person was different. It sat there. Centuries do that to a building.

Daz went quiet beside me somewhere around the second hour of walking. Not his usual quiet. His hand went to his temple and stayed there.

I noticed. I didn't say anything yet.

Grygum noticed too, and asked whether it connected to the voices — the spreading madness we'd watched move through the Underdark like groundwater through stone. He couldn't pin it down, he said. Something wrong, something tenuous. I filed it. Whatever was happening to Daz had started below ground and apparently had not stopped at the surface. One more thing that needed answering. One more thing Candlekeep was presumably for.

We gave them the books at the gate. Bookwyrm — the First Reader, a dragonborn sage — assigned us each to a handler without ceremony. She had clearly been doing this for a long time and had no interest in making it interesting. Daz to Sister Yvenne. Zalthir to Brother  Khell-Vire. Thorin to Master Archivist Philemon. Grygum to Vareth. She said it the way you'd read a duty roster. I respected that.

Then she paused.

She'd noticed the bag.

Specifically, she'd noticed what was in the bag — because when we'd come through the gate, we'd had to open it to show the guards Glabbagool, and the miniaturized House T'sarran spy had been right there alongside him, which she apparently found unacceptable, and which Grygum summarized, with admirable brevity, as: *"We've been caught."*

They summoned someone called  Kalan Strongbranch. Gate Warden. He was efficient about it. He laid out Candlekeep's position without apology:

"The bell tower has cells. Comfortable, localized anti-magic. They are very rarely used. Anyone we find guilty of a major offense is exiled rather than imprisoned. Which is to say, we hold prisoners. We do not keep them. If you give her to us, what you are giving us is a delay. We will not deliver to House T'sarran, and we will not execute her. Eventually, we will release her at our gate with a warning. If you need something else, you will need to do it yourselves."

He said it plainly, which I appreciated. A delay. That was the offer. Release her into Faerûn eventually, with a polite warning to behave.

There was a class at the monastery — I think it was Brother Ferrath, or possibly Brother Aldas, I'm genuinely uncertain — on the difference between solutions and postponements. I remember nothing else from that class. I remember wanting to be in the training hall.

I have thought about it perhaps four times since, at moments exactly like this one.

The spy, for her part, was furious. She turned to Kalan and announced that she was a prisoner and wished to be formally jailed by Candlekeep's guards. She delivered this the way someone delivers a thing they have never had to argue for. The First Reader looked at her with something that might have been sympathy and explained that as a guest of Candlekeep — as a member, technically, of our adventuring party — the decision was ours, not hers.

The spy did not take this well. She had opinions. Several of them.

Thorin, who processes things by talking, asked whether Glabbagool had thoughts on the matter. The party had, apparently, collectively soft-focused on the fact that we were carrying both a grey ooze and a miniaturized House T'sarran operative in the same bag, and that this arrangement had been continuing for days.

Glabbagool was glad to be asked.

He'd been doing prison-watching, he explained, and was kind of bored. But the spy was not without entertainment value. She kept trying to kill him. He'd slap her around. She'd try to run. He'd expand. She'd scream. He'd try to explain the way of the shadow and teach her meditation. She'd scream about the meditation. Then they'd talk. He said they'd become great friends.

Thorin observed that Glabbagool seemed ambivalent.

Glabbagool considered this. Then he turned — and here I want to be precise about what happened, because it mattered to me — Glabbagool paused, and asked me directly:

"Zalthir? Will I be more of a monk or less of a monk by watching her? Is this some kind of pre-monk training you've put me in?"

"Absolutely," I said.

It was. Patience under provocation. Maintaining stillness when the environment is actively hostile. Responding to aggression without being ruled by it. If anything, the spy was giving him a better curriculum than most of what I'd sat through.

Glabbagool took this with complete seriousness. The work counted; he had been told so. He drew himself up, or the ooze equivalent of it.

"I will keep an eye on her!"

Six pseudo-eyes fixed on the spy simultaneously.

"Keep at it, buddy," I said.

The spy was not happy. The decision was made. We kept her.

## Grygum — Zalthir's Trial of the Broken Mirror

The Echoing Hall earned its name. Stone corridors in Candlekeep's north inner ward have a habit of returning sound to you slightly altered — not an echo exactly, more like the library itself was taking notes. I approved of that.

Zalthir had been directed to the lower-gallery office, where the Shadow Discipline Lineage Records were kept, and where a man named Khell-Vire apparently lived among them. Two hundred years old, half-elven, eighty years gone from the Garden of Shadow after refusing an order he never explained. He wore a faded shadow monk under-robe beneath the Candlekeep gray, which told me more about him than the records would have. You don't stop dressing like what you were. You just layer something else over it.

I stayed back. This was Zalthir's work, and I had enough respect for the architecture of the thing to let it proceed without commentary.

The note was the first strange piece. On Khell-Vire's desk, visible from where I was standing: *Zalthir, the Underdark, until the Bone King sings.* Zalthir stared at it. Khell-Vire stared at it. Neither of them could place it. I filed it under "questions to revisit" and did not volunteer an opinion, because I didn't have one.

Then Khell-Vire opened with the question.

"What were you sent toward? Not why were you sent here?"

Zalthir's response was immediate and flat: "Granted, given that constraint or specification, can you clarify?"

Khell-Vire looked at him. Zalthir looked at Khell-Vire.

"I've played this game before, my friends," Zalthir said, which was directed at no one in particular, or possibly at us, or possibly at the entire tradition of shadow monk pedagogy stretching back five centuries. Hard to say.

Khell-Vire tried again. "Perhaps if I asked it differently — does this phrase mean anything to you that you have lived through that resonates with the phrase?"

"Not particularly."

"Is it an entity? An event? A person?"

"That's precisely what I'm asking you."

"Yes," Khell-Vire said, patient as stone, "but this question is for you, not for me."

I found this genuinely interesting. Not the answer — there wasn't one — but the structure of the question. Khell-Vire wasn't asking what Zalthir knew. He was asking what Zalthir had *become* through what he'd lived. That's a different inquiry. You can look up what you know. What you've become is not in the records.

Zalthir, to his credit, didn't pretend to find it profound. He found it annoying, in the specific way that all those shadow monk classes he'd ignored had been annoying, which told its own story.

Then Khell-Vire set the terms clearly: "There are monks here in the inner ward who have read everything and fought nothing. They will want to test you. Let them. You will learn what you do not see, and they will learn what cannot be read. I will not be there. This is your work, not mine."

A fair division of labor, I thought. Also an honest one. He wasn't offering guidance. He was offering a door and declining to walk through it with you.

The blind monk entered without introduction and said: "Give me a standard shadow step."

Zalthir shadow stepped.

The monk did not wait. "You skip the third breath. You don't enter your ki in the hara. You let it bleed into your fingertips like a poison. It is technically wrong in every scroll."

Then he paused.

"And yet, you arrived at your destination three seconds faster than the Grandmaster did. Show me what else you haven't learned."

I wanted to write that sentence down. *Show me what else you haven't learned.* That's the whole problem with formal assessment of unconventional people — the rubric was built around what you're supposed to know, and Zalthir had never particularly cared what he was supposed to know. He'd cared about what worked. There's a case to be made that those are the same thing. There's also a case that they're almost never the same thing. Both cases can be in the notes.

Zalthir asked whether he could punch the monk.

I don't know exactly how I expected the trial to proceed, but I should have expected this.

He could. He did. The sparring match that followed was, by all observation, evenly balanced — Zalthir could have pushed it further if he'd committed, but the monk was also navigating without sight, which was its own variable. They took each other's measure. That part went fine.

Then came the formal demonstration. Three techniques: the Weightless Shadow, the Efficient Strike, the Eldritch Claw. The monk had the rubric. Zalthir had to execute against it.

He chose acrobatics. The Weightless Shadow.

He rolled catastrophically. He fell. The monk watched him fall — insofar as a blind man watches anything — and said, without apparent emotion: "You have learned nothing."

Zalthir's internal calculus was already doing the math on whether this could get worse. It could. He tried the Efficient Strike. Athletics. Another catastrophe. The monk sighed: "The corridor was narrow. The form was irrelevant. Did you not practice your forms while traveling?"

He had not practiced his forms while traveling. He had survived the Underdark, which is a different curriculum.

There was a brief negotiation about alternatives — bribes, a pet spider, various exit routes — all of which closed off with the same finality. The monk was blind, so the spider gambit was dead on arrival. Zalthir tried stealth for the Eldritch Claw. His number landed somewhere that would have been insufficient even with generous interpretation. The monk sighed again and walked away.

Thorin, from the side: "Can he attack him while he's walking away? Blindside him, as it were?"

Zalthir: "I mean, I rolled a 30 to hit. Does that get me anything?"

A pause.

"You literally rolled a 30 to hit?"

"Yes. This character is optimized for combat, not skills."

The monk stopped walking. "Hmm. Perhaps not all is lost." He was, by every available indication, genuinely impressed. Apparently no one had hit him that hard in a long time. He stood there for a moment, something recalibrating behind his blindness, and said — almost to himself: "Perhaps I've been approaching this the wrong way."

This is the thing about assessment rubrics. They tell you what the assessor values. They don't always tell you what the subject is capable of. Stroud couldn't play chess. He rolled a natural 20. Four hundred years of preparation, and nobody had accounted for the person who didn't know the rules.

The monk set a final challenge. He would throw a brick. Zalthir would shadow step to intercept it and strike it mid-air. The brick was thrown.

Zalthir rolled a 29 to hit.

The brick did not survive. The monk observed the results — the pieces, the completely unprecedented form, the continued skipping of breaths, the continued absence of maneuvers — and said: "The results do speak for themselves."

He asked whether there was anything else. Whether any lesson from Zalthir's training might be offered, any insight, anything at all.

Zalthir offered the shadow tentacles. The eldritch tattoo. "I mean, I learned how to grow shadow tentacles."

The monk looked at him the way teachers look at students who have just produced the wrong evidence for the right conclusion. Then he said the way forward was in the combination.

It was around this point that a sage of xenobiology approached, apparently drawn by the news of a gelatinous cube with monastic ambitions.

"Is it true you are teaching a gelatinous cube the way of the shadow?"

"Indeed."

"Can a mindless digestive system achieve—"

Zalthir cut him off. "Wait, wait! She's by no means mindless — she's a student. He's learning the ways of the monk. He's already walking. He's already torturing this poor drow. He's doing well."

Thorin, helpfully: "Pocket Drow."

The sage, apparently satisfied by this defense, produced a vial of Resonant Slime. Glabbagool was very pleased. I noted with some satisfaction that we had not brought a gelatinous cube this far — through the Underdark, through Waterdeep, into the libraries of Candlekeep — to have him dismissed as a mindless digestive system before lunch.

The final demonstration came when Zalthir recalled something from the Underdark — a moment where he'd found a way to punch *through* the shadows themselves, precise as a surgical cut. He proposed showing that. He warned the blind monk, then executed it. His strike landed at a number that left no ambiguity.

Khell-Vire raised an eyebrow.

"Although your skills are weak," he said — and paused — "clearly showing a lack of focus and training." Another pause. "There is something special about how you hit. The way of the Shadow is not about hitting. It is about discrete movement. And it is your combination of the shadow step and the hit that redeems your lack of skills in all other possible areas."

Then: "You are beginning to remember, I think, all those shadow monks that drove you nuts."

"Yeah," Zalthir said. "Yeah, definitely."

Khell-Vire had read the situation correctly. Zalthir found the manual aggravating, ignored it, and built something better. He was now being told that the thing he'd built was the most interesting thing about him. The manual was still wrong about him. But what he'd built in spite of it was genuinely his.

Khell-Vire announced the award of a specialized shadow technique — three options, presented carefully, with the honest note that adjustments might be needed if any proved mechanically unfortunate. Zalthir chose option C.

I filed it under "Zalthir: things that are not in the records but will be."

## Thorin — Therapy for a Sentient Blade

The appointment was Daz's idea, technically, which meant it was my idea that Daz had first. Someone had to book it. Dawnbringer had been through things, and I wasn't in a position to help with the parts I couldn't name.

Master Archivist Philemon's study was small and smelled like old ink and something faintly mineral, which I appreciated. He was an older man — human, which meant he'd spent most of his career reading about things that had outlived him by centuries. He had the look of someone who found that professionally satisfying. He came around from behind his desk when we entered. His eyes went to the sword before they went to me.

"This is a remarkable specimen," he said. "Late Netherese-era recovery, sun-worship origin, sustained darkness exposure of approximately three to four thousand years." He had a small clay tablet ready. He was already writing.

I stopped him there.

"What do you mean, specimen? This is my friend Dawnbringer."

He looked up. There was a pause. The kind where someone resets the question in their head before they say the next thing.

The pronoun question had been sitting with me for a while, and Philemon's reframe gave it room to come up. I tried to explain. "I don't know what to call her, but, you know, whether it's them? Is it her? He, her?" I looked at the sword. I'd been going back and forth on it since Waterdeep. "Yeah, I don't know what to call them, but, you know, it's like — we've gotten to know each other pretty well over, you know, the last stressful ordeals."

"Oh, I see that this is your friend," Philemon said, and he meant it differently than he'd meant anything before. He set the tablet down. "Oh, well — this is interesting. I wasn't expecting this. I'm seeing a better bond between the two of you than I had originally feared."

That landed somewhere I hadn't expected. I hadn't been told to be relieved by that. I was anyway.

I asked him to back up, though, because the phrase he'd used earlier was still sitting with me. "What do you mean, like, stress — what did you say, stress levels, or—"

He explained it carefully. He hit a spot in the middle where the words he had didn't quite carry the thing he was reaching for, and he slowed down. Entities like Dawnbringer, he said — sentient items — experience a kind of deep unease when they spend long periods in darkness, in disuse, in the possession of someone who doesn't really see them. Treasure hoards were the clinical example. Millennia of being an artifact rather than a being. It leaves marks.

"I mean, Dawnbringer does have a hard time with darkness," I said. "And so that's something that we've been struggling with as a pair."

He nodded like he'd already suspected this. He turned to address Dawnbringer directly then, which I appreciated — not everyone does that. Most people talk around the sword, which she notices.

He asked about the previous wielder. The corpse she had been found in, he said. Whether that person had been — had been her wielder.

Dawnbringer went quiet.

Not the silence of someone thinking. The other kind. The kind where the thing you'd been not-saying for a long time just gets asked out loud in a room with other people in it.

I said, "The rebound relationship, I get it, okay."

The words were out before the rest of me caught up. I heard them, and I walked it back immediately: "No, no, it's okay — I'm being flippant. This is a serious topic."

"I understand," Philemon said. He wasn't offended. He'd probably heard worse.

The thing I didn't say out loud was that I understood the impulse behind the joke. The giants who raised me had a saying that translated roughly as: the ground under a joker is the same ground everyone else is standing on. You laugh when the weight gets too specific to name. I'd been doing it since Velkynvelve. Old habit.

Philemon let the silence hold for another moment, and then he did something I hadn't anticipated. He started to reach for a prescription and caught himself mid-sentence.

"Your sword does not need polishing." A pause. He actually looked at her — looked at the blade properly. "She can't be polished, actually." He reset entirely. "Your sword — she needs to remember what daylight feels like without needing to fight. And then, when she's ready, and you are willing, she needs to grieve. The first work is light. The second work is mourning. We will start with the first."

I thought about the months in the Underdark. Total dark. Carrying a sunblade through a place that had no sun. What that had cost her — I knew some of it, and I probably didn't know most of it. We'd done the healing work in Waterdeep on the road south, found orphans and the sick and let her do what she was built to do. She'd been visibly happy. That was real. But happy doesn't close everything.

Philemon confirmed we were enrolled in a two-stage process, and I accepted this the way I accept most things I've already decided to do: by not arguing about it.

"We're all gonna grow out of this," I said. "Hopefully, Dawnbringer grows a plus one."

It was a lighter note to end on than the session probably warranted. But Dawnbringer had been through enough heavy ones already. I figured she could have this one.

## Daz — The Sealed Records of Menzoberranzan

The Hall of Divination smells like old stone and something faintly electric — the residue of spells cast so many times in one room that the air itself has absorbed the habit. Spectral mage hands drift between the shelves, fetching tomes for researchers who don't look up. The tomes move past me in slow, purposeful arcs, and I find myself watching them the way I watch everything I don't yet understand: cataloguing, waiting.

My head had been complaining since we arrived at Candlekeep. The proximity — to the knowledge, or to whatever sat underneath the knowledge — was producing a friction I had been mis-attributing to the sun all day. So when the first tome swept past me, my first thought was not *what knowledge does this contain* but something considerably less dignified.

"Can I see or read any of… do any of them… I'm… at this point, my headache… my headache's been bothering me. Is there anything on any of the tomes that might help me with my headache?"

Sister Yvenne looked at me with the particular expression of someone who has spent decades managing scholars and has nevertheless not encountered this exact request.

"Well, is this the question you wish to have answered?"

I will say this for Candlekeep: they have a policy. A clean one. Information flows both ways across the counter. No ledger gets updated without a corresponding entry on the other side. I find this philosophically admirable and professionally reassuring.

"Can I only get one question answered? I'm an inquisitive person, I have many questions."

She paused. Allowed the observation to settle. Then said, with the patience of someone who has been doing this for a long time, that perhaps we could get to my headache after we addressed the primary purpose of the inquiry.

"I'm in… I'm in the middle of an inquiry."

She waited.

She had, I realized, all the time in the world. This is the advantage of working in a library. Time is not a resource you run short of when your colleagues are ghosts.

She explained the arrangement: when I asked an Avowed for information, I was also expected to provide it. Information for information. Which is, when you strip away the decorum, exactly how Menzoberranzan works. They just don't use words like *reciprocal exchange*. They use knives.

"Do I need to provide some information?"

She confirmed that I did.

There are several ways to handle this situation. The first is to give nothing and get nothing. The second is to give a carefully constructed partial truth, calibrated to yield exactly the information you want without exposing anything useful. The third — and this is the one I would not have considered two years ago, before I woke up in a jail cell with no memories and a headache that apparently never left — is to simply tell the truth.

I told her the truth.

"I have a headache, but I'll tell you the story. This is the short version. I don't have much memory before being locked up with a few people in a jail. Because some spider thing was, like, trying to keep us. And we've made an escape. We were in the Underdark. We've had many, many adventures, yada yada yada. Now I'm here in Candlekeep. My head hurts a little bit, and I don't know who I am, or my history, and I don't know why my head hurts."

I let that sit for a moment.

"That's the information I have to share."

"Wow," she said, "that's quite a bit."

"Well, I mean, I skipped some, yadda yadda."

She was kind of impressed. She was also, visibly, confused — the confusion of someone who has been told to expect a certain kind of creature and has received a different one. I am a drow. Drow do not simply hand over everything they know to a stranger in a library in exchange for nothing yet delivered. The whole culture is structured around the exact opposite of that.

I understood the confusion. The honest answer was that I wanted help and this was a library and librarians are supposed to be helpful. I was aware this reasoning would not fully satisfy a scholar of drow politics. She probably thought I was lying.

I wasn't. I just couldn't afford the usual approach. When you don't know what you're hiding, you can't protect it. Better to lay everything out and watch what someone else picks up.

She decided to trust the disclosure enough to begin. She explained the structure of the noble houses — matron mothers at the head of each, power flowing upward, loyalty transactional at every level. The rule of intrigue: betrayal is rewarded if it strengthens the web. Advance through maneuver. The city selects for it.

I knew most of this. Not as facts retrieved from memory, but as grammar. The language you learn to think in before you learn to question it.

Then she said the thing that matters.

Whenever a house sought to destroy another house, it had to destroy everyone. If you left a single person alive — even a child, even someone inconvenient and useless — your house was forfeit. The wider community would come for you. They would reduce you to a black puddle and call the matter settled.

She told me the story of one such house. They had tried to wipe out another, and failed to account for one survivor. A child. A blithering child idiot, she said, and I noted the language was hers, not mine.

I asked the logical question.

"Okay, what happened to the blithering idiot?"

"Unclear," she said.

I asked the second logical question.

"Am I the blithering idiot?"

She did not answer that directly. She moved instead to her list.

The first thing Menzoberranzan trains you for is the ledger. Who owes whom, who knows what, who has already lost and hasn't noticed. I have been running that audit unconsciously since before I could name it. The second thing Menzoberranzan trains you for — and this one is equally useful but considerably less comfortable — is to notice when a word doesn't fit.

She read me the list. The second seat. The fourth seat struck. The eleventh seat. The twenty-third seat.

*Struck.*

Something in me landed on it before I could explain why. The other entries were eliminations, extinctions, houses reduced to absence. But *struck* is not that. Struck is what you do to a record when you want it gone but it won't cooperate with being gone. Struck is what someone paid for, when they wanted an entry moved out of the column it belonged in.

"Struck," I said. "Didn't mean eliminated. It means they struck it, but maybe they didn't eliminate it, and therefore, like, it didn't happen? Is that why I feel like it's different?"

She confirmed I was asking the right question.

Then she told me there was one house she could not speak of in the room. Not because she would not — because the public record is sealed, and she is today the public record.

"If you want what is behind the seals, you will have to want it through other doors."

I asked if she could point me to the other doors.

She could not.

But the room itself had already told me something. This is a library with, by reputation, all the information in the world. Something is not here that should be here. Someone did something to a place whose entire purpose is preservation. That is not a small act. That is a very large, very expensive, very deliberate act.

"Alright," I said, "so somebody powerful has manipulated the world database library."

Yes. That is precisely what had happened.

She handed me the document with the full list of houses. The word *struck* appeared again. She mentioned, almost as an aside, that the fourth seat holds ritual archive privileges in Menzoberranzan — that having archive privileges means having the legal apparatus to access and alter records. That a struck fourth seat was not merely a political erasure. It was the expensive kind. The kind that requires knowing the right people and paying the right price and having the infrastructure to make the payment look like it was never made.

I held that for a moment.

"So somebody paid a lot of money to erase some stuff. I don't know my history. We have mercenaries following me around, trying to make sure I live, and other mercenaries following me around, trying to kill me. So, perhaps, I am associated with some expensive striking."

She confirmed it. Yes. That was the correct analysis.

She also gave me a book. A heraldry book: *Geography and Cities, Menzoberranzan, Pillars of Woe*. Two hundred years old, give or take. I opened it the way I open anything I'm not sure I trust — slowly, looking for the places where it doesn't quite sit right.

I wanted something that would trigger the headache, or ease it. A name. A cadence. The particular rhythm of a language I spoke before I had words for what I was speaking. I wanted one of those moments from the books I've read where a displaced person touches an object from their past and something slides back into place.

I found a footnote instead.

"I gotta tell you, the first line of this, where it says, *Daz'issin*, and lit., *small shadow*, made me feel like I was reading, like, millennial shorthand. And that I didn't understand it."

The footnote referenced an appendix.

The appendix was missing.

I looked at the cut. It was mechanical. A razor. Not age, not water damage, not the slow forgetting of paper that has been handled too many times. A razor, at least fifty years ago, someone had taken to this book and removed what the footnote pointed to.

So: a name. A softened name, a diminutive, a name ending in *-issin* the way you'd end a name you wanted to feel smaller than it was. A footnote referencing something gone. And the name is Daz'issin. Small shadow. And I am, as far as I know, called Daz.

"Well, isn't my name, Daz? Maybe it's my full name?"

I asked her directly who this Daz'issin was.

She told me it was a softening name. That mothers use it for boys they wanted to live. That there were not many uses of it in the past three centuries. And that the houses that had used it, most of them, did not last.

I asked if she knew my name.

She did.

I asked whether someone might call me Daz, and this was actually a name that, like, my mother used.

She redirected. Let us turn to your patron, she said. Did you not come here to learn who protected you?

"I do want to know who protected me, that would be useful, yeah."

She paused. Thought. Then: it is possible your name is related. But it is unlikely. The houses that had those names have all gone.

I looked at her. At the way she was sitting. At the way she had been sitting for the last several minutes — the practiced stillness of someone who knows more than the public record allows her to say, maintaining the pose of a person who is simply informing rather than carefully navigating.

Menzoberranzan teaches you to read rooms. It teaches you to watch for the moment when someone's body stops matching their words. Yvenne's body had been disagreeing with her words for the last five minutes.

"But what if they weren't successfully all gone?"

She said nothing.

Which is, in the ledger, an answer.
