The appointment was Daz's idea, technically, which meant it was my idea that Daz had first. Someone had to book it. Dawnbringer had been through things, and I wasn't in a position to help with the parts I couldn't name.

Master Archivist Philemon's study was small and smelled like old ink and something faintly mineral, which I appreciated. He was an older man — human, which meant he'd spent most of his career reading about things that had outlived him by centuries. He had the look of someone who found that professionally satisfying. He came around from behind his desk when we entered, and his eyes went straight to the sword at my hip the way a healer's eyes go straight to a wound.

"This is a remarkable specimen," he said. "Late Netherese-era recovery, sudden worship origins, sustained darkness exposure of approximately three to four thousand years." He had a small clay tablet ready. He was already writing.

I stopped him there.

"What do you mean, specimen? This is my friend Dawnbringer."

He looked up. There was a pause — the kind where someone recalibrates something they thought they already had figured out.

The pronoun question had been sitting with me for a while, and Philemon's reframe gave it room to come up. I tried to explain. "I don't know what to call her, but, you know, whether it's them? Is it her? He, her?" I looked at the sword. I'd been going back and forth on it since Waterdeep. "Yeah, I don't know what to call them, but, you know, it's like — we've gotten to know each other pretty well over, you know, the last stressful ordeals."

"Oh, I see that this is your friend," Philemon said, and he meant it differently than he'd meant anything before. He set the tablet down. "Oh, well — this is interesting. I wasn't expecting this. I'm seeing a better bond between the two of you than I had originally feared."

That landed somewhere I hadn't expected. I hadn't been told to be relieved by that. I was anyway.

I asked him to back up, though, because the phrase he'd used earlier was still sitting with me. "What do you mean, like, stress — what did you say, stress levels, or—"

He explained it the way scholars explain things: carefully, and with a slight wobble in the middle where the concept doesn't quite fit the words available. Entities like Dawnbringer, he said — sentient items — experience a kind of deep unease when they spend long periods in darkness, in disuse, in the possession of someone who doesn't really see them. Treasure hoards were the clinical example. Millennia of being an artifact rather than a being. It leaves marks.

"I mean, Dawnbringer does have a hard time with darkness," I said. "And so that's something that we've been struggling with as a pair."

He nodded like he'd already suspected this. He turned to address Dawnbringer directly then, which I appreciated — not everyone does that. Most people talk around the sword, which she notices.

He asked about the previous wielder. The corpse she had been found in, he said. Whether that person had been — had been her wielder.

Dawnbringer went quiet.

Not the silence of someone thinking. The other kind. The kind where the thing you'd been not-saying for a long time just gets asked out loud in a room with other people in it.

I said, "The rebound relationship, I get it, okay."

The words were out before the rest of me caught up. I heard them, and I walked it back immediately: "No, no, it's okay — I'm being flippant. This is a serious topic."

"I understand," Philemon said. He wasn't offended. He'd probably heard worse.

The thing I didn't say out loud was that I understood the impulse behind the joke. The giants who raised me had a saying that translated roughly as: the ground under a joker is the same ground everyone else is standing on. You laugh when the weight gets too specific to name. I'd been doing it since Velkynvelve. Old habit.

Philemon let the silence hold for another moment, and then he did something I hadn't anticipated. He started to reach for a prescription and caught himself mid-sentence.

"Your sword does not need polishing." A pause. He actually looked at her — looked at the blade properly. "She can't be polished, actually." He reset entirely. "Your sword — she needs to remember what daylight feels like without needing to fight. And then, when she's ready, and you are willing, she needs to grieve. The first work is light. The second work is morning. We will start with the first."

I thought about the months in the Underdark. Total dark. Carrying a sunblade through a place that had no sun. What that had cost her — I knew some of it, and I probably didn't know most of it. We'd done the healing work in Waterdeep on the road south, found orphans and the sick and let her do what she was built to do. She'd been visibly happy. That was real. But happy doesn't close everything.

Philemon confirmed we were enrolled in a two-stage process, and I accepted this the way I accept most things I've already decided to do: by not arguing about it.

"We're all gonna grow out of this," I said. "Hopefully, Dawnbringer grows a plus one."

It was a lighter note to end on than the session probably warranted. But Dawnbringer had been through enough heavy ones already. I figured she could have this one.
