The Echoing Hall earned its name. Stone corridors in Candlekeep's north inner ward have a habit of returning sound to you slightly altered — not an echo exactly, more like the library itself was taking notes. I approved of that.

Zalthir had been directed to Calvier's lower gallery, where the Shadow Discipline Lineage Records were kept, and where a man named Khell-Vire apparently lived among them. Two hundred years old, half-elven, eighty years gone from the Garden of Shadow after refusing an order he never explained. He wore a faded shadow monk under-robe beneath the Candlekeep gray, which told me more about him than the records would have. You don't stop dressing like what you were. You just layer something else over it.

I stayed back. This was Zalthir's work, and I had enough respect for the architecture of the thing to let it proceed without commentary.

The note was the first strange piece. On Khell-Vire's desk, visible from where I was standing: *Zalthir, the Underdark, until the Bone King sings.* Zalthir stared at it. Khell-Vire stared at it. Neither of them could place it. I filed it under "questions to revisit" and did not volunteer an opinion, because I didn't have one.

Then Khell-Vire opened with the question.

"What were you sent toward? Not why were you sent here?"

Zalthir's response was immediate and flat: "Granted, given that constraint or specification, can you clarify?"

Khell-Vire looked at him. Zalthir looked at Khell-Vire.

"I've played this game before, my friends," Zalthir said, which was directed at no one in particular, or possibly at us, or possibly at the entire tradition of shadow monk pedagogy stretching back five centuries. Hard to say.

Khell-Vire tried again. "Perhaps if I asked it differently — does this phrase mean anything to you that you have lived through that resonates with the phrase?"

"Not particularly."

"Is it an entity? An event? A person?"

"That's precisely what I'm asking you."

"Yes," Khell-Vire said, patient as stone, "but this question is for you, not for me."

I found this genuinely interesting. Not the answer — there wasn't one — but the structure of the question. Khell-Vire wasn't asking what Zalthir knew. He was asking what Zalthir had *become* through what he'd lived. That's a different inquiry. You can look up what you know. What you've become is not in the records.

Zalthir, to his credit, didn't pretend to find it profound. He found it annoying, in the specific way that all those shadow monk classes he'd ignored had been annoying, which told its own story.

Then Khell-Vire set the terms clearly: "There are monks here in the inner ward who have read everything and fought nothing. They will want to test you. Let them. You will learn what you do not see, and they will learn what cannot be read. I will not be there. This is your work, not mine."

A fair division of labor, I thought. Also an honest one. He wasn't offering guidance. He was offering a door and declining to walk through it with you.

The blind monk entered without introduction and said: "Give me a standard shadow step."

Zalthir shadow stepped.

The monk did not wait. "You skip the third breath. You don't enter your ki in the hara. You let it bleed into your fingertips like a poison. It is technically wrong in every scroll."

Then he paused.

"And yet, you arrived at your destination three seconds faster than the Grandmaster did. Show me what else you haven't learned."

I wanted to write that sentence down. *Show me what else you haven't learned.* That's the whole problem with formal assessment of unconventional people — the rubric was built around what you're supposed to know, and Zalthir had never particularly cared what he was supposed to know. He'd cared about what worked. There's a case to be made that those are the same thing. There's also a case that they're almost never the same thing. Both cases can be in the notes.

Zalthir asked whether he could punch the monk.

I don't know exactly how I expected the trial to proceed, but I should have expected this.

He could. He did. The sparring match that followed was, by all observation, evenly balanced — Zalthir could have pushed it further if he'd committed, but the monk was also navigating without sight, which was its own variable. They took each other's measure. That part went fine.

Then came the formal demonstration. Three techniques: the Weightless Shadow, the Efficient Strike, the Eldritch Claw. The monk had the rubric. Zalthir had to execute against it.

He chose acrobatics. The Weightless Shadow.

He rolled catastrophically. He fell. The monk watched him fall — insofar as a blind man watches anything — and said, without apparent emotion: "You have learned nothing."

Zalthir's internal calculus was already doing the math on whether this could get worse. It could. He tried the Efficient Strike. Athletics. Another catastrophe. The monk sighed: "The corridor was narrow. The form was irrelevant. Did you not practice your forms while traveling?"

He had not practiced his forms while traveling. He had survived the Underdark, which is a different curriculum.

There was a brief negotiation about alternatives — bribes, a pet spider, various exit routes — all of which closed off with the same finality. The monk was blind, so the spider gambit was dead on arrival. Zalthir tried stealth for the Eldritch Claw. His number landed somewhere that would have been insufficient even with generous interpretation. The monk sighed again and walked away.

Thorin, from the side: "Can he attack him while he's walking away? Blindside him, as it were?"

Zalthir: "I mean, I rolled a 30 to hit. Does that get me anything?"

A pause.

"You literally rolled a 30 to hit?"

"Yes. This character is optimized for combat, not skills."

The monk stopped walking. "Hmm. Perhaps not all is lost." He was, by every available indication, genuinely impressed. Apparently no one had hit him that hard in a long time. He stood there for a moment, something recalibrating behind his blindness, and said — almost to himself: "Perhaps I've been approaching this the wrong way."

This is the thing about assessment rubrics. They tell you what the assessor values. They don't always tell you what the subject is capable of. Stroud couldn't play chess. He rolled a natural 20. Four hundred years of preparation, and nobody had accounted for the person who didn't know the rules.

The monk set a final challenge. He would throw a brick. Zalthir would shadow step to intercept it and strike it mid-air. The brick was thrown.

Zalthir rolled a 29 to hit.

The brick did not survive. The monk observed the results — the pieces, the completely unprecedented form, the continued skipping of breaths, the continued absence of maneuvers — and said: "The results do speak for themselves."

He asked whether there was anything else. Whether any lesson from Zalthir's training might be offered, any insight, anything at all.

Zalthir offered the shadow tentacles. The eldritch tattoo. "I mean, I learned how to grow shadow tentacles."

The monk looked at him — the particular look of a teacher who is deeply not impressed but is in the process of identifying the next problem to solve — and said that what would be interesting was combining the two into something: that the way forward was in the combination.

It was around this point that a sage of xenobiology approached, apparently drawn by the news of a gelatinous cube with monastic ambitions.

"Is it true you are teaching a gelatinous cube the way of the shadow?"

"Indeed."

"Can a mindless digestive system achieve—"

Zalthir cut him off. "Wait, wait! She's by no means mindless — she's a student. He's learning the ways of the monk. He's already walking. He's already torturing this poor drow. He's doing well."

Thorin, helpfully: "Pocket Drow."

The sage, apparently satisfied by this defense, produced a vial of Resonant Slime. Glabbagool was very pleased. I noted with some satisfaction that we had not brought a gelatinous cube this far — through the Underdark, through Waterdeep, into the libraries of Candlekeep — to have him dismissed as a mindless digestive system before lunch.

The final demonstration came when Zalthir recalled something from the Underdark — a moment where he'd found a way to punch *through* the shadows themselves, precise as a surgical cut. He proposed showing that. He warned the blind monk, then executed it. His strike landed at a number that left no ambiguity.

Khell-Vire raised an eyebrow.

"Although your skills are weak," he said — and paused — "clearly showing a lack of focus and training." Another pause. "There is something special about how you hit. The way of the Shadow is not about hitting. It is about discrete movement. And it is your combination of the shadow step and the hit that redeems your lack of skills in all other possible areas."

Then: "You are beginning to remember, I think, all those shadow monks that drove you nuts."

"Yeah," Zalthir said. "Yeah, definitely."

I had to concede that Khell-Vire had read the situation correctly. Zalthir's relationship to his monastic tradition was precisely the relationship of a man who had found the manual aggravating and ignored it, developed something better through necessity, and was now being told that the thing he'd developed through aggravation and necessity was the most interesting thing about him. The manual was still wrong about him. But what he'd built in spite of it was genuinely his.

Khell-Vire announced the award of a specialized shadow technique — three options, presented carefully, with the honest note that adjustments might be needed if any proved mechanically unfortunate. Zalthir chose option C.

I filed it under "Zalthir: things that are not in the records but will be."
