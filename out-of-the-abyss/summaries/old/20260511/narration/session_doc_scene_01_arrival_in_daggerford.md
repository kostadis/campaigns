The whole session is a flashback. That detail landed at the table before we'd even set foot in Daggerford, and I filed it away the way I file most things that feel structurally important: quietly, without comment, with a private note that I'd probably want to think about it later.

Later being now, I suppose.

We came down out of the Silver Marches on the road south, which I'll tell you is a different experience entirely when you've spent the better part of a year underground. The Silver Marches have sky. Real sky — the kind that doesn't end. I'd forgotten how that felt. The hills had gone behind us and the road had opened out, and I kept catching myself looking up when there was nothing to look at. Giants do that. You grow up with people who measure the world in terms of horizon, and something in you never fully stops.

Daggerford was quiet when we arrived. Not empty. There were people moving in the streets, going about their business — but carefully, like they'd agreed on something and didn't need to say what. The Silver Marches had its own silence further north, the kind left behind by the barbarian hordes. This was different. Daggerford was sufficiently south that the Uthgardt hadn't reached it. Whatever this quiet was, it was theirs alone.

We stopped for the night at a place called the River Shining Tavern. Good name. The kind of name a town gives its best inn when the town is proud of its river.

But before we got that far, we passed through the village square.

I saw it before I understood it. A stone pillar, and mounted on top of it — a bronze cast of a severed forearm. Just the forearm. No body, no context, no weapon. Just a hand and the arm that used to be attached to something.

I said the obvious thing. "Dismembered. Okay."

Zalthir looked at it with the expression he reserves for things that make no tactical sense. "This is art? Like…"

Daz was already moving toward it. He goes to examine things the way other people breathe. Instinctive, immediate. I had one more question before I followed.

"Wait — is it holding a weapon?"

It was not.

So. Decorative dismemberment. The surface world has its own aesthetic choices, and I respect that.

The inscription told it straight. Travis Houlin, paladin, dead three years, killed in Daggerford itself breaking a Cult of the Dragon that had been actively recruiting here. The casting was funded by the Metalworkers Guild, two years post-war, and there was a secondary inscription in Dwarven on the base that Daz lit up at like a signal fire.

*The move is yours.*

He turned to us. "The move… oh, it's like, the chess… a reference to the chess thing."

I didn't know the chess thing. That's Daz's territory — the theological geometry of Stroud versus Bahamut, the inscriptions under Waterdeep statues, the whole distributed argument conducted through stonework across the length and breadth of the North. I knew enough to know it mattered to Grygum. I watched him go still in the way he goes still when something is clicking into place and he isn't ready to name it yet.

Travis Houlin had been a paladin of Bahamut. Killed fighting the Cult of the Dragon. Commemorated with Stroudite language in Dwarven on the base, and witnessed by Milo Goodbarrel — Order of the Gauntlet, head of his publisher — whose name was on the back in Common. The War of the Dragon had reached every community in the North in one form or another. Daggerford had taken the cult version rather than the barbarian version. Different flavor, same ash.

I stood there reading it and thought about the road we'd come down. How every village north of here has its own monument, or its own ruin that used to be a monument. The giants I grew up with had a saying that translated roughly as: *a rock in the wrong place is just a rock, but a rock in the right place is a wall.* This pillar was trying to be a wall. Whether it was succeeding was a different question.

We made it to the inn before the priest found us.

He came in looking pleased with himself, which is an expression I've learned to read as *I have a pitch.* He was carrying small pewter reproductions of Travis's hand — miniature, cast, available for a modest donation to the Order of the Gauntlet. He was delivering this sales proposition to the room when he noticed what the room contained.

A half-orc cleric of Bahamut. A dragonborn. A dwarf. A drow.

He stared at us for a moment with the expression of a man doing arithmetic that isn't coming out to a recognizable number.

Grygum, who has the best timing in the party and knows it, delivered the line before the priest could recover. "And they all walk into the inn."

The priest absorbed this. Rallied. Went back to his pitch. Grygum donated a gold piece and got a pewter medallion. Then he started asking questions, because Grygum always starts asking questions once he's established goodwill through currency.

The priest was happy to explain. The casting was Metalworkers Guild money. Possibly Stroud-school sponsored, he thought, though he was very clear on where he stood personally: "I am a pure Bahamutian." He said it with the comfortable conviction of a man who has said it many times and found it satisfying each time.

Grygum, also a pure Bahamutian as of this conversation, pressed the obvious point. The inscription. The Dwarven text. The chess language. Why was it there?

The priest looked left. Looked right. Arrived at his answer: the mining guild may have had Stroudites in it. And it had been a gift. And since it was a gift — he spread his hands with the pragmatism of a man who has made his peace with theological compromise — the inscription could remain. "After all, wasn't Travis trying to save us all, regardless of what actually happened in that game of chess?"

Grygum examined the statue further, looking for anything unusual. There wasn't much else to find. It was, as far as these things go, a fairly standard monument to a fairly specific grief — the mark of a war that had touched every community in the North, one way or another, regardless of whether the barbarians had reached them or the cults had or the dragons themselves. Daggerford had drawn the cults. The pillar was what they'd put up after.

I looked at it one more time before we went inside properly.

A severed arm. In bronze. On a pillar in a quiet square, in a town moving carefully through an evening it wasn't ready to explain.

Yep.
