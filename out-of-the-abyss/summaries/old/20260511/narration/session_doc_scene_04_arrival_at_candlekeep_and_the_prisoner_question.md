The Way of the Lion runs along cliff edges above the Sea of Swords, and Candlekeep is visible from miles out — a dark stack of towers against the sky, impossible to mistake. I'd seen it described in documents we'd carried out of the Underdark. Seeing it in person was different. It just sat there, enormous and settled, the way things that have been collecting knowledge for centuries tend to sit.

Daz went quiet beside me somewhere around the second hour of walking. Not his usual quiet. His hand went to his temple and stayed there.

I noticed. I didn't say anything yet.

Grygum noticed too, and asked whether it connected to the voices — the spreading madness we'd watched move through the Underdark like groundwater through stone. He couldn't pin it down, he said. Something wrong, something tenuous. I filed it. Whatever was happening to Daz had started below ground and apparently had not stopped at the surface. One more thing that needed answering. One more thing Candlekeep was presumably for.

We gave them the books at the gate. Bookwyrm — the First Reader, a dragonborn sage, and yes, I noticed the name — assigned us each to a handler without ceremony. She had clearly been doing this for a long time and had no interest in making it interesting. Daz to Sister Yvonne. Zalthir to Brother  Khell-Vire. Thorin to Master Archivist Philemon. Grygum to Varith. She said it the way you'd read a duty roster. I respected that.

Then she paused.

She'd noticed the bag.

Specifically, she'd noticed what was in the bag — because when we'd come through the gate, we'd had to open it to show the guards Glabbagool, and the miniaturized House T'sarran spy had been right there alongside him, which she apparently found unacceptable, and which Grygum summarized, with admirable brevity, as: *"We've been caught."*

They summoned someone called  Kalan Strongbranch. Gate Warden. He was efficient about it. He laid out Candlekeep's position without apology:

"The bell tower has cells. Comfortable, localized anti-magic. They are very rarely used. Anyone we find guilty of a major offense is exiled rather than imprisoned. Which is to say, we hold prisoners. We do not keep them. If you give her to us, what you are giving us is a delay. We will not deliver to House T'sarran, and we will not execute her. Eventually, we will release her at our gate with a warning. If you need something else, you will need to do it yourselves."

He said it plainly, which I appreciated. A delay. That was the offer. Release her into Faerûn eventually, with a polite warning to behave.

There was a class at the monastery — I think it was Brother Ferrath, or possibly Brother Aldas, I'm genuinely uncertain — on the difference between solutions and postponements. I remember nothing else from that class. I remember wanting to be in the training hall.

I have thought about it perhaps four times since, at moments exactly like this one.

The spy, for her part, was furious. She turned to  Kalan and announced that she was a prisoner and wished to be formally jailed by Candlekeep's guards, delivered with the kind of imperious certainty that I imagine comes naturally when you spend your life in a society that runs on hierarchy and has no framework for being three inches tall and thoroughly outmaneuvered. The First Reader looked at her with something that might have been sympathy and explained that as a guest of Candlekeep — as a member, technically, of our adventuring party — the decision was ours, not hers.

The spy did not take this well. She had opinions. Several of them.

Thorin, who processes things by talking, asked whether Glabbagool had thoughts on the matter. The party had, apparently, collectively soft-focused on the fact that we were carrying both a grey ooze and a miniaturized House T'sarran operative in the same bag, and that this arrangement had been continuing for days.

Glabbagool was glad to be asked.

He'd been doing prison-watching, he explained, and was kind of bored. But the spy was not without entertainment value. She kept trying to kill him. He'd slap her around. She'd try to run. He'd expand. She'd scream. He'd try to explain the way of the shadow and teach her meditation. She'd scream about the meditation. Then they'd talk. He said they'd become great friends.

Thorin observed that Glabbagool seemed ambivalent.

Glabbagool considered this. Then he turned — and here I want to be precise about what happened, because it mattered to me — Glabbagool paused, and asked me directly:

"Zalthir? Will I be more of a monk or less of a monk by watching her? Is this some kind of pre-monk training you've put me in?"

"Absolutely," I said.

It was. Patience under provocation. Maintaining stillness when the environment is actively hostile. Responding to aggression without being ruled by it. If anything, the spy was giving him a better curriculum than most of what I'd sat through.

Glabbagool took this with complete seriousness — the gravity of a student who has finally received confirmation that the work counts. He drew himself up, or the ooze equivalent of it.

"I will keep an eye on her!"

Six pseudo-eyes fixed on the spy simultaneously.

"Keep at it, buddy," I said.

The spy was not happy. The decision was made. We kept her.
