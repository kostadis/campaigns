The Hall of Divination smells like old stone and something faintly electric — the residue of spells cast so many times in one room that the air itself has absorbed the habit. Spectral mage hands drift between the shelves, fetching tomes for researchers who don't look up. The tomes move past me in slow, purposeful arcs, and I find myself watching them the way I watch everything I don't yet understand: cataloguing, waiting.

My head had been complaining since we arrived at Candlekeep. Something about the proximity — to all this knowledge, or to something underneath it — that pressed against the inside of my skull with a dull, insistent weight. So when the first tome swept past me, my first thought was not *what knowledge does this contain* but something considerably less dignified.

"Can I see or read any of… do any of them… I'm… at this point, my headache… my headache's been bothering me. Is there anything on any of the tomes that might help me with my headache?"

Sister Yvenne looked at me with the particular expression of someone who has spent decades managing scholars and has nevertheless not encountered this exact request.

"Well, is this the question you wish to have answered?"

I will say this for Candlekeep: they have a policy. A clean one. Information flows both ways across the counter. No ledger gets updated without a corresponding entry on the other side. I find this philosophically admirable and professionally reassuring.

"Can I only get one question answered? I'm an inquisitive person, I have many questions."

She paused. Allowed the observation to settle. Then said, with the patience of someone who has been doing this for a long time, that perhaps we could get to my headache after we addressed the primary purpose of the inquiry.

"I'm in… I'm in the middle of an inquiry."

She waited.

She had, I realized, all the time in the world. This is the advantage of working in a library. Time is not a resource you run short of when your colleagues are ghosts.

She explained the arrangement: when I asked an Avowed for information, I was also expected to provide it. Information for information. Which is, when you strip away the decorum, exactly how Menzoberranzan works. They just don't use words like *reciprocal exchange*. They use knives.

"Do I need to provide some information?"

She confirmed that I did.

There are several ways to handle this situation. The first is to give nothing and get nothing. The second is to give a carefully constructed partial truth, calibrated to yield exactly the information you want without exposing anything useful. The third — and this is the one I would not have considered two years ago, before I woke up in a jail cell with no memories and a headache that apparently never left — is to simply tell the truth.

I told her the truth.

"I have a headache, but I'll tell you the story. This is the short version. I don't have much memory before being locked up with a few people in a jail. Because some spider thing was, like, trying to keep us. And we've made an escape. We were in the Underdark. We've had many, many adventures, yada yada yada. Now I'm here in Candlekeep. My head hurts a little bit, and I don't know who I am, or my history, and I don't know why my head hurts."

I let that sit for a moment.

"That's the information I have to share."

"Wow," she said, "that's quite a bit."

"Well, I mean, I skipped some, yadda yadda."

She was kind of impressed. She was also, visibly, confused — the confusion of someone who has been told to expect a certain kind of creature and has received a different one. I am a drow. Drow do not simply hand over everything they know to a stranger in a library in exchange for nothing yet delivered. The whole culture is structured around the exact opposite of that.

I understood the confusion. The honest answer was that I wanted help and this was a library and librarians are supposed to be helpful. I was aware this reasoning would not fully satisfy a scholar of drow politics. She probably thought I was lying.

I wasn't. I just couldn't afford the usual approach. When you don't know what you're hiding, you can't protect it. Better to lay everything out and watch what someone else picks up.

She decided to trust the disclosure enough to begin. She explained the structure of the noble houses — matron mothers at the head of each, power flowing upward, loyalty transactional at every level. The rule of intrigue: betrayal is rewarded if it strengthens the web. Advance through maneuver. The city selects for it.

I knew most of this. Not as facts retrieved from memory, but as grammar. The language you learn to think in before you learn to question it.

Then she said the thing that matters.

Whenever a house sought to destroy another house, it had to destroy everyone. If you left a single person alive — even a child, even someone inconvenient and useless — your house was forfeit. The wider community would come for you. They would reduce you to a black puddle and call the matter settled.

She told me the story of one such house. They had tried to wipe out another, and failed to account for one survivor. A child. A blithering child idiot, she said, and I noted the language was hers, not mine.

I asked the logical question.

"Okay, what happened to the blithering idiot?"

"Unclear," she said.

I asked the second logical question.

"Am I the blithering idiot?"

She did not answer that directly. She moved instead to her list.

The first thing Menzoberranzan trains you for is the ledger. Who owes whom, who knows what, who has already lost and hasn't noticed. I have been running that ledger unconsciously since before I could name it. The second thing Menzoberranzan trains you for — and this one is equally useful but considerably less comfortable — is to notice when a word doesn't fit.

She read me the list. The second seat. The fourth seat struck. The eleventh seat. The twenty-third seat.

*Struck.*

Something in me landed on it before I could explain why. The other entries were eliminations, extinctions, houses reduced to absence. But *struck* is not that. Struck is what you do to a record when you want it gone but it won't cooperate with being gone. Struck is a ledger entry someone paid to have removed from the column it belonged in.

"Struck," I said. "Didn't mean eliminated. It means they struck it, but maybe they didn't eliminate it, and therefore, like, it didn't happen? Is that why I feel like it's different?"

She confirmed I was asking the right question.

Then she told me there was one house she could not speak of in the room. Not because she would not — because the public record is sealed, and she is today the public record.

"If you want what is behind the seals, you will have to want it through other doors."

I asked if she could point me to the other doors.

She could not.

But the room itself had already told me something. This is a library with, by reputation, all the information in the world. Something is not here that should be here. Someone did something to a place whose entire purpose is preservation. That is not a small act. That is a very large, very expensive, very deliberate act.

"Alright," I said, "so somebody powerful has manipulated the world database library."

Yes. That is precisely what had happened.

She handed me the document with the full list of houses. The word *struck* appeared again. She mentioned, almost as an aside, that the fourth seat holds ritual archive privileges in Menzoberranzan — that having archive privileges means having the legal apparatus to access and alter records. That a struck fourth seat was not merely a political erasure. It was the expensive kind. The kind that requires knowing the right people and paying the right price and having the infrastructure to make the payment look like it was never made.

I held that for a moment.

"So somebody paid a lot of money to erase some stuff. I don't know my history. We have mercenaries following me around, trying to make sure I live, and other mercenaries following me around, trying to kill me. So, perhaps, I am associated with some expensive striking."

She confirmed it. Yes. That was the correct analysis.

She also gave me a book. A heraldry book: *Geography and Cities, Menzoberranzan, Pillars of Woe*. Two hundred years old, give or take. I opened it the way I open anything I'm not sure I trust — slowly, looking for the places where it doesn't quite sit right.

I wanted something that would trigger the headache, or ease it. A name. A cadence. The particular rhythm of a language I spoke before I had words for what I was speaking. I wanted one of those moments from the books I've read where a displaced person touches an object from their past and something slides back into place.

I found a footnote instead.

"I gotta tell you, the first line of this, where it says, *Daz'issin*, and lit., *small shadow*, made me feel like I was reading, like, millennial shorthand. And that I didn't understand it."

The footnote referenced an appendix.

The appendix was missing.

I looked at the cut. It was mechanical. A razor. Not age, not water damage, not the slow forgetting of paper that has been handled too many times. A razor, at least fifty years ago, someone had taken to this book and removed what the footnote pointed to.

So: a name. A softened name, a diminutive, a name ending in *-issin* the way you'd end a name you wanted to feel smaller than it was. A footnote referencing something gone. And the name is Daz'issin. Small shadow. And I am, as far as I know, called Daz.

"Well, isn't my name, Daz? Maybe it's my full name?"

I asked her directly who this Daz'issin was.

She told me it was a softening name. That mothers use it for boys they wanted to live. That there were not many uses of it in the past three centuries. And that the houses that had used it, most of them, did not last.

I asked if she knew my name.

She did.

I asked whether someone might call me Daz, and this was actually a name that, like, my mother used.

She redirected. Let us turn to your patron, she said. Did you not come here to learn who protected you?

"I do want to know who protected me, that would be useful, yeah."

She paused. Thought. Then: it is possible your name is related. But it is unlikely. The houses that had those names have all gone.

I looked at her. At the way she was sitting. At the way she had been sitting for the last several minutes — the practiced stillness of someone who knows more than the public record allows her to say, maintaining the pose of a person who is simply informing rather than carefully navigating.

Menzoberranzan teaches you to read rooms. It teaches you to watch for the moment when someone's body stops matching their words. Yvenne's body had been disagreeing with her words for the last five minutes.

"But what if they weren't successfully all gone?"

She said nothing.

Which is, in the ledger, an answer.
