# Chapter 51 The Ember Grapple's Journey Through the North

Date: May 4th, 2026

## Summary
*Framing note: This chapter is a flashback. In present time the party is at Candlekeep; what follows recounts the weeks of surface travel between Blingdenstone's surface exit and their arrival at the great library.*

The party emerged from the depths of the Underdark through Blingdenstone's surface exit into the Silver Marches on a mid-spring afternoon, blinking in the light of a world they had long been separated from. It was here that Eldeth said her goodbyes, parting ways with the group as they set their faces south. The land around them bore the unmistakable scars of war — burned hamlets, a broken standing stone, and a defaced Tempus shrine that drew Grygum's quiet reverence. A refugee family trudged past them on the road, three generations sharing a single ox and a handcart, heading south with everything they had left. A local at a wayhouse noted an eerie stillness that went beyond the destruction — fewer bears, pixies no longer singing in the sown lands — something that felt older and stranger than the war alone could explain.

The party made a detour to Mirabar, the great dwarven mining city whose forges painted the horizon red and filled the air with smoke for miles around. The city had held during the Uthgardt raids, but the cost was visible everywhere — a massive refugee population slept in shifts at the Smith's Gate while the hammers rang through the night, half the orders being for militia armor. Thorin, a dwarf who had grown up far from his people's traditions, found the city overwhelming and unfamiliar — a fish out of water in the very place that should have felt like home. A bronze Stroud bust stood at the Smiths Guildhall, and a tavern called The Knight Beneath the Knight bore his name. A traveling Stroud School polemicist spotted Grygum in the market and immediately attempted to recruit him to the cause of the "human agency hypothesis" — the argument that mortal will, not gods or dragons, had decided the great conflicts of the age. The polemicist introduced himself as one of the Stroudite school, rejecting both the Bahamutian hypothesis and the idea that Protanther had simply allowed events to unfold, insisting that this was all about mortal agency. When Grygum replied that he stood for strength and luck, the preacher pressed on, asking pointedly, "Are you walking the road for Stroud?" After a brief and fruitless exchange, the preacher pressed a well-formed pamphlet into Grygum's hands, titled *Stroud and the Human Agency Hypothesis: A Critique — Dragons and Gods Do Not Rule*, which Grygum, noting it was at least divided into paragraphs rather than a wall of text, pocketed for later reading.

Within Mirabar, the party also visited an Order of the Gauntlet shrine that housed a skulled bronze frontispiece reproduction of Milo Goodbarrel's Account, Volume 1, and a nearly full-height painting of Malfire, the Vindicator of Nature — a weathered figure in green at the forest's edge, longbow lowered, an enormous gray wolf at his side, stars rising over his shoulder. The painting had been made by Milo Goodbarrel, the famous chronicler of the War of the Dragons, and two half-orcs tended the shrine in shifts. Thorin began quietly considering whether the city's renowned smiths might be able to outfit him with something worthy of the road ahead.

The party pressed on to Triboar, a caravan town that had been devastated by the barbarian raids, with one-third of it still in ruins. Before an inn with recently laid flagstones, a cairn of silver fieldstones named 243 of the Triboar dead. The town buzzed with caravans staging, traders, pilgrims, Concordant Order knights, the occasional Lord's Alliance courier, and those carrying rebuild permits — the party blending into the churn of it all. It was here that the party first heard whispered tales of a figure called the Ardragon — a Kenku hooded in black who hovered a foot off the ground, appearing at a distance along the caravan trails north of Yartar, mimicking a hawk, taking nothing, and vanishing before anyone could approach. One drover offered the fuller story: "The Ardragon — my granddad called him — the one who used to fly the routes for the Black Network. Used to steal anything not nailed down. Then something happened out east. Now he's a… a ranger?" The man paused, then added: "We don't pay him. We leave a coin on a rock, and he never picks it up." A half-orc named Kessler, who kept a small Bahamut shrine at the Mountain's Mouth inn, approached the party with a worried look and declared: "You're going south! You oughta know, there's a temple! The brother who keeps it stopped writing five years ago. I keep a small house down the road from him. I have not seen him in three years. The temple fire still burns. I don't know what that means. I would very much like to know." He asked them to carry a written note to this brother, and Grygum immediately agreed: "Tis an omen! I shall certainly visit the temple, and I will bring your note."

That same evening, Thorin bought a drink for a local man sitting alone at the inn, speaking in two voices — his own, and a younger one that answered questions the first voice never asked. A neighbor explained quietly: "That's Eldred. He ran courier, Triboar to Sundabar in the war, came back from his last run two summers past, and he was both of them. We feed him, and we don't ask." Grygum and Zalthir, listening carefully with insight checks above 14, noticed that both voices were Eldred's — the second one a younger version of himself, as though something else were speaking through him in his own register. Grygum observed that the madness was familiar: it was the same broken, fractured quality they had witnessed throughout the Underdark, and the realization settled over them like a cold fog — whatever had been festering below the surface was bleeding through into the world above. Glabbagool, mercifully unaware of the weight of the moment, was gifted an entire wheel of cheese by the curious innkeeper as a novelty. The intelligent grey ooze dissolved it entirely and declared, "This is very good" — then paused and asked with genuine curiosity, "Why wouldn't you be good with dairy? Is dairy bad for people?" — his first encounter with cheese. Throughout the stay, Glabbagool continued pestering Zalthir about shadow monk training, periodically attempting to demonstrate that he had made progress in walking — painful to watch, but enthusiastic.

Grygum spent part of his time in Triboar performing a ceremonial Prayer of Healing for the many injured refugees, the long ten-minute ritual lending the moment its weight, drawing the attention of local Stroudites, Protantherites, and Bahamutians who wanted to know where he stood on what they called "the only question of the age." His response — "Are we not all the playthings of fate?" — immediately won over the Bahamutian faction, who cried, "Exactly! That's exactly what we believe! It was the great and glorious power of Bahamut that directed everything. Protanther and Stroud were merely agents!" A third of the audience left in frustration, another third cried "Deist!" and departed, and the remaining third clustered around Grygum with evident warmth. It was, by any measure, a successful evening.

The party arrived in Waterdeep, the great city, where Glabbagool was forced to remain hidden inside a bag for the duration of their stay — oozes were not tolerated within the city walls. On Castle Road, they found a bronze statue slightly larger than life depicting the famous chess match between Stroud and Protanther, the chessboard between them rendered in inlaid marble, with a small brass plaque reading: *This is the position. The move is yours.* Grygum, admiring the inlaid marble chessboard, mused aloud about how the game had been studied for years, adding wistfully: "If only Protanther had moved his Rook, all could have gone another way" — a remark that immediately drew a crowd of passionate strangers eager to know where he stood on the question of the age. Daz made his way to Rishaal's Pageturners, a bookshop where a framed original painting by Milo Goodbarrel hung above the desk, depicting Stroud, Milo, Malfire, and Whistler — the Kenku hooded in black, hovering a foot off the ground, beak half-shadowed, one hand raised in mimicry as if quoting a line of metallic dragon speech. Rishaal explained that Milo had delivered Volume 3 nine months ago, that sales had declined when he came to town for the signing, but the painting had brought them back: "Fat painting saved the print run! Milo finished it two days before the signing. We had it framed by boarding. People come here for the painting and left with a book." The "real-world fiction enthusiasts," Rishaal noted, were "exasperating, but they buy three copies of everything." Those enthusiasts were audible at the back of the shop, arguing animatedly about the travel speed of Whistler — specifically whether he could have flown from Triboar to a given location in two days given the wind conditions cited in the Weber Report — and debating variations between different printings of Volume Two.

It was also in that bookshop that Daz discovered something quietly startling: a sign on the wall that read *Whistler, Kenku of the First Faction, fallen at Glaziel's lair, the year of the Alliance, redeemed in life and death. May his silence speak.* Some factions, it seemed, had decided it was more convenient to believe that Whistler had died at the end of the war. The fans were split between the Whistlerites and the Miloites, debating whether Milo's account of Whistler's morally complicated post-lycanthropy stage was too generous, or whether the "operational expenses, cost of living" framing represented a genuine philosophical breakthrough about the impossibility of pure heroism. Daz absorbed all of this in silence.

Zalthir sought out shadow monk training during the party's time in the city and was pointed toward Candlekeep and a master named Khell-Vire — described simply as "the best of the best." A local monastery offered to provide a letter of introduction for a price, citing a leaky roof in need of repair. After some negotiation, and a persuasion roll that yielded a 4 against the monastery's 18, the shakedown settled at 140 gold pieces. Zalthir pulled Daz over to argue on his behalf — noting that if it were Whistler, he would have stolen the roof tiles — and Daz proposed instead to fix the roof himself. He spent the better part of an hour up on the eaves, working tear-by-tear with the Mending cantrip while the monks watched in mild astonishment. The repair landed within the cantrip's per-cast limits, only the labor was tedious. The monks, somewhat sheepishly, provided the letter.

In the Field Ward, the party encountered a ragged street preacher who chanted the same phrase over and over: "The kraken under the keep, the kraken under the keep. The chant will break and the kraken will rise." When the preacher's eyes landed on Daz, he paused, then spoke directly to him: "You came up! You came up, you will go again. You will go down again. Do not—" before cycling back into his chant. Thorin rolled a 16 on insight and got the distinct impression that the man was right about something he could not name, and noticed with a chill that the preacher's broken, looping speech carried the same fractured rhythm as the dual-voiced man back in Triboar. The pattern was becoming harder to ignore. The party noted that Candlekeep, their next destination, might be a place to seek answers — and that "keep" in the chant might warrant investigation there.

The party's time in Waterdeep was not without its lighter moments. As dusk fell over the alley behind the inn, a city guard, suspicious of the bag Zalthir was carrying, insisted on inspecting it — and Thorin rose to the occasion with a passionate public defense of ooze rights, arguing that sentience deserved respect regardless of form. Dawnbringer, the party's sentient sword, chimed in: "One day they're going to ban oozes, the next day they'll ban sentient swords!" The guard, realizing he was dealing with what appeared to be a group of very committed and possibly unwell individuals, stood bewildered — at which point Zalthir, standing in the alley's shadow with another shadowed doorway behind him, Shadow Stepped clear with the bag, leaving Thorin mid-oration and the guard staring at the space where the bag had been. The guard suggested, gently, that Thorin might benefit from the attentions of a cleric. Grygum noted that he was a cleric. Thorin, for his part, finished his coffee and walked away. The argument had been heard by a crowd of mixed reactions — some nodding, some cautious — planting seeds of a strange new idea in the cobblestones of the great city. Before leaving, Thorin moved through the city's poorer quarters channeling Dawnbringer's restorative power to heal orphans and the sick, with Grygum and Zalthir accompanying through their own means — a quiet act of mercy that drew no crowds and asked for nothing in return. Thorin, specifically, had been inspired by the idea of bonding with Dawnbringer over helping others. The party then continued south, arriving at last in Daggerford, with the road to Candlekeep still ahead of them and the weight of unanswered questions — a burning temple, a kraken's chant, and a madness rising from below — riding alongside them every step of the way.

### Silver Marches
A region on the surface where the party emerges from the Underdark near Blingdenstone's exit during mid-spring. The area has been devastated by Uthgardt barbarian raids following the War of the Giants. *Canon timeline:* the War of the Dragons (~1490 DR — the conflict Milo Goodbarrel chronicles, fought by Stroud, Protanther, Malfire, and Whistler) ended first; the War of the Giants (1491 DR, restoring the Ordning under King Hekaton) followed; the Uthgardt raids that scarred this region were a knock-on consequence of the giant war's disruption. Burned hamlets, displaced refugees, a broken standing stone, and a defaced Tempus shrine line the road. An unusual quiet — fewer bears this year, pixies not singing in the sown lands — adds an eerie atmosphere that a local explicitly attributes to something beyond the war: "My grandmother says weather. I'm not certain."

### Mirabar
A major dwarven mining city in the Silver Marches, visible from miles away by the smoke and red light of its forges. The city held during the Uthgardt raids and is currently filled with refugees sleeping in shifts at the Smith's Gate, with the Smith's Quarter ringing with hammers all night as half the orders are for militia armor. Notable for a bronze Stroud bust at the Smiths Guildhall, a tavern named The Knight Beneath the Knight, a traveling Stroud School polemicist preaching mortal agency in the corner of the market, and an Order of the Gauntlet shrine featuring a skulled bronze frontispiece reproduction of Milo Goodbarrel's Account, Volume 1, and a large painting of Malfire, the Vindicator of Nature, tended by two half-orcs in shifts.

### Triboar
A caravan town heavily damaged by barbarian raids, with one-third still in ruins. A cairn of silver fieldstones in the central square names 243 of the Triboar dead, surrounded by recently laid flagstones. It is crowded with caravans staging, traders, pilgrims, Concordant Order knights, Lord's Alliance couriers, and those carrying rebuild permits, making it a hub for trade route rumors. Notable for the Mountain's Mouth inn, where the party encountered Kessler, the dual-voiced local Eldred, and Glabbagool's first wheel of cheese.

### Waterdeep
A massive, bustling metropolis where the party spends several days shopping, exploring, and causing philosophical controversy. Notable for a bronze statue on Castle Road depicting the chess match between Stroud and Protanther rendered in inlaid marble with a brass plaque reading *This is the position. The move is yours*, the bookshop Rishaal's Pageturners, and a Field Ward street preacher shouting prophecies about a kraken. Oozes are not tolerated within the city walls. Described by Grygum as being like New Jersey in its expense.

### Rishaal's Pageturners
A bookstore in Waterdeep featuring a framed original painting by Milo Goodbarrel depicting Stroud, Milo, Malfire, and Whistler — a Kenku hooded in black, hovering a foot off the ground, beak half-shadowed, one hand raised in mimicry — a piece that saved the print run of Volume Three. Frequented by intense "real-world fiction enthusiasts" who buy three copies of everything and debate the finer points of Milo Goodbarrel's historical accounts at the back of the shop.

### Field Ward
A district in Waterdeep where the party encounters a ragged street preacher shouting repeating prophecies about a kraken rising from beneath a keep, who also delivers a cryptic personal warning to Daz upon recognizing him as a drow.

### Daggerford
A settlement south of Waterdeep where the party arrives at the end of this leg of their journey, with further events to be continued next session.

## Items
### Stroud and the Human Agency Hypothesis Pamphlet
A well-formed pamphlet divided into paragraphs — full title: *Stroud and the Human Agency Hypothesis: A Critique — Dragons and Gods Do Not Rule* — arguing against the influence of dragons and gods in favor of mortal agency in determining the outcome of historical conflicts. Given to Grygum by the Stroud School polemicist in Mirabar after a failed persuasion attempt (Grygum rolled a 6). Grygum pockets it for later, specifically noting it is at least divided into paragraphs and not a wall of text with random capitalization.

### Kessler's Note
A written message from Kessler, a half-orc in Triboar, intended for a brother at a southern temple who has not been heard from in five years. The temple fire still burns untended, and Kessler has not seen the brother in three years. The party agrees to deliver it; Grygum immediately volunteers, calling it an omen.

### Wheel of Cheese
A large wheel of cheese gifted to Glabbagool by a curious innkeeper in Triboar. The intelligent grey ooze promptly dissolved it entirely and declared it "very good," then asked with genuine curiosity whether dairy is bad for people. His first experience with cheese.

### Milo Goodbarrel's Account, Volume Three
The latest installment of a popular historical series detailing the War of the Dragons, purchased by Daz at Rishaal's Pageturners in Waterdeep. Daz previously had Volumes 1 and 2. The book's print run was saved by Milo's painting of Stroud, Milo, Malfire, and Whistler, which Rishaal had framed and displayed above his desk to draw customers in.

### Mithril Plate Armor and Shield, +3
Thorin acquired a set of mithril plate (AC 18, no Stealth disadvantage) and upgraded his shield to +3 during the party's time in Waterdeep. Combined with his Stonegnarl Warband Token (+1 when on or adjacent to natural/worked stone), his AC now reads as **22 on stone / 21 off stone**. The DM notes this makes area-of-effect spells the most viable path against him.

### Dawnbringer
A sentient sword carried by the party. During the ooze rights confrontation in Waterdeep, Dawnbringer vocally supports Thorin's argument: "One day they're going to ban oozes, the next day they'll ban sentient swords!" Dawnbringer also offers lesser restoration to the bewildered guard, adding to the surreal scene. Later used to channel restorative magic to heal orphans and the needy in Waterdeep — a purpose Dawnbringer is described as very happy about.

### Javelin of Lightning
A magical weapon that Grygum expresses interest in acquiring as the party prepares to continue their journey south. The DM confirms it is available and approves the purchase.

### Letter of Introduction to Khell-Vire
A letter provided by a Waterdeep monastery, introducing Zalthir to Khell-Vire — described as the best shadow monk trainer, located at Candlekeep. Obtained after Daz used the Mending spell to repair the monastery's leaky roof in lieu of the 140-gold-piece donation the monks were attempting to extract.

## Spells
### Prayer of Healing
Employed by Grygum in Triboar to provide ceremonial relief to the many refugees and injured survivors of the barbarian raids. The ten-minute ritual cast (six creatures per casting) earned the attention of local Bahamutian clerics and triggered a theological debate about "the only question of the age." (Reframed from "Mass Healing Word" — that spell's six-target instant cast did not match the ceremonial framing of the scene.)

### Mending
Used by Daz to repair the leaky roof of a monastery in Waterdeep, serving as a substitute for the 140-gold-piece donation the monks were attempting to extract from Zalthir in exchange for a letter of introduction to Khell-Vire. Daz spent the better part of an hour patching the roof tear-by-tear, working within the cantrip's per-cast one-foot limit; the repair took time but was strictly RAW. The monks provide the letter somewhat sheepishly afterward.

### Shadow Step (Monk 8, Warrior of Shadow)
Used by Zalthir, in a shadowed alley behind the inn at dusk, to vanish with Glabbagool's bag during the city guard's inspection — reappearing in another shadowed doorway nearby. Both origin and destination were in dim light, satisfying the spell's lighting requirement. Thorin distracted the guard with an impassioned argument for ooze rights, leaving the guard staring at the space where the bag had been and Thorin mid-oration.

### Lesser Restoration (channeled through Dawnbringer, attuned to Thorin)
Used by Thorin to heal the ailments of orphans and the needy in Waterdeep's poorer quarters. Grygum and Zalthir accompanied him through their own clerical and martial means; only Thorin can direct Dawnbringer's restorative charge as the attuned wielder. Thorin's explicit motivation was to do good works and to bond with Dawnbringer over helping others. Dawnbringer is described as very happy about this use.

## NPCs
### Eldeth
A companion who had traveled with the party through the Underdark. She says her goodbyes at the Blingdenstone surface exit as the party continues their journey south. She had previously told Thorin about Mirabar, which is part of why the party detours there.

### Stroud School Polemicist
A traveling human preacher encountered in Mirabar who promotes the "human agency hypothesis" — the idea that mortal agency, not gods or dragons, determined the outcome of historical conflicts. He immediately spots Grygum and attempts to convert him: "Brother, have you heard the witness call? Are you walking the road for Stroud?" He identifies as of the Stroudite school, rejecting both the Bahamutian hypothesis and the Protanther doctrine. After failing to win Grygum over (Grygum rolls a 6), he hands him a pamphlet and declares the debate unresolved. He is one of many indicators of a broader religious factionalism surrounding the person of Stroud.

### Malfire
Known as the Vindicator of Nature, depicted in a large nearly full-height painting at an Order of the Gauntlet shrine in Mirabar. The painting shows him as a figure in weathered green at the edge of a forest, longbow lowered, with an enormous gray wolf at his side and stars rising over his shoulder. The painting is by Milo Goodbarrel. Grygum asks what it means to vindicate nature; Thorin suggests that if Grygum has to ask, he's clearly not connected to nature.

### Kessler
A half-orc encountered at the Mountain's Mouth inn in Triboar who maintains a small shrine to Bahamut. His face lights up when he sees the party heading south: "You're going south! You oughta know, there's a temple! The brother who keeps it stopped writing five years ago. I keep a small house down the road from him. I have not seen him in three years. The temple fire still burns. I don't know what that means. I would very much like to know." He asks the party to deliver a written note to this brother on his behalf.

### Eldred (The Dual-Voiced Local)
A man in the Mountain's Mouth inn in Triboar who speaks in two voices — both his own. The first is his current voice; the second a younger version of himself, answering questions the first voice never asks. A neighbor explains he ran courier routes from Triboar to Sundabar during the war, came back from his last run two summers past, and "was both of them." The locals feed him and do not ask questions. Grygum and Zalthir, both rolling above 14 on insight, recognize the broken, fractured rhythm of the second voice as the same quality of madness they witnessed throughout the Underdark — Eldred's voice in his own register, but not him. The madness of below is bleeding into the world above.

### Glabbagool
An intelligent grey ooze traveling with the party. In Triboar, he is gifted a wheel of cheese by a curious innkeeper, promptly dissolves it, declares it "very good," and then asks whether dairy is bad for people — his first encounter with cheese. He is forced to remain hidden in a bag throughout the party's time in Waterdeep due to the city's intolerance of oozes, peering out with two small eyeballs. He has been persistently pestering Zalthir about shadow monk training and periodically attempts to demonstrate that he has made progress in walking — painful to watch, but deeply enthusiastic. Zalthir's advice: "Give it time, you just have to level up."

### Rishaal
The proprietor of Rishaal's Pageturners bookshop in Waterdeep. He explains how a painting by Milo Goodbarrel of the war's heroes saved the print run of Volume Three: "Fat painting saved the print run! Milo finished it two days before the signing. We had it framed by boarding. People come here for the painting and left with a book." He describes real-world fiction enthusiasts as "exasperating, but they buy three copies of everything."

### Whistler
A Kenku hero from the War of the Dragons whose redemption arc and supposed death at Glaziel's lair are subjects of intense public debate. Some factions have declared him fallen, with a sign in Waterdeep reading *Whistler, Kenku of the First Faction, fallen at Glaziel's lair, the year of the Alliance, redeemed in life and death. May his silence speak.* Fans are split between the "Whistlerites" — who debate whether Milo's account of his morally confused post-lycanthropy stage is too generous — and the "Miloites," who argue that the "operational expenses, cost of living" framing represents a philosophical breakthrough about the impossibility of pure heroism. The rumors of the Ardragon (a black-hooded Kenku on the caravan trails who takes nothing and mimics a hawk) circulate separately from the literary debates, though the party recognizes the connection.

### The Field Ward Street Preacher
A ragged and seemingly insincere street preacher in Waterdeep's Field Ward who addresses any crowd that passes, repeatedly chanting: "The kraken under the keep! The kraken under the keep! The chant will break, and the kraken will rise!" When his eyes land on Daz, he breaks from the chant: "You came up! You came up, you will go again. You will go down again. Do not—" before cycling back. Thorin, rolling a 16 on insight, gets the distinct impression he is right about something he cannot name, and notices that the preacher's broken, looping speech rhythm matches that of Eldred in Triboar. The pattern is becoming hard to ignore.

### Ardegon (The Ardragon)
A figure of legend discussed by caravan drovers in Triboar and beyond. Described as a Kenku hooded in black who hovers a foot off the ground, appears at a distance on caravan trails north of Yartar, mimics a hawk, takes nothing, and vanishes before anyone can approach. One drover offers the history: formerly known as the Ardragon, he flew routes for the Black Network and used to steal anything not nailed down — then "something happened out east," and now he stops banditry, takes nothing, doesn't speak, mimics a hawk, and rides the next thermal. Locals leave a coin on a rock for him, which he never picks up.

### Milo Goodbarrel
A famous author and painter whose multi-volume accounts of the War of the Dragons have created a massive following and spawned intense fan factions. His painting of Stroud, Malfire, Whistler, and himself saved the print run of Volume Three, with Rishaal crediting it directly for renewed sales. A reproduction of his account's bronze frontispiece is found at an Order of the Gauntlet shrine in Mirabar. Fans debate the variations between printings of his books, the travel physics implied by his accounts, and whether his treatment of Whistler's redemption arc was too generous or philosophically groundbreaking.

### Khell-Vire
A shadow monk master based at Candlekeep, described by Waterdeep contacts as "the best of the best" for shadow monk training. Zalthir is directed toward him and obtains a letter of introduction from a local monastery — after Daz mends the monastery's leaky roof to satisfy the monks' financial demands.
