## Zalthir — Emergence into the Silver Marches

The air changed first.

It thinned in a way that had nothing to do with depth — it simply became something else. Something with weather in it. Something that moved without being asked to. The passage out of Blingdenstone's northern exit tunnels opened onto the Silver Marches in full late-spring afternoon, and the sky landed on me like a physical thing, like a hand pressed flat against my face.

I did not stop walking. I noticed the sensation, catalogued it, filed it under *adjustment required*, and kept moving. My eyes needed a moment they were not going to get. The light was everywhere — not the bioluminescent smear of fungal colonies, not the cold directed gleam of Dawnbringer, not the faerzress shimmer that had haunted the deep corridors for months. This was light that came from above and had nowhere else to be. It hit stone and grass and ox-flank and refugee and all of us with the same indiscriminate abundance, and none of it asked permission.

The monastery, I thought — almost despite myself — had a curriculum for this. There was a class about returning to surface conditions after extended underground travel. I believe it was Brother Tharusk. Or possibly the one after him. I don't remember the name clearly because the light through the shuttered window of the instruction room was more interesting than whatever was being said. I am paying for that now, in the currency of squinting.

Eldeth said her goodbyes at the mouth of the exit. She had earned them. I watched her go with the same attention I give to anything that has been a known quantity and is ceasing to be — a neutral inventory. She had been capable. She had been, at moments, more than capable. The word I would not use was *good*, because good was imprecise and I was not in the habit of imprecision.

We turned south.

The land told its own story without asking us to listen. The Silver Marches had been swept by something — the evidence was everywhere if you read it correctly, and I read it the way I read tunnels: shape first, then cause, then threat assessment. Burned hamlets. Timbers still leaning in the postures they'd died in, blackened and rain-softened, too far gone to call ruins, not far enough gone to call landscape. A standing stone broken at the base and tipped into the earth at an angle that said weight and intention rather than erosion. A Tempus shrine with whatever consecration it had once held scraped or beaten away, left as hollow architecture. Barbarian hordes, I decided. After the War of the Giants. The land had absorbed it the way land absorbs everything — not recovered, not healed, just quieted.

There was a refugee family on the road. Three generations, hand cart, one ox. No grandfather. I counted the generations and noted the gap without marking it further. They were heading south. So were we.

Grygum slowed near the Tempus shrine. I watched him register the defilement, perform the small internal calculation of a cleric confronted with desecration that is not his god's desecration, and arrive at the reasonable middle position: a brief reverence, a muttered blessing, the genuflection of someone who doesn't like seeing sacred things mistreated regardless of which name they were sacred to. He was a practical man under the theology. I had noticed this repeatedly and not mentioned it once, because it was not my business and was not a threat.

Thorin did not respond to the shrine in any particular way. He was present. His grapple with whatever the months underground had done to him was ongoing and I was not going to name it for him.

When I was asked whether I had anything to offer — the shrine, the hamlet, the moment — I considered the question. The honest answer was that I was still learning to look at the sky without wanting to look away from it. That I was calibrating. That the surface was enormous in a way the Underdark was not, because the Underdark was enormous like a labyrinth is enormous — directional, navigable, ultimately legible — while this was enormous like a ceiling without a ceiling, and something in me that had been working in close quarters for months did not yet have the appropriate category for it.

I said I was supportive. It was accurate. It was also the only thing worth saying.

The road continued south. The family's ox moved at the pace of an ox. I matched my pace to the landscape rather than to my preferences and watched the sky from the side of my vision where it was less immediately insistent.

Glabbagool was in the bag of holding. He had, at some earlier point — I had been there; I had watched — looked up at an open sky and demonstrated curiosity rather than retreat. I had no words for him in that moment, no shared language, the Stonespeaker Crystal being Daz's instrument. But I had noted the response. Filed it. What I remembered was that Glabbagool had come through the ooze crisis, the Pudding King, the bag, the months, and had still looked up.

I found that admirable in a way I was not going to say out loud.

The Silver Marches stretched south. The light did not diminish. My eyes, eventually, would adjust. That was what eyes did — they adjusted to conditions, they stopped fighting what was simply true about the environment, and they began to function. The monastery had a saying about this. I believe it was Brother Heslin who said it, or possibly the tall one who always smelled of incense — I was not paying close attention during that particular lesson either — but the shape of it was something like: *the first step to moving through a new terrain is to stop expecting it to be the old terrain.*

My eyes adjusted. The sky remained enormous. I kept walking south.

## Thorin — The Road Through the Silver Marches

The road south from Blingdenstone's surface exit was everything the Underdark wasn't: wide sky, open ground, wind that came from somewhere you could actually see. After months underground, I kept waiting for the ceiling to press back down. It didn't. That should have felt good.

It mostly felt like reading a body to see how much damage has accumulated.

The Silver Marches had taken a beating. Burned hamlets, foundations gone gray and cold, the kind of ruin that's already stopped smoking and started just being the new shape of things. A standing stone broken at the waist. We passed a Tempus shrine — defaced, whatever icon had been mounted there pried loose or smashed, the stone around it discolored where someone had been thorough about it.

Grygum didn't say anything, just slowed, genuflected, muttered something I couldn't quite hear, and moved on. It was quick and quiet and meant. I watched him do it, and I thought: that's the right-sized response. Not a funeral. Not a ceremony. Just the acknowledgment that leaving it like that would be wrong.

We kept walking.

Not far past the shrine there was a family on the road going the same direction we were: three generations, one ox, a handcart loaded with what they'd managed to carry. Grandmother at the back, moving stiffly. Two small children riding on top of a bundled quilt. Parents at the front, faces aimed south the way you aim at something when you've stopped looking back. No grandfather. I noticed that. I didn't ask.

Giants sit with things a long time before they say them out loud. That family had already done all the sitting.

We picked up what we could at a wayhouse a few hours later — hot food, a fire, the particular luxury of walls that weren't actively trying to kill us. A local filled us in on the wreckage we'd been walking through. The War of the Giants had shaken the Ordning loose — the hierarchy that orders them, Storm Giants at the top, then Cloud, then Stone, then Fire and Frost, with Hill Giants at the bottom — and a band of adventurers, apparently not thinking hard about consequences, had walked off with a pile of sacred artifacts that belonged to the Uthgardt. The Uthgardt, understandably, had taken exception. What followed was a crusade: raids, burnings, the broken standing stone, the defaced shrine, families with handcarts and no grandfathers.

The Ordning. I turned that word over while the local was talking. Storm at the top, Hill at the bottom. My family — my actual family, the ones who raised me — were Hill Giants. Bottom of the hierarchy restored by a war fought to save the world. I know how that reads to most people. Dumb, slow, at the foot of the pile, always have been. The dwarves I'd met underground had opinions about that. The local sharing wayhouse fire with us probably had some too, though he was polite enough not to say them.

I let it sit.

The local had something else to say after the history lesson, though, and this was the part that sharpened my attention. Bears fewer this year. Pixies not singing in the sown lands. His grandmother thought it was weather. He didn't seem certain.

Grygum went still in the way he goes still when he's actually processing something fast. He'd been spending time adjacent to the Fey — I didn't have all the details, but I knew enough — and I watched his face do the quick calculation.

"I spent all weekend just across the way from the Fey," he said, with the particular flatness that meant he was not done thinking about it. "So I'm a little suspicious."

The local didn't know what to make of that. I did.

Fewer bears. Pixies gone quiet. After everything we'd dragged up from the Underdark — demon lords loose in the Prime Material, the whole rot spreading upward through the stone — hearing that the land above was also going wrong in ways nobody had clean words for yet sat heavy.

The sky outside the wayhouse window was still wide and open. I looked at it.

Not the ceiling I'd expected. Just sky.

But the ground under our feet was telling us something our mouths hadn't caught up to yet.

## Daz — A Detour to Mirabar

Mirabar announced itself long before we reached the gates. The smoke came first — thick columns of it, red-bellied from the forge-glow beneath, rising into the pale northern sky like signals from another world. I watched them from the road and did what I always do: I read the column count, tried to estimate the volume, tried to understand what that much forgefire meant in terms of production. A great deal of armor, I concluded. A great many weapons. Either a city preparing for war or a city already in one.

Both turned out to be true, in the way that things on the surface seem to occupy multiple states at once.

Thorin was quiet on the approach. That was unusual enough to notice. Eldeth had told him about Mirabar — I remembered him mentioning it — but knowing a thing and standing in front of it are different propositions, and what he'd been told and what he was looking at clearly weren't reconciling cleanly. He'd been raised by giants. He knew dwarven heritage in the abstract, the way I know the surface world in the abstract: as a category of information, not as a lived experience. What he was walking into was a dwarven city at its most *dwarven* — massive, hierarchical, organized around production and survival — and he was as much an outsider here as I was.

Which struck me as interesting. I had expected to feel alien. I had not expected him to feel the same.

At the Smith's Gate we slowed. The refugee population was substantial — people sleeping in organized shifts, families with what they could carry, a low hum of organized exhaustion that I recognized not from any surface experience but from the slave quarters at Velkynvelve. The same rationing of rest. The same doing with less because less was all there was. The Smith's Quarter beyond the gate never went quiet; the hammers kept ringing through the night, and I was told — by a wall of sound more than any individual voice — that half the orders were for militia armor. The city held during the Uthgardt raids. The farms beyond the walls did not.

I wrote that line in my head twice before I understood what it meant. The city survived. The people who couldn't fit inside it didn't. And now the survivors were sleeping in shifts at the gate of the thing that had outlasted them.

The first rule of the House — any House, the Underdark runs on the same principle — is that walls define who matters. Mirabar's walls were very good walls.

Stroud was everywhere.

That was the other thing. A bronze bust of him at the Smiths Guildhall, catching the forge-light at a flattering angle. A tavern called The Knight Beneath the Knight, which I found either clever or insufferable depending on how charitable I was feeling. And in the corner of the market, a polemicist — a traveling one, the kind who carries his convictions in a satchel and deploys them wherever he finds an audience — preaching mortal agency to anyone within earshot.

He found Grygum immediately. They always do.

"Brother, have you heard the witness call? Are you walking the road for Stroud?"

I watched Grygum's face do the thing it does when he is weighing whether an entity is useful or merely sincere. He is better at concealing this than most people. He is not as good at it as he thinks.

The polemicist was Stroudite in the specific sense — mortal agency as the decisive force, Protanther's neutrality a myth, the Bahamutian framework a comfortable fiction. He was organized and committed and had clearly had this argument many times before. When Grygum replied that he stood for "strength and luck," the man did not flinch. He just identified this as the Bahamutian hypothesis in another hat and kept moving.

When Grygum pressed him — *but who really won in the end? The dragons* — the polemicist wasn't moved. He handed over a pamphlet instead. *Stroud and the Human Agency Hypothesis: A Critique — Dragons and Gods Do Not Rule.* Grygum accepted it. I watched him verify that it was divided into paragraphs before he committed to keeping it. He is, in his own way, methodical.

I noticed the texture of this without fully processing it until later. The factionalism wasn't about whether Stroud mattered. Everyone in Mirabar had clearly already decided Stroud mattered. The argument was about *why* — about the mechanism. Bahamut's hand or mortal will. Protanther's strategy or Stroud's choice. The distinction felt important to people who had very strong feelings about where credit was due.

Milo Goodbarrel would have had something useful to say about that. He had a chapter on the problem of attribution — how the same event gets claimed by every faction present and how that process tells you more about the factions than about the event. I had found the chapter slightly too optimistic. I was beginning to revise that assessment.

The Order of the Gauntlet shrine was small and well-tended, staffed by two half-orcs in quiet rotation. A bronze reproduction of the frontispiece from Volume 1 of Goodbarrel's *Account* was mounted at the entrance. I had read the book. More than once, if I'm being precise, which I generally try to be.

What I had not known was that Milo Goodbarrel could paint.

The back wall of the shrine held it: near full height, weathered and careful, titled *Malfire, the Vindicator of Nature*. A figure at a forest's edge in faded green, longbow lowered at his side, an enormous grey wolf standing easy at his hip, stars just beginning to emerge over his shoulder. The posture wasn't triumphant. It was finished — the specific stillness of someone at the end of something rather than the beginning. Painted by Milo. Signed in the lower corner in the careful hand of a man who took his documentation seriously even when the documentation was visual.

I stood in front of it longer than I intended to.

I had thought of him as a chronicler. A writer who was also, incidentally, an adventurer who had done significant things. Looking at that painting — the competence of it, the restraint of it, the fact that it had survived this long — I recalibrated. He was a chronicler the way I am a mage: the label is accurate and also substantially incomplete.

Grygum, to his credit, asked the useful question. "What does it mean to vindicate nature?"

Thorin told him, with great confidence, that this question revealed an insufficient connection to nature.

Grygum buried himself in his pamphlet. I made a note.

Thorin circled back to something practical — he'd been looking at the armor vendors since we arrived, and Mirabar was, if you were going to acquire dwarven-made equipment, precisely the city in which to do it. He had chainmail. He had a shield. He began asking, in the methodical way he approaches resource acquisition, what the appropriate upgrade path was for a dwarf of his build in a city that had clearly been thinking about this question for generations.

The answer, as far as I could determine, was: make a note, research the options, come back with a figure.

This struck me as sensible. I wrote it down.

## Grygum — The Ruins of Triboar

Triboar had been a caravan town before the raids. You could still see what it had been — the wide staging areas, the permanent hitching posts, the kind of inn that exists to absorb several hundred travelers at once. One-third of it was rubble. Not old rubble. Recent rubble, with the raw edges of it still showing.

I have seen wreckage. The Underdark has its own kind of devastation, the patient geological kind, the kind that accumulates over centuries of bad decisions made by entire civilizations. This was different. This was fast. This was people who had been alive and then weren't, and the town had simply continued around the absence, because what else does a town do.

The memorial square was the center of it. Stone markers, flowers going dry in the cold, the weight of names. And just past it, before an inn with flagstones still pale with newness, a cairn of silver fieldstones. I counted the names without intending to. Two hundred and forty-three. There is a difference between knowing a raid happened and reading two hundred and forty-three names in silver stone. My theology is built on individual agency. Individual agency requires individuals. Someone had come through here and simply removed them from the ledger.

I took notes.

The town was crowded in the way of places still rebuilding — not resident-crowded, traveler-crowded. Caravans staging, drovers and teamsters and outriders filling the inn benches, Concordant Order knights in road-dusted tabards, Lord's Alliance couriers moving fast and speaking to no one. We blended. An orc cleric, a dragonborn monk, a dwarf raised by giants, and a drow exile with architectural cheekbones blended into a caravan crowd. The surface world is very different from the Underdark in this particular respect, and I find it deeply convenient.

The drovers were talking about the roads. I have learned that drovers are always talking about the roads; it is practically a theological commitment. But the rumor circulating through the benches was specific enough to catch my ear. A figure haunting the caravan trails north of Yartar. Kenku, hooded in black. Hovering a foot off the ground. Appearing at distance and gone before approach. The details were consistent across every account, which is the thing that makes a rumor worth noting — consistency is rarer than accuracy.

One drover had a name for it. "The Ardragon, my granddad called him, the one who used to fly the routes for the Black Network. Used to steal anything not nailed down. Then something happened out east. Now he's a… a what? A ranger? Stops bandits, takes nothing, doesn't speak, mimics a hawk, and rides the next thermal."

Another voice from down the bench, without looking up: "We don't pay him. We leave a coin on a rock, and he never picks it up."

I sat with that for a moment. Something happened out east. I have heard that kind of compression before — the five words that contain an entire transformation and refuse to say more. A Zhentarim thief who now stops bandits and refuses payment. The coin left on the rock and never collected. That's not a story about a criminal who reformed. That's a story about a person who became something their former life had no category for.

I believe in individual agency. I have staked my theology on it. A kenku who used to steal anything not nailed down, now hovering above the road and mimicking hawks, is exactly the kind of evidence I find professionally useful. I filed it.

---

Inside the Mountain's Mouth, we had barely settled when a half-orc behind the bar caught sight of us — caught sight of me, specifically, with the particular brightness of someone who has been waiting a long time for a recognizable face. He had a small Bahamut shrine set into the wall above the bottles. Simple, clean, the kind that gets tended to rather than decorated.

His name was Kessler, and he was already moving toward us.

"You're going south! You oughta know, there's a temple! The brother who keeps it stopped writing five years ago. I keep a small house down the road from him. I have not seen him in three years. The temple fire still burns. I don't know what that means. I would very much like to know."

Then: "Would you promise to take a written note to him?"

I did not hesitate. This is the part of the work I trust most — the moment when something is handed to you and you can see exactly what shape it is. A brother whose fire still burns but who stopped writing. A man keeping a small house down the road, watching that fire for three years and not knowing what it means. The note Kessler would write him, sitting unread for years in a dead letter's drawer, or opening something that had been closed too long.

"Oh, 'tis an omen," I said. "I shall certainly visit the temple, and I will bring your note."

It is traditional in Bahamut scholarship to argue about whether divine providence operates through dramatic intervention or through the quiet alignment of travelers heading the same direction. I have my own view. I took the note.

Thorin had found someone to buy a drink for. This is one of the things I value about Thorin — he does not need a reason beyond noticing that someone is sitting alone with a glass that needs filling. The man he'd found was at a corner table, talking.

Talking to himself, except that wasn't quite right. There were two voices. His own, and then another, slightly younger, answering questions the first voice had never asked.

The table around him had the careful space of people who have established a protocol.

I looked. Really looked. Twenty-five years of reading people — in the Underdark, in temples, in the particular social pressure chamber of a party that has been trapped together for months — and this took everything I had. The second voice sounded like his, but didn't. It was his and not his. A quality of madness that sits in the voice like a lodger.

I knew that quality. I had filed it.

A neighbor at the bar saw me looking and offered it without prompting: "That's Eldred. He ran courier, Triboar to Sundabar in the war, came back from his last run two summers past, and he was both of them. We feed him, and we don't ask."

We feed him, and we don't ask. There is a whole civilization in that sentence. I want to write about it at some length but this is perhaps not the moment.

"Let's see," I said, trying to organize the observation into something useful, "I'm concerned that there might be some kind of a magical possession or something of those lines going on here."

And then the thing settled into place, the recognition I had been almost reaching — the specific flavor of unmooring, the particular way the self comes apart at one particular seam. Whatever was going on in the Underdark was bleeding through into the Overbright.

"Oh," I said. "I, I should have — I should have realized that."

Zalthir, characteristically, went straight to the practical question: "You mean he's acting weird, not that he's sprouting mushrooms."

I had been about to ask exactly that. "Okay," I said, once I had confirmation. "That was going to be my next question."

I took a broader look around the room. Nobody else showing the signs. Eldred was the exception, or at least the visible one. I watched him for another moment — the two voices conducting their patient parallel conversation, neither one quite addressing the other.

"I haven't yet started a conversation with him," I concluded. "He seems to be having quite a successful conversation with himself."

This is what I mean about the theology. My faith is built on the premise that individuals make choices and those choices matter — that a kenku can become a ranger, that a drow can refuse what the Underdark asks of him, that a dragon's color is not its destiny. But Eldred came back from a courier run and was both of them, and now we feed him, and we don't ask. Whatever happened out east to the Ardragon, it produced a choice. Whatever happened to Eldred on his last run to Sundabar produced something else. The same forces. Different outcomes. I do not have a clean formulation for this yet.

I took notes.

Glabbagool was given a wheel of cheese.

The innkeeper offered it as a curiosity, which is the correct framing — the innkeeper clearly had questions and had decided that the experimental approach was better than asking them. Glabbagool took the wheel, and dissolved it entirely, with the particular efficiency of something that has been an apex ambush predator for its entire existence.

"Hmm," said Glabbagool. "This is very good."

"Has he ever had cheese?" I asked, and then, because I genuinely wanted to know: "Glabbagool, have you had cheese before?"

He had not. He seemed to be processing this fact about the surface world with the same focused attention he brought to the question of shadow monk training — which he was still pursuing, with Zalthir, with what I can only describe as gelatinous determination. He had apparently been attempting to demonstrate walking. I had watched him try. The enthusiasm was not in question.

"I just hope he, you know, does okay with dairy."

"Why wouldn't you be good with dairy?" Glabbagool asked. Genuinely. With what I have come to recognize as his particular form of intellectual curiosity, which is not distinguishable from the expression he makes when dissolving something. "Is dairy bad for people?"

"Some people," I said. "Some people don't digest it well."

We had not brought a gelatinous cube this far through the Underdark to have him undone by soft cheese. I watched him for another moment. He seemed fine. He was already asking Zalthir whether the shadow step technique required a physical shadow or whether a projected darkness would serve the same purpose.

There were injured in Triboar. A lot of them. Refugees from the raids, people in various stages of mending, the particular population that collects around a town in the process of remembering what it was. I am a cleric of Bahamut and a Life domain cleric specifically, which means there is a sense in which healing is the most direct expression of everything I believe. Individual agency requires a body to inhabit.

I did a ceremonial mass healing word. The theatrical version, the one with the appropriate gestures, because people who are healing need to feel that something has happened to them, not just that a spell has been cast in their general vicinity.

The local Bahamutians found me immediately.

They wanted to know where I stood on the only question of the age. I recognized the shape of this conversation — the eagerness, the particular way the face arranges itself when it expects agreement — and I did not yet know what the question was, so I opted for the diplomatic opening: "Well, I'm in favor of our great Lord Bahamut, and apple pie, and I would like to know what the question is."

A third of the crowd left. Another third cried "Deist!" which in Bahamut circles is apparently the theological equivalent of a very specific insult that I will have to research. The remaining third crowded closer, warm and bright-eyed, and an enthusiastic cleric explained:

"You understand what is the critical question. At the game of chess, what happened? Was it Bahamut? Was it Protanther? Or was it human agency? And you clearly understood what it is. It was the great and glorious power of Bahamut that directed everything. Protanther and Stroud were merely agents."

I thought about Stroud. I think about Stroud often. A dwarf who could not play chess, sitting down across from an ancient gold dragon who had been analyzing that board position for four hundred years, who had mapped every possible move. Stroud rolled the equivalent of a natural twenty and won. Protanther was so stunned he joined the alliance. The story means: four hundred years of preparation still has a blind spot. The story also means: sometimes the person who doesn't know what they're doing is the variable the preparation couldn't account for.

The enthusiastic cleric had just told me that Stroud was merely an agent. I found this interesting.

"Are we not all the playthings of fate?" I said.

"Exactly!" the cleric said. "That's exactly what we believe! And it's the Stroudites and the Protanthians who wish to disagree!"

I looked at the cairn outside, through the window. Two hundred and forty-three names in silver stone. Individuals. Each one a node of agency in the great network of cause and effect that Bahamut supposedly administers. I thought about Eldred, having his successful conversation with himself in the corner. I thought about the Ardragon, hovering over the caravan roads and mimicking hawks, never picking up the coins.

"Yes," I said, "and the fact that there is no gigantic chessboard may lead one to wonder whether there should be a giant die with which the gods may play games with us mortals."

The cleric's expression shifted slightly. "That is heretically close to the Protanther Doctrine."

"But," I said, "the differences between Bahamut and Protanther are legion."

He seemed to find this reassuring. I let him find it reassuring. The Stroud story is still the most honest thing I know about divine agency — not that the gods are absent, not that they are present, but that four hundred years of preparation still has a blind spot, and sometimes the variable that breaks the model is a dwarf who can't play chess and rolls a twenty anyway. Whether Bahamut set that up or whether it simply happened — I genuinely do not know. I have a Candlekeep research question reserved for exactly this problem.

The cleric was still talking. I listened, and I took notes.

## Zalthir — Arrival in Waterdeep

Waterdeep arrives before I have a name for what it is doing to me.

The road narrows and then opens and then the city is simply there — not visible the way a mountain is visible, from distance, in parts, giving you time to adjust. It is just suddenly *present*. Sound first. Then smell. The sound is the harder problem. It layers the way water layers over stone, continuous and self-replacing, and the part of my mind that has spent months reading tunnel acoustics for threat vectors cannot stop reading this. Cart wheels on dressed stone. Someone calling a name from an upper window. The particular frequency of a crowd dispersed across many streets, none of them in the same hurry. I note all of it and none of it resolves into a threat and the threat-reading continues anyway, because the training does not care what I tell it.

There was a class. I am fairly certain it was Brother Tareth — or possibly one of the morning-shift instructors, the one with the grey braid who always lectured with her back to the window — and the subject was environments of sensory saturation. How to move through them without the training becoming the noise. I remember the quality of the light through the shuttered window. I remember wanting to be in the courtyard. I do not remember what she said.

The others take it differently. Grygum's head is on a swivel in the way that means *everything here is worth examining*, which is characteristic of him. Thorin is already looking at armor displays with the focused attention of someone who has made a decision he hasn't announced yet. Daz is quiet, but his kind of quiet is different from mine — it's the quiet of someone reading the room faster than the room knows it's being read.

The ooze ordinance, or whatever Waterdeep calls it, applies to Glabbagool from the moment we cross the gate. No oozes. In the city. At all.

Thorin makes the objection I expected. "Can we take a stand in solidarity?"

I have approximately nothing against solidarity as a principle. The practical geometry of the situation, however, is that the city is large, the guards are numerous, and Glabbagool has spent months in a bag of holding with a miniaturized drow spy without evident distress. I said what seemed relevant:

"But, sorry, doesn't he stay in his bag anyway?"

Grygum floated something about disguise spells. The idea was not unreasonable. The practical ceiling on it was obvious to everyone within a few seconds, and the conversation ended the way these conversations end, with Glabbagool going into the bag and Thorin's moral objection going unresolved but noted.

I watched the bag. Glabbagool has an opinion about containment that he expresses by extruding two small eyeballs to observe proceedings. He did this. The eyeballs assessed the street, found it apparently adequate, and retreated. I have found that admirable — the curiosity without the complaint. He eats cheese when we can manage it, which is genuinely more dignity than the Underdark afforded him in most respects. I note this and do not say it.

Castle Road has the statue.

I registered it the way I register most large objects — dimensions, sight lines, any alcoves that might conceal someone who had decided to be there — and then I registered what it actually was.

Stroud and Protanther, cast in bronze, slightly beyond the scale of living things. The chessboard between them is inlaid marble, pale and dark squares polished to a surface that collects the overcast light. The plaque at the base is small. The script is clean. *This is the position. The move is yours.*

I stood with it for a moment. Not a long moment. The board is mid-game, and the position is not simple, and there are people in this city who have clearly spent years arguing about it — which I learn approximately twelve seconds after Grygum opens his mouth.

He looks at it with the expression he wears when a theological argument is assembling itself behind his eyes. "The game has been studied for years," he says. And then, because Grygum cannot be near a question without answering it: "If only Protanther had moved his Rook — all could have gone another way."

The crowd that manifests is immediate. They emerge from storefronts, from the flow of foot traffic, from what had appeared to be doorways people were passing through rather than loitering in. Everyone in earshot has a position. Everyone with a position has been waiting.

I moved two steps to the side. Not retreat. Tactical repositioning. Daz did the same, without any signal, which is one of the reliable pleasures of traveling with him.

Grygum absorbed the crowd with the expression of a man who has accidentally confirmed something he already suspected about the nature of cities.

Thorin, for his part, found armor. Mithril plate, which he announced with the flat satisfaction of someone whose problem has been efficiently solved. I have no criticism of the decision. If you intend to be the one things hit, you should be difficult to damage. The arithmetic of his protection is now considerably more favorable. I noted this and moved on.

The rest of the afternoon has the texture of a city that has decided it will sell you anything, at the price it has decided anything costs. Grygum is hunting a Bahamut shrine and something that might function as adjacent to Bahamut for purposes he is characteristically indirect about. He mentioned a javelin of lightning in the same tone he might mention wanting eggs for breakfast — as though it were an ordinary item that happened to call lightning, which in Waterdeep it possibly is.

Grygum described the city as New Jersey, which I noted and filed under references I lack the context to evaluate. The comparison was delivered with the confidence of someone who has been to both places and found them structurally equivalent. I will take this on faith.

I walk the streets and catalogue the exits. I note the sightlines from the upper windows. I identify, by the way they move, three people who are watching the street with the same attention I give it, and I watch them back. None of them are watching us specifically. None of them have the posture of people who have already decided they've won.

Not today.

Glabbagool's eyeballs are probably back in the bag. The cheese situation is manageable. The city is immense and loud and sells mithril plate and javelins of lightning and positions on questions of the age, and I have been inside it for half a day, and I have not been killed or significantly inconvenienced, and I am beginning to construct a working map of the streets we have walked.

Waterdeep is a grid of noise and movement. I am learning its shape the only way I know how.

## Daz — Literary Debates at Rishaal's Pageturners

Waterdeep had been good for me in a way I didn't have a word for. The bookshops, the shrines, the absence of anyone actively trying to kill me — I had started, cautiously, to let my shoulders settle.

But the soul of a sage doesn't stop working just because the day's errands are done. So when I remembered that Rishaal's Pageturners was exactly the sort of establishment that would stock the third volume of Milo Goodbarrel's *Account of the War of the Dragons*, I set out to remedy the gap in my collection.

I had Volumes One and Two. I had read them both twice. That is all I will admit to in writing.

Rishaal's was small the way good bookshops always are — crammed and slightly airless, with the particular smell of old paper and lamp oil that I have found, in my experience, reliably indicates a place worth one's time. And there, above the desk, hanging in an actual frame: the painting.

I had heard about it in the way one hears about famous things — secondhand, incrementally, always slightly undersold. It showed the four of them together: Stroud, Milo, Malfire, and Whistler. The Kenku was hooded in black, hovering a precise foot above the ground, beak half-lost in shadow, one hand raised in that gesture he had — the mimicry posture, holding a line of metallic dragon speech like an actor mid-soliloquy. The painter had understood something. The way Whistler occupied space had always been a kind of argument about whether he was entirely present in any given room. The painting caught that.

I was still looking at it when Rishaal noticed me reaching for the third volume and offered his assessment of the situation.

"That painting saved the print run!" he said, with the satisfaction of a man who has delivered this line before and knows it lands. "Milo finished it two days before the signing. We had it framed by morning. People came here for the painting and left with a book."

He explained that sales had declined in the months before the signing — nine months since Milo had delivered the manuscript — and that the painting had reversed the trend entirely. The real-world fiction enthusiasts, he said, were *exasperating*, but they bought three copies of everything. He said *exasperating* the way you say a word when you are very fond of the thing you are describing.

At the back of the shop, I could already hear them.

The argument was about flight speeds. Specifically: whether Whistler could have flown from Triboar to a given location in two days, given what the Weber Report had documented about wind conditions across that same period. Someone was citing page numbers. Someone else was citing a variation in an earlier printing that the other party appeared to regard as apocryphal. The debate had clearly been running for some time before we arrived and showed no signs of resolution.

I found this delightful. I will not pretend otherwise.

I joined the debate. I had an opinion. I have always had an opinion about this sequence of events — the timeline around Triboar is genuinely contested, and the Weber Report's methodology has problems that Milo's most enthusiastic readers tend not to engage with seriously. I made this point. It was received with the kind of passionate disagreement that indicates the other party has been waiting for someone to challenge them properly.

Thorin, who I will note had access to rather more primary source material on the subject than anyone in that room, said — with the precise, deadpan expression of a man determined not to say what he was thinking — "If only we had a primary source."

I did not look at him. That was the correct move, and we both knew it.

The redemption arc debate was thornier. The room was split along fairly clear lines: one faction held that Milo's account of Whistler's post-lycanthropy stage was too generous, glossing over a period that the text itself described as morally confused. The other faction — and this was the position I found more interesting — argued that Malfire's framing was the philosophical core of the entire book. *Operational expenses. Cost of living.* The argument being: you cannot assess a person's choices without accounting for the material conditions that structured those choices, and Milo's willingness to let Malfire speak that framing without refutation was itself a kind of authorial statement about the impossibility of uncomplicated heroism.

Grygum, who I sometimes forget has a Sage's background until he deploys it at precisely the right moment, observed that the book also had an unreliable narrator, which was the most useful contribution anyone made to the discussion in the time we were there.

"You say the real hero might have been the accountant?" he added, and the Whistlerites — this was the terminology, I gathered — did not know quite what to do with this.

*Whistlerites.* I am going to be thinking about that word for days.

I noticed the sign while the debate was still running. It was on the wall, slightly to the left of the poetry section, hand-lettered in the careful script of someone who had thought about what they were writing:

*Whistler, Kenku of the First Faction, fallen at Glaziel's lair, the year of the Alliance, redeemed in life and death. May his silence speak.*

I read it twice.

The thing is — I have a working file in the back of my head about what is true and what various parties have found it *convenient* to believe is true. It's a long file. Menzoberranzan keeps excellent records, but Menzoberranzan's records are also edited by whoever holds power at the moment of editing. I know what that looks like from the inside.

Some factions, apparently, had decided it was convenient to believe that Whistler died at the end of the war. When he disappeared, they had closed the file with a label that suited them.

I looked at the painting. The hovering Kenku with his hand raised, quoting dragon speech to his companions. The half-shadowed beak. The specific way the painter had rendered the quality of his attention — present and absent at once, the way someone stands in a room when they are not sure the room fully knows they're there.

*May his silence speak.*

I bought my book. I paid full price. I did not say anything about the sign, because there was nothing to say that the situation required me to say, and because some things are better left in the exact category where you found them.

I carried Volume Three out of the shop with Volumes One and Two already waiting for it at the inn, and I was, I think, the most content I had been in a very long time.

## Thorin — Shadows and Shakedowns in Waterdeep

Waterdeep is a city that runs on implications. Nobody says what they want. They say something adjacent to what they want and wait for you to do the arithmetic. I grew up with giants, who say exactly what they mean and then don't speak again for three days. The adjustment is ongoing.

Zalthir had been looking for shadow monk training since before we surfaced — the kind of quiet, methodical pursuit you'd expect from a man who processes a threat the way a glacier processes a mountain. Patient. Thorough. Not cheap. He found a monastery willing to talk, and while he was there he learned what he actually needed: the best training on the continent was waiting in Candlekeep, from a master named Khell-Vire. Best of the best, they said. So: useful. So: worth the trip we were already making.

What came next was a performance.

The monks offered a letter of introduction to Khell-Vire. Then there was a pause — that specific kind of pause that isn't about thinking, it's about positioning — and they mentioned the monastery had a leaky roof.

I will give them this: it was efficient. No preamble, no pretense, no extended metaphor. Just the pause and the roof. Waterdeep shakedown, three seconds, no wasted syllables. I almost admired it.

Zalthir asked whether a hundred and forty gold worth of his own time fixing the roof might serve instead. The monks explained, with genuine patience, that donation money and operational money are different things.

They are correct. Donation money and operational money are different things. This is true. It is also exactly the kind of sentence that sounds like logic and means *pay us what we said.*

The persuasion went poorly. The monks had their position. Zalthir had his. The monks had a better position. The price landed at a hundred and forty gold, which is the number that ends the conversation on their terms.

So Zalthir pulled Daz over to argue.

Grygum, watching this, observed that if it were Whistler, he'd steal the roof tiles. Which is funny, and also the kind of observation that tells you exactly where you are: in a city where the options are *pay the shakedown*, *argue the shakedown*, or *steal the roof and call it even*. There's a version of me who would have picked the third. I'm trying to work out whether that version of me is the smarter one.

Daz, being Daz, found the fourth option.

The monks looked at him with that specific expression Waterdeep gives a drow it wasn't expecting — surprised, recalibrating, not hostile, but clearly doing the arithmetic on how he ended up here with a dragonborn and an orc and a dwarf. Zalthir told them, with a straight face, that Daz was very good with roofs.

And then Daz cast Mending.

The monks, somewhat sheepishly, provided the letter.

That's the thing about Daz. He doesn't argue the position or steal the tiles. He removes the problem the position was built on. I've watched him do this in drow politics with load-bearing lies that held entire negotiations together by changing what people thought was true. Watching him do it to a leaky roof is almost restful. Same instinct. Different scale.

A rock in the wrong place is just a rock. A rock in the right place is a wall. The giants used to say something like that. Daz would say something like that too, if he ever wasted words on maxims, which he doesn't.

We found the street preacher in the Field Ward.

Ragged, persistent, addressing whoever walked by with the practiced volume of someone who'd been doing this long enough that the words had stopped requiring thought. *The kraken under the keep! The kraken under the keep! The chant will break, and the kraken will rise!*

I listened.

There was something in it. Not the words, exactly — or not just the words. Something underneath the rhythm. I got the distinct impression he was right about something he could not name. I couldn't name it either. But I know what it feels like when someone is standing on correct ground without knowing why, and this man was standing on it.

Then he saw Daz.

The chant broke. Just — cracked, mid-repetition, like something had seized his throat and rerouted it: *"You came up! You came up, you will go again. You will go down again. Do not—"* and then back into the loop. *The kraken under the keep, the kraken under the keep, the chant will break, and the kraken will rise.*

The broken rhythm. The interrupted thought that cycles back without landing. I'd heard this once before, in Triboar, from a man who'd spoken in two voices at once — one warning and one underneath it, trying to say something the warning kept cutting off. Same shape. Different words.

I didn't say anything. The pattern was there and I was watching it assemble itself from pieces that didn't yet fit, and I know better than to speak before I know what I'm saying. Giants hold things a long time before speaking. I'm trying to remember that.

Grygum started working through it aloud — *keep, Candlekeep, are we above water, yes we are, well that makes the kraken more likely* — which is the kind of sentence that sounds absurd and might be exactly right. He is frequently exactly right while sounding absurd. He has a gift for it.

We're going to Candlekeep. The preacher said *keep* and meant something. A man in Triboar said a warning in two voices. Both of them spoke in the same broken loop.

I don't know what the kraken is yet. But I know what it feels like to have the ground under your feet say something your mouth doesn't need to say yet.

It said: *pay attention.*

## Grygum — The Ooze Rights Confrontation

The bag never left my sight. Not really. Even sitting on Thorin's back, even carried by Zalthir, even crammed into a crowded Waterdeep street — I was always running a thread of attention back to where Glabbagool was. Old habit, from the Underdark. Once you've carried something fragile through the dark long enough, you stop being able to turn that off.

We had managed several days before anyone took official notice. The watch, when they finally did, sent a man with the look of someone performing a duty he found personally undignified — the kind of public official who has rehearsed authority so long he'd forgotten why it was supposed to matter. He wanted to open the bag.

I was noting the exits when Thorin stepped forward.

He does this. I've seen him face down duergar, stone giants, a genuinely very angry dragon. He brings the same posture to all of them: feet planted, chin forward, a quality of weight that his giant-raised frame has made second nature. He put that posture in front of the guard and announced, more or less, that we were discussing ooze rights today.

The guard did not appear to have anticipated this.

Thorin turned to the sword at his hip, because of course he did. "Dawnbringer — are we a team here? Where does Dawnbringer stand on whose rights?"

Dawnbringer has opinions. I have learned, over the course of knowing this sword, that Dawnbringer has opinions about almost everything, and they are delivered with the certainty of a creature who has been waiting centuries for a decent conversation partner. The sword's answer came quickly: sentience is more than mere existence. Then Dawnbringer found the fully formed version of the argument, the one that cut straight to the structural concern.

"What? One day they're going to ban oozes, the next day they'll ban sentient swords!"

Thorin said, "That's a lot." He was agreeing, I think. Affirming, even.

"Low blow," I said. I wasn't wrong. As political arguments go, the slippery-slope-from-oozes-to-sentient-blades is not rigorous. But it is vivid, and it landed on the crowd gathering at the edges of this spectacle like a well-placed torch. Thorin confirmed they were on the same page. Dawnbringer, for its part, seemed satisfied.

The guard, I noted, had the expression of a man revising his understanding of how his morning was going to go. He was not hostile. He was simply outmatched by the situation. You could see him recalculating — this wasn't a threat he had a procedure for. These were just very committed, possibly unwell individuals who had strong feelings about ooze personhood, one of whom was arguing through a sword.

He looked at Thorin. He said, haltingly, something about whether we needed to take him to a cleric.

"I'm a cleric," I said.

He looked at me. "Is he with you?"

"Some days he's a —" I started, and did not finish, because some sentences don't need to close.

Thorin helpfully volunteered that Dawnbringer could offer lesser restoration. Dawnbringer appeared to confirm this, eagerly. The guard's face passed through several distinct phases of bewilderment.

That was when Zalthir, who had been standing at the edge of the crowd with the stillness of a man doing absolutely nothing in particular, simply ceased to be present. He did not run. He did not sneak. He just stepped through the moment the way he does — folded out of the visible world and back into it elsewhere, with the bag, leaving no meaningful trace except a small atmospheric discontinuity and the absence of one large piece of luggage.

The guard noticed.

There was a pause. Then the guard looked at Thorin and said, with a weariness I recognized as the voice of someone choosing their battles wisely: *I think you may want to walk away.*

Thorin finished his coffee. Then he walked away. There is a kind of dignity in that. I have always thought so.

The crowd dispersed, mostly, but not all of it. There were people at the edges — I had watched them throughout — and some of them had been nodding. Not many. But enough. The idea had been said aloud on a Waterdeep street by a dwarf with conviction and a talking sword, and some ideas, once said, don't unsay themselves. They move through cities the way water does. Finding the cracks. I filed it under *seeds* and let it go.

Glabbagool was fine. I checked. This is what I do.

Thorin's instinct, once the immediate problem was resolved, was to do something with the momentum — not politically, just practically. He wanted to take Dawnbringer somewhere useful. He said he wanted to find orphans and heal them; that he and the sword needed to do that together, specifically. Not as a crusade. As a way of being in the same room with something good.

Dawnbringer, I'm told, was very happy about this.

Zalthir was in immediately. I was in. We spent some time in the parts of the city that don't usually receive visiting clerics and their parties, where people have learned not to expect much from strangers. Dawnbringer's light, channeled through Thorin's hands, is particular — it's warm in a way that doesn't feel borrowed, and the children who received it didn't flinch from it. That matters. A lot of them flinch.

I took notes. It's what I do.

Bahamut, somewhere in the structural fabric of things, was perhaps reading the same set. Either way, the notes exist. Either way, the children had been healed.

Glabbagool was still fine. I checked again. We had not carried him this far to leave him to anything.

## Daz — Onward to Daggerford

The road south of Waterdeep smells like salt and distance.

I have been walking it for days now, and I find myself doing what I always do when there is nothing immediate to solve: I run the ledger. The Duskryn sisters are gone. House T'sarran's mage is ash. The extraction contract is closed on their end, though not, I suspect, on T'sarran's. That house will send someone else. They always do. What they want is not me — I have been clear-eyed about this since Kaelira said it plainly, and I will not pretend otherwise. They want the patron. The unknown entity that, for reasons I cannot reconstruct from any angle I have tried, decided that keeping me alive was worth Menzoberranzan rates.

I keep auditing the question. I keep coming up short.

*Someone powerful paid substantially to protect a man she could see was nobody special.* Kaelira had said it without cruelty — just assessment, which is worse. And I had managed, *"So then maybe there's more to me than meets the eye?"* The most restrained possible response. I was proud of the restraint for approximately thirty seconds, and then I was alone with the question again.

I literally have no idea why there's all this focus on me.

I didn't reframe it when I said that to the party. I didn't file it. I just said it. Which is new. Which is, if I'm being precise, the part that bothers me more than the mystery itself.

The Sword Coast rolls past in shades of green and grey. Somewhere behind us, Waterdeep recedes. Somewhere ahead, Candlekeep and Khell-Vire wait with their archives and their five questions and whatever answer has been sitting in the dark between the stacks, patient as a trap.

Milo Goodbarrel, in chapter eleven of his annoyingly readable book, described this feeling as *standing at the edge of the question that changes everything.* He meant it as an encouragement. I have been trying, for most of a week, to decide whether it is.

Grygum broke my auditing somewhere on the second day's walk with the particular energy of a man who has been waiting to show you something.

"Oh," he said, with the satisfaction of a scholar who has located a citation, "I figured out one of the things I want to buy."

He had, apparently, acquired it before we left Waterdeep — a javelin of lightning, produced from wherever Grygum keeps things he is pleased with, displayed to the group with the reverence of a cleric presenting a relic. Which, in his theology, it possibly was. The man will find a way to make any weapon feel like a sacrament. I noted it. Filed it under *Grygum: ranged options now exist, adjust formation assumptions accordingly.* 

Milo would have said something warm about it. I noted the placement.

The road keeps producing stories. The Ardragon. The Whistler. Some hero — the details shift with every telling, because that is what happens to names when they travel far enough from their origins. Each village we pass through has its own version, its own embellishments. By the third or fourth iteration, I stopped tracking the inconsistencies and started paying attention to the shape of the thing: what people need the story to mean, which is never quite what happened.

I know something about names that travel too far from their origins.

The Ember Vanguard is a rumor network entry now, apparently. Celebrated in Blingdenstone in a form none of us would recognize. Wanted in Gracklstugh under descriptions inaccurate enough to be useful. The party line is: deny it. I am in favor of this policy. A legend that precedes you is a resource and a liability simultaneously, and the ratio shifts depending on who's asking.

I did not mention this to anyone. I made a note.

We arrive at Daggerford in the early evening, and it is a settlement the way a crossroads is a destination — useful by virtue of being on the way to somewhere else. The smell of river mud and tallow candles. Low buildings. The kind of town that generates stories about itself mostly to have something to talk about during winter.

There is the ordinary hum of a settlement going about its business. Someone is healing someone. Grygum has probably already found them. I take a moment to locate the angles — entrances, exits, who is watching the watching — and find nothing that requires immediate attention, which I treat as provisional rather than reassuring.

Candlekeep is ahead. Khell-Vire is ahead. The answer to the question I can't reconcile is ahead, somewhere in the stacks, if we ask the right thing of the right book.

I am, for the first time in longer than I care to calculate, genuinely uncertain what I will find when I get there.

I file that too, under a category I haven't named yet.
