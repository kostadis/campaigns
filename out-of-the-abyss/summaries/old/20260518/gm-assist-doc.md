# Chapter 52 Candlekeep's Cold Case: The Grygumite Gambit



## Grygum — The Immortal Chambers And The Question Of The Age

The West Inner Ward smells like old paper. People here take themselves seriously, and the walls have absorbed it. Brother Vareth moves through it like a man who has been navigating institutional gravity for forty-one years and knows which way to lean into it.

I pay attention to his feet.

The Immortal Chambers have a tile floor with one deity symbol per tile, and the problem is immediately apparent. You have to choose which deity you want to step on. I try to watch where Vareth steps and follow in the same path, which is the correct policy, and also exactly the kind of procedural compromise that a strong Stroudite would say proves the whole Bahamutian theory.

I file that thought for later.

We are briefly interrupted by Pizwog, who is an ancient tortle and moves at the speed of an ancient tortle. I wait. He distributes what appears to be a religious tract, which turns out to be one of his own research papers — a syncretic analysis of different religions across the Sword Coast, deeply theoretical, internally inconsistent in ways I can tell are meaningful without yet being able to specify how, and written in the style of a man who has strong views about what the word *however* is for.

"Is it on the merits of doing things slowly, or…" I ask.

It is not.

"Academics," I say, and tuck it into my satchel. I tell Pizwog I will read it in due time. This is sincere. Vareth, behind me, has the expression of a man exercising tremendous institutional restraint across approximately five minutes of tortle handoff. He remains respectful throughout. I note this. It costs him something.

---

His side office is small and full. He has been keeping this corner of the library for forty-one years, he says, and he knows what a fashion looks like.

This is not a fashion.

He has prepared this speech. I can tell because he launches it without looking at his notes.

Libraries are arguing. Taverns are arguing. Chapter houses are arguing. The courts of two cities have asked for written opinions. Three duels have been fought at Candlekeep over which polemic shelf gets the larger window. *Three*, in a library where nobody has dueled in two hundred years. Sister Yvenne threatens to confiscate everyone's bookplates. She has not yet, but he expects it.

"Yes," I say, settling into the chair. "The question of our age, of course."

He tells me what it actually is, underneath the chess game and the dwarf and the partisan colors. What is being asked, he says, is this: what kind of universe do we live in?

He lays out three schools.

The Stroudite school says mortal choice is sovereign. The gods exist. The dragons exist. Providence may or may not exist. But the choosing belongs to the chooser, and is not borrowed, and is not loaned from any throne above or below. Tiamat was denied her power because a dwarf chose. The chain of choices that followed was a chain of free acts. Strike out anyone, and the chain falls.

"Yes," I say. "The choice belongs to the chooser. This is a principle I can get behind."

He nods, with the patience of a man who expected exactly this, and continues.

The Bahamutian school says divine providence prevails. Bahamut moved his pieces beforehand. The mortals served as instruments. Choice was real, but not sovereign. Stroud chose what he was fitted to choose. The chain was an arranged chain.

Then — remembering that I travel with a dragonborn and apparently blanking on the name for a moment — he gets to the third. The Draconic school says ancient agency rules. Protanther honored Stroud's move because Protanther is honorable, not because Bahamut compelled him. The metallic dragons followed Protanther because they trust their elders. The choosing is real, but the scope of the choosing is what the dragons permit.

Three answers, he says. Three universes. They cannot all be right at the same time.

I am sitting with this — genuinely sitting with it, which I do not always do when someone presents me with a structured argument, because usually the structure is doing the work of hiding something — when I notice that none of them quite fit what I actually think. Which is inconvenient, because I had not previously been aware that I actually thought anything about this.

"But… but what if there's a fourth?"

He stops.

"What if there are certain fixed points around which our actions must revolve? And the closer we come to one of those fixed points, the more constrained we are, but between them, perhaps we have a great deal of freedom of action."

The moment of utter panic that crosses his face is brief and genuine.

"A *fourth* school of thought?"

"Hi," I say. "Have you considered the other possibilities?"

What happens next is difficult to describe with total scholarly precision. Vareth is excited. He dismisses the Bahamutians. He waves off the dragons. He says things about quadrangles and the center of circles described by three points, and then — this is the part that lands — he pauses and says that a god who wished to control the outcome of mortal lives would not need to compel a single hand. She would only need to shape the rules of the evidence. The form of polemic. The architecture of a chapter house.

I think about Ilvara's prayer book. The Zuggtmoy marginalia. The theological document from Asha Vandree's chain mail — the Lolth/Zuggtmoy equivalence, written in a hand that wanted very much to seem incidental. The quiet. The architecture.

"Yes," I say. "The universe can be silent and indifferent outside of the fixed framework, and then as we approach it, yes, it gets silent and it gets *conspiratorial* to compel us toward the invariance."

He is articulating the theory back to me now — someone had to arrive at a particular point, and at that point their choices were constrained, but who would arrive there was unconstrained — and I am nodding because yes, that is exactly it.

"Aye! It could have been Stroud, our great philosopher. It could have been, say, another party of adventurers."

I think about Stroud making the decisive move — the perfect throw, the unrepeatable instant — against a dragon who had spent four hundred years studying that exact position. The universe, silent and conspiratorial. Someone had to sit down across from Protanther. The chess game didn't care who.

Vareth calls it a compromise of the Bahamutian school. He calls me a weak Stroudite — a strong Stroudite holds there are no fixed points at all — and a strong Bahamutian around fixed points, and a weak Bahamutian outside of them. He names this the strong-weak hypothesis with the satisfaction of a man who has just filed something correctly.

Then he hands me a stack of Stroudite papers, barely able to contain himself, and rushes off to begin his own research.

"I'll give you a marked-up version when I'm done," I say.

He is already half out the door.

I sit in the office with his papers and Pizwog's research tract and the thought that I just proposed a school of theological thought by accident, and that an aging scholar with forty-one years in this corner of the library is now treating it as the question of the age.

"I feel like I handled that competently," I say, to no one in particular.

The Grygum school of thought. Filed under: things that started as offhand remarks. Bahamut may or may not have been watching. I took notes either way.

## Thorin — Dinner at the Refectory

The Refectory was built for scholars, which meant the ceiling was high enough that a hill giant could have sat comfortably at one end and not embarrassed herself. I noticed that first — the ceiling — before I noticed anything else. Months underground will do that to you. You stop expecting sky, stop expecting space, and then a room gives you both and your whole chest does something involuntary.

We found seats at a long table in the middle of the hall, away from the high table where the eight Great Readers sat like they were posing for a tapestry. I watched them the way the giants taught me to watch a disputed ridge line: settle in, say nothing, let the information come to you. The Underdark had bent that instinct a little — the patience was still there, but now it came wrapped in the awareness that a tunnel can reverse on you while you're standing still.

The room was loud with people who knew they were important. Candlekeep scholars don't chat. They debate at conversation volume.

I tried reading the room the right way — not just where people were sitting, but what they meant by it. One of the Great Readers had a cup that kept getting refilled. Drunk, visibly, in the way that wasn't an accident. Another looked genuinely unwell, the particular gray of someone who had pushed through several days of bad sleep and hadn't yet admitted it to themselves. In the far corner, two readers sat with their heads angled together, voices below audible, the kind of hushed conversation that usually means either something has gone very wrong or something is about to.

I said as much to the others.

And then a hooded figure from the Avowed went past the dining hall at a half-run, something tucked against their ribs under the robe. They weren't trying to be obvious about it. They also weren't trying very hard not to be. In the Underdark that would have been something to move against — I would have already been on my feet, already measuring the gap between me and the exit. Here, I just watched them go and clocked it.

The prophecies had been going the whole time, floating down from the gallery above like water finding its level. Vareth had explained it to Grygum earlier in the day, and Grygum had explained it to the rest of us in that careful way he has, like he was building a theological argument out of practical facts: there was a man named Alaundo whose prophecies had come true with uncomfortable regularity, so Candlekeep had been reciting them ever since. Whenever one came true, it was removed. What remained was the unfulfilled inventory. Nobody knew what happened when the inventory ran out.

I thought about that for a moment. The giants used to say that a wall is just a rock in the right place. A prophecy that hasn't happened yet is just a warning waiting for its moment. I didn't say that out loud. It wasn't useful.

Most of the recited lines slid past me. Background noise. The kind you stop hearing in a tavern after the first round. Then one of them came down from the gallery and didn't slide past.

*On the longest night of winter's deepest cold, the keeper falls, the heart is sold.*

It landed. I sat with it for a second. Winter's deepest cold. I looked at Daz. I looked at Grygum. Neither of them had visibly reacted. Either they hadn't heard it, or they'd done what I was already doing: kept it.

I kept it.

We finished dinner without incident, which at Candlekeep seemed to mean finishing dinner with five simultaneous conversations happening around you at academic register and a drunk Great Reader who had graduated to the stage of laughing at things no one had said. Normal, probably. For here.

Back at the House of Rest, Glabbagool was in a state. The grey ooze had been confined to the bag of holding for most of the day, which in hindsight was probably like keeping a puppy in a closet during a thunderstorm — technically fine, genuinely unkind. Whatever Glabbagool had been doing in the bag all day — thinking, absorbing, trying to formalize whatever Zalthir had been patiently teaching him into monastic discipline — he was done with it. He wanted out.

I didn't know what *out* meant for an ooze. Neither did he. But the want was there.

Yep. Been there.

I found a corner of the hearth room, set Dawnbringer against the wall within reach — close but not drawn, which had become its own negotiation over the weeks — and sat down. I was tired. Not the sleep kind. The fire was going. The ceiling was still high enough. Above us, in a gallery I couldn't see, someone was still reciting.

*The keeper falls.*

I didn't say anything. The fire was warm. That was enough for tonight.

## Daz — A Grim Awakening

The knock came before the light did.

I was awake before Fembris got through the second syllable — not because I sleep lightly by preference, but because a month in the Underdark had rebuilt my nervous system around that precise calibration: a sound that doesn't belong, at an hour that doesn't invite it. The surface world had given me a bed and a ceiling with actual architecture, but it hadn't yet convinced my body that nothing was coming through the walls.

The voice on the other side of the door was young, breathless, and badly in need of a pause. *Quick! The first reader sent me! The Keeper of Tomes is dead! She wants you! Now, through the emerald door, before the avowed awake!*

I was already cataloguing before the sentence finished. The Keeper of Tomes was dead. The First Reader wanted us specifically, not the Avowed, not the city watch — us, through the emerald door, before the avowed awake. That clause was doing a great deal of work. *Before the avowed awake* meant she wanted this contained. It also meant she thought we were the right instrument for containing it. The question of why she thought that was, I noted, a question I would need answered.

Grygum opened the door. I heard his voice land somewhere between inquiry and bracing for bad news: "The Keeper of Tomes?"

The messenger confirmed it. He was commoner-born, I could see that much — enthusiastic in the way of someone who has been trusted with important information for the first time and found the experience clarifying. Boyish. Very slightly delighted by his own urgency, which I didn't hold against him. He looked like someone who had sprinted across several corridors of very old stone and intended everyone to notice.

I began dressing. The running account in the back of my head was already sorting columns: one entity dead, position of institutional significance, first reader bypassing standard channels, party summoned by name, timing engineered to precede institutional awareness. In Menzoberranzan, that combination resolved to one thing: someone wanted the scene read before it was managed. The surface world ran different politics, but the bones of information control looked the same everywhere. Whoever wanted us through that emerald door was working a timeline.

Grygum, to his credit, was moving. He said something about not having thought through what orcs wear to bed, which struck me as honest and also not the logistical priority it probably felt like from the inside. I filed it in the column marked *Grygum is processing the morning* and kept dressing.

We were all rested, at least. Properly, fully rested — the kind that had felt almost suspicious after the Underdark, where a full night's sleep had been a luxury good and rarely in stock. Candlekeep's beds had done their work. Whatever was waiting through the emerald door, we weren't walking into it depleted.

Glabbagool had been watching from the corner with the patient attentiveness he'd developed since acquiring eyes. When the party began to move, he assessed the situation — door open, party dressed, urgency established, no visible threat — and made his decision with the unhurried pragmatism of a creature that has lived inside a bag of holding before. He flowed toward the bag, arranged himself inside it, and peered out from the opening with two eyeballs tracking the room. Ready. Equipped. The spy somewhere inside him, presumably also equipped.

I watched him do it and thought: *at least we have that.*

The messenger was already moving back toward the corridor, gesturing for us to follow. Somewhere inside Candlekeep, the Keeper of Tomes was dead, the First Reader was waiting, and the Avowed were still asleep. The window was narrowing with every minute we spent in this room.

I picked up my notes.

## Zalthir — The Tour of the Bastion

The snow was unexpected. Not alarming — just a texture I had no frame of reference for after months below stone. Candlekeep's grounds were blanketed in it, white and silent, the kind of silence that had nothing hunting inside it. Fembris picked his way up the stairs carefully, testing each step. I followed without testing. Snow was new. Stairs were not.

He swept an arm toward the courtyard with the pride of someone who had memorised the gesture along with the words.

"Behold, the great library."

I beheld. Exits: three visible archways, one service passage, one guarded door to the north. Foot traffic: light, scholars moving in pairs, nobody moving fast. No elevated positions. No obvious coverage points. The exaltation bastion rose above us and I filed it as the dominant structure, tallest fixed point, and moved on.

The Astronomicon caught my eye first — a stack of blue globes, ascending in scale, pale against the white sky. Fembris gestured at it with the enthusiasm of someone who had given this tour many times and still meant it.

"You see that curious blue stack of globes? That's the Astronomicon. Everything we know about the stars can be found there."

I noted it. Surface navigation, if we needed that. Everything the Underdark doesn't have.

The Whispering Dome sat further along, gold under the snow — striking, even by surface standards. Fembris pointed it out as part of the Hall of Momentous Deeds, explained that voices from the past lingered there, that you could hear them if you stood in the right place. He said *inspired* the way people say it when they want you to be.

Then the Drakonoikos.

I noticed it before he named it. The structure was built from scales — actual scales, enormous, overlapping — and bones worked into the walls where another building would have used stone. Fyrentennimar, Fembris finally produced the name after a pause and a visible effort, dragon of some age and significance, now a wall. The early light hit the scales. They held it.

"Those scales really gleam in the sunrise," Grygum said. "That's amazing."

He wasn't wrong. I had been thinking the same thing and had not said it. There was a monastic logic to the building: the thing that was dangerous becomes the thing that contains the knowledge of how dangerous it was. I wasn't sure the monks at the Garden Shadow would have approved of the metaphor, but Brother Heslin — or possibly Brother Arrath, I was never certain which of them ran the theory sessions on repurposed threat — had said something along those lines once. I had been watching the window.

Fembris warned us about the Dragon Breath Simulation Chamber inside. One of the Avowed had died in it, he said, which was why the Keeper's permission was now required. His tone suggested we should find this discouraging.

"That sounds like a great game to play," Grygum said. "Later."

"I've still got plenty left in me," he added, more quietly.

I said nothing. The chamber existed, the danger was real, and one of us had already decided it was theoretical. This was the same register in which Thorin had once described demon lord proximity as *ominous sounding fun*. I filed it and kept walking.

The interior of the bastion was a different kind of assessment. High ceilings — good. Multiple corridors branching at irregular intervals — less good. Oil paintings on every staircase, stern-faced sages in various states of disapproval, busts on pedestals at each landing. The tapestries were heavy and could conceal anything or nothing. I checked as we passed.

Fembris climbed with increasing effort. The rest of us were not experiencing any comparable difficulty. The Underdark does not offer gentle grades; what it offers is drop-offs, uneven rubble, tunnels that require you to crawl for twenty minutes before opening into a space large enough to stand, ledges over nothing where the fall is long and the landing is not the concern because something will have found you by the time you get there. A few staircases in a library was not a test of anything.

We arrived at the Chapter House. A purple-robed watcher stood at the entrance, assessed us briefly, and stepped aside. No challenge. They had been told to expect us.

Fembris's legs were done. Mine were not, and I was aware of the gap — not with pride, just as a data point. The surface had weather and stairs and buildings made of dead dragons. The Underdark had other things. After months of training under conditions the Garden Shadow had never specified in their curriculum, a few flights was nothing.

I noted the Chapter House entrance: single guard, single visible point of entry, no secondary exit apparent from the doorway. Someone had positioned us here deliberately.

I stepped inside.

## Grygum — The Council of Twelve in Uproar

The Chapter House was exactly as described — twelve chairs around a twelve-sided table, rose window throwing colored light across the stone floor, western gables pressing down overhead like a building that took itself seriously. I took notes on the seating arrangement before anyone had finished shouting.

Which they were doing with commitment.

"We must have the vote and restore leadership to Candlekeep." That was A'lai Aivenmore — tall, handsome, braided beard, the kind of smooth presentation that means either he's been waiting for this moment or he's very good at pretending he hasn't.

"First, we must determine if Janussi named a successor—" The dowdy halfling with the pince-nez. She adjusted her glasses as she said it. Filing a motion in a burning building. I noted that.

"First, we must find the murderer!" The slender human with the heavy wrinkles, voice sharp enough to cut. She at least had the right priority. I gave her a small note in the margin.

"The gate warden needs to be dismissed with immediate effect! This happened on HIS watch!" That was the bronze dragonborn in the bejeweled robes — formidably sized, beautifully tailored, entirely aware of both facts. He had a theory and he'd come prepared to prosecute it.

And then Daral — gray slick-backed hair, thin lips doing something sour and disappointed at the same time: "why isn't Sylvira Savikas here?"

Bookwyrm was trying to hold it together. Green dragonborn, shaken, weary in the way you get when you've been awake since someone died and also people keep talking. "Let's be calm ourselves…"

I said, not quite helpfully: "Well, probably because she's feeling sick, still."

Daral's eyes found me. The whole table found me, actually. A group of academics in full crisis protocol is still, at its core, a group of academics. They cataloged us immediately — orc, dragonborn, dwarf, drow — and sorted us into the correct drawer: *arrivals. unknown provenance. examine.*

Daral opened his mouth to ask the obvious question, which is who we were and why we were in this room, and I heard something in that question I recognized. The implication underneath was sharper — *who gave you access, and should I object to them.*

"Oh, merely, merely guests," I said. And then I added: "I am the head of the Grygumite School." A beat. "Founder, in fact."

I will say this for academics: they are helplessly interested in new schools of thought. The murder — the murder of their colleague, the chaos and the unanswered questions — all of it receded slightly in the face of *an unfamiliar framework*. Several of them leaned in. One pulled out something to write on. It was exactly as reliable as I expected.

Then someone at the table — I was tracking the bronze dragonborn and missed which one asked — noted that Brother Khell-Vire had mentioned Zalthir possessed a unique skill and seemed to want an elaboration. Zalthir, being Zalthir, said: "Do you have a request to go on?" Which is not, technically, a deflection — it just makes the other person aware they're asking without a good reason. The council member pushed: "I was hoping we would learn more about your unique skill." I stepped in.

"I thought we were here to learn about a death."

Bookwyrm said, with the tone of someone who has said this sentence before in faculty meetings and never fully recovered from needing to: "FOCUS, PEOPLE! Focus! Focus! Focus! Focus! Focus!"

They sat down. Credit where it's due.

Bookwyrm proposed delegating the investigation to us. She used language about fate — "seekers who the gods have sent us" — which is the kind of language you deploy when you want people to accept something they'd normally reject. I noted her for the column marked *capable*.

I said: "It could be one of those fixed points," which was either theological framing or agreeing with her rhetorical approach, and I didn't much mind which one the table heard.

Daral, of course: "This is ridiculous, we don't know them from Alaundo. They look like a bunch of sell swords to me!"

Daz made the case — Underdark investigations, multiple factions, fungal scourges, the whole abbreviated catalogue of what we'd actually done over the past months. Thorin noted that he and his wife had been watching a great deal of Poirot lately. I added: "And I have found a new philosophy of the universe." Thorin said we were students of the great Masters Poirot and Colombo, and I said, "Well, just one more thing," which landed exactly as well as I'd hoped. Dawnbringer, housed in her scabbard, took the opportunity to note that we must surely be the greatest discoverers of all, having found her tomb after two thousand years. Thorin clarified, accurately, that he hadn't been around for that part.

I am not used to being auditioned when someone wants me to help them. But I took notes on the experience.

Then Thorin asked about remuneration.

The silence was beautiful. The academics looked at one another. They had never, in their professional lives, been asked to pay for something by the person they were asking. "Stipend," Thorin said. "Honorarium." He proposed one book each. Bookwyrm said the library was not in the habit of giving out books. Thorin said we were not in the habit of investigating for free.

Bookwyrm was diplomatic about it — *suitable remuneration, upon successful conclusion, dignity and support* — and I thought about the architecture of that sentence for a moment. Vague, but workable. "Vague, but… but maybe we should take it," I said. And then I noticed the actual value on the table.

"See, this gives us an excuse to poke our nose in everywhere. That's valuable."

Thorin pushed for carte blanche — full access, anywhere in the library, anything. The council looked at us. We had just asked for something they had not realized they were protecting, and the request had landed harder than the murder. I watched the red-cheeked wizard with the shrewd gray eyes. I watched A'lai Aivenmore calibrate. I read the room the way I read a Bahamut text — not for what it says, but for what it is conspicuously not saying.

"We need an all-access library card," I said.

Thorin added, with the serenity of someone who already knew the answer: "Are we supposed to investigate if we can't poke around?"

A'lai Aivenmore decided that the fastest resolution was the best one, and that he was not in a position to judge Bookwyrm's choices, and that leadership restoration was in everyone's interest. He did not say he thought it was a good idea. He said he didn't see a reason to object. I filed that distinction.

Bookwyrm agreed. One book each, copied at the House of the Binder, upon successful completion. Confidentiality papers first. "Let's do it," I said.

Then Kalan Strongbranch walked in.

I recognized him — the gray-white beard, the piercing blue eyes, the posture of someone who expects to be listened to and usually is. He'd been the one who wanted to put Daz in irons when we arrived. Before he could speak, Bookwyrm cut him off with the precision of someone who had prepared that sentence in advance: his troubled relationship with the keeper of tomes, the need for impartial investigation, the council's decision, focus on securing the defenses, no creature enters or leaves.

He took it. He bowed. The bow was correct and the eyes were not.

He was the first person I planned to question. I didn't say that until he'd left the room.

Bookwyrm walked us toward the chamber where the investigation would begin, and as she did, she gave us the piece she'd been holding back: Janussi, the morning before his death, had asked Kalan to tender his resignation. Mental faculties. Negligence. She declined to elaborate — she didn't want to prejudice the investigation, she said. Daz pointed out, reasonably, that her testimony was itself evidence. Thorin said she should be examined.

Bookwyrm paused. Not a long pause. Just long enough to weigh something. "Everyone should be examined," she said. "But I can assure you, I had nothing to do with it."

Thorin, immediate: "Where were you?"

I did not write down what I thought her expression meant when he asked that. But I took notes.

## Daz — Arriving at the Crime Scene

The staircase was narrow enough that we had to go single file, which meant Bookwyrm set the pace and I had no choice but to listen.

"I think it's important that you know," she said, without turning, "that yesterday morning, Janussi asked the gate warden to tender his resignation. The Keeper was concerned that Kalan was losing his mental faculties and becoming negligent in his work."

She delivered it like a gift. Information, freely offered, before anyone had thought to ask. I filed that immediately — not the content, the timing. People who volunteer the right fact at the right moment usually know which facts they're not volunteering.

"What type of work was he negligent in?" I asked.

"I don't want to prejudice your investigation. I'm sure if you examine the evidence—"

I mean, that's a deflection dressed as courtesy. "Investigating," I said, keeping my voice even, "part of investigating is getting input from everybody we talk to. If you don't want to tell us, then we—"

"No, no, I don't want to prejudice! I'm sure if you examine the evidence." She said it again, which meant she'd decided on it as a position.

Thorin, to his credit, cut straight to the structural problem. "But your testimony is evidence." A beat. "Maybe you are evidence, and we should examine you."

That landed. She paused — actually paused, mid-step — and something moved across her face before she smoothed it back. "Everyone should be examined," she said. "But I can assure you, I had nothing to do with it."

Thorin asked where she was.

She didn't answer before we arrived at the tower.

*Everybody that did it says that,* I did not say aloud.

---

The staircase spiraled up through the tower's interior — reception chamber at the first landing, a shrine of Oghma at the second, a large apartment at the third with its door left conspicuously ajar. I noted the apartment. Noted that the door was open. Filed it for later.

We found the cat on the stairs between floors.

Gray. Motionless. Yellow eyes that opened the moment we got close. The cat had decided it need not move for anyone, and tracked us anyway. Bookwyrm stepped around her. She had clearly done this many times. She preferred not to think about why.

Thorin asked about it. Bookwyrm paused and offered what I can only describe as a theological disclaimer: nobody had been able to conclusively prove the cat was some kind of power that lived here, and nobody had been able to conclusively prove that it was not. So the cat stayed where the cat wished.

"That's kind of how cats always are, I think," I said, because it is.

Fembris had been quietly bursting since the second landing. He finally broke. He turned to us and announced that these Avowed had taken a cat sitting wherever it liked and turned it into an interstellar, interspatial, interdimensional horror — that's just what cats do.

Thorin allowed that the cat could be a familiar, or a shapeshifter, and that we should remain open to every possibility. Fembris restated his position: it's just a cat.

Zalthir said, very quietly, "Is it?"

Fembris went still. Reconsidered. If Bookwyrm was scared of the cat, maybe he should be scared of the cat. I watched him reach that conclusion in real time and found I didn't disagree with the method — presented with sufficient weight, even nothing can become something.

---

Tadric was guarding the door: short, human, young, bowl-cut hair, the look of someone who takes his post seriously and is currently experiencing the professional anxiety of having his post become considerably more significant than expected. Bookwyrm told him Kalan had been relieved and that the four of us would be taking over. Then she turned on her heel: report back as soon as we were done. Remove nothing from the room.

She departed for other business.

Fembris offered himself as our adjutant, enthusiastically, with the word *anything* doing a lot of work. Then he actually looked at us — the drow, the dragonborn, the dwarf, the half-orc — and quietly revised his definition of anything toward the legal end of its range. Things like bathrooms. Rooms of a particular description.

"May not be the same," I agreed.

---

We questioned Tadric for a timeline. He gave us one. Janussi found dead by the Great Reader A'lai Aivenmore and the housekeeper, one Miss Hollypocket, around half past seven in the morning. Gate Warden arrived with Tadric around eight, having spoken briefly with both discoverers. Then: Kazryn Nyantani, distraught, shortly after eight — allowed to see the body but only from the doorway. Fheminor Scrivenbark at half past eight, appearing shocked, wanting to inspect the chamber, turned away. Bookwyrm at nine, arriving from the baths, allowed a brief look before confirming the existing orders. The Gate Warden again at eleven, alone, for a full inspection — careful, deliberate, left to make his report to the Council.

Tadric noted that Kalan looked more determined coming out than going in.

Zalthir asked whether Tadric had been watching carefully. Tadric admitted his assignment had been crowd control. Not observation. The embarrassment landed on his face as he said it — he had just understood his job was smaller than the job that needed doing. Making sure only the right people came in and out. He ran through the list again, and then stopped himself, and asked if he'd done a good job.

He had not done a good job. But I didn't say that either.

What I noted: the Gate Warden had been back inside alone, with a stated goal of full inspection, and had walked out looking like a man who had found something. Tadric hadn't been watching closely enough to track what he'd done in there.

That was a gap. Gaps were interesting.

On the question of speaking with the dead: Tadric confirmed it had been tried. The dead had not spoken. Someone — presumably the Gate Warden, before his removal — had already used the rite on the corpse, which meant we'd be waiting if we wanted to try again. Long enough that Thorin observed the corpse would be somewhat fragrant by then and asked about ice.

There was, somewhere in all of this, something deliberate. The rite attempted and failed. The Gate Warden removed before he could be asked what he'd found. A door left ajar on the third floor landing.

I hadn't been inside yet. That was where I needed to be.

## Thorin — Investigating the Keeper's Chambers

The Bookwyrm's parting instructions had been clear enough: report back when done, remove nothing from the room. Short sentence. No ambiguity. I respected that. Clear orders are one of the things I still like about the surface world.

Tadric was standing guard at the door when we arrived — human, young, holding himself the way people do when they're trying to look like they're not rattled. I had questions. I always have questions when something has gone wrong and the people nearest to it are standing very still.

"What is the timeline? Who found them? Has anybody actually left the crime scene since they were found?"

He walked us through it. A'lai Aivenmore and a servant called Miss Hollypocket found Janussi dead around half past seven in the morning. Tadric himself arrived with the gate warden at eight. Then the weeping woman — Kazryn, apparently — showed up shortly after, wanting to see the body. Then Fheminor Scrivenbark at half past eight, stern, shocked, wanting to inspect the chamber but turned away. Then the Bookwyrm at nine, in from the baths, taking a brief look before confirming the gate warden's orders. And finally the gate warden himself back at eleven for a full investigation — careful, Tadric said, not to disturb the evidence.

"Did it look like he discovered something?" I asked. About the gate warden.

"He definitely looked more determined after he walked out."

That was worth noting. Determined is different from satisfied. Determined means he found something and hadn't decided what to do with it yet. I noted it.

Zalthir pushed on whether Kalan had touched anything. Tadric admitted his job had been crowd control, not surveillance — watching who came in and out, not watching what they did once they were inside. He heard himself say it. The realization landed on his face. He should have been doing both. He seemed like a decent person. I didn't say anything about the gap in the protocol. It wasn't going to help him now.

We went in.

The room was circular, ringed with lancet windows, full of pale morning light that had gone slightly yellow and hazy from something I placed almost immediately: tobacco smoke. Lingering. The windows were shut. Nobody had been smoking recently. The smoke was old.

I clocked that and kept moving.

The body was near the desk. Janussi — around seventy, white robes, vacant eyes aimed at nothing. The robes were soaked dark around the torso. His rib cage had been caved in. There was a hollow in his chest where something used to be.

My read, first pass: "So it looks like a robbery — like somebody raided the safe, and what they were looking for is more valuable than blue gemstones?"

The safe on the far wall had been opened. Blue gems and empty pouches scattered on the floor in front of it. The thief had looked at the gems, decided they weren't what he came for, and left them. Whatever he took, it wasn't jewelry.

Fembris took one look at the body, crossed the room in four steps, flung a window open, and vomited out of it. A gust of wind moved through the room. Nothing in the evidence shifted. He retreated outside and didn't come back. I didn't hold it against him. Most people, if they're honest with themselves, are not prepared for a caved-in rib cage first thing on a Tuesday.

The desk was neatly kept, which was itself a data point. The chair near it was toppled. The marble statue and its pedestal were on the floor. The candelabra — seven arms, bronze — had burned its candles entirely to wax puddles. A spellbook sat open in the center of the desk: The Discoveries. To one side, notes in Celestial, generic academic work, someone musing on unfamiliar workings and how they might be performed. Janussi's own research, most likely. To the other side, a larger pile of notes pinned under a quartz weight, with more annotations on top in midnight blue ink — and below those, older notes in purple. Dry. The blue was recent. The purple, less so.

A quill on the floor. Nib covered in midnight blue. A wicker basket nearby holding a linen ink carrier, slightly damp, which had been the vessel for the ink now pooling from the spilled pot.

"Well, it said that there was ink spilled. Is that the ink pot that was spilled?"

It was. Simple question. Somebody had knocked it over. The quill had gone with it. Neither the ink nor the quill had been moved since.

The book the blue ink was annotating — The Golden Ass — wasn't in the room. Whatever Janussi had been working on most recently, writing about in the freshest ink, on the most recent night of his life, was gone. That felt deliberate in the way that things feel deliberate when you stop trying to find an innocent explanation for them.

I came back to the tobacco smoke while the others were working the desk.

"What is — is there a source for the tobacco that we can see? Because I would have expected… what time is it now?"

After eleven, I was told.

"What happened between seven-thirty and eleven, where the tobacco smoke is still here?"

Nobody smokes in a sealed room and leaves smoke that lingers for hours unless the room has been sealed for hours. The candles told the same story. I said it out loud: "That tells me that it had been some time since he was murdered, because there was enough time for the candle to burn out."

Tadric confirmed it from the doorway: Miss Hollypocket would never have allowed the Keeper to let candles burn down to nothing. Full stop. It wasn't her way. Which meant Janussi had been dead before the candles finished. Which meant the murder happened well before anyone found him.

"So likely, he was murdered quite some time ago."

Right, Tadric said.

Then I crouched over the body.

"How did they appear to die?" And then, before anyone could answer that: "Was this cut neatly with a sharp scalpel-like thing, or was it, like, torn out with somebody's bare hands?"

Zalthir looked more carefully than I did. He's better at the precise reading of things — the monk's eye for controlled force. His verdict: the rib cage had been smashed by something like an axe, multiple blows, close together, deliberate. The heart was missing. No other organs. And the blood was localized. Wrong pattern for what should have been a violent death. Far less than the wound demanded.

"The question: was he dead before his heart was hacked out?"

Zalthir agreed. It tracked.

"Is there any blue — blood splatter around? Is it directional? Can we tell the velocity?"

The pattern suggested he might have been dead before the chest was opened. Which meant two things happened: something killed him, and then someone opened him up afterward. The missing heart wasn't the cause of death. It was a message. Or a precaution.

Grygum named the precaution out loud, and once he said it I couldn't believe I hadn't thought of it first: you can't bring someone back from death by ritual if their vital organs aren't present. Whoever took the heart wasn't souvenir-collecting. They were preventing resurrection. They wanted Janussi to stay dead in every sense that mattered.

Which meant — I thought this through in order, because the Underdark taught me to think through implications in order — someone had tried to make him permanently inaccessible. But not necessarily inaccessible in every way.

"Is there any way that we can speak to what remained of him, even though they don't have all their parts? I mean, we don't need the heart — we just need the brain to be able to talk to him, you know."

Tadric said they'd tried that. The dead were not speaking.

"Wait, who tried when? The city watch guy — did he try and, like, spoken to the dead?"

Yes. The gate warden. On his careful, evidence-preserving inspection.

"Gotta interview that dude."

Such workings have narrow limits: once performed, the same corpse cannot be reached again for some time. Which meant either the gate warden had performed it and gotten nothing, or someone else had used it first and the gate warden hadn't known it was already spent.

"So if we wait long enough, then we can try again?"

Yes.

"Corpse will be a little stinky by then." I let that sit for a moment. "Is there any ice around?"
