[Scene 1] The Immortal Chambers And The Question Of The Age
Narrator: Grygum
Focus: Grygum's radical Bahamut theology — and his unspoken suspicion that Bahamut is "boring" next to Stroud — makes him the only voice for a scene where Vareth lays out three schools of cosmic agency and Grygum casually proposes a fourth. He would frame the conversation through the tension between performed faith and the genuine doubt he has never said aloud, and through the disquieting moment when an aging scholar treats his offhand idea as the question of the age.

- Brother Vareth escorts Grygum to the West Inner Ward, arriving at the Immortal Chambers — described as a tile floor with one deity symbol per tile and green rectangular roofs. Grygum notes the risk of choosing which deity to step on, and tries to watch where Vareth steps so he can follow in the same path.

- Pizwog, an ancient tortle librarian, shuffles past at approximately one mile per hour and distributes what appears to be a religious tract but turns out to be one of his own dense research papers — a syncretic analysis of different religions across the Sword Coast. Grygum notes that Vareth looks irritated and annoyed but remains respectful during the approximately five-minute handoff. Grygum tucks it into his satchel and promises to read it in due time.

- Vareth leads Grygum to his side office and explains he has been keeping this corner of the library for forty-one years, and he knows what a fashion looks like — this is not a fashion. Libraries are arguing. Taverns are arguing. Chapter houses are arguing. The courts of two cities have asked for written opinions. Three duels have been fought at Candlekeep over which polemic shelf gets the larger window — three, in a library where nobody has dueled in two hundred years. Sister Yvenne threatens to confiscate everyone's bookplates.

- Vareth lays out the three dominant schools of thought regarding the Question of the Age: the Stroudite school (mortal choice is sovereign — Tiamat was denied her power because a dwarf chose, and the chain of choices that followed was a chain of free acts); the Bahamutian school (divine providence prevails — Stroud chose what he was fitted to choose, the chain was an arranged chain); and the Draconic school (ancient agency rules — Protanther honored Stroud's move because Protanther is honorable, not because Bahamut compelled him, and mortals operate in the frames the dragons set).

- Grygum proposes a fourth school of thought involving fixed points where choices are constrained, while remaining free in between, describing it as a grand compromise. He notes that it could have been Stroud at that fixed point — or it could have been another party of adventurers — but someone would have arrived there.

- Vareth is ecstatic, immediately labeling Grygum a "weak Stroudian and strong Bahamutian around fixed points, and a weak Bahamutian outside of fixed points" — and declaring this the "strong-weak hypothesis." He speculates about a philosophical quadrangle and the center of a circle described by the three existing schools. He theorizes that a god who wished to control the outcome of mortal lives would not need to compel a single hand — she would only need to shape the rules of the evidence, the form of polemic, the architecture of a chapter house.

- Vareth provides Grygum with a collection of Stroudite notes and papers to study, barely able to contain himself as he rushes off to begin his own research. Grygum promises to return a marked-up copy when he is done.

- Vareth calls the compromise closer to the Bahamutian school than the Stroudite, and classifies Grygum as a weak Stroudite — a strong Stroudite would hold that there are no fixed points at all.
