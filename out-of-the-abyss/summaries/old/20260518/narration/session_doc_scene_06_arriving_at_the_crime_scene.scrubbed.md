The staircase was narrow enough that we had to go single file, which meant Bookwyrm set the pace and I had no choice but to listen.

"I think it's important that you know," she said, without turning, "that yesterday morning, Janussi asked the gate warden to tender his resignation. The Keeper was concerned that Kalan was losing his mental faculties and becoming negligent in his work."

She delivered it like a gift. Information, freely offered, before anyone had thought to ask. I filed that immediately — not the content, the timing. People who volunteer the right fact at the right moment usually know which facts they're not volunteering.

"What type of work was he negligent in?" I asked.

"I don't want to prejudice your investigation. I'm sure if you examine the evidence—"

I mean, that's a deflection dressed as courtesy. "Investigating," I said, keeping my voice even, "part of investigating is getting input from everybody we talk to. If you don't want to tell us, then we—"

"No, no, I don't want to prejudice! I'm sure if you examine the evidence." She said it again, which meant she'd decided on it as a position.

Thorin, to his credit, cut straight to the structural problem. "But your testimony is evidence." A beat. "Maybe you are evidence, and we should examine you."

That landed. She paused — actually paused, mid-step — and something moved across her face before she smoothed it back. "Everyone should be examined," she said. "But I can assure you, I had nothing to do with it."

Thorin asked where she was.

She didn't answer before we arrived at the tower.

*Everybody that did it says that,* I did not say aloud.

---

The staircase spiraled up through the tower's interior — reception chamber at the first landing, a shrine of Oghma at the second, a large apartment at the third with its door left conspicuously ajar. I noted the apartment. Noted that the door was open. Filed it for later.

We found the cat on the stairs between floors.

Gray. Motionless. Yellow eyes that opened the moment we got close, tracking us with the particular economy of a creature that has decided it need not move for anyone. Bookwyrm stepped around her with the practiced delicacy of someone who had done this many times and preferred not to think about why.

Thorin asked about it. Bookwyrm paused and offered what I can only describe as a theological disclaimer: nobody had been able to conclusively prove the cat was some kind of power that lived here, and nobody had been able to conclusively prove that it was not. So the cat stayed where the cat wished.

"That's kind of how cats always are, I think," I said, because it is.

Fembris, who had been maintaining the politely suppressed energy of a man who desperately wanted to comment on something, finally broke. He turned to us and announced that these Avowed had taken a cat sitting wherever it liked and turned it into an interstellar, interspatial, interdimensional horror — that's just what cats do.

Thorin allowed that the cat could be a familiar, or a shapeshifter, and that we should remain open to every possibility. Fembris restated his position: it's just a cat.

Zalthir said, very quietly, "Is it?"

Fembris went still. Reconsidered. If Bookwyrm was scared of the cat, maybe he should be scared of the cat. I watched him reach that conclusion in real time and found I didn't disagree with the method — presented with sufficient weight, even nothing can become something.

---

Tadric was guarding the door: short, human, young, bowl-cut hair, the look of someone who takes his post seriously and is currently experiencing the professional anxiety of having his post become considerably more significant than expected. Bookwyrm told him Kalan had been relieved and that the four of us would be taking over. Then she turned on her heel: report back as soon as we were done. Remove nothing from the room.

She departed for other business.

Fembris offered himself as our adjutant, enthusiastically, with the word *anything* doing a lot of work. Then he actually looked at us — the drow, the dragonborn, the dwarf, the half-orc — and quietly revised his definition of anything toward the legal end of its range. Things like bathrooms. Rooms of a particular description.

"May not be the same," I agreed.

---

We questioned Tadric for a timeline. He gave us one. Janussi found dead by the Great Reader A'lai Aivenmore and the housekeeper, one Miss Hollypocket, around half past seven in the morning. Gate Warden arrived with Tadric around eight, having spoken briefly with both discoverers. Then: Kazryn Nyantani, distraught, shortly after eight — allowed to see the body but only from the doorway. Fheminor Scrivenbark at half past eight, appearing shocked, wanting to inspect the chamber, turned away. Bookwyrm at nine, arriving from the baths, allowed a brief look before confirming the existing orders. The Gate Warden again at eleven, alone, for a full inspection — careful, deliberate, left to make his report to the Council.

Tadric noted that Kalan looked more determined coming out than going in.

Zalthir asked whether Tadric had been watching carefully. Tadric admitted, with the particular embarrassment of someone who had suddenly understood that his job was smaller than the job that needed doing, that his assignment had been crowd control. Not observation. Making sure only the right people came in and out. He ran through the list again, and then stopped himself, and asked if he'd done a good job.

He had not done a good job. But I didn't say that either.

What I noted: the Gate Warden had been back inside alone, with a stated goal of full inspection, and had walked out looking like a man who had found something. Tadric hadn't been watching closely enough to track what he'd done in there.

That was a gap. Gaps were interesting.

On the question of speaking with the dead: Tadric confirmed it had been tried. The dead had not spoken. Someone — presumably the Gate Warden, before his removal — had already used the rite on the corpse, which meant we'd be waiting if we wanted to try again. Long enough that Thorin observed the corpse would be somewhat fragrant by then and asked about ice.

There was, somewhere in all of this, something deliberate. The rite attempted and failed. The Gate Warden removed before he could be asked what he'd found. A door left ajar on the third floor landing.

I hadn't been inside yet. That was where I needed to be.
