The Chapter House was exactly as described — twelve chairs around a twelve-sided table, rose window throwing colored light across the stone floor, western gables pressing down overhead with the particular weight of a building that takes itself seriously. I took notes on the seating arrangement before anyone had finished shouting.

Which they were doing with commitment.

"We must have the vote and restore leadership to Candlekeep." That was A'lai Aivenmore — tall, handsome, braided beard, the kind of smooth presentation that means either he's been waiting for this moment or he's very good at pretending he hasn't.

"First, we must determine if Janussi named a successor—" The dowdy halfling with the pince-nez. She adjusted her glasses as she said it. Filing a motion in a burning building. I noted that.

"First, we must find the murderer!" The slender human with the heavy wrinkles, voice sharp enough to cut. She at least had the right priority. I gave her a small point in my internal ledger.

"The gate warden needs to be dismissed with immediate effect! This happened on HIS watch!" That was the bronze dragonborn in the bejeweled robes — formidably sized, beautifully tailored, entirely aware of both facts. He had a theory and he'd come prepared to prosecute it.

And then Daral — gray slick-backed hair, thin lips doing something sour and disappointed at the same time: "why isn't Sylvira Savikas here?"

Bookwyrm was trying to hold it together. Green dragonborn, shaken, weary in the way you get when you've been awake since someone died and also people keep talking. "Let's be calm ourselves…"

I said, not quite helpfully: "Well, probably because she's feeling sick, still."

Daral's eyes found me. The whole table found me, actually. A group of academics in full crisis protocol is still, at its core, a group of academics. They cataloged us immediately — orc, dragonborn, dwarf, drow — and sorted us into the correct drawer: *arrivals. unknown provenance. examine.*

Daral opened his mouth to ask the obvious question, which is who we were and why we were in this room, and I heard something in that question I recognized. The implication underneath was sharper — *who gave you access, and should I object to them.*

"Oh, merely, merely guests," I said. And then I added: "I am the head of the Grygumite School." A beat. "Founder, in fact."

I will say this for academics: they are helplessly interested in new schools of thought. The murder — the murder of their colleague, the chaos and the unanswered questions — all of it receded slightly in the face of *an unfamiliar framework*. Several of them leaned in. One pulled out something to write on. It was exactly as reliable as I expected.

Then someone at the table — I was tracking the bronze dragonborn and missed which one asked — noted that Brother Khell-Vire had mentioned Zalthir possessed a unique skill and seemed to want an elaboration. Zalthir, being Zalthir, said: "Do you have a request to go on?" Which is not, technically, a deflection — it just makes the other person aware they're asking without a good reason. The council member pushed: "I was hoping we would learn more about your unique skill." I stepped in.

"I thought we were here to learn about a death."

Bookwyrm said, with the tone of someone who has said this sentence before in faculty meetings and never fully recovered from needing to: "FOCUS, PEOPLE! Focus! Focus! Focus! Focus! Focus!"

They sat down. Credit where it's due.

Bookwyrm proposed delegating the investigation to us. She used language about fate — "seekers who the gods have sent us" — which is the kind of language you deploy when you want people to accept something they'd normally reject. I noted her for the column marked *capable*.

I said: "It could be one of those fixed points," which was either theological framing or agreeing with her rhetorical approach, and I didn't much mind which one the table heard.

Daral, of course: "This is ridiculous, we don't know them from Alaundo. They look like a bunch of cell swords to me!"

Daz made the case — Underdark investigations, multiple factions, fungal scourges, the whole abbreviated catalogue of what we'd actually done over the past months. Thorin noted that he and his wife had been watching a great deal of Poirot lately. I added: "And I have found a new philosophy of the universe." Thorin said we were students of the great Masters Poirot and Colombo, and I said, "Well, just one more thing," which landed exactly as well as I'd hoped. Dawnbringer, housed in her scabbard, took the opportunity to note that we must surely be the greatest discoverers of all, having found her tomb after two thousand years. Thorin clarified, accurately, that he hadn't been around for that part.

I am not used to being auditioned when someone wants me to help them. But I took notes on the experience.

Then Thorin asked about remuneration.

The silence was beautiful. The academics looked at one another with the particular expression of people who have never, in their professional lives, been asked to pay for something by the person they were asking. "Stipend," Thorin said. "Honorarium." He proposed one book each. Bookwyrm said the library was not in the habit of giving out books. Thorin said we were not in the habit of investigating for free.

Bookwyrm was diplomatic about it — *suitable remuneration, upon successful conclusion, dignity and support* — and I thought about the architecture of that sentence for a moment. Vague, but workable. "Vague, but… but maybe we should take it," I said. And then I noticed the actual value on the table.

"See, this gives us an excuse to poke our nose in everywhere. That's valuable."

Thorin pushed for carte blanche — full access, anywhere in the library, anything. The council looked at us the way you look at someone who has just correctly identified which window you left unlocked. They were uncomfortable in the specific way of people who are being asked to give something they had not realized they were protecting. I watched the red-cheeked wizard with the shrewd gray eyes. I watched A'lai Aivenmore calibrate. I read the room the way I read a Bahamut text — not for what it says, but for what it is conspicuously not saying.

"We need an all-access library card," I said.

Thorin added, with the serenity of someone who already knew the answer: "Are we supposed to investigate if we can't poke around?"

A'lai Aivenmore decided that the fastest resolution was the best one, and that he was not in a position to judge Bookwyrm's choices, and that leadership restoration was in everyone's interest. He did not say he thought it was a good idea. He said he didn't see a reason to object. I filed that distinction.

Bookwyrm agreed. One book each, copied at the House of the Binder, upon successful completion. Confidentiality papers first. "Let's do it," I said.

Then Kalan Strongbranch walked in.

I recognized him — the gray-white beard, the piercing blue eyes, the posture of someone who expects to be listened to and usually is. He'd been the one who wanted to put Daz in irons when we arrived. Before he could speak, Bookwyrm cut him off with the precision of someone who had prepared that sentence in advance: his troubled relationship with the keeper of tomes, the need for impartial investigation, the council's decision, focus on securing the defenses, no creature enters or leaves.

He took it. He bowed. The bow was correct and the eyes were not.

He was the first person I planned to question. I didn't say that until he'd left the room.

Bookwyrm walked us toward the chamber where the investigation would begin, and as she did, she gave us the piece she'd been holding back: Janussi, the morning before his death, had asked Kalan to tender his resignation. Mental faculties. Negligence. She declined to elaborate — she didn't want to prejudice the investigation, she said. Daz pointed out, reasonably, that her testimony was itself evidence. Thorin said she should be examined.

Bookwyrm paused. Not a long pause. Just long enough to weigh something. "Everyone should be examined," she said. "But I can assure you, I had nothing to do with it."

Thorin, immediate: "Where were you?"

I did not write down what I thought her expression meant when he asked that. But I took notes.
