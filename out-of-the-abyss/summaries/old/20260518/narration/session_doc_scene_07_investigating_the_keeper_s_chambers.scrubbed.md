The Bookwyrm's parting instructions had been clear enough: report back when done, remove nothing from the room. Short sentence. No ambiguity. I respected that. Clear orders are one of the things I still like about the surface world.

Tadric was standing guard at the door when we arrived — human, young, holding himself the way people do when they're trying to look like they're not rattled. I had questions. I always have questions when something has gone wrong and the people nearest to it are standing very still.

"What is the timeline? Who found them? Has anybody actually left the crime scene since they were found?"

He walked us through it. A'lai Aivenmore and a servant called Miss Hollypocket found Janussi dead around half past seven in the morning. Tadric himself arrived with the gate warden at eight. Then the weeping woman — Kazryn, apparently — showed up shortly after, wanting to see the body. Then Fheminor Scrivenbark at half past eight, stern, shocked, wanting to inspect the chamber but turned away. Then the Bookwyrm at nine, in from the baths, taking a brief look before confirming the gate warden's orders. And finally the gate warden himself back at eleven for a full investigation — careful, Tadric said, not to disturb the evidence.

"Did it look like he discovered something?" I asked. About the gate warden.

"He definitely looked more determined after he walked out."

That was worth noting. Determined is different from satisfied. Determined means he found something and hadn't decided what to do with it yet. I filed it.

Zalthir pushed on whether Kalan had touched anything. Tadric admitted his job had been crowd control, not surveillance — watching who came in and out, not watching what they did once they were inside. He said it the way someone says something when they've just realized they probably should have done both. He seemed like a decent person. I didn't say anything about the gap in the protocol. It wasn't going to help him now.

We went in.

The room was circular, ringed with lancet windows, full of pale morning light that had gone slightly yellow and hazy from something I placed almost immediately: tobacco smoke. Lingering. The windows were shut. Nobody had been smoking recently. The smoke was old.

I clocked that and kept moving.

The body was near the desk. Janussi — around seventy, white robes, vacant eyes aimed at nothing. The robes were soaked dark around the torso. His rib cage had been caved in. There was a hollow in his chest where something used to be.

My read, first pass: "So it looks like a robbery — like somebody raided the safe, and what they were looking for is more valuable than blue gemstones?"

The safe on the far wall had been opened. Blue gems and empty pouches scattered on the floor in front of it. The thief had looked at the gems, decided they weren't what he came for, and left them. Whatever he took, it wasn't jewelry.

Fembris took one look at the body, crossed the room in four steps, flung a window open, and vomited out of it. A gust of wind moved through the room. Nothing in the evidence shifted. He retreated outside and didn't come back. I didn't hold it against him. Most people, if they're honest with themselves, are not prepared for a caved-in rib cage first thing on a Tuesday.

The desk was neatly kept, which was itself a data point. The chair near it was toppled. The marble statue and its pedestal were on the floor. The candelabra — seven arms, bronze — had burned its candles entirely to wax puddles. A spellbook sat open in the center of the desk: The Discoveries. To one side, notes in Celestial, generic academic work, someone musing on unfamiliar workings and how they might be performed. Janussi's own research, most likely. To the other side, a larger pile of notes pinned under a quartz weight, with more annotations on top in midnight blue ink — and below those, older notes in purple. Dry. The blue was recent. The purple, less so.

A quill on the floor. Nib covered in midnight blue. A wicker basket nearby holding a linen ink carrier, slightly damp, which had been the vessel for the ink now pooling from the spilled pot.

"Well, it said that there was ink spilled. Is that the ink pot that was spilled?"

It was. Simple question. Somebody had knocked it over. The quill had gone with it. Neither the ink nor the quill had been moved since.

The book the blue ink was annotating — The Golden Ass — wasn't in the room. Whatever Janussi had been working on most recently, writing about in the freshest ink, on the most recent night of his life, was gone. That felt deliberate in the way that things feel deliberate when you stop trying to find an innocent explanation for them.

I came back to the tobacco smoke while the others were working the desk.

"What is — is there a source for the tobacco that we can see? Because I would have expected… what time is it now?"

After eleven, I was told.

"What happened between seven-thirty and eleven, where the tobacco smoke is still here?"

Nobody smokes in a sealed room and leaves smoke that lingers for hours unless the room has been sealed for hours. The candles told the same story. I said it out loud: "That tells me that it had been some time since he was murdered, because there was enough time for the candle to burn out."

Tadric confirmed it from the doorway: Miss Hollypocket would never have allowed the Keeper to let candles burn down to nothing. Full stop. It wasn't her way. Which meant Janussi had been dead before the candles finished. Which meant the murder happened well before anyone found him.

"So likely, he was murdered quite some time ago."

Right, Tadric said.

Then I crouched over the body.

"How did they appear to die?" And then, before anyone could answer that: "Was this cut neatly with a sharp scalpel-like thing, or was it, like, torn out with somebody's bare hands?"

Zalthir looked more carefully than I did. He's better at the precise reading of things — the monk's eye for controlled force. His verdict: the rib cage had been smashed by something like an axe, multiple blows, close together, deliberate. The heart was missing. No other organs. And the blood was localized. Wrong pattern for what should have been a violent death. Far less than the wound demanded.

"The question: was he dead before his heart was hacked out?"

Zalthir agreed. It tracked.

"Is there any blue — blood splatter around? Is it directional? Can we tell the velocity?"

The pattern suggested he might have been dead before the chest was opened. Which meant two things happened: something killed him, and then someone opened him up afterward. The missing heart wasn't the cause of death. It was a message. Or a precaution.

Grygum named the precaution out loud, and once he said it I couldn't believe I hadn't thought of it first: you can't bring someone back from death by ritual if their vital organs aren't present. Whoever took the heart wasn't souvenir-collecting. They were preventing resurrection. They wanted Janussi to stay dead in every sense that mattered.

Which meant — I thought this through in order, because the Underdark taught me to think through implications in order — someone had tried to make him permanently inaccessible. But not necessarily inaccessible in every way.

"Is there any way that we can speak to what remained of him, even though they don't have all their parts? I mean, we don't need the heart — we just need the brain to be able to talk to him, you know."

Tadric said they'd tried that. The dead were not speaking.

"Wait, who tried when? The city watch guy — did he try and, like, spoken to the dead?"

Yes. The gate warden. On his careful, evidence-preserving inspection.

"Gotta interview that dude."

Such workings have narrow limits: once performed, the same corpse cannot be reached again for some time. Which meant either the gate warden had performed it and gotten nothing, or someone else had used it first and the gate warden hadn't known it was already spent.

"So if we wait long enough, then we can try again?"

Yes.

"Corpse will be a little stinky by then." I let that sit for a moment. "Is there any ice around?"
