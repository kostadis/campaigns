## Grygum — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The insect plague hit like a wave breaking through a cracked hull — one moment the cavern was loud with shouting and the clatter of steel, and the next it was *screaming*. Not from us. From everything between us and Ilvara.

I felt the swarm before I saw it. A wall of noise, of biting, chittering fury, the kind of sound that bypasses the thinking part of the brain and goes straight to the older, smaller part that just says *run*. I did not run. I want to note that. But I was also very, very aware of exactly how many insects were currently conducting a theological inquiry into the structural integrity of my skin.

Ilvara had conjured it at the cave entrance, a sphere of plague large enough to catch the whole middle ground between us — me, Daz, and, as I registered with a cold sinking feeling, Glabbagool. Our faithful, wobbling, occasionally conversational gelatinous cube companion, who had been attempting to participate in the fight with the earnest commitment of someone who was very new to fighting and very sincere about it.

"Oh, how about Glabbagool?"

That was me. That was the question I asked first, before I assessed my own position, before I checked the tactical map for who was flanking whom. Glabbagool. Twenty-six points of damage, I was told. The cube had survived, churning and trembling in the swarm, but the number landed in my chest like a stone. Twenty-six points. We had not brought a gelatinous cube this far through the Underdark to lose him to biting insects conjured by a woman who had traded her dignity to a fungal demon lord.

Bahamut rewards the protection of the vulnerable. I'm not certain gelatinous cubes were what the Platinum Dragon had in mind. But I took notes on the principle, and the principle applies.

---

Jorlan was already moving.

I saw it happen — that particular quality of motion that isn't tactical, isn't strategic, is just *rage with legs*, a man crossing a battlefield toward the person who broke him. Ilvara. The woman who had looked at his scars and decided he was no longer useful. He shoved through the swarm like it wasn't there. Which, for a man running toward his former lover with the intention of expressing his feelings through bladed instruments, it genuinely wasn't.

I watched this. I considered my options. I considered what Bahamut would say about inserting a half-orc cleric into a drow lovers' quarrel at the height of an active combat in a fungal cavern. I concluded that Bahamut, wise beyond measure, would say: *this is between them*.

"I don't want to get in the middle of it, but I'll follow up if necessary."

The GM — and I mean the narrative fabric of the universe — seemed to agree. There's obviously some couples issues happening. I don't want to be between them. This is Bahamut's restraint, applied to interpersonal drow drama. I moved past Jorlan, kept my distance from whatever was unfolding between a scarred warrior and a corrupted priestess, and filed it under *situation: monitoring*.

---

It was around this time that Thorin descended from the elevated walkways.

I want to be honest about the Big Heart Fungus. The GM had *prepared* for it. You could tell. The icon was elaborate — lovingly crafted, positioned with care, clearly the centerpiece of an encounter that was going to demand something from us. I had been thinking about it. I had, in my methodical way, begun assembling my approach. The fungus among us. The Heart that had to be stopped. I had plans.

Thorin drove Dawnbringer into it.

Radiant light cracked outward through the cavern like a second sunrise. The massive creature — the whole elaborate, carefully designed creature — recoiled, clenched, and folded inward, spent. One round. Gone.

The sunblade does not know or care what anyone had planned.

"It's not resistant to swords," I said, which was both accurate and, by that point, entirely irrelevant.

"I do what I can," said Thorin.

The DM sat with this. We all sat with this. "I spent so much time creating this icon," he said.

"I know," I said. "It was beautiful. I liked it very much, Gustatus."

*Boy, I had so much prepared for the fungus.* I closed the file. I continued.

---

There was a moment — a genuine moment of sheepishness — when the fight churned on and bodies began dropping around us and I did an honest accounting of who had fallen and what we owed them, and I said:

"If anybody is too saddened by the loss of any of our minions, I do have a Revivify spell that I could use to bring one of them back."

Someone shouted immediately. Not our minions. *Her* minions. The fallen fungal servants belonged to Ilvara, not to us. Of course they did.

"We used to have so many people traveling with us," I said, "I forgot."

This is, I think, a reasonable accounting of what happens when you spend extended time in the Underdark with an expanding roster of companions, allies, prisoners, and one sentient gelatinous cube. The bookkeeping gets away from you. At some point the question of *whose* minion has collapsed becomes genuinely difficult to answer on the fly. I'm not embarrassed by this. I take notes. The notes had just not caught up with the situation.

---

I tried a Guiding Bolt at Ilvara. The bolt missed. I note this without rancor. Ilvara, battered from her forty-foot drop courtesy of Zalthir's extraordinary grip-and-release approach to aerial combat, was still moving, still conjuring, still calling down divine fire on Daz and Nym Duskryn with the focused fury of someone who had not yet accepted that this was over. The bridge cracked under her. Thorin's work. She leapt clear anyway.

A miss that bought time for the battle to grow worse before it got better. These are the outcomes you accept and continue from.

---

"Just when you thought everything was under control..."

The GM said this with a tone I recognized immediately. The tone of a person who has not, in fact, run out of material.

"Are there 3 more unnamed creatures?"

"5 Dark Elves, it looks like? 5 Drow?" Zalthir processed this with his characteristic meditative calm. "That's awesome."

"This is turning into the 12 days of Christmas," I said.

Five drow warriors emerging from the cavern shadows. The insect bloom still hanging in the air. The new arrivals were already inside the poison cloud's range. I could see it in their posture — the particular quality of someone who has committed to a dramatic entrance and is now realizing they have stepped into a catastrophe they had no part in making.

"Dark Elves!" I added, in a singing tone. The twelve days of Christmas. Five of them. Very irritated about the poison. Really had hoped to avoid the poison.

It's tough being a minion. This is a truth that applies universally and transcends factional allegiance. You show up to someone else's fight, you discover someone else's insect plague, and you spend the rest of the encounter just trying to manage consequences you didn't generate.

---

"More fungus among us?" I asked, when the next shape emerged from the shadows.

"No, no," the GM said. "No. It's different this time."

A drow mage. A blind lightning bolt that found nothing. Then Daz, who I have watched transform over these long months from a detached intellectual into someone who *refuses to be managed*, answered it with a fireball that lit the tunnel orange and erased the problem outright.

The session's running fungus joke, meeting a moment where the threat had genuinely changed shape.

---

And then Ilvara was still standing. Still radiating darkness and spite and the unmistakable spiritual signature of someone who has been promised the Underdark by a demon lord and is not prepared to surrender that claim without fully expressing her grievances.

"Ilvara seems like she's still trouble," I said, "so I'm going to cast a guiding bolt in that direction."

I was amused by the Jorlan-Ilvara drama. I want to be clear about that. The romantic betrayal. The scarring. The fury. The way he had broken from us to pursue her through an active insect plague. It was genuinely interesting. I filed it, appreciated it, and continued.

I am still going to go ahead and attack her.

The bolt found her this time. Radiant light. Clean and bright and final. Ilvara — Zuggtmoy's instrument, the corrupted high priestess of House Mizzrym, the reason we had been prisoners in the first place — folded and went still, and then, because the Underdark does not allow even death to be dignified, she did not simply fall.

She *erupted*. A cloud of poisonous spores, billowing outward from what had been a person. A woman who had traded herself to a fungal demon lord, and what was left was biology, not identity. Not even a body. Just spores.

"Not ideal," I said, immediately. "How big is the cloud?"

I was glad I was standing back. This is the whole of my commentary on the death of Ilvara Mizzrym. Pragmatic wisdom. I was standing back. The cloud was noted. I took notes.
