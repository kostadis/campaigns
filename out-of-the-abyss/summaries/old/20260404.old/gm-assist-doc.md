## Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

He announced it like it was barely worth saying.

*I'm just gonna try annoying her then.*

That was the plan. That was the whole plan. Ilvara Mizzrym, high priestess of House Mizzrym, Zuggtmoy's beloved instrument, corrupted vessel of a demon lord of fungal nightmare — and I was going to *annoy* her. The eldritch tendrils of the tattoo were already unreeling from my arm before the words finished leaving my mouth.

The cavernous space above the heart fungus was dark and vast and exactly the kind of silence I prefer to operate in. I crossed it fast, using the reach of the tentacles to close the distance before she had a chance to process what I was doing. The first strikes landed. Sixteen damage, the grapple secured.

*She's 16 damage and grappled, right?*

She was. I was holding onto her. That was the important part.

I do not, as a rule, enjoy monologue. I leave that to Daz, who has the cheekbones for it. What I enjoy is the moment when a thing is in your hands and you have not yet decided to let go of it. That moment. That is the thing I came to the monastery for, even if I did not know it when I arrived, even if I spent the first three years thinking I was there to learn to fight.

I dragged her up. Forty feet. The stone floor dropped away beneath us, and the enormous obscene mass of the heart fungus shrank below, and Ilvara was — for a moment — entirely subject to gravity and to me.

Then the dice agreed with me. Twice.

*Crit.*

I do not celebrate at the table. It is not my custom. But the second one surprised even me, and I recall something warm and undignified moving through my chest that I chose not to name.

*Oh, wow. Another crit.*

Forty feet up, gripping a corrupted high priestess, delivering consecutive critical strikes — and then I let her go. That was the point. The drop was always the point. The grapple was just delivery.

She hit the ground.

And then she looked up at me.

I have trained for years to read danger in the environment before I read it in the faces of creatures. The shape of the tunnel, the angle of a weapon, the particular quality of silence before something moves. I am rarely caught by what happens *after* the decisive action.

But Ilvara Mizzrym, bleeding, furious, struck from forty feet of free fall, still had something left. She reached into whatever dark place Zuggtmoy had hollowed out inside her and she *pushed* — not against my body, not against my grip, but against the part of me that the training does not fully reach.

*Roll a DC-14 Wisdom saving throw.*

Well. That didn't work.

The fear was immediate and specific and entirely unwanted. Not a general dread — not the crawling ambient horror of the Underdark that you learn to fold into background noise. This was targeted. This said: *she is down there, and you will not approach her, and you will not approach her, and you will not approach her.* Repeated. Insistent.

*You are frightened. Which is very convenient, because you cannot now approach her.*

*Indeed.*

I am a monk. My entire tactical vocabulary is built around being adjacent to the problem. Range is something other people deal with. Range is Daz's whole personality. I hit things. I grapple things. I drag things into the air and then drop them from inconvenient heights. All of that requires *proximity.*

And Ilvara's legendary reaction had, in one economical move, made proximity impossible.

I stayed where I was. Forty feet up. The frightened condition is not subtle — it is not a suggestion. I turned it over, the way you turn over a rule that has been applied against you, looking for the seam where the letter and the spirit diverge.

The spirit seems to be that I should do nothing to become closer to her.

Right.

*Alright. Fine.*

I am supposed to apply the rule honestly, even against my own interest. That is the monastic training operating at the level of self-adjudication, or so I was told during the years I spent thinking I was there to learn to fight. I spent a lot of time dismissing lessons that turned out to be useful. I am still working through the implications of that.

So I stayed up. I did not approach.

And the heart fungus, which had apparently been paying attention, chose this moment to remind me that fifteen feet of reach is a very generous number when the thing reaching lives on the floor and I have been hovering at the ceiling being useless.

It found me. The blow was enormous — the kind of damage that lands differently when you have no immediate recourse, when you cannot immediately close back in and return the favor. I deflected twelve of the bludgeoning. The rest I accepted. The poison damage I noted and filed away beside the indignity.

*Thanks, buddy.*

I said it quietly. I doubt anyone heard. It felt like the appropriate response — dry, clipped, and entirely sincere in its lack of sentiment. The heart fungus had earned its acknowledgment. It had done exactly what the situation called for, which is the most I ask of anything.

I remained where I was. Frightened. Grounded by something I could not approach. Watching from the ceiling while the rest of them worked.

It was not where I intended to be.

## Thorin — The Heart Fungus and the Drow Betrayal

The Heart Fungus stood at the center of the cavern like something that thought it had time. Massive, pulsing, ringed with spore-choked air and difficult terrain between me and it — a whole careful geography of annoyance. Ilvara had retreated into its shadow, and everything that mattered in this fight was on the wrong side of a cave full of fungal servants and an insect swarm that hadn't arrived yet but was coming, the way bad things always are in the Underdark. Coming, and then suddenly here.

I've been in enough fights now to know the shape of a bottleneck. The cavern was doing most of Ilvara's work for her — keeping us spread out, keeping me in particular at range from the thing I wanted to hit. I am, in my heart, a dwarf who wants to walk up to the problem and apply Dawnbringer to it until the problem stops. The Underdark keeps presenting me with long rooms.

So I descended from the elevated walkway and pushed through the terrain toward the heart fungus, and I did the sensible thing first: I asked if there was anything I needed to know about it.

"Is there anything I need to... anything I know about the fungus in terms of, like, is it, like, resistant to stuff or whatever?"

"It's resistant to the usual stuff that funguses are resistant to, but if you hit it hard enough, it should die."

Grygum, from somewhere behind me, helpful as always: "It's not resistant to swords."

And then the DM, with the particular cadence of a man setting up something he was proud of: "It actually is doubly not resistant to radiant damage."

Dawnbringer was already in my hand. She'd been in my hand since we walked in. She's not the sort of sword that waits politely in a scabbard when there's something this obviously in need of being hit with radiant light.

The answer I was looking for had been at the end of my arm the entire time.

I struck. And then struck again. The heart fungus wilted.

One round. That was all it got.

"And so you kill it. So it wilts."

"I do what I can."

There was a pause — the particular pause of a dungeon master looking at a carefully prepared battle map and making peace with what just happened.

"I spent so much time creating this icon."

"I know, it was beautiful," Grygum said.

"It was pretty," Zalthir agreed.

"I liked it very much, Gustatus."

The party offered their condolences, and I accepted them on the fungus's behalf. It had been a fine-looking icon. Big. Pulsing. Lovingly rendered. It deserved a longer fight than it got. These things happen. Dawnbringer doesn't negotiate, and she doesn't do gradual.

Then Ilvara cast Insect Plague.

The swarm hit the cavern entrance like a wall of sound and motion — a biblical chittering that shredded the fungal minions instantly, all thirteen hit points of them, gone before they could do anything useful. And then Grygum said what we were all thinking:

"Oh, how about Glabbagool?"

Twenty-six points of damage. The grey ooze took twenty-six points of damage from a spell he had nothing to do with, while he was hovering at the edge of the chaos doing his best to look like he was following the conversation. He'd been trying to participate. He had eyes extended toward us and everything. He survived, which is the important thing, but still — twenty-six points for a sentient ooze who just wanted to belong. The Underdark is not kind to bystanders.

I filed that away. I would be checking on Glabbagool when this was over.

Then Jorlan moved.

The scarred drow warrior had been fighting with us — or near us, which in a battle this chaotic amounted to roughly the same thing — and then Ilvara appeared wounded, and something older than tactics took over. He broke from position and charged toward her across the swarm-infested terrain, and the name that came out of him was not a battle cry.

"Dovara."

It came out like a wound reopening. Not rage exactly — rage is clean, rage has direction. This was something uglier and more honest than rage.

I had an opportunity attack. I stood there with Dawnbringer in hand and thought about it, which is not usually how I operate, but this particular situation had enough moving parts to give even me pause. He'd attacked us. That was true. He'd attacked us first, back at the beginning of this whole catastrophe, and you don't just set that aside because the political geometry shifted. On the other hand, he was now charging Ilvara, who we needed dead, which made him functionally useful at exactly the moment when we needed useful things.

"So, what happens if he kills her and we don't? Does that change anything? Does he get, like, first dibs on booty or something? And by booty, I mean, like... they used to be Lover's Booty, I mean, like... the goods."

The table responded to this with exactly the energy it deserved.

The DM explained the situation — Ilvara had dumped Jorlan when he became Scarface, because Drow society rewards beauty and punishes everything else, because Drow society is a whole list of things I find exhausting — and Zalthir, deadpan as ever, offered the only appropriate summary:

"The tides have turned."

Eight on the die. Eight says I do the sensible thing.

"Alright, so 8 says I do the sensible thing. Which I guess is let them go and let the enemies... let the girls fight."

I stepped aside and let Jorlan pass.

It's a strange thing, choosing not to act. Thorin Giantfriend, raised by creatures who settle disagreements by picking up boulders, standing in a fungal cave in the Underdark while a scarred drow with a grudge and an old love charges through an insect swarm toward his former partner. There's something that feels almost like mercy in it. There's something that feels almost like tactics. I'm not sure they're different.

The truth is I was mostly just relieved someone else was handling that particular problem. There is a specific kind of Underdark drama — the faction grudge, the Drow lover's quarrel, the old wound dressed up as ideology — that I have no useful purchase on. I understand hills. I understand stone. I understand the weight of a sword and the arc it needs to travel to hit something. I do not understand six layers of drow politics wrapped around a heartbreak, and I am not ashamed to say so.

I'm just sitting here watching them trade blows like a tennis match.

Sometimes the most useful thing a paladin can do is stay out of someone else's reckoning.

## Grygum — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The insect plague hit like a wave breaking through a cracked hull — one moment the cavern was loud with shouting and the clatter of steel, and the next it was *screaming*. Not from us. From everything between us and Ilvara.

I felt the swarm before I saw it. A wall of noise, of biting, chittering fury, the kind of sound that bypasses the thinking part of the brain and goes straight to the older, smaller part that just says *run*. I did not run. I want to note that. But I was also very, very aware of exactly how many insects were currently conducting a theological inquiry into the structural integrity of my skin.

Ilvara had conjured it at the cave entrance, a sphere of plague large enough to catch the whole middle ground between us — me, Daz, and, as I registered with a cold sinking feeling, Glabbagool. Our faithful, wobbling, occasionally conversational gelatinous cube companion, who had been attempting to participate in the fight with the earnest commitment of someone who was very new to fighting and very sincere about it.

"Oh, how about Glabbagool?"

That was me. That was the question I asked first, before I assessed my own position, before I checked the tactical map for who was flanking whom. Glabbagool. Twenty-six points of damage, I was told. The cube had survived, churning and trembling in the swarm, but the number landed in my chest like a stone. Twenty-six points. We had not brought a gelatinous cube this far through the Underdark to lose him to biting insects conjured by a woman who had traded her dignity to a fungal demon lord.

Bahamut rewards the protection of the vulnerable. I'm not certain gelatinous cubes were what the Platinum Dragon had in mind. But I took notes on the principle, and the principle applies.

---

It was around this time that Thorin descended from the elevated walkways.

Thorin drove Dawnbringer into the Heart Fungus. One round. *Boy, I had so much prepared for the fungus.* I continued.

---

There was a moment — a genuine moment of sheepishness — when the fight churned on and bodies began dropping around us and I did an honest accounting of who had fallen and what we owed them, and I said:

"If anybody is too saddened by the loss of any of our minions, I do have a Revivify spell that I could use to bring one of them back."

Someone shouted immediately. Not our minions. *Her* minions. The fallen fungal servants belonged to Ilvara, not to us. Of course they did.

"We used to have so many people traveling with us," I said, "I forgot."

This is, I think, a reasonable accounting of what happens when you spend extended time in the Underdark with an expanding roster of companions, allies, prisoners, and one sentient gelatinous cube. The bookkeeping gets away from you. At some point the question of *whose* minion has collapsed becomes genuinely difficult to answer on the fly. I'm not embarrassed by this. I take notes. The notes had just not caught up with the situation.

---

I tried a Guiding Bolt at Ilvara. The bolt missed. I note this without rancor. Ilvara, battered from her forty-foot drop courtesy of Zalthir's extraordinary grip-and-release approach to aerial combat, was still moving, still conjuring, still calling down divine fire on Daz and Nym Duskryn with the focused fury of someone who had not yet accepted that this was over. The bridge cracked under her. Thorin's work. She leapt clear anyway.

A miss that bought time for the battle to grow worse before it got better. These are the outcomes you accept and continue from.

---

"Just when you thought everything was under control..."

The GM said this with a tone I recognized immediately. The tone of a person who has not, in fact, run out of material.

"Are there 3 more unnamed creatures?"

"5 Dark Elves, it looks like? 5 Drow?" Zalthir processed this with his characteristic meditative calm. "That's awesome."

"This is turning into the 12 days of Christmas," I said.

Five drow warriors emerging from the cavern shadows. The insect bloom still hanging in the air. The new arrivals were already inside the poison cloud's range. I could see it in their posture — the particular quality of someone who has committed to a dramatic entrance and is now realizing they have stepped into a catastrophe they had no part in making.

"Dark Elves!" I added, in a singing tone. The twelve days of Christmas. Five of them. Very irritated about the poison. Really had hoped to avoid the poison.

It's tough being a minion. This is a truth that applies universally and transcends factional allegiance. You show up to someone else's fight, you discover someone else's insect plague, and you spend the rest of the encounter just trying to manage consequences you didn't generate.

---

And then Ilvara was still standing. Still radiating darkness and spite and the unmistakable spiritual signature of someone who has been promised the Underdark by a demon lord and is not prepared to surrender that claim without fully expressing her grievances.

"Ilvara seems like she's still trouble," I said, "so I'm going to cast a guiding bolt in that direction."

I was amused by the Jorlan-Ilvara drama. I want to be clear about that. The romantic betrayal. The scarring. The fury. The way he had broken from us to pursue her through an active insect plague. It was genuinely interesting. I filed it, appreciated it, and continued.

I am still going to go ahead and attack her.

The bolt found her this time. Radiant light. Clean and bright and final. Ilvara — Zuggtmoy's instrument, the corrupted high priestess of House Mizzrym, the reason we had been prisoners in the first place — folded and went still, and then, because the Underdark does not allow even death to be dignified, she did not simply fall.

She *erupted*. A cloud of poisonous spores, billowing outward from what had been a person. A woman who had traded herself to a fungal demon lord, and what was left was biology, not identity. Not even a body. Just spores.

"Not ideal," I said, immediately. "How big is the cloud?"

I was glad I was standing back. This is the whole of my commentary on the death of Ilvara Mizzrym. Pragmatic wisdom. I was standing back. The cloud was noted. I took notes.

## Daz — The Drow Reinforcements

The first bolt hit my shield ward before I'd consciously decided to cast it.

That's the thing about Shield — it's a reaction, which means the body decides before the mind does. One moment I was watching three Drow warriors step out of concealment with the particular ease of people who think they've caught something interesting in their sights. The next moment arcane force was already flaring around me, and the bolt that would have found my ribs clattered away into the dark instead. My hand was still raised. My breathing had not changed.

I spent a moment noting the look on their faces. That specific expression — the slight recalibration when the target doesn't go down. I find it useful to catalogue.

*Three Drow warriors. Crossbow formation. Tactical positions chosen in advance. Someone prepared for this.*

The cost was one spell slot. The benefit was that I was still standing. These are acceptable terms.

Then Kaelira's hand closed around my arm like a vice.

"Come with me if you want to live, you idiot."

I went. Not gracefully — she was hauling me northeast through the chittering wall of it while the poisonous gas did its work on both of us. She had my arm, she had the direction, and she had strong opinions about my continued survival that she was enforcing without my consent. Nym was already there, waiting.

Asha saw them at almost the same moment I registered where we'd ended up. Her hand moved toward her weapon. The energy of a zealot who has decided she is being betrayed — that tight, righteous recognition — came off her like heat.

"Who are you? You must be traitors! Traitors to Lolth!"

There was a window of perhaps two seconds before this became catastrophic.

"No, these are friends of Lolth, and they're here to help."

The words came out smooth. No hesitation, no announcement, no explanation. I have been maintaining a Lolth-follower identity with Asha Vandree for long enough that the fiction has its own momentum — you learn to stop thinking of it as lying and start thinking of it as consistent characterization. Asha is a Lolth-devotee; she has a ready-made slot in her worldview for "Lolth-aligned drow who are here to help." I put the Duskryn sisters in that slot and moved on. The lie barely costs anything when you're already committed to the architecture.

Grygum, at some point in this sequence, observed that the situation was "accurately described" by Asha's characterization of me as an insane mage who had converted to Lolthism. He meant it kindly. Or as kindly as Grygum observes anything, which is with the steady, clear-eyed precision of a man keeping accurate records.

*What's important? That she thinks it, or that it's real?*

I know what's real. I know what I've left behind and what I haven't and what Menzoberranzan made me and what the Underdark has cost me and what I believe now, privately, about slavery and free people and the basis of any society. I know all of that. But right now, in this cavern, Asha thinks I serve Lolth, and that belief is load-bearing.

So we let it stand.

Nym Duskryn had been watching all of this with the particular expression of someone who is calculating whether their contract is still viable. She had been sent — she and Kaelira both — to extract me. Deliver the package intact. Get paid. It is a simple arrangement and I had been making it complicated since the moment she arrived.

"Can you please keep your head down and out of combat? We only get paid if you live."

I find transactional arguments interesting when they're offered as though they should be persuasive to the person whose life is being treated as currency. The logic runs: *Daz, you should hide, because we get paid when you live.* The invisible premise is that I should care about whether Nym gets paid. I don't, particularly. What I care about is that Thorin and Grygum and Zalthir are in active combat with a corrupted high priestess and a field of biting insects and what appears to be an inexhaustible supply of Drow reinforcements, and the idea of standing in a shadow while that happens is — I want to find a precise word — *unacceptable.*

"I am not gonna leave. Everyone here... to fight for me without me also participating. I don't give a damn if you get paid. The best way for you to get paid, then, is to continue the fight and prevent all of us from dying."

There it was. Said. Not eloquent — I am capable of elegance, and that was not it. But it was accurate, and it was what I meant, and Nym heard it correctly because her expression shifted from exasperated professionalism to something more pointed.

She turned away from me.

"Can I kill your brother?"

Delivered flat. A genuine inquiry, redirected frustration finding the nearest available target with a professional's efficiency.

"He was always the dumber of the ones, and if I understood correctly, he's covered in fungus?"

What I had not fully accounted for was the T'sarran soldiers themselves, who were busy at the tunnel mouth having an argument that I can only describe as profoundly unhelpful given the circumstances.

"I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill."

"And you don't have a Dispel Magic."

"No, I don't have a Dispel—"

"So I have to go through this. I don't know what else to tell you."

Elite drow soldiers. Trained. Armored. Standing at the threshold of a crisis they had presumably accepted a contract to resolve, now stymied by a wall of insects and a mage with insufficient spell preparation. There's a category of failure I find almost aesthetically interesting — not the grand collapse, but the petty logistical one. They had the firepower. They had the numbers. What they did not have was Dispel Magic, and so they stood in the tunnel debating whether to charge while a 4th-level fireball was introduced into the conversation.

The mage launched a lightning bolt into the cavern with real commitment. It hit nothing. Just open air and ambition.

The question of what spell to use is, in the end, always a question of geometry. You are managing a volume. You are thinking about who is inside it, who is outside it, and how much of what you need is inside relative to the damage you're prepared to accept on the edges. An Evoker — and I am an Evoker, trained in Menzoberranzan where the destructive arts were considered the only magic worth taking seriously — learns to carve exceptions into area spells. The ability is called Sculpt Spells, and it means I can reach into a Fireball and pull specific individuals out of harm's way, leaving the fire to do its work on everything else in the radius.

Glabbagool — our sentient, gentle, luminously good-natured ooze companion who had been, from what I could tell, simply trying to participate in the ambient conversation of the battle — had already taken twenty-six points of damage from Ilvara's plague and survived. He doesn't deserve what this cavern keeps throwing at him. I identified the exclusion carefully.

I cast Fireball in a shaped arc around everything I wanted to keep.

The cavern went very bright for a moment.

The Drow warriors who had been irritated about the Insect Plague were no longer irritated about the Insect Plague. The mage was still standing when the smoke cleared, barely, and she had the presence of mind to deliver her parting observation:

"The matron mother of House T'sarran will hear about this."

She was not wrong. That is probably going to matter later. I filed it accordingly.

The lightning bolt that followed was not, technically, my problem. Jorlan Duskryn went staggering across the cavern floor, and Nym's professional frustrations had found their outlet, and I remained exactly where I had said I would remain.

Not hiding. Not extracted. Not managed.

The feared mage of the Ember Vanguard, standing in the smoke of his own fireball, surrounded by people who were fighting because he refused to leave.

I find, somewhat to my own surprise, that this is what purpose feels like.

## Zalthir — Ilvara's Desperate Stand

The frightened condition is, in theory, simple. You avoid the source of your fear and you wait for it to pass. Clean. Mechanical. Zalthir understood it immediately — and hated it with the particular hatred reserved for things that cannot be argued with.

He had opened this fight by *picking Ilvara up*. Used the Eldritch Claw Tattoo to wrap tentacles around her, hauled her forty feet into the air, hit her twice in the ribs during the ascent, and dropped her. She'd hit stone like something that had stopped mattering. The sequence had lasted perhaps four seconds. He had delivered it with the emotional register of someone pushing a chair under a table.

And then she looked at him. And something in that look — something Zuggtmoy-deep, something that had nothing to do with Ilvara Mizzrym's personal opinion of Zalthir and everything to do with the demon lord wearing her face — *landed*. The fear dropped into him like a stone into still water. No noise, just depth. And suddenly he could not go forward. Could not cross the distance. Every trained instinct read the direction toward Ilvara as simply, absolutely wrong.

He stood there and accepted it.

*I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right.*

Not a question. Just the rule, stated plainly, applied against his own interest the way you check a knot even when you know you tied it correctly. He didn't fight the condition. He didn't look for angles. The spirit of it was that he shouldn't close on her, and he knew the spirit of it, and so he didn't. The discipline is self-administered. Always has been.

It was still humiliating.

"How do I not be frightened?" He had asked, because he wanted to know the timeline. One turn. He just needed to wait one turn.

"I'm a monk."

This came out in response to someone noting — correctly, irritatingly — that his entire contribution to a fight was predicated on being in contact with the thing he was supposed to be destroying. The observation landed without malice. It was just the fact on the table. *Yeah. I mean, how do you slap?* No answer to that. A monk frozen by fear is a monk who has no tools. He had the Eldritch Claw Tattoo — fifteen feet of reach — but the distance between him and Ilvara was not fifteen feet. She was busy reshaping the battlefield around herself, throwing up fungal growth, buying room she knew she needed. Even injured. Even howling.

She was still muttering. Something about Zuggtmoy. Something about the bride, and chaos, and the mayhem to come. She said it in the tone of someone who genuinely believed it, which was the worst kind. Not a threat. A prophecy she'd already made her peace with.

He watched from across the cavern and could not move toward her and filed it away and waited.

One turn is a long time when you have nothing to do in it.

The T'sarran mage was still standing. Barely. Daz had already run an empowered fireball through him; the man was singed at the edges and blinking with the stunned expression of someone who had entered this fight expecting an easy kill and received instead a detailed education in why that assumption was wrong. He had fired a lightning bolt into the cavern when he arrived — a dramatic entrance, the kind with real commitment — and hit nothing. Just open air and ambition.

*I shall punch the mage.*

Zalthir said this without particular enthusiasm. He teleported across the battlefield — shadow-step, the distance closing in the space between one breath and the next — and hit the mage once, and that was enough. The man went down. Zalthir straightened.

The fear cleared at the end of his turn, the way it had been promised to. He noted this without celebration.

Ilvara was still on the far side of the cavern, still howling, her voice ragged with the particular desperation of someone who has run out of futures but not yet run out of throat. *Vengeance. Zuggtmoy. The bride.* All of it tumbling out between strikes and retreats. Even cornered, she filled the space with prophecy rather than offers. He could respect the discipline of that, abstractly. She wasn't going to beg.

The mage's body settled on the cavern floor. Behind Zalthir, someone — Grygum, probably — was already moving forward. Someone else was dealing with the insect swarm at the entrance. The fight was contracting toward its end the way fights do, the radius shrinking as each threat resolved.

*We barely knew you, man.* He didn't say this aloud. Just noted it, and turned toward where the remaining work was being done.

## Grygum — The Fall of Ilvara

The bolt was already building in my hand when I made the decision.

Let me back up. For most of this fight I had been in observation mode — which is a polite way of saying I was watching Jorlan Duskryn and Ilvara Mizzrym tear at each other like competing bad ideas, and finding it genuinely entertaining. You don't often get to watch your primary antagonist and a lover's grudge collide in real time. There's a whole drama there — she discarded him when he got the scar, he's been carrying that wound ever since, and now here they both are at the bottom of a ruined bridge with Zuggtmoy's rot flowering through everything she touches. I took mental notes. It's what I do.

I'm amused by the Jorlan-Ilvara drama, I thought. But I think I'm still going to go ahead and attack them.

The entertainment of watching enemies tear at each other is free. But Bahamut does not, in my experience, favor spectatorship. I have never once received a divine message that said *wait this one out, it's interesting.* He is more of a *you see the weakness, now act* kind of god. And Ilvara was still standing, still murmuring pieces of prophecy about Zuggtmoy and the bride and all the chaos to come — the kind of muttering that sounds unhinged until it turns out to be accurate, and I have seen enough of this Underdark to know better than to dismiss it. She was already something other than the woman she had been. The corruption had been working on her for a long time.

The bolt was already building.

Thorin had already toppled the bridge, sent both of them crashing to the cavern floor — classic Thorin, total commitment, Dawnbringer still humming with residual radiance the way a tuning fork hums after you strike it. Zalthir had been up and down and around, doing what Zalthir does, the entire fight a series of surgical moves delivered with the emotional register of someone sorting correspondence. But Ilvara was still here. Still muttering. Still Zuggtmoy's.

There is a version of Guiding Bolt that I think of as administrative — you point it, it works, enemy takes radiant damage, case closed. And then there is the other version, the one that happens when everything has led to this particular moment and the light in your hand feels like it has been waiting. I have seen enough corrupted things in this Underdark — the egg, the grove, the Pudding King's kingdom of rot — to know what it looks like when something has been claimed entirely by a power it should never have touched. Ilvara was not being aided by Zuggtmoy anymore. She *was* Zuggtmoy's work, walking around in a drow priestess's bones.

The bolt left my hand.

It lanced across the cavern floor in a concentrated column of radiant light and found her where she stood.

She did not die quietly. She detonated. Her body ruptured outward in a massive toxic bloom — a cloud of poisonous spores billowing through the cavern in every direction, the demonic corruption venting itself all at once in the only form it had left. Zalthir and Thorin staggered in it. I was already standing back.

Not ideal, I thought, immediately practical again. How big is the cloud?

I was glad I was standing back.

That is the whole thought. That is the entire reflection on delivering the killing blow to a demonically corrupted high priestess of Lolth in the middle of the Underdark: *not ideal, how big is the cloud, glad I was standing back.* Bahamut gets the credit for the radiance. I get credit for the positioning. We understand each other, Bahamut and I.

The cavern settled into a thick, spore-choked silence. The heart fungus was already a collapsed ruin — Thorin had seen to that in one devastating, almost insulting round, while I stood there with everything I'd prepared for it and nowhere to deploy it. *Boy, I had so much planned for the fungus.* The bridge was rubble. The poisonous bloom was already beginning to thin and drift. And somewhere in the settling quiet, Asha Vandree dropped to her knees and began murmuring prayers, her voice the only sound left — words to Lolth, rising from the wreckage, already reshaping what she'd witnessed into something her goddess could use.

Whatever theology she was building out of this moment, I filed it under *watch this space.*

The last House T'sarran warrior looked at all of it — his mage dead, his guards incinerated, Ilvara nothing but settling spores and a fading prophecy about a bride — and stepped back toward the tunnels without urgency. "We will meet again," he said. "Enjoy your victory for the moment."

I took note of that too. A threat delivered without heat is always more credible than one made in anger. I'll want to remember that name. The matron mother of House T'sarran would be hearing about this. I believe I should find out who that is before she finds out who we are.

My first real question, once the cloud was thin enough to breathe through, was about Glabbagool.

## Thorin — Aftermath at Velkynvelve

The bridge going down was my idea, technically, but I can't take full credit for it feeling satisfying. You swing at a bridge long enough, the bridge falls. That's not strategy. That's physics.

Ilvara and Jorlan both cleared the drop. I watched them hit the cavern floor and thought: *still breathing*. Then I thought about how much tunnel I'd crossed to reach that bridge, how many times the fight had relocated while I was still arriving at wherever it used to be, and I stopped tallying. Some battles you just chase until they end without you.

The fungus, at least, was mine. Clean and total. Dawnbringer lit the thing up from the first swing and it never got a second chance — big pulsating horror that must have taken the DM weeks to stat out, and it folded before anyone else could touch it. I said *I do what I can* because what else do you say when you've just ruined someone's evening in two rounds of radiant damage. Grygum said it was beautiful. Zalthir said it was pretty. I felt a little bad, in the way you feel bad about something you'd do exactly the same way again.

After that I was mostly running.

That's the honest accounting of the rest of the battle: I was running. The map spread out across half the cavern and the fight kept happening somewhere I wasn't. Drow fighting drow. Ilvara dropping, recovering, dropping again. Lightning hitting things I couldn't see from wherever I was standing. There's a version of this where I say I was *holding the center* or *covering the approach*, but the real version is that I'm a dwarf with a glowing sword in a sprawling cavern fight and at some point you stop pretending you've got angles on all of it and just pick a direction and go.

Grygum's bolt found her. Spores everywhere — the kind of death that punishes you for being nearby. I caught some of it. Zalthir caught some of it.

Dawnbringer pulsed once, steady warmth against my palm.

The spores drifted. The T'sarrans were already gone. Somewhere behind me, Grygum was probably checking on Glabbagool. Daz was probably explaining to someone why exactly this was all fine.

I stood there and let the quiet be quiet.

We'd won. I couldn't tell you yet what winning this one cost, or what it opened up ahead of us — that kind of math takes time, and the Underdark has a way of correcting your optimism before you even finish the equation. But right now the fungus was dead and the bridge was down and Ilvara Mizzrym was a cloud of dissipating spores, and Asha was still praying in the dark like she expected to be answered.

Maybe she would be. I've seen stranger things answered.
