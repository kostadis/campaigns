## Daz — The Mercenary Contract

The two of them hadn't lowered their weapons so much as they'd decided we weren't worth the effort. I filed that away. There's a specific kind of professional contempt that tells you more about a situation than any interrogation would.

Nym spoke first, in that tone Menzoberranzan produces naturally — the one that sounds like courtesy and isn't.

"Daz, I understand why your concern for your safety existed."

I appreciated the past tense. It did a lot of work.

The pieces assembled themselves quickly, the way they do when you've been trained to read a room before you've fully entered it. Two Drow. Duskryn name. Hired in Menzoberranzan. Hired to protect me — specifically, *me* — from Ilvara's House and from the T'sarran goons we'd just finished burning. Someone had paid for this. Someone who knew I was here, knew who was hunting me, and knew enough to contract professionals rather than handle it directly.

That last part was the interesting part.

I'd been running a list in the back of my head since Velkynvelve — the kind of list you keep when your life depends on knowing who wants what. On that list: entities with motive to see me dead. The list was substantial. Entities with motive to see me alive and sufficiently protected to require hired blades from Menzoberranzan — that list had, until approximately this moment, been empty.

I asked the obvious question. You always ask the obvious question, because the answer is rarely the obvious answer.

"What is the terms of your contract, just to make sure that I... am alive, or that I won this particular battle?"

Nym's expression didn't change. "Our terms of our contract were to make sure that the other drow factions didn't get you killed."

Kaelira gestured toward the scorched remnants of the extraction contract the party had produced — the document that had apparently been at the center of all this — and addressed me with the brisk precision of someone who had already decided the conversation was almost over. There were two threats named, she said. House Mizzrym. House T'sarran. Those were the parameters. They'd been prepared to handle the T'sarran contingent personally if it came to that. It hadn't.

"We hoped that you'd be smart enough to avoid Ilvara... well, we could have handled House T'sarran and whatever goons they threw at you. But you were able to do it quite on your own. Bravo, bravo, bravo."

Three *bravos*. I have found, as a general principle, that a third *bravo* is never sincere.

I pressed the logical extension of the argument, because that's what you do when the other side thinks they've finished the transaction.

"If you haven't eliminated all the other factions, and I go and get myself killed in the next 15 minutes, and you guys go and collect on the bond, and then your employer finds out — it's not gonna be a good day for you."

The sisters exchanged a look. A brief one. The kind that means *we talked about this already and the answer is still no.*

When the subject of Asha came up — our own Lolth cultist, devotedly genuine in her faith and thoroughly deceived about mine — Nym dismissed it with a kind of elegant indifference that I found, privately, somewhat reassuring. A window into what they actually thought of the threat assessment.

"If Daz can't protect himself from a low-level cult initiate of Lolth, then quite frankly, whoever paid for his survival deserves to lose his money."

And then, with equal efficiency: "You could probably kill her in a few seconds."

I chose not to confirm or deny this. What I said instead was: "Hey guys, thanks for the help."

Thorin, to his credit, tried to retain them. He asked what it would cost. Nym's answer was three words and they landed like a portcullis.

"That is a fee... that is a price you cannot afford."

No elaboration. No negotiation. Just the statement of a fact as though she'd assessed our resources at a glance and found them inadequate — which, to be entirely fair to her, she probably had.

Then Kaelira said the thing that made me go still.

She was looking at the contract. Looking at the numbers. And then she looked at me, and there was genuine puzzlement in it — not contempt, not theater. Actual, professional confusion at an anomaly that didn't fit the pattern she'd been given.

"That is an excellent question. You don't belong to any noble house, your background is a complete mystery, you suddenly show up, and all of a sudden, we're contracted to protect a nobody. Do you know how much money that required us to get, for us to do? A lot. Like, they interrupt you. A lot."

A nobody. The word landed cleanly, without malice. It was the professionally accurate term. I had no house. No lineage with the right names. No claim on any of the structures that give a Drow life its currency in Menzoberranzan. And yet someone had paid *a lot* — her emphasis, not mine — to keep this nobody breathing.

I said what I said, because there wasn't much else to say: "So then maybe there's more to me than meets the eye?"

Kaelira's response was sharp and not unkind. "Yes, yes, yes, that's what every orphan thinks. Do you have daddy issues? Do you have mommy issues? Every drow thinks there's some noble things. For all we know, this was a game being played by the houses."

And when I held the line, because I couldn't quite let it drop: "Perhaps, but we're not paid to think that hard."

Right. They weren't. That was my problem.

I will say this about Kaelira Duskryn: she had the manner of someone who had seen through a great many self-mythologizing idiots and wasn't interested in entertaining another. It is, in certain lights, a useful quality in a professional. In the context of someone disassembling the question of why I was apparently worth a significant expenditure to some unknown party in Menzoberranzan — it was less convenient.

I said what I actually believed, which is a thing I do less often than I probably should: "I literally have no idea why there's all this focus on me."

The party fell into the kind of quiet that follows a statement that everyone believes. Thorin had that look he gets — the one where he's deciding whether to charge at the problem or stand very still and wait for it to explain itself. Grygum was probably filing away some theological observation about mysterious dispensations. Zalthir, characteristically, was simply watching.

The sisters departed. Professionally. Without ceremony. The contract was fulfilled; they were done.

I considered what remained. Someone in Menzoberranzan had spent generously — *they interrupt you, a lot* — to ensure that a male Drow apostate with no house affiliation and no apparent strategic value survived long enough to do whatever it was I was apparently supposed to do. They had done this anonymously. Through cutouts. In a manner that ensured even the hired blades didn't know who was paying.

In Menzoberranzan, that level of insulation around a financial transaction means exactly one thing: the payer has significant reason not to be associated with the purchase. Which means the purchase is either embarrassing, illegal, or dangerous to be seen making.

Protecting me was one of those three things to someone powerful enough to fund it.

"You know what would help?" I said. "To audit their books."

No one laughed. Grygum looked thoughtful, which is his equivalent.

I sat with it privately, the thing I wasn't going to say aloud: *for all you know, the reason this all got started was because they all decided to be friends with the wrong drow.*

The thought had a particular shape. Elegant, in the way that retrospective clarity sometimes is. The whole of it — the capture, the escape, Velkynvelve, the months of running — threading back to a single point. Me. A nobody. A nobody that someone in Menzoberranzan had interrupted their schedule over, more than once.

Milo Goodbarrel, that relentlessly hopeful halfling whose book I'd read too many times to admit, had a phrase for the sensation I was experiencing. He called it *standing at the edge of the question that changes everything.* He said you could always recognize it because it made you feel very small and very important at the same time.

He was, annoyingly, not wrong.
