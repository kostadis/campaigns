## Grygum — Looting the Drow Outpost

The keys were the first thing I noticed. A jailer's ring, iron and heavy, each key worn smooth from years of use. I turned them over in my hands and felt the particular weight of irony settle in my chest.

"You know, they would have been very useful in the very beginning," I said, mostly to myself. "These are the very keys that had you imprisoned."

Daz looked over from where he was already cataloguing the rest of the room. "We've come a long way."

He said it lightly. He meant it seriously. We both knew.

The payroll was next — a chest of coin, Velkynvelve's operational budget rendered in gold and silver. A hoard is always valuable, as the maxim goes. I took notes. I didn't touch it yet; I wanted to understand the room before I started carrying things out of it.

Then I saw the other chest.

Confiscated prisoner belongings. Ilvara had been carrying it. Of course she had — she was exactly the sort of high priestess who would keep trophies, who would drag along evidence of her ownership of people the way some clerics keep relics. I opened it, and the contents were exactly what you would expect from someone who understood that stripping a prisoner of their possessions is the first act of stripping them of themselves.

"If there was anything that was missing from the very beginning," I said, not quite finishing the thought, "like your tooth, Thorin."

Thorin looked up. Something shifted in his face — recognition, and then something quieter beneath it.

"Oh, yeah. I want my tooth back."

He reached in and found it. He held it for a moment without saying anything. Then:

"Very sentimental."

Very sentimental. That's one word for it. Another word is: she had been carrying his tooth around the Underdark. She had collected it like a curiosity. I filed that under Ilvara, and closed the file, because she was already dead and I had killed her, and whatever she had been carrying was ours now.

We also found a water-damaged journal from a prisoner we would never meet — someone who had written things down and then, presumably, stopped being able to. I held it carefully. The pages were swollen and the ink had run in places, but the handwriting was still visible in the drier sections, the marks of a person who had kept records right up until they couldn't. I took note of that too. It's what I do.

The flask of Overbright brandy I received with considerably more personal interest.

---

Asha Vandree's body was next.

I should say something pious here about the dignity of the dead. My mentor Gorg'Bahamut would have wanted me to. But Asha Vandree had been a cleric of Lolth who had also become, in some capacity, a cleric of Zuggtmoy, and in the end she had been indistinguishable from the rot she served. So I looted her with the efficient respect one extends to a theological problem that has been resolved.

The holy symbol of Lolth. Chain mail. Prayer beads. And a sealed wax pouch.

Zalthir took the Drow cloak she'd been wearing — the fine spider-silk kind that drinks shadow — and I did not begrudge him that. I had the wax pouch, and I suspected it was the more interesting item.

I was right. I was also, immediately, very tired.

The seal broke cleanly. Inside were pages of close, careful script — the handwriting of someone who had once been methodical, who had trained as a theologian and brought that training to bear on a question that should not have been asked. I read far enough to understand what it was, and then I looked up.

"It's basically a long theological exploration of why Zuggtmoy and Lolth — and claiming that Lolth and Zuggtmoy are the same thing," I said. "It's very tedious and boring."

Tedious and boring. That was me being diplomatic. What it actually was, was the written record of a mind finding its way toward the conclusion that two incompatible things were secretly one thing — the kind of reasoning that starts from a desired destination and works backward. Clerics of Lolth have been doing this kind of theological contortion for centuries; what was unusual here was the destination. Not just *we serve the Spider Queen.* Also: *and the Demon Queen of Fungi is the Spider Queen, so the rot in everything sacred is also sacred.* It was very well-argued. It was also completely mad.

"Is this pre-craziness or post-craziness, do you think?" I asked.

No one answered with any certainty. I went back to the pouch.

---

Ilvara's prayer book was still on the body when we got to her.

"You know, I'm the cleric," I said, "so I'll examine it gingerly."

Gingerly was the right word. A prayer book belonging to a high priestess of Lolth who had been actively subverted by Zuggtmoy was not an object you wanted to open carelessly, the way a letter from a diplomat who has recently defected is not an object you open over your breakfast. You approach it with the assumption that the text will tell you things you may not want to know.

It told me things I did not want to know.

The main text was a standard Lolth prayer book — the kind of thing they give to promising young acolytes in the outer sanctums of any major drow religious house. Formatted prayers. Liturgical responses. The hierarchy of supplication laid out in careful descending order. Ilvara had presumably carried it since early in her career, which meant it was old, which meant she had been using it as a devotional object for years before something began to change.

The marginalia told the story.

"It's just a prayer book," I said, turning pages slowly, "but you notice it has Zuggtmoy marginalia — the margins in a different hand — since it shows the corruption timeline. So basically, you can see, like, she was normal, and then things started to go sideways."

A different hand. That detail mattered to me more than any of the specific words. Someone else had written in her prayer book. Or — and this was the reading that settled in my stomach like cold stone — she had written in it herself but in a state so altered that the handwriting had changed. Two hands because two minds had been working the same pen. The early entries were neat and formal and exactly what you would expect. Then, gradually, around the margins, a second voice crept in. Less structured. More associative. The sentences didn't always end. Some of them were questions that answered themselves with other questions.

I had seen something like this before, in a completely different context — a merchant I once knew who had been Tongued by a bardic curse, whose correspondence from that period was eerily similar: coherent on the surface, and then underneath the coherence, something working against the grain of itself. I had taken notes then too. I was taking notes now.

The corruption timeline was legible if you knew how to read it. Ilvara had been normal — a functioning, ambitious, politically dangerous high priestess — and then something had found the edge of her and started pulling. The marginalia documented the pull. She had not resisted it. I could not tell from the text whether she had known she wasn't resisting or whether she had genuinely believed she was still moving in her own direction. That ambiguity was the most troubling part.

I thought, briefly, of Stroud sitting down across from Protanther. Four hundred years of preparation. Still had a blind spot. Sometimes the thing that undoes you isn't the opponent you've been studying — it's the thing you didn't know to look for.

---

The sealed orders from House Mizzrym were more immediately practical, and I was almost grateful for them.

I read them aloud, more or less: "Where are you? What have you been doing? Respond — kind of thing. You've gone missing. Followed by more frantic: where are you, where are you? Didn't you read the previous memo? What's up?"

Daz was paying attention in the way he pays attention to things — still, cataloguing, already working the implications.

The letter from Neverlight Grove completed the picture, in a grim sort of way. A missed meeting. Political correspondence that had gone nowhere, because the person it was addressed to had gone somewhere else — somewhere a Demon Queen's influence had been a stronger pull than any political alliance.

I put the letters with the other items. They were someone's records of a relationship that had dissolved without anyone on one end knowing why. House Mizzrym would learn eventually, I supposed. We had helped with that.

---

The last item was on Ilvara herself.

"The Living Serpent Head Scourge sounds..." I paused, considering the thing, which was exactly what it sounded like — a scourge whose head was an actual living serpent, or had been, in some intermediate state between alive and not. "It sounds like a great museum piece."

Someone laughed, or made a sound adjacent to laughter.

I meant it, partly. There is a tradition, in certain temples I have visited, of displaying items that are terrible in origin and remarkable in craft, on the theory that acknowledging the craft is not the same as endorsing what the craft served. A great museum piece. Bahamut's treasury is full of things taken from hoards that should not have existed. You document them. You label them carefully. You do not pretend they aren't what they are.

I set it aside with the other items. The prayer book. The wax pouch. The sealed orders. The serpent scourge. A cleric's catalogue of the things a high priestess of Lolth becomes when something older and stranger finds the seam in her theology and begins to work.

I had so much I could have said about any of them. I took notes instead.
