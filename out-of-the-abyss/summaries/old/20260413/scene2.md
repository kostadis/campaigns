## Daz — Choosing Feats and Abilities

There is a particular kind of pleasure in having options.

Not the pleasure of having made the decision — that comes later, and sometimes not at all. The pleasure I mean is the one that arrives in the moment before: the spread of possibilities laid out like a hand of cards, each one representing a different version of how the next fight might go. Menzoberranzan trained me to read that moment with surgical attention. *Know what you hold before you commit to playing it.* Old instruction. Never stopped being useful.

Level seven. I turned the question over the way I turn most problems — by organizing it into categories first. What do I need? Survivability under fire. Sustained casting. Escape options when something has its hands on me and the alternatives are bad.

Warcaster was the obvious answer, and I noted it without sentiment. If I'm maintaining concentration on a spell worth maintaining — Hypnotic Pattern, Maximilian's Grasp — then the ability to hold that concentration when something lands a hit is not decorative. It is the difference between the spell persisting and the spell dissolving at precisely the wrong moment. *The other one that helps wizards a lot is Warcaster, which gives them improved concentration checks. So if you're going to cast spells that require concentration, that is not a bad thing to have.* Obvious. Noted. Set aside temporarily.

Because there was the other option.

*Fey Touched gives me two additional spells to prepare. Plus free castings.*

I held that thought for a moment. The spellbook we'd taken from Princess Ebonmire had been sitting in my pack like a promissory note I hadn't finished reading. Prepared spells are the real resource for a wizard — not the castings themselves, not the slots, but the *selection*. What you've loaded before the situation becomes the situation. Fey Touched was a key, and the Ebonmire spellbook was a door I hadn't fully opened yet. *Part of why I think Fey Touched might be good, because then I can prepare additional spells.* The logic was clean.

And there was Misty Step.

I'd already had it, technically. But that's not the point. *No — being able to cast it without expending a spell slot is extremely useful. Because if you're in combat, it allows you to get out without incurring attacks of opportunity. It allows you to get out of a grappled situation.* A slot is a resource. A free casting is a resource that costs nothing in the moment you need it most, which is usually the moment you can least afford to spend anything. I've been grabbed before. I have an instinctive and deeply personal objection to being grabbed.

Fey Touched. That was the one.

But I was not done thinking.

"What about Telekinetic? Have you seen anybody use that?"

I asked it as a genuine question, not a rhetorical one. I already had a theory, but theories benefit from pressure. I turned the feat over while I waited for an answer.

*Interesting. Telekinetic — people can choose to intentionally fail a saving throw. So I can aim this at allies and do things like get them out of a grapple. Move them five feet.*

That reframing pleased me. A combat utility that functions on allies as readily as on enemies, specifically because allies can cooperate with it. The same logic applied to positioning — there are situations where moving Zalthir five feet in the right direction is more valuable than a Scorching Ray. He is, when correctly positioned, substantially more dangerous than a Scorching Ray. I was beginning to build a picture of Telekinetic not as an offensive tool but as a geometry tool. The Underdark had taught me repeatedly that battles are often decided by who is standing where.

And then — *also, don't forget: you found that spellbook with all those spells.*

Ebonmire's book. Right. The door I hadn't fully opened.

"Because I have my ability to shape spells," I said. "If I'm calling Cloud of Daggers on an enemy—"

The idea was forming as I spoke it. Sculpt Spells lets me carve exclusions into area effects. But the area effects I could now access, combined with the concentration durability Warcaster would provide, combined with the escape utility of free Misty Step — these were not independent features. They were a system. The Evoker's toolkit is not a collection of separate spells; it is an interlocking argument about where enemies should be, where allies should be, and which parts of a room I am willing to set on fire.

Milo Goodbarrel had a chapter about this. He called it "the long arrangement" — the idea that the fight before the fight, the positioning, the preparation, is actually most of the fight. He meant it as a lesson about terrain. I always thought he was selling short the part about spell selection. We were probably both right.

Across from me, Zalthir was working through his own options. He said very little, which is Zalthir's preferred mode. Just: "Feats, huh." And then, with the considered weight of someone who was not actually complaining but acknowledging a genuine complexity: "Yeah, this'll take some time."

I found the Street Justice feat on his behalf — the one that let him use rope to bind enemies he'd already grappled. I watched the recognition arrive in his expression. The monk whose hands were already his primary weapon, now with a formal mechanism for making *staying* as interesting as *hitting*. The geometry tool from a different direction.

He would need a minute with it. I went back to my own list.

The spellbook. Additional prepared spells. Free Misty Step. Sculpt Spells meeting Cloud of Daggers meeting the particular spatial logic of whatever room we were going to be standing in next, probably soon, probably underground.

Fey Touched. That was the one. I wrote it down with the quiet satisfaction of a calculation that checks out.
