# Chapter 48 The Ember Grapple

---

## Thorin — The Level Eight Milestone

Something hit me, walking those last tunnels out of Blingdenstone.

Not literally. Though, given how the last several months have gone, that's worth specifying. No, this was the other kind of thing — the quiet knock inside the chest that means you've crossed some threshold you didn't notice crossing. I've felt it before, in the dark, after bad fights. The body takes stock before the mind does. I was bigger somehow. More there.

"Alright, so the first thing is that we're all leveled up."

I heard myself think it before I quite understood it. Leveled up. Which meant — I ran the count back. Sloobludop. Gracklstugh. Neverlight Grove. Blingdenstone. The Pudding King. Ilvara. Everything we'd crawled through, fought through, negotiated and lied and burned our way through.

"Whoa, whoa, what?"

Daz made a noise of genuine surprise — the kind he usually reserves for things like demon princes and sentient oozes. "Oh!" And then, with that particular tone he uses when he's already processed something and moved to judgment: "We're gonna level up." He arrived at the same conclusion.

I thought about all the things I'd need to reconsider now — the new techniques settling into muscle and bone, decisions about what kind of fighter I was going to be going forward. I knew immediately how long that was going to take. Not because I couldn't decide, but because I take it seriously, and taking things seriously takes time.

"Alright, well, that's gonna take the rest of the day."

Flat statement. True statement.

I looked over at Zalthir — who was, as usual, already thinking several steps ahead of everyone else, in some private tactical space behind his eyes. Something about the way the Dragonborn operated had always impressed me, even when I didn't understand it. He builds narrow and builds sharp. Everything pointing in one direction, so that in exactly the right situation, he becomes something close to impossible.

"Is your strategy that you just build a super lopsided powers and just go with that? Actually, do you try and create some… because, like, right now, I have like, zero range ability, and I'm wondering, do I try and fix that, or do I just double down with the shit that I can't, you know?"

Zalthir considered this for a moment in the way he considers most things — like he's checking the weight of the stone before deciding where to put it.

"I find it more fun to double down with some sort of obscure specialization and then try to break the universe with it."

"I mean, you're really good at it."

Not flattery. Just accurate. I'd watched him pull the Pudding King out of its lair by sheer force of tactical geometry. That was a man who had committed to a direction and refused to apologize for it.

The honest truth is that the game punishes you for being cautious about this. Spreading your strengths thin feels responsible. Feels dwarvish, maybe — the sensible hedge, the covered flank. But enemies get bigger, and if your damage doesn't grow with them, you spend your time bouncing off. At low levels, a solid strike matters. At our level, you need to mean it. By the time you're facing whatever's waiting for us on the surface, you'd better be doing something significant every time you swing.

Which is also why not having anyone who can reach a flying enemy is going to come back and bite us. The Underdark is tight and low. But we're almost out. And this is a dangerous world, and we have a dangerous job,  and we none of us have ranged weapons, which means there will absolutely be something that needs hitting from a distance. The universe is that kind of petty.

"So question, so Dawnbringer is not slashing, right? It's actually… Radiant damage."

Confirmed. The blade doesn't cut so much as it burns — clean, divine, the kind of light that argues with darkness on a philosophical level. Which is useful for some things. Less useful for creatures that armor against it. Worth knowing what you're working with.

"So I am really strong." 

 Should I get stronger? The point stands. You can push to the absolute ceiling and there's a kind of satisfaction in that, but it's diminishing. Some of these options I'd been considering offered to increase strength "to a maximum of twenty," which sounds like an improvement until you do the math on where you already are.

You can do it later, sure. Or you can go sideways instead of up. The logic: skip the investment, drink the potion of giant strength when it matters. Which — I recognized, even as I thought it — had given me one more bad habit to account for.

"Okay, so I'm the type, when I adventure, I'm always like, oh, I don't know, I'm gonna save this potion for later, I might need it."

I knew it was wrong when I said it. It's always wrong. The potion you don't drink is the potion you die holding.

"Wrong strategy, but that's just how I roll."

There was also the question of what kind of fighter I was becoming. There's a school of thought — brutal, efficient, not entirely reputable — that goes by the name Zhentarim Tactics. Ruthless by design. Effective in ways that decent people find uncomfortable.

"Zhentarim Tactics."

The Zhentarim are not good people. I know that. The hill giants weren't always good people either, and I carry their lessons in the shape of every fight I've won. I've had what you might call a dark transition. The happy-go-lucky kid who went underground expecting something survivable has spent months watching demon lords and eating mushrooms and coming to understand that the Underdark doesn't care what kind of person you want to be.

Zalthir looked at me with that particular expression that meant he'd thought four moves ahead already.

"That would be a whole change of character for you."

"Well, I mean, I can work it into my story."

I could. It would be honest, even. Not the honesty I prefer — I've always liked honesty you can stand behind without flinching — but there are things you absorb down here whether you mean to or not. The Zhentarim have survived by being willing to do what others won't. I understood that better now than I would have liked.

It could be made to work. Things can usually be made to work, if you're willing to admit what you've already become.

## Daz — Choosing Feats and Abilities

There is a particular kind of pleasure in having options.

Not the pleasure of having made the decision — that comes later, and sometimes not at all. The pleasure I mean is the one that arrives in the moment before: the spread of possibilities laid out like a hand of cards, each one representing a different version of how the next fight might go. Menzoberranzan trained me to read that moment with surgical attention. *Know what you hold before you commit to playing it.* Old instruction. Never stopped being useful.

I turned the question over the way I turn most problems — by organizing it into categories first. What do I need? Survivability under fire. Sustained casting. Escape options when something has its hands on me and the alternatives are bad.

Warcaster was the obvious answer, and I noted it without sentiment. If I'm maintaining concentration on a spell worth maintaining — Hypnotic Pattern, Maximilian's Grasp — then the ability to hold that concentration when something lands a hit is not decorative. It is the difference between the spell persisting and the spell dissolving at precisely the wrong moment. *The other one that helps wizards a lot is Warcaster, which gives them improved concentration checks. So if you're going to cast spells that require concentration, that is not a bad thing to have.* Obvious. Noted. Set aside temporarily.

Because there was the other option.

*Fey Touched gives me two additional spells to prepare. Plus free castings.*

I held that thought for a moment. The spellbook we'd taken from Princess Ebonmire had been sitting in my pack like a promissory note I hadn't finished reading. Prepared spells are the real resource for a wizard — not the castings themselves, not the slots, but the *selection*. What you've loaded before the situation becomes the situation. Fey Touched was a key, and the Ebonmire spellbook was a door I hadn't fully opened yet. *Part of why I think Fey Touched might be good, because then I can prepare additional spells.* The logic was clean.

And there was Misty Step.

I'd already had it, technically. But that's not the point. *No — being able to cast it without expending a spell slot is extremely useful. Because if you're in combat, it allows you to get out without incurring attacks of opportunity. It allows you to get out of a grappled situation.* A slot is a resource. A free casting is a resource that costs nothing in the moment you need it most, which is usually the moment you can least afford to spend anything. I've been grabbed before. I have an instinctive and deeply personal objection to being grabbed.

Fey Touched. That was the one.

But I was not done thinking.

"What about Telekinetic? Have you seen anybody use that?"

I asked it as a genuine question, not a rhetorical one. I already had a theory, but theories benefit from pressure. I turned the feat over while I waited for an answer.

*Interesting. Telekinetic,  I can aim this at allies and do things like get them out of a grapple. Move them five feet.*

That reframing pleased me. A combat utility that functions on allies as readily as on enemies, specifically because allies can cooperate with it. The same logic applied to positioning — there are situations where moving Zalthir five feet in the right direction is more valuable than a Scorching Ray. He is, when correctly positioned, substantially more dangerous than a Scorching Ray. I was beginning to build a picture of Telekinetic not as an offensive tool but as a geometry tool. The Underdark had taught me repeatedly that battles are often decided by who is standing where.

And then — *also, don't forget: you found that spellbook with all those spells.*

Ebonmire's book. Right. The door I hadn't fully opened.

"Because I have my ability to shape spells," I said. "If I'm calling Cloud of Daggers on an enemy—"

The idea was forming as I spoke it. Sculpt Spells lets me carve exclusions into area effects. But the area effects I could now access, combined with the concentration durability Warcaster would provide, combined with the escape utility of free Misty Step — these were not independent features. They were a system. The Evoker's toolkit is not a collection of separate spells; it is an interlocking argument about where enemies should be, where allies should be, and which parts of a room I am willing to set on fire.

Milo Goodbarrel had a chapter about this. He called it "the long arrangement" — the idea that the fight before the fight, the positioning, the preparation, is actually most of the fight. He meant it as a lesson about terrain. I always thought he was selling short the part about spell selection. We were probably both right.

Across from me, Zalthir was working through his own options. He said very little, which is Zalthir's preferred mode. Just: "Feats, huh." And then, with the considered weight of someone who was not actually complaining but acknowledging a genuine complexity: "Yeah, this'll take some time."

I found the Street Justice feat on his behalf — the one that let him use rope to bind enemies he'd already grappled. I watched the recognition arrive in his expression. The monk whose hands were already his primary weapon, now with a formal mechanism for making *staying* as interesting as *hitting*. The geometry tool from a different direction.

He would need a minute with it. I went back to my own list.

The spellbook. Additional prepared spells. Free Misty Step. Sculpt Spells meeting Cloud of Daggers meeting the particular spatial logic of whatever room we were going to be standing in next, probably soon, probably underground.

Fey Touched. That was the one. I wrote it down with the quiet satisfaction of a calculation that checks out.

## Zalthir — Tactical Synergies and Final Decisions

The Darkness spell has always been the problem between Daz and me.

He throws fire. I throw shadow. For months we have been apologizing to each other in the aftermath — *your spell blocked my line of sight, my darkness ate your targeting* — working around it the way you work around a bad step in a tunnel you know well. You learn where to place your foot. You don't fix the tunnel.

This was different.

"If you hold one in place," Daz said, "the daggers keep finding them."

I had been thinking about it too. Cloud of Daggers: a rotating cage of blades, hungry in one fixed point in space. The problem has always been that enemies move. They step out of it, or we drag them out of it by accident, or the fight shifts and the spell becomes decoration. But a grapple — a proper hold, weight-to-weight, the kind that pins something to a specific square of floor — that solved it. The daggers would find them exactly where I left them.

We talked through the geometry of it. The question was whether I would catch myself in my own trap, whether holding something at arm's length meant I'd be eating Daz's spell alongside whatever I was holding. The answer was no. A grapple keeps the target where I put them, but I am not inside them — I am beside them, adjacent, occupying my own space while they occupy theirs. Daz could put his cloud directly on the grappled target and I would walk away without a scratch.

I said: there was a class on this. At the monastery. Something about battlefield geometry and the difference between control and proximity. I think it was Brother Heslin — or possibly that was the one who taught falling. I'm genuinely uncertain. Either way, I remember the light through the shuttered window and thinking the form was taking too long. I did not remember what they said. I am currently learning it from a Drow wizard in a tunnel.

The next thing to work through was the action economy.

Grapple is a quick thing — one motion, a grab, a test of weight and leverage, done in the space of a heartbeat. That leaves the hands. Rope is slower. Tying something takes focus, takes both hands, takes the better part of a turn spent crouching over a target that is probably trying to bite you. The grapple could happen in the spare moment before my main action. The binding would consume the action itself. The whole turn — both pieces of it — spent on one enemy, pinned to the floor, with Daz's daggers already spinning above them.

That was a full turn spent on setup. The next turn, I would be free — and whatever I had tied down would still be there, still bleeding, while I moved to the next problem.

It was not lost on me that "Zalthir ties things to the ground for Daz to cook" is not exactly the heroic narrative I had constructed for myself. I have killed an ettin with my hands. I dragged the Pudding King through his own throne room. And now here I am, discussing the correct knot.

Grygum made a sound. Thorin said something about "the Ember Grapple." Someone — possibly Thorin again, possibly Grygum, it is hard to tell when they are both making noise — found this very funny.

I did not correct it. The name was not wrong.

Thorin mentioned javelins. He had apparently acquired some facility with thrown weapons — a Sharpshooter's instinct, trained in, applied to range — and he wanted something with reach for the next engagement. Something to hold the gap while I was busy tying knots and Daz was busy making things into fire. Practical. I noted it and moved on.

There would be no long rest before whatever came next. I had run the tunnels in my head — the distance we had traveled, the pace, the absence of obvious threats immediately ahead. Whatever we were carrying into the next fight, we were carrying in. Nothing replenished itself on account of wishing.

I turned that over the way I turn most things over: once, completely, and then I put it down. The math was the math. What we had was what we had.

The Ember Grapple.

I let it settle. As names go, I have heard worse. The Ember Vanguard charges in with fire and flash and the announcement of itself. The Ember Grapple holds something still until there is nothing left to hold.

Both accurate. Possibly the same party described from two different positions in the room.
