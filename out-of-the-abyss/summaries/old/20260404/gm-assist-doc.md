## Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The last time I tried to grab Ilvara, she simply left. One moment I had her in reach; the next she was somewhere else entirely, and I was holding nothing but air and the faint impression of having looked foolish. A legendary action. I had misremembered the mechanism. It doesn't change what happened, which is that she left, and I didn't stop her.

*Second try is the charm, or give up on that whole plan?*

I said it aloud. I didn't mean to frame it as a question — there wasn't really a question. I can only melee. The plan is the plan. *I mean, I can only melee anyway, so I guess I'll just… try that same thing again.*

The useful thing, it turned out, was that forcing her to burn that reaction had value even when it felt like failure. If I put pressure on her and she teleports away, she's not doing something worse with that action. She's just not near me. That's a trade I can accept. There was probably a class for this at the monastery — the tactical theory of forcing suboptimal responses, or whatever the formal term was. Brother Quellin, maybe. The one who used to draw diagrams with wet chalk and speak very slowly, as if patience could be transmitted by demonstration. I remember the chalk. I don't remember the content. I was likely meditating somewhere else, or doing something I considered more productive, which at the time meant hitting things.

I flew toward her.

The Eldritch Claw tattoo on my arm does what I tell it to do, which is the quality I appreciate most in any tool. Fifteen feet of reach, tentacle strikes that extend and retract without requiring me to close completely before I'm ready. I use them to hit her first — enough to register, enough to make her attention land on me — and then I close the remaining distance and I take hold of her.

The grapple lands. She is in my hands, forty feet above the protective sphere, and the ground is a long way down.

*Now that I'm holding her, I will punch her again, because why not?*

I said this with perhaps more brightness than the situation required. But the tactical logic was clean: she was restrained, I was not, and the distance between her and anything useful to her was currently forty feet of open air. Why would I not punch her. The first hit landed. The second landed harder. Two critical strikes, back to back, which is the sort of thing that happens occasionally and means everything you've built toward is briefly, perfectly true — the read was right, the angle was right, the decision to fly in and grab instead of circle and wait was right. All of it converting into force, delivered against the corrupted priestess of a demon lord who has been trying to kill us since before I had any sense of how deep this place goes.

I felt the second hit connect and thought: *and then I dropped.*

I said this afterward — *and then I dropped* — and what I meant was: I released her. Opened my hands and let her fall the forty feet I had worked to take her up. She hit the ground. She hit it badly.

It costs nothing to appreciate clean work. I watched her fall and filed the feeling away — not satisfaction, exactly. More like the moment when a calculation proves out. The estimate was right. The result matches the projection.

Then Ilvara used her legendary reaction.

The fear came in like cold water — not a thought, just a presence, filling in the spaces where certainty had been. Her source. Her location. The fact that I would need to move toward her to finish what I started. All of it suddenly wrong in the way that visceral responses don't argue with. I could not approach her. My body was extremely clear on this point.

*Indeed.*

That was what I said. One word, and even that came out deflated — the sound of a calculation failing mid-execution, a plan that was working up until the moment it wasn't. I looked at the distance between us and understood, in the way that the frightened condition leaves no room for debate, that I was not closing it.

*Oh, is… it's that tall?*

I asked this when I registered how far up I currently was and how that mapped onto where she was. The surprise was genuine. The geometry had not been fully in my mind until that moment.

*Yeah, how do I not be frightened?*

A practical question. There was no point in sitting inside the feeling — I wanted the mechanism, the counter, the actionable step. End of my turn, or lesser restoration. Those were the options. I noted them.

I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right?

Alright.

Fine.

I held position above the battlefield and waited for the feeling to pass, which is all you can do. The monastery had a class on this, too. Resistance to fear, emotional regulation under magical compulsion, the discipline of continuing to function when your instincts are not your own. I remember the room was cold. I remember thinking it wouldn't apply to me. The monk who taught it — Brother Tharusk, or possibly Brother Sennavel, someone quieter than Quellin, someone who taught without diagrams — had apparently known something I didn't.

This is the part where I acknowledge that.

## Thorin — The Heart Fungus and the Drow Betrayal

The mushroom thing hit Zalthir like a wall falling over, which is more or less what it looked like from my position — a mass of bludgeoning bulk followed by whatever poison it carried, and Zalthir absorbed most of it through sheer monk stubbornness. He's good at that. Absorbing things without showing the cost.

"Death to mushrooms," I said, because that's where we were.

Then Jorlan moved.

He'd been fighting alongside us — or close enough that the distinction hadn't mattered — and then Ilvara took a wound that finally tipped his calculus, and suddenly he was breaking formation with a single shouted word: "Dovara!" Just her name, or whatever that name meant to him, and he was gone. Disengaging. Running toward her.

I started to process -  *He's disengaging to go attack Ilvara. I get to choose. Do I want to attack him or let him go?*

Zalthir said, "The tides have turned," in the dry way Zalthir says everything dry, which is most things.

I stood there and ran the numbers on Jorlan Duskryn. He'd come at us. He'd been working with us. He wanted to kill Ilvara. We wanted to kill Ilvara. The enemy of my enemy was running screaming toward my other enemy, and I had a triggered swing waiting if I wanted it.

"But he attacked us first to begin with, so it's like—"

And then I remember something Daz had said about Drow politics and figured it out, *He didn't think we could defeat her. But now that she's wounded, he wants to take care of her himself.*

That's Drow politics. The whole thing compressed into two sentences. Someone who was your enemy becomes your ally when they think you're losing, and then breaks away from your alliance the moment you start winning, so they can steal the kill. I've had cleaner dealings with hill giants who had genuine grudges.

"So, what happens if he kills her and we don't? Does that change anything? Does he get, like, first dibs on booty or something? And by booty, I mean, like—"

"I mean, like, they used to be Lover's Booty. I mean, like. The goods."

Nobody laughed. That was the right reaction. I have a certain appreciation for a joke that lands exactly as badly as expected.

I didn't know what to do. And when I don't  know what to do, I let my mind wander and then it came back and  said do the sensible thing. Which I interpreted as let Jorlan go, let whatever Drow drama unfold, let the girls fight. I set down the attack of opportunity I'd been holding.

"That makes me sad, though," I said, "because I like my attacks of opportunity."

I meant it. There's a satisfaction to a properly timed swing that you don't get from standing still and watching drow politics sort itself out like a very violent tennis match. But I had decided sensible. 

The one comfort was Glabbagool. In the midst of Ilvara conjuring a sphere of biting insects at the cave entrance — *Insects plague, that doesn't sound good,* Daz observed, with the tone of a man reviewing unexpected weather — Grygum asked about the ooze. Zalthir wanted to know if he was alive. Someone shouted that Glabbagool was alive. He had been sitting out there trying to participate in the conversation, chillaxing with his eyes, doing his best. Glabbagool always does his best. It's one of his best qualities.

I turned my attention to the heart fungus while Ilvara's insect cloud made its political statement at the entrance. The big one. The thing we'd come here to unmake.

"Is there anything I know about the fungus in terms of, like — is it resistant to stuff or whatever?"

And I remember my training about fungi, it was probably resistant to the usual things, the way fungus usually is, but that if I hit it hard enough it should die. And then I remembered something about radiant damage and how fungi really hate it. I shouted, *It actually is doubly not resistant to radiant damage.*

Grygum said, "Oh, that's good to know."

Dawnbringer had opinions about this information. She always has opinions. We'd worked out our arrangement — her light, my darkness preferences, the compromise that keeps both of us functional — and in that moment she didn't need to say anything because radiant damage against something doubly vulnerable to radiant damage is exactly the kind of problem she was built for.

I leaped down into the difficult terrain below the ledge. The ground underneath the heart fungus was wrong the way the whole cavern was wrong — Zuggtmoy's influence spreading through the rock itself, corrupting the floor with something that resisted clean footing. I planted, swung, and let Dawnbringer do what Dawnbringer does.

The fungus wilted.

 Grygum said, "Oh, the giant fungus?" like he needed confirmation, and his eyes said yes, the giant fungus, the one that had dominated the cavern. Gone.

"I do what I can," I said.

There was a thing that was trying to corrupt the world, and now it wasn't anymore. That's the whole transaction. A rock in the wrong place is just a rock; a rock in the right place is a wall. Dawnbringer in the right place, against something doubly not resistant to her, is a conversation that ends in one round.

Zalthir had taken the poisoned blow badly enough that I glanced over. He was holding together the way Zalthir holds together, which is to say completely outwardly and with significant internal cost. I thought about saying something and decided what he needed from me was not words. Sometimes what a person needs is for you to stand near them and not make it worse. I've had chicken pox. Zalthir had just taken a club the size of a cart wheel and a face full of poison, and he was still upright.

I thought, vaguely: *There, there. It'll be.*

The heart fungus was gone. Jorlan was somewhere across the cavern, running toward his revenge or his death or whatever Drow drama awaited him with Ilvara. Glabbagool had survived the insect plague and was staying close to the party, doing his ooze best. The insect cloud was still eating at the cave entrance.

I stood in the difficult terrain where the heart fungus had been, Dawnbringer still warm in my grip, and waited to see what the eight wanted me to do next.

## Daz — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The battlefield had become a taxonomy problem.

I run them through the list while ducking a crossbow bolt that misses me by what feels like courtesy: Ilvara, wounded and venomous, holding the center. Grygum angling toward Ilvara. Jorlan — Jorlan, who we'd watched barrel through our lines screaming his former lover's name — apparently now someone else's problem. The Big Heart Fungus: already gone, carved apart by Thorin in the time it takes most people to decide whether to act. And the insects. Always the insects. Ilvara's plague fills the middle distance with biting, stinging, churning noise, a wall of biological interference that had begun to feel personal.

Five new drow stepped out of the shadows.

Zalthir's voice, dry and somewhat delighted: "Five dark elves. That's awesome."

Grygum, quieter: "This is turning into the twelve days of festivus."

I agreed, privately. House T'sarran markings — I clocked that before the first bolt cleared the bowstring. A rival house, then. Not Vandree, not Mizzrym. Someone who had apparently decided the chaos of this particular cavern represented an opportunity worth pursuing. The tactical reasoning was not without merit. The execution, however.

Three crossbow bolts came at me. All three ate insects instead of wizard. The swarm, for once, earning its misery by absorbing the shots that Ilvara's spell had apparently been too generous to warn these newcomers about. I watched the T'sarran elite warrior turn to the mage with an expression I recognized from every failed operation I had ever observed from a comfortable distance.

*I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill.*

The mage had no good answer. He also, it emerged, had no Dispel Magic. The elite warrior processed this information with the grim resignation of a professional who has been let down by every supporting structure in his professional life.

I should have felt threatened. Instead I felt something closer to embarrassment on their behalf.

They looked a little too smug, too sure of themselves — the posture of people who had planned an ambush and not yet updated their confidence to reflect that the ambush was actively being digested by magical insects. They would update. The insects were thorough. But in that moment they still had the bearing of professionals arriving to collect, and the shots went wide, and I noted it the way you note a poorly shuffled deck: the tell is in the hands.

Grygum was already offering healing. Thorin somewhere behind me, navigating what he'd called the worst map in the Underdark. Asha Vandree praying to Lolth or preparing to pray or preparing to accuse someone of not praying — the volatility of her devotion made her difficult to predict on a round-by-round basis, which was the primary management problem I had been handling since before the fight started.

Then Kaelira stepped out of nowhere and walked directly into the insect cloud.

Professional. I'll give her that. She came through the swarm — taking hits, grimacing, not stopping — and arrived at my position with the expression of someone who has had simpler assignments and is not sure what she did to deserve this one.

"Come with me if you want to live, you idiot."

She waited a beat, perhaps in case I had missed it, then elaborated with the careful patience of a woman who has said this line before: "If you don't want to live, I'll still take you, but could you — it would be much easier if you'd just come."

The first rule of the House, as the training goes: *know who holds the debt before you decide whether to collect it.* Kaelira was holding something — a contract, a commission, someone's standing order to extract me from this cavern, someone who had paid and expected delivery. I did not know who that was. I did not know what I would owe them for it. Both gaps in my information seemed important.

What I knew was this: I was upright. I was functional. The T'sarran bolts had missed. The insects were, in their horrible way, protecting me. Ilvara was still in the room, and Ilvara was the one who had put the collar around my neck, and I had not come this far and burned this many spell slots to be carried out before the accounting was finished.

I had one other problem, which was Asha Vandree, who had already looked at the T'sarran drow with that edge in her eyes that precedes accusations. Zealotry is readable, once you've catalogued enough of it. She was three seconds from calling someone a traitor to Lolth. I had watched her manage Jorlan's abrupt defection from our engagement with religious gravity. Fresh unknown drow appearing from shadow would read to her as sabotage, infiltration, the work of enemies of the Spider Queen.

The con never stops. That's not a principle; it's just a fact about cons.

Before she could speak, I told her they were friends of Lolf. Here to help. Said it smoothly, said it fast, said it with the absolute confidence of someone who has never once doubted that Lolf was watching. The adaptation cost me nothing — it never does, when the groundwork is already laid. I just extended the fiction another room.

I filed Kaelira's look away for later. The dice were rolling. We'd see where they landed.

What I was not going to do was leave.

## Grygum — The Drow Reinforcements Arrive

The insect plague was mine. I want that on the record.

Not in any proud, chest-beating sense — more the way you'd note that a fire you lit to warm a camp has now spread to three buildings and the neighbors are arguing in the street. I cast it. I intended it. And when the House T'sarran drow materialized out of the tunnel darkness and walked straight into it, I had approximately one second to decide whether that was a disaster or a gift before the screaming started.

I filed it under gift.

I heard them before I saw them clearly — the particular cadence of Drow soldiers who expected a clean operation and are now receiving insects at high velocity into every exposed inch of skin. There were two of them doing most of the talking. An elite warrior and a mage, and the warrior was not happy.

"I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill."

The mage had no answer to this, which was its own kind of answer. What followed was the sort of conversation you have when a plan has visibly collapsed and someone needs to assign blame before they can do anything useful.

"And you don't have a Dispel Magic."

"No, I don't have a Dispel—"

"So I have to go through this. I don't know what else to tell you."

*It's tough being a minion.* I didn't say it to anyone in particular. Just a private note, filed away. The warrior's frustration was so familiar — the operative tone of someone who signed up for a straightforward contract and has just realized their employer oversold it. I've been that soldier, metaphorically speaking, in enough bad situations to recognize the register.

The mage tried something decisive. A lightning bolt, aimed at the darkness where Daz had been, where the mage probably calculated a Drow wizard would logically be standing. The bolt cracked out, lit the cavern sideways for half a heartbeat, and hit nothing of consequence. I watched Daz's face from across the room. He looked personally offended.



The new faction complicated everything. I watched Asha Vandree register their arrival and go rigid.

"Who are you? You must be traitors! Traitors to Lolth!"

She wasn't wrong to be suspicious. They were Drow she didn't recognize, arriving unannounced into a battle that was already a theological crisis — Ilvara corrupted, Zuggtmoy's influence spreading, the whole sanctum compromised. From Asha's perspective, any unknown Drow in this cavern was a suspect. She had all the reactive certainty of the genuinely devout, which is to say she was wrong in a way that was completely internally consistent.

Daz, without missing a beat, said: "No, these are friends of Lolth, and they're here to help."

I watched him say it. The smoothness of it still impresses me, though I've had time to think about it since. He wasn't just lying — he was giving Asha a framework she could accommodate, handing her a category she already believed in. *Friends of Lolth.* Of course. That's who shows up when things get hard. I watched Asha's posture shift from accusatory to something more like provisional acceptance, and I thought: he's very good at this, and that is both useful and worth keeping notes on.

On the other side of the battlefield, Zalthir was having his own problems of a different variety. The frightened condition was on him — Ilvara's doing — and I could see the calculation happening in his face. He wanted to go after Ilvara. The direct path was closed. He was looking for the loophole.

"I mean, that is the way it was going," he said, "but the spirit seems to be that I should do nothing to become closer to her, or have her closer to me. I am supposed to be trying to not be near her."

He was acknowledging the spirit of the thing. Not just the mechanical restriction — the actual meaning of it, the thing the fear was doing to him. I found that unexpectedly honest for Zalthir, who generally prefers to treat emotional states as obstacles to be circumnavigated rather than phenomena to be observed. Someone asked how he was going to slap someone from a distance.

"I'm a monk."

Flat. Final. The kind of answer that means *I have solved for this already, stop asking questions.* And he had — he turned from Ilvara entirely, teleported across the battlefield to the House T'sarran flank, and killed the wounded mage in a single punch. The GM said something that amounted to *we barely knew you*, and that was appropriate. The mage had made exactly one contribution to this battle, which was missing with a lightning bolt.

Meanwhile, across the room, Nym Duskryn was having a conversation with Daz that I could hear in pieces but was following as best I could.

"Can you please keep your head down and out of combat? We only get paid if you live."

*Perhaps your benefactor has a good idea,* she'd said first, trying flattery. Daz didn't move.

"I am not gonna leave. Everyone here — to fight for me without me also participating. I don't give a damn if you get paid. The best way for you to get paid, then, is to continue the fight and prevent all of us from dying."

I've thought about that speech since. There's a version of it that's just pride — the wizard refusing to be managed, the ego that can't accept protection. But listening to it in the moment, I heard something else. He meant the *everyone here* part. The Ember Vanguard. He wasn't going to be extracted while we were still in it.

Nym turned to Kaelira. "Can I kill your brother?"

Kaelira didn't pause long. "He was always the dumber of the ones, and if I understood correctly, he's covered in fungus?"

"Yes."

"Very well."

I noted this. I note a lot of things. The Duskryn family dynamic is apparently exactly what you'd expect from a Drow aristocratic house, which is to say: dismissive, transactional, and willing to authorize fratricide via quiet conversation in the middle of a multi-faction battle. Jorlan's own family was discussing his disposal the way you'd discuss returning a faulty piece of equipment.

Daz had one more moment that cut through the chaos. Someone — a warning, a shout — indicated that Ilvara was casting Flamestrike directly at him. His response was immediate.

"Fuck that lady. Let's kill her."

Simple. Clear. No philosophical apparatus required. I appreciated it. The T'sarran drow were retreating or dead, the mage was eliminated, Asha had been redirected, the insect plague was still working, and Ilvara remained. The situation had clarified itself down to its essential problem.

Then Daz did what Daz does when he's offended: he threw a fireball into the darkness.

There is a particular quality to a fireball detonating in an enclosed Underdark cavern. It's not just the light, though the light is considerable. It's the way the sound wraps around the stone and comes back to you changed, and the heat arrives a beat after you expect it, and then for a moment everything is very quiet. Several of the T'sarran guards were not quiet for much longer than that. The mage survived, barely — I could see that much even from my angle, the singed silhouette still upright in the aftermath, recalculating their life choices.

That's when they started talking about retreat, with the particular injured dignity of people who are embarrassed and want to sound threatening anyway. "The matron mother of House T'sarran will hear about this." I filed that one too. *They'll be back. They won't be better organized.* The last thing I heard them say before the chaos reshuffled was a nervous question about whether she was going to cast Fireball — *she* meaning Ilvara, who was still extremely present and extremely dangerous, and who was muttering something unsettling about Zuggtmoy. Something about "the bride" coming to cause chaos and mayhem. Said it like a prayer, which was exactly what it was, and that was the detail that made my shoulders tighten more than any of the flying spells.

A fanatic praying in the middle of a losing fight is either about to surrender or about to do something catastrophic. Ilvara was not the surrendering kind.

There's a story I find myself returning to in moments like this. Protanther the Ancient Gold Dragon had replayed an unfinished chess game in his mind for four hundred years, certain he'd mapped every possible move. A dwarf named Stroud who couldn't actually play chess sat down across from him, rolled a natural 20, and won. Protanther was so astonished he joined the alliance against Tiamat. Four centuries of preparation. One impossible roll.

I'd had plans for the Big Heart Fungus. I had something ready. And then Thorin killed it in a single round with Dawnbringer and I closed that file and moved on. Now the T'sarran drow had walked into my insect plague and that plan, the one I'd held loosely, had paid out sideways in a way I hadn't arranged.

I took notes. It's what I do.

The battle wasn't over. But the new faction had arrived, identified itself as incompetent, and begun disintegrating, and Daz had smoothly redirected Asha before she could fragment our already complicated alliance further, and Zalthir was punching things on the far side of the cavern, and Ilvara was still standing and still devoted to her demon bride and still needed to be dealt with.

I checked who needed healing. Then I looked for an angle.

## Zalthir — Ilvara's Desperate Stand

The frightened condition is an interesting thing when you are a monk.

There was a class for it, I'm fairly certain. Brother Quellin — or possibly Brother Verath, the one who always had chalk dust on his sleeves — taught something about fear magic in the third year. I remember the light through the window was very good that afternoon. I do not remember anything else.

What I remember about the Ilvara fight, in the immediate moment before the condition took hold, is that everything had been going well. Perfectly, even. I had her in my grip. I had hit her twice, hard, the way you hit something you need to stop permanently, and the numbers in my mind — distance, angle, force — had all resolved cleanly. Then the magic landed, and the numbers stopped mattering, because my legs had developed their own opinions about proximity to Ilvara Mizzrym.

*I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right? Alright, fine.*

Not resentment, exactly. The condition says what it says about the spirit of the thing, and the spirit of the thing is that I should not be closer to her. I applied this against myself without needing anyone to tell me. The monastery trained me to be honest about constraints, even inconvenient ones. Especially inconvenient ones. I filed the frustration away and looked for something else to do.

This is what I was doing — finding something else to do — when Ilvara turned her attention to the ground.

I watched her let go of whatever she had been maintaining. You can see it, if you're watching for it, the small internal release of concentration, the way a spellcaster's shoulders settle differently when they stop holding a working. She dropped it deliberately. Which meant she was building toward something worse.

The fungal bloom came up around Jorlan like the floor itself had opinions. Difficult terrain. Thorin had been complaining, since approximately the beginning of this fight, about the layout of the cavern, the sprawling distances, the way every engagement required sprinting to reach it.

"All I know is this map sucks, because I feel like I'm running around trying to get to things."

He wasn't wrong. I had watched him cover the same ground three times, arriving slightly after the moment he was needed, sword already raised for something that had moved. "I'm running around just trying to catch up with everybody." This from a dwarf who had already destroyed the Big Heart Fungus in a single pass, which I mention only because he seemed not to have noticed how significant that was, being already frustrated about the geography.

The fungal bloom was Ilvara telling Jorlan to stay where he was. She had other things to do.

She called on Zuggtmoy. You could feel the register shift — this was not Ilvara expending tactical resources. This was Ilvara making a declaration, the way you speak when you're not talking to the people in front of you but to something behind them, something farther back. Promising chaos. Promising mayhem. The corruption that had been growing through her since Velkynvelve made her voice resonate oddly, like sound off wet stone, and I found myself recalibrating what I was looking at. She was not a high priestess fighting a difficult battle. She was a channel.

Then the Flamestrike came down.

Divine fire. Not conjured from ambient heat or ignited material — called down, the way Zuggtmoy's power calls things down, from somewhere else, through her. It hit Daz and Nym Duskryn, and the sound of it was enormous in the enclosed space, and the light was the wrong color, too orange and wrong at the edges, like fire that had traveled too far from where it was supposed to be.

I watched it from the far end of the cavern where my legs had decided I should remain.

There was nothing precise I could do about that. I noted it. I filed it. I looked for my angle.

## Grygum — The Fall of Ilvara

The bolt left my hand like a prayer I meant.

That's the honest way to say it. Guiding Bolt — the textbook says it calls down radiant energy from the divine. My version always looks like someone lit a signal flare inside a cathedral. I don't know if that's Bahamut's doing or just the way I visualize it. I stopped trying to separate those things a while ago. What matters is that it flew true, and it hit Ilvara Mizzrym square in the chest, and Ilvara Mizzrym, Zuggtmoy-corrupted high priestess of House Mizzrym, longest-standing nemesis of the Ember Vanguard, exploded.

That is not a metaphor.

Let me back up, because the chaos that preceded that moment deserves its own account.

Thorin had destroyed the bridge. I want to be clear that I did not know he was going to do that, and I want to be equally clear that it was exactly the right call. The structural integrity of that span had been bothering me since we first crossed it — I took notes — and Thorin apparently reached the same conclusion through the more direct method of hitting it until it stopped existing. Ilvara and Jorlan went down. I watched them fall, and I held my breath, because people with that much combat experience do not die from falls that civilized. And indeed, they did not. Quick reflexes, both of them. Landed hard but landed functional. Drow are annoyingly athletic.

That was when I took stock. Old habit from training: before committing to offense, check the room. I called out across the noise — "Does anybody need any serious healing?" — because if Zalthir had been reduced to single digits while I was busy preparing a spell I wasn't going to get to use, that was information I needed before I pointed radiant energy at anything.

Daz called back, "I think I can take one more round of hits."

Classic Daz. Eighteen out of forty-four, and his read of the situation is "one more round." There's a version of faith that works like that — not certainty, just the calculus of acceptable risk and the refusal to stop moving. I respect it. I do not share it, but I respect it.

I was standing back. That was intentional. It always is. The tactical position of a cleric who has learned from hard experience is: close enough to reach people who are falling, far enough that you are not the person falling. I had the angle. I had the opening. Ilvara was vulnerable to radiant damage — I had taken notes on that too, somewhere between Blingdenstone and here — and she was on the ground, and Zalthir and Thorin had made her bleed.

I thought, briefly, about what I was about to do to another person. Even a person who had enslaved us, who had handed us to the Underdark, who had allowed Zuggtmoy's corruption to sink into her bones until she was more spore than soul. I thought about it for approximately half a second, which is the appropriate amount of time to spend on that thought in the middle of a fight that's been going on for longer than most meals. Then I cast Guiding Bolt.

She was particularly vulnerable to radiant damage. I knew it before — that's why I chose the spell — but there is a difference between knowing something theoretically and watching it happen.

She detonated. That's the only word. Ilvara Mizzrym ceased to be a drow priestess and became a cloud of poisonous spores. A large cloud. Moving outward in all directions, including toward Zalthir and Thorin, who had been in the middle distance and were now inside something that could kill them.

My first reaction was not theological. My first reaction was: "Not ideal. How big is the cloud?"

Because Zalthir and Thorin were in it, and I needed to know whether I was about to spend the next several rounds casting Lesser Restoration, or whether there was a faster problem to solve. Pragmatic. It's not a lack of faith; it's paying attention to the actual situation rather than the symbolic shape of victory. She was dead. That was good. My friends were in her explosion. That was the new problem.

I was glad I was standing back. I said that out loud, actually, which probably sounded callous but was just true. The radius of a poisonous spore cloud is not a mystery I wanted to solve from the inside.

What came after was the kind of scene you watch with one part of your attention while the rest of you is still running triage. The remaining House T'sarran warrior — the one who had been bickering with their mage in the tunnel about Dispel Magic and easy kills and who was supposed to handle what — looked at Ilvara's spore cloud, looked at us, made the only sensible assessment available to them, and retreated. With exactly as much dignity as you'd expect from someone who had just watched their entire plan collapse.

"We will meet again," they said. "Enjoy your victory for the moment."

And then there was Asha Vandree. Who had watched all of this — the fall, the lightning, the Guiding Bolt, the detonation, the spores — and who found, in the settling dust and the poisonous aftermath, something that looked like religious revelation. She dropped to her knees. She began to pray. Beatitudes to Lolth, rising in cadence, the fervor of a priestess who has just witnessed something she can frame as holy.

"The evil!" she said. "The undead!"

Thorin, who had been navigating this entire fight from the tactical disadvantage of being the most straightforwardly violent person in a room full of drow political maneuvering, looked around at the praying priestess, the dispersing spores, the retreating T'sarran warrior, and said, with the exhausted certainty of someone who has been watching a tennis match while trying to find a clear swing:

"Nobody knows what the hell's going on."

I thought about the chess game.
