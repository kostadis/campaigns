## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The last time I'd tried this, she'd slipped away with a legendary action before I could follow through. That was the fact I'd spent however many hours since then filing under *information I now possess*. The plan was the same, because I am a monk and my options remain approximately what they have always been: get close, hit the thing, repeat. The question was whether she'd burn the teleport to survive the first hit — and if she did, whether she could do something more dangerous with that action instead.

I worked through it while the rest of the cavern was still happening around me. The GM — Kostadis — laid out the math plainly: she can move once, as a legendary action, so if I force that response, she doesn't have it for anything worse. The entire plan reduced to movement as cost, my presence as a resource drain. A monk's entire fighting philosophy, applied to a Zuggtmoy-corrupted drow priestess hovering forty feet above a fungal abomination.

"You know what a monk does," Kostadis said. "You run, run, run, and then slaps."

"Pretty much."

The Eldritch tendrils snapped outward as I closed the distance through open air — the tattoo across my knuckles extending reach I shouldn't have, reaching her before she could process that I was there. Sixteen damage. And she was grappled.

She didn't teleport.

Good.

"Now that I'm holding her," I said, "I will punch her again. Because why not."

The first critical hit landed. The second followed immediately after. Somewhere between those two strikes, with Ilvara suspended in my grip forty feet above the cavern floor while the table erupted around me, I became aware that this was one of those moments. I filed it away. Back to punching.

Sixteen damage from the first crit. Eighteen from the second.

Then I opened my hands and let her fall.

There was a class, somewhere in the monastery curriculum, that probably addressed what happens after you execute a plan perfectly and the enemy is still standing. I think it was Brother Tharusk — or possibly the quiet one with the ink-stained hands, I'm genuinely not certain — something about not treating the fall as the end of the engagement. I remember the quality of the light through the window. I do not remember what they said.

Forty feet of stone cavern rushed up to meet her. The fall was brutal. And then Kostadis asked for a DC-14 Wisdom saving throw.

Ilvara, broken and furious and crumpled on the cavern floor, reached into whatever darkness Zuggtmoy had left in her and poured it directly into my mind.

"Well," I said, "that didn't work."

The frightened condition is specific. It means I cannot voluntarily move toward the source of my fear. I floated at the cavern ceiling and looked down at the drow priestess I had just sent plummeting forty feet, and my legs refused to carry me toward her. Everything I had built in the opening exchange — the grapple, the altitude, the two criticals — she undid with a single legendary reaction, and the fight was now happening somewhere I couldn't reach.

I thought about it for a moment. The spirit of the thing seemed fairly clear: I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right. Alright. Fine. I applied the rule against myself without needing to be told twice. That's the discipline.

What filled the gap was the heart fungus.

Kostadis mentioned it had fifteen feet of reach. I was at the ceiling. The math, which I had not done in advance, resolved unfavorably: the enormous fungal mass could reach all the way up to where I was floating, pinned there by supernatural terror and poor timing, and the blow it landed was the kind that rearranges priorities mid-thought. Fifty-two damage, before I deflected twelve of the bludgeoning.

"Wow," said Grygum, from somewhere safer. "You got a 1 and added an oven there."

The table had opinions about my optimization. I acknowledged them and returned to the immediate problem, which was that I was taking significant punishment from a creature that could reach me, I was unable to close on the creature I'd actually been fighting, and my options for ending either of these conditions were: wait for the end of my turn, or receive a lesser restoration from someone with a lesser restoration available.

"Yeah," I said. "How do I not be frightened?"

Waiting, apparently. I was a spectator in my own fight, pinned at the ceiling while Ilvara's destruction played out below me — Thorin's Dawnbringer, Grygum's guiding bolt, the whole ensemble — doing nothing except not approaching the thing that had frightened me, because that was the rule, and the rule was being applied whether I liked it or not.

At least I'd gotten the two criticals in first.
