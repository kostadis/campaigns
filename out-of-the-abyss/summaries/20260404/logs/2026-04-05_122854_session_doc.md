## Section — Thorin — The Heart Fungus and the Drow Betrayal

The handoff puts me somewhere in the middle of this fight, which is where I've spent most of the last hour. Everywhere and nowhere at once, because this map, as I have said out loud to no one who cared, genuinely sucks.

I don't mean that as a complaint. I mean it as a tactical assessment. Walkways, drop-offs, difficult terrain on the cavern floor, Ilvara bobbing around like she's been told the ceiling is the safest place to be — and me, a dwarf, built for the ground, built for the close press of stone on both sides, spending half the fight running to things that have already moved. "All I know is this map sucks, because I feel like I'm running around trying to get to things." I said it out loud. Zalthir didn't bother responding. That's basically his version of agreeing.

But then I heard it: the heart fungus is doubly vulnerable to radiant damage.

I stopped moving. Looked down at Dawnbringer, already lit, already warm in my hand — she'd been warm since we entered this cavern, actually, because she doesn't fully dim regardless of what the situation calls for, which is a whole ongoing conversation between us. I asked. She confirmed. The enormous pulsating mass of Zuggtmoy's corruption, the thing that had been squatting at the center of this whole festering pocket of the Underdark like a second heart —

Radiant damage. Doubly.

I had the right question, the right weapon, and a straight line to the target. The giants who raised me would say that's not luck. They'd say you wait for those three things to align and then you don't hesitate. A rock in the wrong place is just a rock, but a rock in the right place is a wall. Dawnbringer in the wrong hand against the wrong creature is just a sword. Here, now, it was something else entirely.

I pushed through the difficult terrain on the cavern floor and I swung.

Dawnbringer crackled. Holy light split the air — that particular quality of radiance she makes when she's not holding back, which is something between sunlight and a rebuke, and I have never asked her to explain the difference because I don't think I want to know. The fungus shuddered. Then again. The flesh came apart in ways that flesh, even fungal flesh, is not supposed to come apart — recoiling from the light, caving inward, folding. The heart fungus wilted.

One round. The whole thing.

"I do what I can," I said.

Kostadis made a sound I recognized. I'd heard it before, in the way a stone giant elder looks after he's held a thought for three days and someone solves it by accident in three seconds. Not anger. Something more complicated. He'd built this thing. It was detailed and layered and probably had a whole second phase nobody was ever going to see.

"I spent so much time creating this icon," he said.

"I know," said Grygum, and he meant it. "It was beautiful."

Zalthir said it was pretty. Grygum told Kostadis he liked it very much. There was a brief moment of genuine mourning, the kind you can only have for a thing that was well-made and is now gone. I did not apologize. That felt like the right call. You honor a thing by destroying it cleanly, not by pretending you didn't.

Then Jorlan moved.

I heard him before I saw him clearly — the name first, screamed across the cavern from somewhere in the chaos of bodies and spores and Ilvara's Insect Plague eating the air at the cave entrance.

*"Dovara!"*

And I understood it immediately, the way you understand certain things without needing them explained: the old wound, the old score, the man who had been cast aside and beaten down and used until he wasn't useful and then discarded — he was looking at Ilvara on the cavern floor, broken by a forty-foot fall that Zalthir had arranged with his usual clinical satisfaction, and something in Jorlan that had been waiting a very long time had just decided it was done waiting.

He broke ranks. Turned his back on us entirely. Sprinted toward her.

And my ability to swing — the discipline I've spent months in the Underdark developing around exactly this kind of moment — it triggered. His disengage. Five feet. I had the right, clean and mechanical, to put a radiant strike into his back.

I didn't swing.

I held Dawnbringer, still warm, and I watched him go, and I said: "But he attacked us first to begin with, so it's like—"

And Kostadis said yeah, but he didn't think we could defeat Ilvara. Now that she was wounded, he wanted to handle her himself.

Zalthir said, "The tides have turned."

Which is true. Accurate. Also not the question I was stuck on.

The question I was stuck on was the logistics of it. That's how I solved the Underdark: turn the moral tangle into a logistics question, find the answer at the bottom of that, and work backward. So: *if he kills her and we don't, does that change anything? Does he get, like, first dibs on booty or something? And by booty, I mean, like—*

Kostadis said he didn't know.

*I mean, like, they used to be Lover's Booty, I mean, like… the goods.*

Nobody disagreed with the framing. That felt like progress. The question was real: does Jorlan getting the kill matter? Does it reroute resources, rewards, faction dynamics, all the political machinery I will never fully understand and have mostly stopped pretending to navigate?

Kostadis said: you decide. This is a classic roll-the-dice moment.

So I rolled.

Eight. The eight means I do the sensible thing. Which I had already decided meant: let them go. Let the girls fight. Let years of drow cruelty and abandoned loyalty and whatever Jorlan and Ilvara were to each other play out between the two of them, because the honest truth is I could stop him, and stopping him might be tactically correct, and stopping him might even be *right* in some absolute sense — he attacked us first, he chose his side badly, the attack of opportunity is earned and clean and mine.

But.

He chose a different enemy to spend it on. And this far down in the Underdark, that felt like a kind of justice I didn't want to interrupt.

"That makes me sad, though," I said, "because I like my attacks of opportunity."

I lowered Dawnbringer and watched him go.

And then I stood there, the fight still churning around me, Glabbagool absorbing twenty-six points of insect damage out in the swarm because he had been trying to participate in the conversation and nobody had warned him to move — and I watched Jorlan run toward the woman who had unmade him, and I held the strike, and it was the second time in one scene I'd chosen restraint over the obvious swing, and neither time had it been easy, and that's the thing nobody tells you about discipline.

It doesn't get easier. It just gets more clearly chosen.

"And I'm just sitting here," I said to no one in particular, "watching them trade blows like a tennis match."

Dawnbringer was still warm. The heart fungus was gone. Jorlan was almost there.
