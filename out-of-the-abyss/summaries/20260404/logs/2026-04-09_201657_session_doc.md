## Section — Grygum — The Drow Reinforcements Arrive

The insects arrived before the drow did.

That's the thing about an insect plague — it doesn't announce itself with war drums or torchlight. It just *is*, a churning cloud of biting, crawling wrongness that doesn't care about your tactical plans or your faction allegiances. Ilvara had cast it originally. Which meant the buzzing darkness filling that tunnel mouth was, in a technical sense, her fault. I noted that with some satisfaction.

What I did not note with satisfaction was the new shapes emerging from within it.

Three Drow warriors, crossbows leveled. And behind them, robes catching no light, a mage I did not recognize.

I'd been running a mental ledger all fight — who was wounded, who was running low, which threats needed addressing in what order. It's what I do. I had entries for Ilvara, for the Big Heart Fungus (now a closed file, courtesy of Thorin and Dawnbringer — "I do what I can," he'd said, understated as always), for Jorlan and whatever grief-fueled suicide run he was apparently on. Now I had to open a new column and start filling it in.

*House T'sarran.* That's what I was hearing from them, the recriminations already beginning before they'd even fully entered the room. One warrior and one mage, the mage having apparently made several promises about how easy this was going to be.

"I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill," the warrior said.

The mage said nothing. Which was, honestly, the correct response.

"And you don't have a Dispel Magic."

"No, I don't have a Dispel—"

"So I have to go through this. I don't know what else to tell you."

*It's tough being a minion.* I didn't say it to anyone in particular. Just a quiet observation for my own amusement, watching these two bicker inside a swarm of magically conjured insects while everything around them was on fire in at least two different ways.

The lightning bolt, when it came, went somewhere other than where Daz was. The mage had taken a guess at the darkness and guessed wrong. I watched the flash illuminate nothing useful and thought: four hundred years of preparation still has a blind spot. I don't know why I thought of Stroud and Protanther in that moment. Probably because there was something almost poignant about a mage who had clearly planned this very carefully, right down to the entry point and the target identification, and had still gotten the most fundamental thing wrong. Sometimes the person who doesn't know what they're doing rolls perfectly. Sometimes the person who does know what they're doing fires into empty air.

The fireball, when it came back, did not miss.

Daz, still somehow upright despite everything that had been thrown at him, sent it straight into the tunnel mouth. The light was extraordinary. Brief, but extraordinary. I watched the guards go and the mage stagger and thought: he is *not* going to stand down, is he. Some people you can read in one exchange. Daz I'd read in approximately the first ten minutes of knowing him, and the reading had not changed. The man would rather be on fire alongside his allies than safe in the dark somewhere being carried.

Which was, frankly, why Nym Duskryn's request had gone over so well.

I hadn't caught all of the exchange — I was busy, there was a lot happening — but I caught enough. "Can you please keep your head down and out of combat? We only get paid if you live." And Daz: "I am not gonna leave. Everyone here... to fight for me without me also participating. I don't give a damn if you get paid. The best way for you to get paid, then, is to continue the fight and prevent all of us from dying."

Nym had then turned to Kaelira and, with what I could only describe as professional exhaustion, asked: "Can I kill your brother?"

Kaelira had considered this. "He was always the dumber of the ones, and if I understood correctly, he's covered in fungus?"

"Yes."

"Very well."

This is what Drow family reunions look like. I took notes.

What pulled my attention back to the immediate problem was Asha Vandree, who had apparently noticed the new arrivals and reached her own conclusions. She was pointing, her voice rising toward the fervent register she used for Lolth's name in prayers.

"Who are you? You must be traitors! Traitors to Lolth!"

I watched Daz process this. You could see the calculation happen — the quick assessment of how many different fires were currently burning and which one needed to be put out first. He was already running a deception with Asha, had been since before this fight started. One more layer wouldn't cost him anything. And if Asha decided the Vandree extraction team were enemies, the entire alliance collapsed and we had a third faction to fight.

"No," Daz said, smooth as anything, "these are friends of Lolth, and they're here to help."

Asha looked at them. The Vandree warriors looked back. Nobody contradicted him. I filed this under *things Daz is better at than most people* and moved on.

The T'sarran mage was still standing, which was a problem. Not for long, as it turned out. Zalthir, who had been visibly unhappy about the constraints Ilvara's magic had placed on his preferred approach to problems, found a different direction to channel that unhappiness. He crossed the battlefield in a way that I have stopped trying to fully process — shadow monks do what shadow monks do — and arrived in front of the wounded mage.

"I'm a monk," he'd said earlier, when someone had questioned his methodology. It remained, somehow, a complete answer.

The mage went down. The T'sarran warrior, now alone and considerably less smug than he'd been at the tunnel mouth, delivered his final line with the hollow conviction of someone who knows they've lost but is committed to the exit speech anyway.

"The matron mother of House T'sarran will hear about this."

And then: "We will meet again. Enjoy your victory for the moment."

They left. The insects kept buzzing. Kaelira and Nym and their team held their positions, watching, not quite allies and not quite enemies, the whole situation balanced on Daz's claim about friends of Lolth and Asha's willingness to believe it.

This is turning into the twelve days of Christmas, I thought. Every round, something new. Every round, another faction, another threat, another obligation to add to the ledger.

I checked on everyone's condition. Quietly. It's what I do.
