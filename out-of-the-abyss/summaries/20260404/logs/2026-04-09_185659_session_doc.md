## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The second attempt. That's what I was calling it, privately, as I crossed the cavern — though calling it that implied the first one had been an attempt rather than a lesson. Last session I'd flown in, reached for her, and she'd simply not been there anymore. Gone before my hands found her. I'd filed it under *note the reaction, account for it next time*, and moved on.

Except there hadn't been a next time. Until now.

*Last time, I tried to grab her, and then she used a legendary action to teleport away, right?*

The correction came back flat: not a lair action. A legendary action. The distinction mattered, tactically — I'd been thinking about the wrong resource. I recalibrated without comment. Second try is the charm, or give up on that whole plan. And I couldn't give up, because the math was simple: I had no ranged options. None. I was a monk at distance from a wounded high priestess floating in corrupted darkness, and my entire contribution to this fight was closing with something and hitting it until it stopped. There was a class at the monastery for this kind of tactical limitation. I think it was Brother Quellin — or possibly the one with the ink-stained hands who never seemed to wash them. Whoever it was had tried to explain the difference between a weapon and the absence of alternatives. I had not paid attention. I was paying attention now.

The other thing they'd told me — and this I *had* processed, just slowly — was that forcing her legendary action was itself a form of pressure. Every time she teleported away from me, that was a reaction she wasn't spending on something worse. *You're burning her,* the logic ran. *You'd only get one attack, but she'd only have the one response.* The losing proposition was a trade, not a loss. I'd spent enough time at sea to understand trades.

So I moved.

The Eldritch Claw tattoo along my forearm came to life in the dark, and the tentacles reached before I'd fully arrived — fifteen feet of reach on dark tendrils that don't care about altitude or positioning or the corrupted air between me and the target. They found Ilvara and they *took*. The strike landed, the grip closed, and for one moment that felt longer than it was, the most dangerous thing in this cavern was suspended forty feet above the stone with nothing under her feet and my hold around her body.

*Sixteen damage. And she's grappled.*

I'd thought — when I'd bothered to think about Ilvara at all over the months of the Underdark — that she would feel significant. The thing we'd been running from, the priestess of House Mizzrym who'd had us in chains, the Lolth-chosen face of everything this place wanted to do to us. But holding her aloft, what I actually felt was: *this is working.* That was all. The tactical satisfaction of a thing proceeding as planned.

And she was *there.* In my grip. Couldn't teleport from a grapple.

*Now that I'm holding her, I will punch her again. Because why not.*

The first hit was clean, the kind of strike where your body finds the angle before your mind articulates it — the sort of hit that doesn't feel like effort at all, just *correct.* Critical. The second one landed even harder. Another critical, because apparently the ceiling of Velkynvelve was where I kept all my best form. Eighteen more damage. Back-to-back. She was bleeding at altitude and I had her.

And then I released.

She fell forty feet. The stone said something definitive about that.

For a moment, the fight felt resolved. It wasn't — I knew it wasn't, there were still fungal horrors and whatever Jorlan was doing and the heart-thing pulsing behind us — but there was a breath of something that might have been satisfaction before the situation corrected itself.

She was on the ground. Broken and bleeding and barely upright. And then she reached into whatever she had left and *pushed.*

Something cold flooded my mind. Not pain — the strikes had been pain, this was different. This was a presence, insistent and enormous and wrong, pressing through every disciplined wall I had. I held for a moment. The moment ended.

Fear. Real fear, the kind that doesn't care that you just dropped her forty feet, the kind that doesn't negotiate with your tactical assessment of the situation. She was below me and I could not move toward her. Could not.

*Indeed.*

What else was there to say? I was frightened. She'd protected herself against the one offensive asset that had been working — against me specifically, against the thing she'd watched drop her from the ceiling. The most elegant counter available to a creature that can no longer run.

I hung at the cavern ceiling, motionless, watching the fight continue below me. Watching Thorin and Grygum and Daz and the newcomers do the work I couldn't do. The heart fungus found me up there — reached its fifteen feet upward and struck with everything it had, bludgeoning and worse, and I deflected what I could and absorbed the rest, which was too much, and I filed that away too under *note the gap, account for it later.*

The fungal minions died. The fear held. Below me, the war continued.

*I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right? Alright, fine.*

The discipline was still there, even if the body wasn't. I worked through the spirit of it myself, applied it against my own interest without needing to be told. That much, at least, had stuck from the monastery. I couldn't tell you which monk taught it. I don't remember the content. I remember the quality of the light through the eastern window and wanting to be somewhere else.

I waited for it to break.
