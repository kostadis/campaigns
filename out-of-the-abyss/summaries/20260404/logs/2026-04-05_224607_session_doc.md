## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The memory of the last time sat in my chest like a stone.

She had slipped free. Legendary action, not a lair action — Kostadis corrected me when I said it wrong, and I filed the correction away because precision matters. What mattered was that I had closed on her, and she had simply *not been there anymore*, and I had spent the next several minutes pretending I had planned for that. I had not planned for that.

"Second try is the charm," I said, "or give up on that whole plan? I don't know. I mean, I can only melee anyway, so I guess I'll just... try that same thing again."

Not inspiring. But accurate.

Kostadis walked me through the mechanics — if she teleports on a hit, I burn her legendary action, which prevents something more dangerous. "You know what a monk does," he said. "You run, run, run, and then slaps."

"Pretty much."

He wasn't wrong. That's the entirety of the discipline, condensed. The monastery had seventeen forms of non-physical combat, a semester on pressure points, a full course on the philosophical underpinning of restraint as force. I attended approximately none of these with any sustained attention. I was there for the running and the hitting. Somewhere in the deep catalog of what I chose not to learn, there had probably been a class on fighting a drow priestess who can vanish at will. Brother — Quellin, maybe, or the one with the ink-stained hands, I'm genuinely not certain — had probably covered this exact scenario. "What do you do when your enemy removes herself from the encounter?" The answer was not "hit her again until she runs out of places to be," but that's what I had.

That was what I was going to do.

---

The heart fungus dominated the cavern floor below like an argument I didn't have time to engage with. I moved past it, up — the ceiling, the walls, whatever angle got me to her fastest. The whole body is the weapon. That part of the training I retained.

I reached her.

The Eldritch Claw tattoo across my forearm bloomed into tendrils of force, fifteen feet of reach that I didn't have to earn with a running start, and they closed around Ilvara's limbs with the finality of a knot pulled tight. The grapple landed. Sixteen damage.

She had nowhere to go.

There's a particular quality to that moment — when something that has been moving freely is suddenly *fixed in space* — that the monastery probably tried to teach me to feel as something philosophically meaningful. I felt it as: *now I can hit her again.*

"Now that I'm holding her, I will punch her again," I said. "Because why not?"

The first strike was a critical hit. The second was a critical hit. I am not sure what Thorin said — something about Nazis, which I parsed as enthusiasm — and I know Grygum said something, and the table was loud, and none of it reached me inside the fight. Sixteen more, then eighteen more. The numbers landed like stones into still water and I watched the ripples and threw another stone.

Two critical strikes in succession, both delivered at the precise register of a monk who has stopped thinking and is simply hitting. This is what I came to the monastery to learn to do. I absorbed this part. I was paying full attention during this part.

---

Then I dropped.

Not strategically. The grapple, the altitude, the force of the blow — she fell forty feet to the cavern floor, and I was there when she hit.

"Roll a DC-14 Wisdom saving throw," Kostadis said.

I rolled.

"Well," I said. "That didn't work."

And then he explained what I already knew was coming, because I had felt it arrive the moment the save failed — the cold thing that moved into my mind like water into a crack. *Frightened.* Which is, as Kostadis helpfully observed, very convenient, because now I cannot approach her. She had protected herself against me using the most efficient possible mechanism: she made me protect her from myself.

"Indeed," I said.

There was a class for this. Fear resistance, fear mitigation — I believe it was Brother Tharusk, or possibly the tall one who always smelled of incense, the one who said something about the difference between danger and dread that I remember thinking was very interesting before I stopped listening. He had probably explained exactly what happens when a drow priestess reaches into a monk's mind and installs an irrational prohibition. He had probably offered countermeasures.

I did not have the countermeasures. I had the condition.

*I am supposed to be trying to not be near her.* That's what it meant, mechanically and literally. *Right. Alright, fine.*

---

The heart fungus, which I had been mentally deprioritizing because there were other things to hit, solved the problem of my inaction by reaching fifteen feet across the cavern and introducing itself to my ribcage. Fifty-two points of bludgeoning damage. Four of poison. I deflected twelve of the bludgeoning — which was technically correct, technically the right call, and felt like telling someone you remembered to close the window before the storm blew the roof off.

Grygum said something about minimaxing. He wasn't wrong either.

I stood at the ceiling, unable to pursue Ilvara, and the fungus hit me again, and I counted the turns remaining on the frightened condition the way you count waves when the sea is rough and there's nothing to do but let them pass.

Around me the insect plague was eating the T'sarran faction alive. The fungal minions were dying — thirteen hit points, Kostadis confirmed, as if that was supposed to make me feel better about being unable to reach the target who mattered. The minions fell. The room churned. Thorin was somewhere muttering about the map. Daz was refusing to be extracted by people who were theoretically there to save him. Grygum was holding everything together the way Grygum always holds everything together.

And I was waiting.

"Yeah," I said, to no one in particular. "How do I not be frightened?"

"You wait till the end of your turn," Kostadis said. "Or lesser restoration."

End of my turn. Fine. I could wait. I had closed on her once, burned her legendary action, delivered the two hardest strikes of the fight, dropped her forty feet onto stone. The fear she had planted in me was real and it was operating exactly as designed and I was applying it against my own interest, because that was what the condition required, and I was not going to pretend otherwise.

I watched the rest of the fight from a distance, counting.
