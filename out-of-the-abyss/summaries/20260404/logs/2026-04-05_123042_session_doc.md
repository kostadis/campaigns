## Section — Daz — The Drow Reinforcements Arrive

The bolt found me before I finished the thought.

I had been watching the shadows at the tunnel mouth with a professional's eye — three shapes, crossbows already leveled, the particular stillness of people who believe they have not yet been seen. They were too confident. Too settled into their positions. I thought: *they are not good shots.* I thought: *they will miss.*

They did not miss.

The first bolt struck, and I had Shield up before the sting fully registered — the barrier snapping into existence with the reflexive economy of long practice. Bravado, I have found, is best delivered before the evidence contradicts it, not after. I adjusted my assessment. They were, in fact, reasonable shots.

Three House T'sarran warriors, stepping from the darkness like they had rehearsed it. The mage behind them was already whispering something with the smug energy of someone who had been told this would be simple.

Then Kaelira grabbed me.

She came from the northeast with the blunt purposefulness of a woman who had been hired to deliver a package and found the package arguing with her. "Come with me if you want to live, you idiot." I had half a second to catalogue what I knew about her: elite warrior, Vandree-affiliated by the look of her, and exhausted in the precise way of someone professionally competent surrounded by amateurs. She steered me through the Insect Plague's churning edge — Ilvara's own spell, Ilvara's biting little ironies — putting herself between me and the worst of it.

I did not say thank you. She did not look like she wanted me to.

"If you don't want to live, I'll still take you," she added, as though this were a clarification rather than a threat, "but it would be much easier if you'd just come."

The T'sarran contingent, meanwhile, were having a productive conversation at the tunnel's entrance. Their advance had stalled against the insect bloom — a wall of teeth and wing-membrane, sealing the approach as efficiently as any fortification. From where I stood, half-extracted and entirely unwilling, I could hear them.

*"I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill."*

A pause. The mage said nothing useful.

*"And you don't have a Dispel Magic."*

*"No, I don't have a Disp—"*

*"So I have to go through this. I don't know what else to tell you."*

I felt, not for the first time, the particular Menzoberranzan reflex: identify the leverage, count the players, calculate who doesn't know they've already lost. The T'sarran mage had come to this ambush without Dispel Magic. The elite warrior beside him knew it. I could hear the debt accruing between them in real time, and neither of them was the one doing the math. The first rule of the House: *know who holds the debt before you decide whether to collect it.* House T'sarran, it appeared, had sent people who could not perform basic arithmetic.

I almost felt sorry for them.

*Almost.*

Then Asha Vandree's voice cut through the swarm-noise, sharp with the particular outrage of the genuinely devout: *"Who are you? You must be traitors! Traitors to Lolth!"*

She had spotted Kaelira's team. Of course she had. Asha had been managing her theological suspicions since we arrived, and now there were unfamiliar drow pulling me through the battlefield and she had drawn the obvious, incorrect conclusion. The zealot in her went straight to accusation.

The deception runs or it doesn't. There is no middle state.

"No," I said, and my voice was entirely calm, because I had decided it would be. "These are friends of Lolth, and they're here to help."

No announcement. No explanation. I adapted and rolled the dice, and I watched Asha's certainty waver and resettle around the new information, the way water finds a new level. The con, I have found, rarely needs to be elegant. It needs to be delivered with the confidence of someone who has never considered that they might be wrong.

It held.

Then Nym Duskryn arrived, and told me to stay out of combat.

She was impeccably drow about it — the aristocratic register, the light contempt distributed equally across everyone present, the dry measurement of every person in the room as an obstacle or an asset. She turned to me with the expression of someone addressing a problem that had previously been described to her as minor.

*"Can you please keep your head down and out of combat? We only get paid if you live."*

I looked at her. I looked at the battlefield — at Zalthir somewhere overhead, at Grygum moving with careful deliberateness, at Thorin, who I could hear even through the chaos, perpetually arriving one step after whatever needed hitting. At the Ember Vanguard, doing what we do, in the middle of everything, without me.

"I am not gonna leave," I said. "Everyone here is fighting for me without me also participating. I don't give a damn if you get paid. The best way for you to get paid, then, is to continue the fight and prevent all of us from dying."

The thought that I was, technically, a contracted package to be delivered did not inspire in me any particular desire to cooperate with the delivery. Being told that my survival had been monetized only confirmed what I already knew: I had a role here that was not *passenger.*

Nym held my gaze for a moment with the flat professional patience of someone recalculating. Then she turned to Kaelira.

*"Can I kill your brother?"*

Kaelira considered this with the detachment of someone resolving a minor administrative question. *"He was always the dumber of the ones,"* she said, *"and if I understood correctly, he's covered in fungus?"*

*"Yes."*

*"Very well."*

I had known, intellectually, that family loyalty among the drow was transactional. It is in every text, in every political primer, in the curriculum of Menzoberranzan from the first year of House education onward. Knowing it and watching Kaelira sign off on her own brother's death with the efficiency of someone approving a supply requisition are, I have found, rather different experiences. Not better or worse. Just differently specific.

The darkness in the tunnel was deep and deliberate — a Silence, a Darkness, the T'sarran mage making himself difficult to locate. A lightning bolt crackled out of it and found nothing, which I took as encouraging, and then I stopped taking things as encouraging and threw a Fireball into the dark instead.

The explosion lit the tunnel in orange. *They'll get to see for just a short period of time*, I had thought, *while they're getting flamed up.* The blast answered the blind lightning bolt with something considerably less blind. I was already at the edge of what I had left to spend. I spent it anyway. The mage behind me was barely standing by the time the light died.

Ilvara, meanwhile, had been muttering — something about the bride, about Zuggtmoy, about the chaos that was coming. I had heard it through the noise of insect-wings and combat boots and Asha praying. She dropped her concentration on earlier workings, threw up fungal growth to slow Jorlan's advance, and then called a column of divine fire down directly onto Daz and Nym.

The scorching was not subtle.

*Fuck that lady,* I thought, with the flatness of someone who has arrived at a conclusion and sees no reason to elaborate on it. *Let's kill her.*

Zalthir handled it from there, in his particular way — clinical, geometrically precise, working around Ilvara's fear-effect with the literal-mindedness of someone who had read the restrictions carefully and found a loophole. The T'sarran mage went down in a single strike. The elite warrior, outnumbered and outmanoeuvred, offered the cold parting promise that the matron mother of House T'sarran would hear about this, that we should enjoy our victory for the moment, that we would meet again.

*"We will meet again. Enjoy your victory for the moment."*

I watched them go. The retreat had the hollow dignity of people who had arrived expecting an easy kill and were leaving with the evidence of their own dysfunction. I have faced more frightening things in the Underdark than a T'sarran mage who forgot to prepare Dispel Magic.

The cavern settled. Asha Vandree dropped to her knees somewhere in the spore-haze, praying with the full-throated beatitude of someone for whom the world had just confirmed everything she believed. The insect swarm thinned. The darkness at the tunnel mouth was just darkness again.

I had refused extraction. I had refused to hide. I had thrown everything I had into a tunnel full of enemies who wanted me as a package, and I was still standing — barely, and not without cost, but standing. The Ember Vanguard had not done it for me; we had done it together, which is a different thing entirely, and one I have come to understand the value of more than I expected.

If Milo Goodbarrel had a word for the specific satisfaction of refusing to be sidelined and surviving it anyway, I imagine he called it something optimistic. He probably would have written a chapter about it.

Annoyingly, he would not have been wrong.

The spores drifted. Asha prayed. I took a breath and assessed the remaining situation, because that is what you do next.

Lots of circles. Lots of things going on. Fireballs, lightning bolts, darkness.

I had found, despite everything, that I was enjoying it.
