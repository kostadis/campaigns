## Section — Thorin — Ilvara's Desperate Stand

The map was the problem. The map was always the problem.

I'd been running since the fight started — across elevated walkways, down through difficult terrain, around the edges of that churning insect swarm Ilvara had planted at the entrance like she knew exactly how to make my life difficult. Which she probably did. She'd had months to think about killing us.

Zalthir had opened the fight by doing something I can only describe as applied fury: grabbed her mid-air with those tentacle-arm things, hit her twice with everything he had, and dropped her forty feet onto the stone below. The kind of opening that should end a fight. Instead she stood up, flooded his mind with something cold and terrible, and the most dangerous person in our party spent the next stretch of time standing completely still, trying to remember what courage felt like. The heart fungus swung its massive limbs at the ceiling. Jorlan — Ilvara's former lieutenant, her sometime-lover, and apparently a man who'd been waiting his whole life for the moment she was weak enough to murder personally — broke from the fight and charged toward her screaming her name.

"Dovara!"

And I was standing in the middle of it.

"I'm just sitting here watching trade blows like a tennis match."

That's what I said. It was true. It was also not helping anyone, so I picked a direction and moved.

The heart fungus was the closest thing I could actually reach. That was fine. I'd been told it was doubly vulnerable to radiant light — the kind of intelligence that makes a sunblade feel like the correct tool and not just the one I happen to carry. Dawnbringer has opinions about being useful. She practically hummed when I closed the distance.

I carved through it in a single pass. One round of Dawnbringer's light cutting into something that hated light, and the massive, elaborate thing that had been dominating the center of the chamber just... folded. Collapsed inward. The vast fungal shape wilted under the radiance and fell silent, leaving the room suddenly, starkly emptier.

"I do what I can."

Somewhere behind me, I heard the GM make a sound like a man watching his careful work disappear. I had a feeling that fungus had taken a lot of preparation. I didn't ask. Some condolences are better left unsaid.

What mattered was that the terrain shifted, and Ilvara was still standing.

She'd survived the fall. She'd survived Zalthir's assault. She was bleeding and cornered and surrounded by enemies on three sides — her own people had apparently decided this was the moment to try to take her out, because some other faction's drow had materialized at the cavern's edge, only to get stuck behind her own insect swarm. Their own ambush, blocked by their own target's spell. I heard them arguing in the tunnel.

"I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill."

"I don't have a Dispel—"

"And you don't have a Dispel Magic."

"No, I don't have a Dispel—"

"So I have to go through this. I don't know what else to tell you."

Grygum, somewhere to my left, quiet and dry: "It's tough being a minion."

Jorlan was still running. He'd disengaged from us — from the party he'd been actively trying to kill a minute ago — because the moment Ilvara started looking vulnerable, nothing else mattered. I had the ability to stop him. One strike as he passed, one attack of opportunity, and he'd be down or slowed or at least interrupted.

I rolled for it. Eight. Eight meant restraint. Eight meant I deferred.

"Alright, so 8 says I do the sensible thing. Which I guess is let them go and let the enemies... let the girls fight."

I let him pass.

It made me sad. I like my attacks of opportunity. But I was running the numbers — what happens if Jorlan kills her and we don't? Does that change anything? Does he get first dibs on whatever she's carrying? They'd been lovers once, or something like it. "Lover's booty," I said, and then clarified immediately that I meant the goods, because Grygum was right there and he has a threshold.

The sensible thing. I'd done it. I was not thrilled about it.

Then I looked at the bridge.

Ilvara had the high ground. She'd had it most of the fight, which meant she had angles we didn't and room to maneuver we couldn't match. The walkway she was using was the reason I'd spent half this fight running and not swinging.

"Is the bridge undead?"

Nobody answered. I swung anyway.

The bridge cracked, listed, and collapsed. Not elegant. Not subtle. Just stone and weight and the right application of force to the right point. Ilvara and Jorlan both leapt clear — she wasn't going to be finished by falling twice — and landed on the cavern floor below. Shaken. Ground level.

No more high ground. The fight was flat now, and flat was where I lived.

What came next happened fast. Grygum leveled his guiding bolt, and when it hit, it hit clean — the radiant streak finding Ilvara at the moment she'd run out of margin. She detonated. Not a death so much as an unraveling: a cloud of poisonous spores exploding outward from where she'd been standing, filling the air with something that smelled wrong.

Grygum, immediately: "Not ideal. How big is the cloud?"

That's the man. That's exactly the man. He kills our longest-standing nemesis, the drow who has been hunting us since before I could name half the party, and his first concern is the radius of the spore cloud.

The chamber went quiet the way chambers go quiet after a thing that was making noise stops. Ilvara was gone. The insect swarm was dissipating. Jorlan was somewhere on the floor, alive and probably furious, trying to work out whether to be relieved or devastated.

Asha Vandree — the Vandree faction priestess who'd been fighting alongside us in a grudging provisional way — dropped to her knees and started praying. Then she looked up at the group of drow who'd arrived with Daz's extraction team and immediately accused them of being traitors.

"Who are you? You must be traitors! Traitors to Lolth!"

Daz smoothed it over without missing a beat, something about friends of Lolth, the lie landing clean in the chaos. His cover held. I don't know how he does it. I couldn't do it. I can barely manage a straight face when I make a bad pun on purpose.

The cavern floor was littered with what used to be a fight. The heart fungus was gone. The bridge was rubble. The insect swarm was fading. Somewhere in the tunnel, the T'sarran drow were retreating.

"We will meet again. Enjoy your victory for the moment."

Sure. Probably. But right now the moment was ours, and the moment was enough.
