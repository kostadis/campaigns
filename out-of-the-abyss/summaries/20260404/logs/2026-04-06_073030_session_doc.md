## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The second attempt. That was the thought I was entertaining as I watched Ilvara hold her position across the cavern — the same thought, the same calculation, the same reluctant conclusion I'd reached before, and that she'd answered by simply ceasing to be where I'd aimed.

Legendary action. Not a lair action. I'd said lair action. It didn't matter; the point was she had moved, and now she hadn't, and I had to decide whether to try again.

I run through it the way I run through everything: what do I have, what does she have, what happens if I commit. The answer is not encouraging. I cannot put distance between us and her and win anything. I have fists and I have shadow and I have reach, and every single one of those things requires me to be near her. There is no other version of this plan. The only question is whether the plan is worth running twice.

The logic reshapes itself, and I find the thing hiding inside what looked like a losing proposition. She can move when I hit her. Once. If she moves, I move after her. Every legendary reaction she burns on escaping me is a legendary reaction she isn't burning on anything else. I'm not spending myself — I'm spending her. The cost of making Ilvara teleport is one attack. The cost of letting Ilvara stand still and do whatever she intended to do is something I'd rather not find out.

*So*, I think. *We run, run, run, and then slaps.*

Pretty much.

---

I come in across the ceiling. Shadow-steps eat the distance; the Eldritch Claw tattoo along my forearm flexes and extends, dark tendrils reaching thirty, forty feet out ahead of me before I've even fully committed to the crossing. She sees me coming. She doesn't move.

Maybe she thinks I'll miss again.

The tentacle strike takes her in the chest — sixteen points of force impact, and then the grip closes, coiling around her torso and arms, and she is no longer standing on the floor. She is forty feet up and getting further, because I have decided that if she has a legendary action and I have her in my hands, the correct place for this fight to happen is somewhere that punishes her for using it.

For a moment she just hangs there, suspended and held, and the cavern floor is a long way down.

I look at what I'm holding. The corrupted priestess. The source of the pursuit, the hunt, the months of running through lightless tunnels. She's right here in my grip.

*Why not*, I think. And I punch her.

Critical. I feel it the way you feel a perfect strike — everything aligned, no wasted motion, the force delivering through exactly the right angle. Sixteen damage. I don't stop. The flurry continues before she can respond, before she can think, and the second punch lands harder than the first.

Another critical hit. Ten, plus the six. Eighteen more.

And then I let go.

She falls.

Forty feet is a long time to think about what just happened to you. I don't watch the landing — I'm already calculating where I need to be — but I hear it, and the table erupts around me, and I file the sound away as confirmation.

---

She reaches for me from the floor.

That's what it feels like — not a spell, not a word, just something that travels the distance between us and finds the part of the mind that knows what fear is. Cold, insistent, looking for a crack. I've been in worse than this. I've been in — 

There was a class for this. I'm almost certain there was. Something about magical terror, psychic intrusion, the spirit staying grounded when the body wants to run. Taught by — Brother Quellin, possibly, or the one with the grey robes, the one who always seemed vaguely disappointed when he entered the room to find students already seated. I don't remember his name. I remember the afternoon light through the high windows. I remember thinking I could be practicing forms instead.

I do not remember what he said to do.

Whatever he said: it didn't hold. Something in me turns — not the body, the *will* — and suddenly Ilvara is a thing I cannot be near. The instruction goes all the way down, past choice, past discipline. I am frightened, and she is forty feet away bleeding on the stone, and I cannot cross that forty feet.

I sit with this.

*I am supposed to be trying to not be near her.* Right. That is the spirit of it. I am not near her. I should not be trying to become near her. These are the conditions as they exist. I apply the rule against myself the way the training always intended — not grudgingly, not looking for the loophole, just the rule and what it means and what I do next.

What I do next turns out to be absorbing what the heart fungus has been waiting to deliver.

It has fifteen feet of reach. I discover this the same moment I discover that I cannot easily leave where I am, suspended at the ceiling, pinned by fear to the wrong side of the battlefield. The strike lands with a weight that reorganizes my understanding of what weight means — bludgeoning first, then the slow seep of something toxic working through the deflection I managed, the part that got through settling into my bones with a patience that's almost insulting. I deflect what I can. I absorb the rest.

*Thanks*, I think, with complete sincerity, the way you thank a lesson for arriving on schedule.

Below me the fight continues. The fungal minions are falling — too few hit points, never a threat, and they go down one by one while I wait at the ceiling for the fear to pass. I watch the others work. I wait. The cold grip in my mind loosens in beats, the way cold always does when you stop fighting it and just endure.

Ilvara is down there. Bleeding. And I am still standing.

That's enough, for now. I'll get back to her when the fear decides it's finished with me.
