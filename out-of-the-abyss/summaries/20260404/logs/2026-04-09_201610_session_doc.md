## Section — Daz — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The insect swarm hadn't thinned. Ilvara's plague had settled into the cavern like weather — a constant, maddening pressure that turned every breath into an argument and every movement into a negotiation with pain. I had stopped noticing the individual bites somewhere around the third minute. That's either discipline or dissociation. I've never been sure which.

Grygum was moving toward Asha Vandree, and somewhere in the middle distance Glabbagool had joined the fight in whatever way a sentient gelatinous cube joins a fight. I noted both facts and filed them. The battlefield had become a series of competing problems, and the only sensible approach was triage.

Ilvara was still the problem. Ilvara was always the problem. Grygum apparently agreed — I watched him draw on whatever vein of Bahamut's power he had left and send a bolt of radiant light burning toward her. It missed. Clean miss, nothing ambiguous about it. He said "oh, well, that's a shame," with the equanimity of a man who has already accepted that this fight was going to be long. I respected that. I didn't share it.

Then Zalthir said, with what I can only describe as appreciation, "Five Dark Elves, it looks like? Five Drow? That's awesome."

Grygum, quietly: "This is turning into the twelve days of Christmas."

He was not wrong. Five House T'sarran warriors emerging from the shadows on the far end of the cavern, stepping out of positions they'd clearly been holding for some time, blinking into the insect plague they had absolutely not accounted for. I could read the tactical miscalculation from across the room. They'd planned this as an interception — wait for the fighting to do the work, walk in when things were settled, collect whatever prize they'd come for. Instead they walked into Ilvara's own insect plague, which had no opinions about faction allegiances.

*I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill.* One of the warriors, to their mage. I didn't hear the response, but I could reconstruct it from the silence.

I had approximately two seconds to process the new arrivals before three of them raised hand crossbows in my direction.

Here is the thing about being shot at through a swarm of insects: you assume the swarm will help you. You assume that any bolt passing through a cloud of biting, disorienting creatures will go somewhere usefully wrong. This is a reasonable assumption. It is not a guarantee.

The first bolt found me.

I didn't think. The shield went up on reflex — the kind of magic that lives in the fingers rather than the mind, faster than intention. The bolt deflected into nothing. But the fact of it landing, even partially, even blunted — I was not pleased. I told myself I was not pleased in a measured, analytical way.

I was lying to myself, but it was a useful lie.

The T'sarran warriors stood there, irritated, increasingly covered in biting insects, having just wasted their best opportunity on a man who was already surrounded by four people actively trying to keep him alive. Watching them recalibrate was, I will admit, briefly satisfying. They had that look — that particular look of someone who thought they had all the cards and has just been asked to show their hand.

I felt, genuinely, a little embarrassed for them.

Then Kaelira appeared.

She came out of the insect swarm like someone who had decided the insects were simply not her problem. Dark, close-cropped hair, the expression of a professional who has been dealing with difficult cargo for too long and is now at the end of a very specific kind of patience.

"Come with me if you want to live, you idiot."

I knew who she was. Not personally — I hadn't met her before this moment — but I knew the *type*, and I knew the voice behind the contract, and I knew that whoever was paying for my extraction had sent someone competent enough to walk through Ilvara's plague without breaking stride.

She looked at me. I did not move.

"If you don't want to live, I'll still take you," she said, "but could you — it would be much easier if you'd just come."

There was a moment — brief, almost invisible — where I considered the offer on its merits. A clean exit. A professional escort through whatever chaos remained between here and somewhere not on fire. Entirely rational. The city would have approved of it. *The first rule of the House: know who holds the debt before you decide whether to collect it.* Someone was paying to keep me alive, and I didn't yet know who, which meant I didn't know what I would owe them when this was finished.

But that wasn't, ultimately, the calculation that mattered.

I looked at the T'sarran warriors still picking insects out of their hair. I looked at the space where Ilvara had been, and where Grygum and Zalthir and Thorin still were. I thought about Asha Vandree, volatile and fervent and currently armed, three meters away, who I had told five minutes ago that the newly arrived drow were *friends of Lolf and here to help* — a sentence I had produced with no preparation and complete confidence and which had, so far, held.

The con doesn't stop because the scenery changes. That's the first thing they teach you, and it's the only lesson that stays true everywhere.

I decided to follow Kaelira.

Not because she asked. Not because it was safe. Because the board had shifted, and following her was the move that kept the most pieces in play — mine included.
