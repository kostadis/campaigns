## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The plan was the same as last time. That was the problem.

She had teleported away from me before — not a lair action, I was corrected on that, legendary action — and I'd been left standing in empty air with nothing to show for it. So the question I turned over while the fight opened up around me was a simple one: *do you run the same play twice, or do you admit it doesn't work and find another one?*

A monk with no ranged options doesn't have a third answer. I run, I close, I hit. That's what I do. The monastery had other ideas about what a monk could be — I remember a week of instruction on something, the quiet monk, the one who smelled of incense, Brother Quellin maybe, who apparently had a great deal to say about patience and positioning and working around an enemy's advantages rather than through them. I was elsewhere in my head that week. I remember the quality of the light through the window. I remember wanting to be training.

She was forty feet away and dangerous and she could teleport the moment I touched her. And the only plan I had was: touch her anyway, make her spend the action, eat the cost. Make her burn through her tricks while I ran her down again. A losing proposition, except that a losing proposition was everything I had.

There was a logic to it, if I turned it the right way. Every time she moved to avoid me, she wasn't doing something worse. Every time she burned that legendary reaction on not being grabbed, she wasn't using it to kill someone else. I was the pressure. That was the role.

*Run, run, run, and then slap.* Yes. That's the summary.

I crossed the cavern on the dark tendrils of the eldritch tattoo, the reach of it extending outward like a second set of hands, and I hit her before she had time to react. Sixteen damage. She was in my grip before she could decide what to do about it — the grapple locked, and I had her, the two of us suspended above the cavern floor, the stone somewhere far below her dangling feet. The most dangerous creature in the room, held.

Then I punched her.

I don't have a more sophisticated account of it than that. She was grappled. She was already hurt. The logic was self-evident: *now that I'm holding her, I will punch her again, because why not?*

The first hit was a critical. The second hit was a critical. I don't know what to tell you about that except that it happened and it felt right in the way that the body sometimes knows before the mind does — the angle was correct, the timing was correct, everything landed exactly where it was supposed to land. Sixteen damage, then eighteen more. The kind of strikes where you know, halfway through, that they're going in.

Then I let go.

She fell forty feet.

That's not metaphorical. I opened my hands and she dropped like something that had been waiting to fall, and somewhere below me the stone received her, and the table made noise, and I said nothing because there was nothing to say. The math spoke for itself.

She hit the ground and reached for something I hadn't counted on.

There was a moment, after she landed, when I thought it was over — not the whole fight, but *that* part, the part where she was the problem I needed to solve. And then she looked up from the cavern floor, broken and bleeding, and she pulled from somewhere deep in whatever she had become, and something cold and enormous pressed into the front of my mind.

It wasn't pain. It wasn't even quite fear in any form I recognized. It was more like the certain knowledge that I could not go near her. Full stop. The knowledge arrived fully formed, without argument, the way cold arrives.

I stood at altitude and understood that I had just successfully grappled and dropped a severely wounded enemy from forty feet, and I was now absolutely unable to follow up on any of it.

Elegant, actually. I can appreciate an elegant counter even when I'm the one it's been applied to.

*I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right? Alright, fine.*

There was a class on this. There was definitely a class on this. Brother Tharusk, possibly — or the other one, the tall one with the ink-stained hands, I could never keep them sorted — but someone stood at the front of a room and talked about the discipline of fear, about how to hold the mind together when something outside is pressing against it, and I did not listen, and I am currently experiencing the consequences of that in real time.

The fungus found me while I was pinned in place. It had reach I hadn't accounted for — fifteen feet, apparently, which is quite a lot — and it struck from below with everything it had. I deflected what I could. Twelve points turned away by the sheer trained reflex of the body reading incoming force. The rest came through, and it came through hard, bludgeoning and poison both, the kind of combined impact that reorganizes your sense of what sustainable damage means. I absorbed it. I stayed at the ceiling. I did not move toward Ilvara.

I watched the fight continue below me. The fungal minions died around me in small irrelevant clusters — thirteen points each, not worth discussing. And I stood frightened and battered and fundamentally useless, waiting for the terror to release its grip on whatever part of my mind it had closed around.

I had made her bleed. She had made me pay for it by removing me from the war entirely.

The math still favored the party. I told myself that while I waited.
