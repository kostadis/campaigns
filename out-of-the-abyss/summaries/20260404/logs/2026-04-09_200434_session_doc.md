## Section — Daz — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The battlefield had, at this point, become a cataloguing problem.

I have always found comfort in categories. It is one of the cleaner gifts Menzoberranzan offers its children — the ability to inventory a situation before it inventories you. Know who holds the debt before you decide whether to collect it. The city would call sentiment a vulnerability. The city was, on balance, correct about most things, even the things worth abandoning.

So. Categories.

Ilvara: wounded, dangerous, personal. The spore servants shambling in her wake. The Big Heart Fungus, now ash, because Thorin had done what Thorin does and simply removed a problem from the universe with extreme prejudice — the understated "I do what I can" still audible under the insect roar. Glabbagool pressing forward, translucent and terrible and, somehow, ours. Grygum calling his Guiding Bolt into the chaos, trying to land something radiant on a woman who frankly deserved every kind of divine judgement available. Jorlan — I had watched him break away, screaming *Dovara,* sprinting toward a woman who had probably ruined him in more ways than one. Thorin had let him pass. Die roll, apparently. I understand the impulse better than I would admit.

Then: Zalthir, still working through whatever Ilvara's magic had done to him, that rattled quality he would never name aloud.

And now, emerging from the dark where they had been crouching in the hopes of a clean ambush that Ilvara's own Insect Plague had thoroughly spoiled for them — five Drow warriors of House T'sarran.

"This is turning into the 12 Days of Christmas," Grygum said.

I could hear one of the T'sarran soldiers, voice clipped with professional frustration, turning on his colleague: "I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill."

A pause that required no words to understand.

"And you don't have a Dispel Magic."

"No, I don't have a Dispel—"

"So I have to go through this. I don't know what else to tell you."

*I feel embarrassed for him.* Not for the indignity of the conversation, exactly, but for the operational failure of it — they had positioned themselves for an ambush, waited in the dark, and now stood fully inside a plague of biting insects because their mage had failed to account for a sixth-level spell they couldn't counter. The matron mother of House T'sarran was presumably not going to hear about this particular planning detail. She would hear about a bold engagement. She would hear about the Ember Vanguard, names attached, and a vow of future reckon.

The crossbow bolts they fired at me didn't land. The insects were good for something. I have observed that I survive more often than the tactical analysis suggests I should, and I have stopped examining this too closely.

What I did not stop examining was Asha Vandree, because Asha was watching the new arrivals with the exact expression of someone who was about to say something that would complicate everything.

"Who are you? You must be traitors! Traitors to Lolth!"

There was no pause between her accusation and my response. This is the advantage of running a deception continuously rather than in isolated bursts — the machinery is already warm.

"No," I said. "These are friends of Lolf, and they're here to help."

*Lolf.* I had almost said Lolth. I said Lolf. My mouth had made a small tactical compromise with plausibility and moved on. I did not explain it. Explanations are for people who need the other person to believe them. The con requires only that the other person hesitate, and Asha hesitated.

The first rule, and it was the city's rule, and I hated that it was still mine: information is a resource. The fiction I had been maintaining since the first moment I met Asha Vandree — the loyal servant of the Spider Queen, the Lolth-convert who had found his path back to the fold — was holding. Barely. The way a good hand of cards holds when the stakes keep climbing and you can't quite see the other player's tell.

Milo Goodbarrel would have called this the long play. He would have been smiling in his irritating halfling way and making it sound like an honorable strategy instead of what it was: improvisation on the edge of credibility, with several different factions pointing weapons at the same wizard.

I will confess — privately, to no one, in the way I confess all things — that being covered in biting insects while simultaneously managing an active Lolth impersonation, a T'sarran assassination attempt, and whatever Jorlan was doing at the far end of the cavern was not among the anticipated variables when we began this engagement.

And then Kaelira came through the swarm.

She had that look of someone who had been paid to do a job and found the job actively resisting her. Tired competence. She moved like she had done extractions before — probably under worse conditions, probably for less.

"Come with me if you want to live, you idiot."

I considered the word *idiot* briefly and let it go. She wasn't wrong on the technical merits.

"If you don't want to live," she added, with the tone of a woman extending a professional courtesy, "I'll still take you, but could you… it would be much easier if you'd just come."

I went.

Not because I was afraid, though the insects were making a strong argument for a change of scenery. Not because Kaelira's conditional offer of rescue was particularly persuasive. But because the calculus, run fast in the way I've learned to run it, said: the fight had enough combatants. Ilvara was already wounded. The T'sarran faction had been exposed as poorly resourced and would shortly be retreating to compose a dignified threat. Grygum and Zalthir and Thorin — whatever his map complaints — were holding.

And there were things I needed to understand about who had sent Kaelira, and what they expected to collect.

Someone was paying for my survival. Someone had arranged this. That was a debt I didn't yet have a number for, and I preferred to count debts before they counted me.

I followed her.
