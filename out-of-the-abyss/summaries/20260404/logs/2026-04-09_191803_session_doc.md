## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The plan was simple. It had failed once before, which was either a reason to abandon it or a reason to refine it. I had spent some time deciding which.

"Last time, I tried to grab her," I said, surveying the cavern from whatever height I'd claimed, "and then she used a lair action to teleport away, right?"

It was a legendary action, apparently. Not a lair action. The distinction mattered in the way that precise language always matters when you are planning to do a thing and would prefer not to have it fail in the same way twice. I filed the correction. Legendary. Once. Burnable.

"Second try is the charm," I said, "or give up on that whole plan? I don't know. I mean, I can only melee anyway, so I guess I'll just... try that same thing again."

There was, I understood, a class for this at the monastery. Probably. Someone had explained legendary actions to us — the mechanics of powerful enemies, how their reactions worked, how you could turn an attack into a resource drain rather than simply an attack. I think it was Brother Verath. Or possibly the one after him — the short one who always had chalk dust on his robe. I don't remember. I was not paying attention. I remember the quality of the light in that hall, late afternoon, the kind that makes you want to be outside. I do not remember what he said.

What I had instead was the logic of it, assembled from first principles in real time. She could teleport once. If she teleported on the first strike, she burned it. She moved; I moved. She had no second teleport. The problem, as I understood it, was that I'd only get one attack if she ran, unless I could close the distance again fast enough — and a monk can run, run, run, and then slap. That was the plan. That was approximately all of it.

So I ran.

The eldritch tendrils snapped out from my wrist before she could register the distance I'd covered, wrapping around her with the kind of grip that doesn't negotiate. Ilvara — the high priestess, the woman who had caged us, the reason we were still in this cavern at all — found herself seized. Not by Lolth. Not by Zuggtmoy. By me.

Sixteen damage worth of *hello*. And she was grappled.

I had done this math already: she was now forty feet above the cavern floor. I had carried her here. She had nowhere to stand, no ground under her feet, nothing to push off from. She had me.

*Now that I'm holding her,* I thought, with the pure unadorned logic of someone who has just noticed an obvious opportunity, *I will punch her again. Because why not.*

The first critical hit landed and the second followed it before she could adapt to the first. Back to back. I felt both of them — felt them the way you feel a thing that has gone exactly right, clean and total, the full weight of every strike landing without obstruction or deflection. Each blow doubled by some alignment of force and intent that the monastery called discipline and I privately called getting lucky in the best possible direction.

I heard something at the table — laughter, Thorin saying something about Nazis, Grygum responding — but it was distant, the way all sound becomes distant when you are very focused on a thing that is working. I was winning. Forty feet in the air, Ilvara suspended in my grip, nowhere to run and nothing to brace against. The damage was mounting. The legendary action hadn't fired.

Then I let go.

She fell.

Forty feet of stone cavern, straight down. Deliberate. Unhurried. The cleanest possible punctuation. It cost me nothing but a loosening of my fingers, and it spent her legendary action before she could use it for anything worse than survival.

Or so I thought.

She hit the cavern floor and something entered my mind. Cold. Vast. Wrong. Not pain — something that doesn't have a clean word, that touches not the body but the part behind the eyes where you keep the understanding of what you are willing to do. She was broken and bleeding and forty feet below me, and she reached up from the floor and put *fear* inside me.

I stood in the air above the battle and could not approach her.

Not *would not*. Could not. The distance between us had become the one distance I was not capable of crossing. She was directly below me. She was the exact target of every decision I had made in this fight. And I stood there unable to close it.

"Well, that didn't work," I said.

There was, I was now quite certain, a class for this. Resistance to magical fear. I think it was Brother Quellin — the tall one, the one who always stood very still when he spoke, which I had found either impressive or tiresome depending on my mood. He probably had a great deal to say about it. I do not remember any of it. I was, I recalled with fresh and useless clarity, somewhere else in my mind during that class. I had found it theoretical.

I was supposed to be trying not to be near her. That was what the fear was doing — imposing distance, making approach impossible, using my own body's survival instinct as the barrier. The spirit of it was clear: I was not going to close that gap. Not right now.

*Right,* I thought. *Alright. Fine.*

The heart fungus, which had apparently not been consulted on whether I should get to stand here unmolested, reached fifteen feet upward and hit me hard enough to compress something important. The cavern shook. I absorbed it — barely — and held my position in the air, unable to advance on the enemy I had spent the entire opening of this fight dismantling piece by piece.

Below, the minions were collapsing — insect plague sweeping through them, numbers dropping, chaos converting itself into order with the grinding patience of a fight that is going, on balance, in the right direction. The fungal servants died. The problem they'd been obscuring remained exactly where Ilvara had placed it, which was inside my skull.

I waited. The cold weight of the fear pressed against the edges of my mind, and I held still and let it run its course. I had opened the fight, won the grapple, spent her legendary action, sent her to the floor, and delivered a sequence of critical strikes that had cost her more than anything else in this room today.

Now I could only watch and wait for the feeling to pass. Which, being fear, it eventually would.

I am supposed to be trying not to be near her.

Right. Alright. Fine.
