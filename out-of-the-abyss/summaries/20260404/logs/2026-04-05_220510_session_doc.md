## Section — Thorin — The Heart Fungus and the Drow Betrayal

The heart fungus was enormous.

I don't mean large in the way a barrel is large, or a boulder, or even a particularly ambitious mushroom. I mean it occupied the cavern the way a bad idea occupies a room — filling corners you didn't think anything could reach, pulsing with something that had no business pulsing. It had veins. It breathed. It sat at the center of Ilvara's corrupted temple like it owned the place, which, in a sense, it did.

I asked the obvious question.

"Is there anything I need to know about the fungus? Like, is it resistant to stuff?"

The answer came back clean and useful: doubly weak to radiant. Which meant Dawnbringer, already humming against my palm like she'd been waiting for exactly this moment, was the right tool for exactly this job.

There's a particular satisfaction in that. A problem. A sword. A straight line between them. The Underdark doesn't hand you those often.

I pushed down from the walkways, through terrain that had no interest in cooperating — the cavern floor was nothing but difficult going, wet and cluttered and uneven — and I made straight for the thing. Dawnbringer lit the way. She always does. We had our agreement about how much light was too much, but in this moment I wasn't inclined to negotiate, and neither was she.

The first strike went through the fungal mass like a blade through something that didn't expect to be a blade's problem. The second connected with a crack of radiant energy that I felt from my shoulder down to my boot. The thing recoiled. It shuddered — not the controlled shudder of a creature defending itself, but the involuntary shudder of something realizing it had already lost.

Then it wilted.

I lowered Dawnbringer and looked at what had been, thirty seconds ago, the elaborate center of an encounter someone had clearly spent considerable effort designing.

"I do what I can," I said.

Grygum mourned it, in his way. Zalthir called it pretty. The quiet afterward had a particular quality — the respectful silence you give something that deserved to be honored, even if the honoring was done by destroying it efficiently.

Then Jorlan screamed "Dovara!" and all of that evaporated.

He was already moving when I registered what was happening — the scarred drow warrior, who had started this fight very much against us, had taken one look at Ilvara broken and bleeding on the cavern floor after Zalthir had dropped her forty feet, and made a decision. Not a tactical decision. Not the kind that gets made after weighing options. The kind that happens below the mind, where years of rage and grief have been waiting for exactly this moment.

He was disengaging. Sprinting toward her. And my hand was on my weapon before my brain caught up.

Here is the thing about attacks of opportunity: I like them. I like them specifically and personally, in the way a craftsman likes a well-fitted tool. The ability to punish a creature for thinking it can just leave — there is something deeply satisfying about that. It's honest. You make a choice to run, you take the consequence. Clean.

I had the right. The mechanics gave it to me clearly.

"He's disengaging to go attack Ilvara," I was told. "You get to choose. Do you want to attack him or let him go?"

And I thought — but he attacked us first. That's still true. He didn't become a good person just because Ilvara looks worse than we do right now.

"Yeah, but he didn't think you guys could defeat Ilvara. Now that she's wounded, he wants to take care of her himself."

"The tides have turned," Zalthir offered, with the dry patience of someone who had been watching this particular drama unfold for what felt like three acts too many.

I stood there with the opportunity unused in my hands and tried to think through what it actually meant. The politics of it. The drow grief of it. The question of whether Jorlan killing Ilvara was better or worse for us than us killing Ilvara, and whether it mattered who got first claim on whatever she had on her.

"So what happens if he kills her and we don't? Does he get first dibs on booty or something? And by booty, I mean—"

I could feel Zalthir's patience thinning without looking at him.

"I mean, like, they used to be Lover's Booty. I mean like the goods."

There was a moment of silence that I chose to interpret as thoughtful rather than exasperated.

"You decide," I was told. "He's running away. This is a classic roll-the-dice moment."

So I rolled.

Eight says I do the sensible thing.

I looked at the die. I looked at Jorlan's retreating back. I thought about what sensible meant in a cavern full of drow politics and demon-lord corruption and a sentient sunblade currently warming my grip with obvious enthusiasm, and I made my peace with the number.

"I guess I let them go," I said. "Let the girls fight."

I watched him run.

"That makes me sad, though. Because I like my attacks of opportunity."

An elder I remembered from childhood — the one who settled every dispute by sitting down and not speaking — would have called this wisdom. He could hold a silence for three days. A rock in the wrong place is just a rock, but a rock in the right place is a wall. He'd say it once, and then he'd say nothing else, because the giants never felt the need to repeat themselves.

I'm not a giant. But I understood, in that moment, what he meant by it. Jorlan running toward Ilvara wasn't a rock I needed to be the wall for. Whatever accounting was happening between those two, it was older than my grudge, and it was going to happen whether I swung or not.

I held Dawnbringer at my side, still warm with the light that had unmade the heart fungus, and I watched the former enemies race toward their reckoning.

Two choices to withhold the strike, in the same scene. Neither of them easy.

I'm just sitting there watching them trade blows like a tennis match, and I've made my peace with it.

Mostly.
