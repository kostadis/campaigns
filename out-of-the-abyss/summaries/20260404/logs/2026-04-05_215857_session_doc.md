## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The plan had worked. Once.

Last time — the fight that wasn't quite the same fight, the one where Ilvara had slipped loose just as the grip closed — she'd burned a legendary action to teleport. Gone before the first blow landed. Zalthir had filed that away the way he filed everything: without judgment, without frustration, just as information. She has an escape. She will use it when pressured. The question is what she does with the time she bought herself.

This time, he intended to make the escape more expensive than whatever she was saving it for.

The cavern opened below him — the Big Heart Fungus still dominating the floor, Ilvara hovering somewhere above in the dark, the whole fight arranged vertically in a way that suited exactly one person in the party and none of the others. Below, he could hear Thorin somewhere, muttering about the map. Grygum was checking on someone. Daz was, presumably, about to throw fire at something that didn't strictly need fire thrown at it.

Zalthir looked up at Ilvara, corrupted and terrible, wreathed in the particular wrongness that Zuggtmoy's presence had draped over everything in this place, and thought: *she can move once. If I hit her, she'll spend it on survival. And then I hit her again.*

A monk's entire vocabulary reduced to that loop. Movement as cost. Presence as pressure. The thought arrived fully formed and clean, the way all good plans do: not clever, just true.

There was probably a class for this at the monastery. Reading a battlefield vertically. Something about aerial engagement, the physics of pursuit, accounting for an opponent's known resources. Brother Quellin, maybe — or possibly Brother Tharusk, the one with the permanent ink stains on his left hand who taught something Zalthir had categorized as *optional*. He didn't remember what they said. He remembered the quality of the afternoon light through the high windows, and wanting to be elsewhere.

He was elsewhere now. He was here, in fact, which was worse.

The Eldritch Claw tattoo pulsed against his arm as he moved, tendrils of shadow-force snapping outward across the open air, crossing the distance to Ilvara before she had time to calculate what was reaching for her. They closed. She pulled back. The grip held anyway.

Sixteen points of contact, and she was grappled — suspended in his arms forty feet above the cavern floor, two bodies sharing the same altitude, nothing below them but stone.

*Now that I'm holding her,* Zalthir thought, with what he considered to be airtight internal logic, *I will punch her again. Because why not?*

The first strike landed clean. Then something clicked — the kind of alignment that happens maybe once in a hundred repetitions, when the body is simply right and knows it before the mind catches up. Two critical blows in the same breath, Flurry of Blows operating at a frequency Zalthir didn't experience often enough to take for granted. Sixteen damage. Then ten. Then eighteen. The numbers existed somewhere; what he experienced was the percussion of impact, the particular correctness of fists meeting a target that had run out of ways to not be there.

Somewhere below, the table erupted.

He dropped her.

Forty feet. The cavern floor came up fast and Ilvara hit it hard, and Zalthir followed the fall with his eyes and thought: *that's the plan done, and she's still moving, and there's more fight left.*

There was always more fight left.

Then she looked up at him from the ground — broken, furious, still burning with something that wasn't entirely her own anger anymore — and she reached into whatever Zuggtmoy had put inside her and pushed it outward.

The fear arrived without permission. That was the worst part. Not the sensation itself, which was cold and specific and nothing like what he would have designed if fear had been assigned to him as a training exercise — but the way it simply appeared, occupying the space where his legs were supposed to be. His legs were still there. They would not carry him toward her. The distinction was precise and absolutely infuriating.

Ilvara had just burned her escape on surviving the drop. And then, with whatever she had left, she had burned his offensive strategy in return.

*Well,* he thought. *That didn't work.*

The enormous Heart Fungus, apparently deciding this was a reasonable moment to contribute, chose the monk clinging to the cavern ceiling. Its reach extended further than anything with that much fungal mass had any architectural right to achieve — Zalthir registered this geometrically, noted the arm length, noted that fifteen feet of reach was enough to bridge the distance — and then the blow connected.

He deflected twelve points.

Out of fifty-two.

The poison came after, threading through whatever the deflection had left open. Below him, Grygum said something with the inflection of a man who was both deeply sympathetic and quietly delighted, and Zalthir filed this away for later and focused on the problem of being frightened, poisoned, and approximately forty feet off the ground with no plan.

*There was a class for this,* he thought. *Resistance to magical compulsion. Brother — someone — the tall one, always smelled of incense, ran it every second season. I think it was optional.*

He had taken it as optional.

He was currently paying for that at forty feet.

The condition was clear in its terms: he was supposed to be trying not to be near her. The spirit of the thing, not just the letter. He applied this against himself without being asked, because that was how the discipline worked — self-adjudication, the monastery's only reliable export. *I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right? Alright, fine.*

So he held his position at the ceiling, absorbed what the Heart Fungus had left for him, and waited for the cold clutch of it to loosen — beat by beat, breath by breath — until his legs were his own again and he could think about what to do next.
