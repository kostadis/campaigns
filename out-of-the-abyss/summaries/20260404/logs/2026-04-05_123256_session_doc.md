## Section — Grygum — The Fall of Ilvara

The insect swarm still shrieked at the mouth of the tunnel. The bridge was down. Ilvara and Jorlan had hit the stone floor hard, and somewhere in the roiling chaos of it all, I was standing at the edge of the fight doing what I always do first: counting.

Daz had eighteen hit points left out of forty-four. He'd told me so himself, with the flat honesty of a man calculating acceptable risk rather than asking for help. Thorin was still standing. Zalthir had taken hits but was moving. Nobody was dying *right now*, which meant the question was what would serve them better — a healing word, or ending this.

"Does anybody need any serious healing?"

Daz said he thought he could take one more round. I filed that under his name. Eighteen of forty-four. One more round.

I believed him, which might say something about either his constitution or my judgment.

The thing is, I was amused. I knew it was a bad moment to be amused, but there it was — Jorlan, who had been fighting us, had apparently decided that Ilvara's vulnerability was more important than our combat. He'd broken off. Screamed something. Sprinted toward her. Thorin had watched him go, muttering about booty — and yes, he clarified that — and the whole cave had briefly become a drama I recognized from exactly nowhere in my training but somehow felt deeply familiar. Wounded priestess, devoted ex-commander, collapsing bridge. Some things don't require a Stonespeaker Crystal to understand.

"I'm amused by the Jorlan-Ilvara drama," I said, mostly to myself, "but I think I'm still going to go ahead and attack them."

The Stroud story surfaces sometimes when something feels too large. Ilvara had been our nemesis since before we'd learned what a nemesis was. She had power we'd been working around for longer than I could easily summarize. She had survived Zalthir dropping her forty feet. She was radiant-vulnerable and wounded and standing right there, and I had one clean shot at the kind of moment that either works or doesn't.

I raised my hand. I let Bahamut's light gather.

I thought briefly about whether Bahamut was watching, and then I thought: *I took notes.* That's all preparation ever is. You study the position, you map the angles, you track who has eighteen of forty-four hit points left, and then you send the beam and wait to see what happens.

The guiding bolt crossed the cavern in a line of concentrated radiance that I will be honest about — it was beautiful. I'm a little theatrical about Bahamut and I know it. But it was genuinely beautiful.

Ilvara did not fall.

She *detonated*.

The body erupted outward — not blood, not shadow, but a billowing cloud of poisonous spores that rolled through the air in every direction, thick and yellow-gray, swallowing Zalthir and Thorin whole before either of them could move. Zuggtmoy's parting gift. A priestess of demon-lord corruption does not simply die; she shares the experience.

Bahamut's light ended her, and she answered in poison.

I stood in the silence that followed — which was relative silence, because Asha Vandree immediately dropped to her knees and began murmuring prayers to Lolth amid the settling spore-cloud — and I looked at the radius of that cloud, and I looked at where I had been standing, and I said:

"Not ideal. How big is the cloud?"

And then: "I'm glad I was standing back."

Both of those things were true. The killing blow on our longest-standing enemy, the radiant light of Bahamut striking down a corrupted Zuggtmoy priestess, and my primary takeaway was tactical geometry. I didn't feel the need to be more ceremonial about it than that. The record shows what happened. Ilvara is dead. I was at a useful distance. *I took notes.*

The last drow of House T'sarran, the elite warrior who'd spent the whole fight blaming his mage for not bringing Dispel Magic, surveyed the wreckage of the cavern — the spore cloud, the collapsed bridge, the kneeling Asha, the two half-poisoned members of the Ember Vanguard — and made the only sensible professional decision available to him.

"We will meet again," he said. "Enjoy your victory for the moment."

Then he was gone into the tunnels.

Thorin, watching him go, offered the observation that nobody knew what the hell was going on.

I thought that was a little ungenerous. I knew what was going on. I had been taking notes the entire time.

But I understood the feeling. The cave smelled of spores and expended magic and old stone, and we were standing in it — alive, victorious, Ilvara reduced to a toxic cloud — and it felt less like triumph and more like aftermath. Which is usually what triumph feels like, in my experience. You prepare carefully, you send the radiant bolt, and then you stand in the consequence of it and figure out what the poisoned air costs and who needs Lesser Restoration and what happens next.

Asha was still praying to Lolth. The spores were still settling. Somewhere under that cloud, Thorin and Zalthir were breathing something I was very glad I was not breathing.

A hoard is always valuable, a blessing from Bahamut. That's a maxim. The compass bearing version of it, the one I was holding right now, is simpler: *sometimes the right thing costs the people next to you, and you still have to do it.*

I opened a new set of notes.
