## Section — Thorin — The Heart Fungus and the Drow Betrayal

The cavern floor hit my boots with a crunch I felt up through my knees. The walkways above were cleaner ground, tactically speaking, but you can't end a fight from above something that lives down below. So I dropped.

Dawnbringer was already warm in my grip before I'd found my footing in the difficult terrain — that particular warmth she reserves when something truly needs burning. She'd been restless the entire fight, pulling toward the massive fungal structure at the cavern's center the way a lodestone pulls toward north. I'd asked, before I jumped, whether there was anything worth knowing about the thing. Whether it had protections. Whether I was walking into a wall.

"Doubly not resistant to radiant damage," I was told.

Grygum said "oh, that's good to know" and he was right, but I'd already stopped listening because Dawnbringer had gone from warm to *bright*, and I don't ignore that.

The heart fungus was enormous. That's the only word for it — not tall, not wide, not looming, just enormous in the way a thing becomes when it has grown unchecked in a place where nothing checks anything. Pulsing. Networked with veins of bioluminescence that made it look almost alive in a way that fungus shouldn't. I'd been inside something like this once, or something in the neighbourhood of it, in a way I still don't fully talk about. I knew what it felt like from the inside.

This was better.

I hit it once with everything I had, radiant light cracking through the flesh of it like sunlight through a cracked shutter. Then again. The second strike split something fundamental — I could feel it through the blade, through my hand, that particular give when a thing stops being a problem and starts being a memory. The heart fungus recoiled, shuddered in a way that had no dignity to it, and then just — wilted. Collapsed inward. The veins went dark.

"I do what I can," I said, to nobody in particular.

The cavern was very quiet for a moment.

The quiet didn't last.

Up above, and back toward the cave entrance, Ilvara was still standing — though "standing" might be generous after what Zalthir had done to her. She was wounded. That was the word Jorlan had apparently been waiting on. I heard him first: the sound of boots hitting rock at speed, and one word ripped out of him like it had been waiting years for its release — *Dovara* — and then he was past me, through the difficult terrain I'd just navigated, running not *at* something but *toward* something. Running the way someone runs when they've stopped being a soldier and started being a grievance.

My free hand found the hilt on instinct. My body knew what to do with a retreating enemy. I've trained for it, I have the stance for it, and Jorlan had done his best to make himself our problem before the tides had turned. The strike was right there. Available.

I didn't take it.

I stood there with Dawnbringer still hot with radiant residue and watched Jorlan sprint toward his former lover — or whatever Ilvara had been to him before she'd decided his scarred face was an inconvenience — and I did the arithmetic a dwarf from the surface doesn't usually need to do. If he kills her and we didn't — what happens? Who gets first claim on what she's carrying? I said this out loud, which earned me the confused silence of men who had never once thought about looting logistics in the middle of a multi-faction drow political collapse. "Lover's booty," I said, by way of clarification. "The goods."

Nobody had a good answer. The dice settled it. An eight says you do the sensible thing.

I lowered my weapon and let him go.

It stung. I won't pretend it didn't. I like my opportunity attacks. I like the clean satisfaction of the mechanics doing what they were designed for — the thing tries to leave, you punish it for trying. Simple. Direct. No subtext. But I watched Jorlan run and I thought of something one of the elders used to do back in the hills, when two younger giants had a grievance they hadn't finished yet. He would just — sit. Pull his weight down into stillness and wait. A rock in the wrong place is just a rock. Let it find where it belongs.

Jorlan belonged in whatever was about to happen with Ilvara. That wasn't my weight to interrupt.

*That makes me sad, though*, I thought, *because I like my attacks of opportunity here.*

Then Ilvara did something petty, because Ilvara was petty. A sphere of insects erupted at the cave entrance — the sound of it hit before the sight did, a roar that was somehow also a hiss, the air going dense and hostile all at once. Grygum caught it. Daz caught it. And Glabbagool, who had been doing his earnest best to observe from the periphery as though he were a legitimate participant in the conversation, took twenty-six points of insect damage because that's what happens when you're an ooze with feelings and poor positioning.

He survived, because he's Glabbagool, and apparently that's what Glabbagool does.

I looked at the collapsed ruin of the heart fungus, still faintly radiating the heat from Dawnbringer's strikes. I looked up at the chaos above — Jorlan and Ilvara and insects and Daz doing something that would definitely be described later as "inadvisable but effective," and Grygum moving with the careful economy of someone recalculating everything in real time.

Dawnbringer was cooling in my hand. I kept my grip on her anyway.

Jorlan had his reckoning. Ilvara had her insects and her fury and whatever was coming for her from above. I had already done my part in this scene — one swing, one bright light, one enormous elaborately grown creature now wilting in the dark.

I stood on the cavern floor and watched, and waited, and let the other fights finish themselves.
