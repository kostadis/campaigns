## Section — Daz — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The insects were everywhere.

That is the only way to describe the center of that cavern in those moments — a screaming, chitinous wall of Ilvara's making, her parting gift to the battlefield, suspended in the middle of the room like a god's afterthought. Crossbow bolts couldn't get through it cleanly. Spells bent around it. People made choices about which way to run based entirely on where the cloud was thickest. It had become, functionally, the most powerful combatant in the room, and it didn't even have a name.

Grygum had pulled away toward Asha, moving with the steady purposefulness that I had learned to read as *someone is about to receive either healing or a very large hammer.* Glabbagool — I will never not find it slightly absurd to watch a gelatinous cube enter a fray — was pressing forward somewhere to my left. The battlefield had that particular quality that good ones sometimes achieve: total illegibility. Everyone was doing something that made sense to them and nothing added up to a coherent picture.

Grygum leveled a guiding bolt at Ilvara. Clean invocation, proper form, the kind of prayer you could feel in your molars. It missed. Even now, with Ilvara diminished, wounded, Zalthir having put her through what could only be described as a structural inspection from forty feet up — even now, she was still *trouble.*

"Oh, well, that's a shame," Grygum said, in the tone of a man who has been disappointed by divine intervention before and has made his peace with it.

Then the T'sarran drow emerged.

Five of them, from the shadows along the far edge of the cavern, and Zalthir counted them with the flat enthusiasm of someone tallying cargo. "Five Drow, that's awesome." Which I chose to interpret as tactical assessment rather than excitement, though with Zalthir the line has always been thin.

Grygum, for his part, raised an eyebrow. "This is turning into the twelve days of Christmas."

Someone — it might have been Grygum himself — sang it back: *Dark Elves.* The GM seemed to find this hopeful.

I did not find it hopeful.

House T'sarran. I knew the name. Menzoberranzan keeps meticulous records of which houses are ascending and which are quietly shedding members into unfavorable postings — the Underdark equivalent of reassigning someone to a position with no budget. T'sarran had been the latter for at least two generations. Which meant these five were either very capable field operatives, or they were the expendable forward element of a plan being run by someone smarter and further back. Either way, they had crossbows, they were pointing them at me, and they looked, as I noted aloud, *too smug, and too sure of themselves.*

Three bolts came through the insect cloud at roughly the same moment.

The cloud did most of my work for me. Two bolts disappeared into it entirely — I heard one clatter somewhere below, presumably into the abyss. The third found a gap.

It hit. Not seriously. Not *well.* But the impact was real, and in the half-second between contact and thought, some old reflex from seventeen years of applied magical education caught the thread: *reaction, Shield, now.* The barrier flickered up and caught the follow-through, and the bolt dropped harmlessly.

"You are hit," the GM said, as if confirming something I hadn't noticed.

"Yes," I agreed. "I had."

Grygum was already calculating. "How long have they been hiding there? They might have gotten hit by the plague of insects." The GM confirmed they had been holding in the affected zone, were very irritated about it, and had genuinely been hoping things would not reach this point.

Grygum nodded with something that looked almost like sympathy. "It's tough being a minion."

It *is* tough being a minion. The T'sarran elite warrior was, at that moment, having a pointed conversation with their accompanying mage about the general concept of preparedness. I could hear the specific shape of it even from across the cavern.

*I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill.*

*Silence.*

*And you don't have a Dispel Magic.*

*No, I don't have a Dispel—*

*So I have to go through this.* A gesture at the insect cloud. *I don't know what else to tell you.*

I have felt embarrassed for people before. This qualified. The ambush architecture was sound in concept — catch us mid-fight, overwhelmed, and collect whatever the T'sarran matron mother was paying for — but they had stacked it on the assumption that Ilvara would be the controlling factor in the room, and Ilvara was currently losing a debate with Grygum's guiding bolt. The plan was built on a foundation that had shifted.

*The first rule of the House: know who holds the debt before you decide whether to collect it.*

I know. It's not warmth I feel for them. It's professional recognition. They were given bad information and sent somewhere they couldn't win, and the elite warrior at least had the clarity to identify *exactly* who was at fault for that.

Grygum, steady as always, offered the round's final assessment: "If people are in need of healing, we can do that this next round."

And then Kaelira came through the insects.

She moved like someone who had been forced to relocate a fire that was inconveniently placed in the middle of her route, not stopping, not wincing, just repositioning and continuing, and when she was close enough for it to be a private conversation she said, with the specific exhaustion of a professional who has been assigned an unreasonable task by an unreasonable client:

"Come with me if you want to live, you idiot."

There is something almost flattering about an extraction team. Someone, somewhere, decided I was worth paying for. I had been trying to work out who that was since the moment Kaelira's people had materialized into the fighting, and the answer was not yet fully assembled. But I knew the shape of it — someone with access to House Vandree resources or the appearance of them, someone who knew I would be in this cavern, someone whose interests in my survival were probably not entirely sentimental.

Milo would have said something about not looking gift horses in the mouth. Milo would also have followed the nice mercenary immediately and felt good about it.

Kaelira read something in my expression that she correctly identified as resistance, and revised:

"If you don't want to live, I'll still take you — but could you… it would be much easier if you'd just come."

I looked at her. I looked at the insect cloud, the T'sarran mage arguing with her superior, the cavern full of things that were trying to kill me or adjacent to things that were trying to kill me, Asha Vandree somewhere in the middle of it all with her prayers and her suspicions and her very reasonable question of who exactly these new drow were.

Asha, I noted, had already clocked the arrivals. I needed to close that aperture before it became a problem.

I did not make an emotional appeal. I did not explain myself. I said, at a volume Asha could hear, without breaking stride: "No, these are friends of Lolf, and they're here to help." The fiction was already running. The dice were already rolling.

Then I followed Kaelira.

The con is always running. Sometimes you just have to move while it does.
