## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The plan was the same plan. I knew it was the same plan.

"Last time, I tried to grab her, and then she used a lair action to teleport away, right?"

It wasn't a lair action, apparently. Legendary action. I filed the correction away. The distinction mattered — a lair action recharges on a schedule; a legendary action is finite, a resource she carries with her. There was a class for that. I was fairly certain it was Brother Quellin, or possibly the older one — the one who always smelled faintly of salt, which had struck me as strange this far underground — who had spent a very long time explaining the difference between reactive and environmental power. I had spent that particular morning watching a spider cross the ceiling. The spider was more interesting. I regretted that now, but only briefly.

"Second try is the charm, or give up on that whole plan? I don't know. I mean, I can only melee anyway, so I guess I'll just… try that same thing again."

There was no better option. When you have one tool, you sharpen it.

What I understood, turning it over while Daz and Thorin and Grygum sorted themselves into position, was that the legendary action was a defence, not a punishment. If I closed on Ilvara and hit her, she would use it to move away. That was the exchange she had built her survival around: I hit, she vanishes, I have nothing. But the action was singular. She only had the one. If I burned it — if I made her spend it on repositioning instead of something worse — I had taken something from her without taking it by force. The attack itself became the real weapon. The damage was almost beside the point.

Almost.

I crossed the cavern on foot and shadow and whatever the eldritch tattoo makes of the air between one place and another, and I hit her once with the tentacles — just enough, just the contact needed to secure the grip — and then she was mine. Forty feet of open air beneath us. The great vaulted stone ceiling pressing down from above. Ilvara Mizzrym, corrupted high priestess of whatever Zuggtmoy had made of her, suspended in the dark with nowhere to go.

She had a very long way down, if I chose to let her fall.

I did not choose that yet. There were things to do first.

"Now that I'm holding her, I will punch her again, because why not?"

The first one was a critical strike. The kind where everything clicks at once — weight, angle, the precise line from shoulder through knuckle, the exact moment of extension — and you feel the hit complete itself before it lands. I knew it was perfect before the impact registered. I almost never feel that with certainty. This time I did.

The second one was also a critical strike.

I am a monk. This is what I do.

She had no footing to brace against. No surface to push from. I was the only stable thing in forty feet of empty air, and I was hitting her with it. Two strikes, both landing exactly where they should, while she hung in my grip and the cavern floor waited patiently below.

Then I let go.

Not out of mercy. Not out of any complicated intention. It was simply the most efficient conclusion available to me, and it spent her legendary action in one clean motion. She fell forty feet. Stone floors are not forgiving. The landing would do what the landing did, and she could not teleport away from gravity.

"And then I dropped."

I heard it, rather than saw it. The impact. The sound of a priestess hitting stone from a height.

And then, from somewhere below, she reached into whatever remained of her power — Zuggtmoy's power, Lolth's power, something older and colder than either — and put it directly into my mind.

The fear arrived without warning. Not as a sensation I could parse or dismiss, but as an absolute fact about the world: she was down there, and I could not go toward her. The thought of closing the distance produced something I do not have a clean word for. The monk who had, thirty seconds prior, dragged Ilvara Mizzrym forty feet into the air by sheer audacity and force of will, stood on the ceiling of the cavern and could not make himself descend.

"Well, that didn't work."

The deflation in my own voice surprised me. Not the fear — fear is a condition, manageable, temporary — but the abruptness of the reversal. I had controlled the opening of this fight completely. And then she had answered it with something I could not deflect.

She had protected herself against me. Against exactly me. It was, in its way, almost a compliment.

The heart fungus reached up from below — fifteen feet of reach, as it turned out, which is sufficient to span most of the relevant distance between a cavern floor and its ceiling — and the blow landed with the weight of something that had not enjoyed watching me work. It barely blunted. I absorbed what I absorbed and stayed where I was, pinned by terror I could feel but could not argue with.

There was a class for this too. Resistance to fear, or something adjacent to it. I was increasingly confident it had been Brother Tharusk — or possibly the quiet one with the ink-stained hands, who had demonstrated something with a candle flame that I had found unimpressive at the time. I was now finding it retroactively impressive. The information had been available to me. I had elected not to acquire it. The bill was arriving.

The fungal servants died while I stood there. The insect plague churned through them with the thoroughness of something that has no opinion about what it destroys. Around me, the battle continued — Thorin somewhere below with Dawnbringer burning through everything it touched, Grygum sustaining the party, Daz throwing fire at problems — and I held still and waited for the terror to exhaust itself.

"Yeah, how do I not be frightened?"

Wait. Let the end of the moment pass. Or someone reaches me with a restoration and pulls the thing out by its root.

Neither option particularly appealed to me, but the discipline is what it is. I had won the opening. I had spent her legendary action. I had taken something permanent from her options before she had a chance to use it. The fear she'd put in me was the price she extracted for that, and now I stood with the battle churning around me and took stock of what I'd bought.

She was on the ground. She had fallen forty feet. She had used her last extraordinary defence on keeping me away from her.

I was supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right?

Alright, fine.
