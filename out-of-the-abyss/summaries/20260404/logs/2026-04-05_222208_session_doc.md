## Section — Grygum — The Fall of Ilvara

The soap opera of Jorlan and Ilvara was genuinely interesting. I noted it the way I note most things — quickly, in the back of my mind, with a small amount of appreciation for the drama — and then I set it aside. Because the drama was not my job. My job was the killing blow.

"I'm amused by the Jorlan-Ilvara drama," I said, more to myself than anyone, "but I think I'm still going to go ahead and attack them."

First, though: "Does anybody need any serious healing?"

This is the other job. The one before the killing blow. You take inventory before you commit. Daz answered, and the number he gave me wasn't good — he was somewhere past the halfway point and closing on the edge, the kind of hurt that means one more bad round and the math stops working in your favor. He said he thought he could take one more round. I filed that under *monitor closely* and made the calculation that a dead Ilvara was better medicine than a spell.

The cavern below was a ruin. The bridge had come down. Ilvara and Jorlan had landed hard on the stone floor, and the biting swarm at the entrance was still loud enough to rattle thought. It was, in Thorin's precise formulation, like watching a tennis match — enemies cycling through in waves, the political drama cycling underneath, and somewhere in the middle of all of it, one corrupted priestess of Zuggtmoy still breathing.

I had prepared so much for the Big Heart Fungus.

That was over. Thorin had seen to it — Dawnbringer in one clean sweep, and the fungus was simply gone, and I had closed that file without ceremony. *Boy, I had so much prepared for the fungus. Oh well.* The Underdark rewards preparation and punishes attachment to it in equal measure, and I have made a habit of holding my plans lightly. What matters is what's in front of you. What was in front of me was Ilvara.

I held my ground at the edge of the fighting, the chaos arranged around me at a useful distance, and I reached for what I had.

There's a thing that happens when I invoke Bahamut's light in earnest — when it isn't a cantrip or a utility prayer but the real thing, the concentrated beam of radiant judgment I was trained to direct at exactly the kind of abomination standing in front of me. It feels, honestly, a little theatrical. The inner preacher comes up. I'm self-aware enough to know that's happening and to let it happen anyway, because the theater is not the lie. The light is real. Ilvara was real. The corruption that Zuggtmoy had worked into what had once been a functioning priestess — twisted, rotted, wrong in the way that things get wrong when a demon lord gets her mycelium into them — was real. And Bahamut's opposition to exactly that kind of desecration is real. I believe in the principle even when I'm uncertain about the cosmology.

The bolt left my hands.

I knew it the moment it connected. That clean, decisive sensation — not lucky, not barely — the light found its mark with a precision that made me think, just briefly, of Protanther across a chess table from a dwarf who couldn't play chess. Four hundred years of Ilvara building her power in the Underdark. She had mapped the position. She had studied every possible move. And then Bahamut's light came across the cavern and she simply detonated.

Not fell. Detonated.

She erupted outward — her body dissolving into a billowing cloud of poisonous spores that rolled through the air like a last act of spite. I watched it swallow Zalthir and Thorin where they stood. Bahamut's light had ended a priestess of Zuggtmoy, and the answer had come back as poison. This felt theologically appropriate in a way I didn't have time to examine.

"Not ideal," I said. "How big is the cloud?"

The practical question. Always the practical question. I was already measuring the radius, already checking who was inside it, already running the inventory of what I had left to address the consequences of my own victory.

"I'm glad I was standing back."

This is not false modesty. Distance is a resource. I had deployed it correctly.

Asha Vandree dropped to her knees in the settling toxic haze, murmuring prayers to Lolth over the ruin of her former superior. The last of the House T'sarran drow surveyed what was left — the collapsed bridge, the poisoned air, the party still standing despite everything — and delivered their exit line with the hollow conviction of people who had watched their plan fall apart from inside it.

"We will meet again. Enjoy your victory for the moment."

Thorin said, with perfect accuracy: "Nobody knows what the hell's going on."

He was right. We had won, and the cavern felt less like triumph than aftermath, and two of my allies were standing in a poison cloud, and somewhere behind me Asha Vandree was still praying. I took notes. It's what I do.
