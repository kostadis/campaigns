## Section — Grygum — The Drow Reinforcements Arrive

The insects came first.

Not metaphorically — literally, the cave filled with the thick drone of Ilvara's Insect Plague, her parting gift to the battlefield before everything else arrived. I had been tracking the swarm's edges, noting who was inside the sphere of buzzing torment and who wasn't, keeping a running inventory of who needed what, when, how badly. Thorin. Zalthir. The myconids. The Vandree extraction team that had materialized out of the dark like a invoice I hadn't agreed to sign. I take notes. It's what I do.

And then the cave gave us more to take notes on.

They came from the far tunnel — House T'sarran, as it turned out, though I didn't know that yet. Drow warriors and a mage, stepping into a fight that was already five kinds of wrong, and the first thing their mage did was hurl a lightning bolt into the darkness toward where he thought Daz was.

He missed.

The bolt cracked off stone and went nowhere near Daz, which was either bad intelligence or bad aim, and from the tone of what followed I suspect it was both. I watched the Drow warrior turn to the mage with the specific exhaustion of a professional who has just been let down by an amateur.

"I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill."

The mage did not respond immediately, which was answer enough.

"And you don't have a Dispel Magic."

"No, I don't have a Dispel—"

"So I have to go through this." He gestured at the insect cloud, at the whole catastrophic situation — the combat, the fungi, the three other factions already in motion around them. "I don't know what else to tell you."

*It's tough being a minion,* I thought. I didn't say it out loud. I didn't need to. The insect swarm was already saying it for me, in its own buzzing, biting way, doing to the T'sarran scouts what most of my best-laid plans do when they meet reality: persisting regardless, demanding tribute from whoever is in the way.

They were arguing about whether to charge through the plague when Daz answered the lightning bolt the only way Daz knows how to answer anything: with fire. The fireball came out of the darkness, enormous and orange, and I watched several Drow guards simply stop existing. The mage staggered. The remaining warrior produced a threat I recognized immediately as the threat of someone who knows they've already lost.

"The matron mother of House T'sarran will hear about this."

I filed that under *problems for later* and kept my eyes moving.

The survivors pulled back, trading strained looks at each other. "What if she casts Fireball?" one of them said, which struck me as a fair question approximately thirty seconds too late to be useful. And then the last of them, retreating into the tunnel with what dignity remained: "We will meet again. Enjoy your victory for the moment."

*Noted,* I thought. *House T'sarran. Incompetent mage. No Dispel Magic. Matron mother with opinions. Hollow threat, probably backed by genuine resources.* I wrote it in the ledger of my mind and kept going.

What happened next was the kind of thing that makes sense if you've spent enough time in the Underdark, which is to say it made no sense at all. Asha Vandree — all religious fervor and coiled suspicion — caught sight of the Vandree extraction team through the chaos and the dark and immediately drew the worst possible conclusion.

"Who are you? You must be traitors! Traitors to Lolth!"

Her voice had that particular quality of someone about to do something irreversible. I was watching Daz, and I watched him make the calculation in the space of about one breath.

"No," he said, smooth as stone — *oh, he is good at this* — "these are friends of Lolth, and they're here to help."

*Oh, great. Pit one weird religion against another.* But I watched it work. I watched Asha's posture shift, that hair-trigger fury recalibrating itself toward the T'sarran remnants instead of the Vandree team. Daz had done that. The feared mage of the Ember Vanguard, standing in the wreckage of a fireball he'd just thrown, playing three factions at once with the particular calm of someone who has decided that survival and deception are not separate arts.

I was, I admit, impressed. I was also watching his hit points and doing math.

Somewhere in the middle of this — the insects buzzing, the T'sarran retreat, Asha redirected, Nym muttering darkly about the stupidity of people in general — Zalthir had reached his limit. The frightened condition Ilvara had laid on him was doing exactly what it was designed to do, keeping the most lethal member of our company from closing with her, and I could see him working through the geometry of it with the methodical focus he brings to everything.

"I mean, that is the way it was going," he said, "but the spirit seems to be that I should do nothing to become closer to her, or have her closer to me. I am supposed to be trying to not be near her."

The GM had asked, mildly, how exactly a frightened monk expresses his restriction.

"I'm a monk," Zalthir said.

Which is, technically, a complete answer. What Zalthir did instead was redirect — not toward Ilvara, but across the battlefield toward the T'sarran mage, who was having a very bad night that was about to become significantly shorter. I watched him cross the distance with the particular economy of movement that always makes me grateful he is on our side, and I watched the mage fall.

*We barely knew you,* I thought, which is something of a kindness.

The whole thing — the T'sarran arrival, the lightning bolt, the fireball, the retreat, the mage's abrupt departure from the proceedings — had taken what felt like moments. The cave was rearranging itself again: new factions, new angles, new obligations. Asha Vandree was still praying, still volatile, still convinced that Daz's companions were servants of Lolth, which was both useful and potentially catastrophic depending on the next thirty seconds.

I keep notes. I note the shape of things. What I noted here was this: we had just survived a second wave while still inside the first, in a room full of people who wanted different things and were variously on fire, frightened, poisoned, or buzzing with insects. And somehow the architecture of it held. Daz had lied at the right moment. Zalthir had moved at the right moment. The T'sarran mage had failed to bring Dispel Magic, which was possibly the most useful thing he ever did for us.

I'd had plans for this fight. I'd had contingencies mapped for the fungus, angles worked out, resources allocated. Then Thorin had killed the Big Heart Fungus in a single round and a new faction had walked in and the mage had missed with his lightning bolt and everything had gone sideways in the most productive possible direction.

Sometimes the person who doesn't know what they're doing rolls a natural 20. Sometimes the person who didn't bring Dispel Magic ruins the whole ambush. I find this deeply comforting, and I have no idea why.

*This is turning into the twelve days of Christmas,* I thought, and scanned the room again for the next thing that was about to go wrong.
