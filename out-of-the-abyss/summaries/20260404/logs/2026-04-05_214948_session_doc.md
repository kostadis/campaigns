## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The plan was simple. Same as last time: close, seize, hold.

Last time, she'd used one of her legendary movements to rip herself free before I could follow through. I'd spent a whole session replaying the mechanics, which is not something I typically do — I prefer not to think about fights I've already left. But she had slipped away, and the slipping had stayed with me. This time, I'd make her burn the escape just to survive it.

The cavern stretched below: the Big Heart Fungus pulsing at the center, the floor crawling with chaos, Ilvara hovering above it all like she owned the air. She didn't.

I ran. That's what a monk does. You run, run, run — and then you slap.

The Eldritch tendrils snapped from the tattoo across my forearm before I'd consciously called them, coiling outward and closing around her, hauling me into contact at altitude while she was still registering that I was there. Sixteen points of damage and she was grappled, suspended, her feet finding nothing beneath them.

Below us: forty feet of stone and screaming.

*Now that I'm holding her, I will punch her again.* Why not? She was right there. She had stopped moving. These are the only relevant facts.

The first strike cracked something in her and she barely reacted — and then the second found a different angle entirely, one of those rare moments of mechanical perfection where the body does exactly what the mind intended and also a little more. Two critical strikes in sequence, delivered to a mid-air prisoner. Thirty-four points of damage in about a second and a half. Somewhere below me, the others were making noise, but the noise was irrelevant. What was relevant: I was still holding her, she was still failing to escape, and we were still forty feet above the floor.

I opened my hands.

She fell.

There was a class at the monastery — I believe it was Brother Tharusk, or possibly the one who kept cutting his own hair, I was never fully certain — that dealt with the limits of controlled-drop strategies. Something about yielding pressure at the right moment versus the wrong one. Something about what the target does when they land. I had not retained the specifics.

Ilvara landed hard. The plan had worked exactly as intended.

Then her power hit me.

I don't know what she pulled from, exactly — whether it was the Zuggtmoy corruption or whatever was left of the priestess underneath it. All I know is that it arrived without warning and it was not like pain. It was more like suddenly understanding something about Ilvara that made perfect biological sense: *that thing is going to destroy you. Do not go near it.* Not a thought. A certainty. The kind your legs believe before your mind can contest it.

"Well, that didn't work."

I was frightened. Genuinely, structurally frightened — legs unwilling to carry me toward her, every instinct rerouted away from the threat I had spent the entire fight building toward.

And she had used one of her legendary reactions to do it. She had turned my entire strategy into a cage.

I stayed where I was.

There was a moment — not long, a breath, maybe two — where I tried to audit the rule. *I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right?* The spirit of the thing seemed to be: you should not close with her. You should not allow her to be closer to you. The restriction applied. I could see that it applied. I applied it against myself without requiring anyone to remind me, because the monastery had at least instilled that much, even in the parts I had been ignoring.

*Alright, fine.*

Then the Big Heart Fungus found me.

It was tall — I had not properly accounted for how tall — and its reach extended all the way to where I was hovering at the ceiling, which is a fact I registered approximately one-tenth of a second before its limb connected. The blow was tremendous. I channeled everything I had into deflecting the worst of it — twelve points redirected, fifty-two received, the monastery's training earning its keep in the only way it could while I remained unable to move. Grygum commented on my efficiency with a warmth that suggested he found it funny.

I filed that away and waited.

I was pinned at the ceiling by a supernatural terror I hadn't been able to resist, taking punishment from a creature I had no angle on, watching Ilvara regroup below me on the cavern floor. The monk who had controlled the entire opening exchange.

There was a class for fear resistance. I'm certain of it. Brother Quellin — or possibly the tall one, the one who always smelled of incense and made every lesson sound like it was also about something else — had taught it during a period when I was focused on footwork applications. The year I had decided the meditation components were supplementary. They were not supplementary.

I waited for the end of my turn. The fear would break then, or someone would provide lesser restoration, and either way I would move again.

Below me, the fight continued without me. I watched it. I waited.
