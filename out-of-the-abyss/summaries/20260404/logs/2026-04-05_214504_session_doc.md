## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The plan had almost worked once. 

Last time — I tell myself it was last time, though down here the gaps between encounters blur into one long dark — I had grabbed her. Eldritch tendrils, the grapple locked in, clean. And then she was gone. One legendary action and thirty feet of air where Ilvara used to be. So the question I was now turning over, watching her drift above the heart fungus with that particular quality of self-assurance that high priestesses wear like a second skin, was whether I was stubborn or stupid for considering the exact same approach again.

There was probably a class on that distinction. I want to say it was Brother Quellin — or possibly the one who always came to morning practice smelling of tallow and something worse — who spent three sessions on the question of tactical repetition. When is persistence wisdom? When is it failure to adapt? I don't know. I was not listening. I remember the sun was at a bad angle through the eastern window and it was making me squint.

She could only spend that escape once.

That was the thing Kostadis had handed me, and I filed it away before he finished saying it: she teleports to survive my grapple, but she has burned the resource. Whatever she meant to do with it instead — gone. I would spend my turn to cost her a legendary action. A monk's purpose distilled. Run, run, run, and then you make the other creature waste something precious just to stay alive.

I went for the ceiling.

The tendrils snapped out before she could track me. I felt the grapple take hold — the specific sensation of having actually seized something, not almost-seized it, not brushed it — and then I was in contact with Ilvara Mizzrym, the woman who had put chains on us, who had catalogued us as inventory, who had been slowly absorbed by something ancient and festering, forty feet above the cavern floor.

*Now that I'm holding her, I will punch her again. Because why not?*

The first blow cracked her in a way that felt exactly right. The second one felt better. There are moments in a fight where everything clicks into its correct position — where the body executes what the mind intended without negotiation, without the usual tax of doubt and adjustment — and I was inside one of those moments, suspended at altitude with a corrupted drow priestess locked in my grip, and I hit her again, and again, and each strike landed with the clean inevitability of a proper calculation reaching its proper answer.

Someone at the table said something about Nazis. Grygum said *those guys*. I didn't stop punching.

Then I let her go.

She fell forty feet. The sound of it was satisfying. The plan was complete: I had grappled her, I had hit her until the table went quiet with the math of it, I had dropped her. 

I descended.

Ilvara hit the stone floor and did not die, which I had anticipated. She hit the stone floor and then she looked up at me — and I want to be precise about what happened next, because I've replayed it several times in the stretch of cavern ceiling where I subsequently found myself unable to move. It wasn't a spell, or not exactly. It was something older than a spell. It came out of whatever Zuggtmoy had been growing in her, something that lives in the dark between the walls and knows what living things are afraid of. She looked at me and she reached into the part of a creature that precedes language, precedes training, precedes the seventeen years of standing on cold stone in the predawn silence learning to be still —

And it worked.

*Well. That didn't work.*

My legs understood before I did. They had decided, with complete conviction and against everything I thought I had built, that they would not carry me toward her. The grapple, the crits, the drop — she had dismantled all of it with a single look. I was forty feet away and I might as well have been walled off. She had protected herself against the one thing I could actually do, and she had done it with me still in the room, still watching.

There was a class on fear resistance. I'm almost certain it existed. The quiet one — Brother Tharusk, maybe, or possibly the tall monk who kept ink-stained notes in the margins of everything — spent what felt like weeks on breathing through terror, on the anchor points you find when something external tries to rewrite your emotional register. I remember thinking it was the most tedious possible use of morning practice. I remember thinking I would never need it.

I needed it.

The heart fungus, because it turned out the fight had more participants than I had been tracking while I was busy being elegant at altitude, selected this exact moment to remind me of its existence. Fifteen-foot reach. I was at the ceiling and its limbs found me there anyway. The blow landed with the quality of a decision being reversed — all those optimized choices about health and deflection and positioning, and the fungus just... reached up and erased the math. Twelve points I turned away. The rest arrived anyway, plus the poison moving through the bruising.

*Thanks, buddy,* I thought at it. Filed it away.

Somewhere below, Zalthir — the version of me who had spent the first three rounds of this fight being precisely, devastatingly correct about everything — was waiting for his legs to come back. The frightened condition, Kostadis had told me with a sympathy I did not request, resolves at the end of my turn. Or lesser restoration would do it. Whichever came first.

I pressed myself against the ceiling. I watched the fight happen below me. Thorin had his thing going with the heart fungus — Dawnbringer bright and purposeful in his hands — and somewhere in the chaos of fungal servitors and drow reinforcements and the strange Vandree soldiers who had appeared from directions I hadn't anticipated, Ilvara was still moving, still dangerous, still breathing.

*I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right?*

The discipline doesn't stop operating just because the situation is unfair. That was the point of it — not to function when you feel like functioning, but to keep functioning when you most want to stop. I had let her spend the legendary action. I had forced the terror response. I had accomplished what I came up here to accomplish.

It just hadn't felt like enough.

I waited.
