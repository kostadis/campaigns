## Section — Thorin — The Heart Fungus and the Drow Betrayal

The handoff left me on the elevated walkways, watching Zalthir take a mushroom-sized blow and keep standing. Below, the cavern floor was chaos — difficult terrain, fungal growth, the heart fungus pulsing at the center of it like something that believed it was important.

It was large. I will give it that.

Jorlan moved first in the way these things always do — not a tactical decision, just pure old hurt finally finding permission. He was five feet from me, and then he wasn't. He screamed "Dovara!" at Ilvara's broken form and bolted, and I felt the familiar pull of instinct: *weapon up, step forward, that's an opening.*

I had the right. The ability was right there, waiting like a good dog. He'd come at us first — that mattered, or at least it felt like it should. But Zalthir said "the tides have turned" in that flat way he says things that are true, and something made me pause.

I stood there holding the moment like a coin I hadn't flipped yet.

"What happens if he kills her and we don't?" I asked the room. "Does he get first dibs on booty or something? And by booty, I mean, like—" I stopped. "I mean, like, they used to be Lover's Booty. The goods."

Nobody had an answer. The situation didn't have an answer. Which meant it needed a die.

Eight came up. Eight says: do the sensible thing. Let them go. Let the girls fight.

I lowered the weapon.

I want to be clear that this made me sad. I like my attacks of opportunity. I am *good* at my attacks of opportunity. There is a hill giant elder I grew up watching who once held a dispute in silence for three days before speaking a single word, and I have never understood how he didn't just throw something at the problem. I'm better at it than I used to be. That's not the same as being good at it.

Jorlan ran. I watched him go.

Then Ilvara, bleeding and furious on the cave floor, filled the entrance with insects. Not metaphorical insects — a roaring, biting cloud of them, dense enough to eat through intention, flooding the space where Grygum and Daz and Glabbagool were standing. The air became a weapon. Glabbagool, who had been hovering near the edge of the fight with that particular quality of presence he has — watchful, gelatinous, earnest — absorbed the full force of it. Twenty-six points of insect, delivered at once to a creature who had only wanted to participate.

He survived. Because he's an ooze, and oozes are built for exactly this kind of indignity.

I didn't have time to worry about the insects. I had a question to ask.

"Is there anything I need to know about the fungus? Like — is it resistant to stuff?"

The answer came back: doubly vulnerable to radiant damage.

I looked at Dawnbringer. Dawnbringer, characteristically, was already bright.

I dropped off the elevated walkways into the terrain below — rough, cluttered, not built for dwarven movement — and pushed through it. The heart fungus was ahead of me, massive and pulsing, the kind of creature that fills a room with its own significance. It had clearly been constructed with care. It looked like the centerpiece of something.

The radiant strikes hit clean. The first crackled through the outer flesh. The second went deeper. The third — Dawnbringer throwing light in every direction, the fungus recoiling, the whole enormous structure wilting like something finally told the truth about itself — and then it was done.

It collapsed. The heart fungus, doubly vulnerable to the exact thing I was holding, fell in a single turn.

"I do what I can," I said.

There was a silence after that which felt like something more than tactical pause. Someone said it had been beautiful. Someone agreed that it was pretty. And that was right — it had been elaborate, and well-made, and it had deserved a more protracted death than I gave it. But I had the right weapon and I asked the right question, and sometimes that's all a problem needs.

The cavern floor was quieter now. The fungus was gone. Jorlan was somewhere ahead, closing in on Ilvara. Grygum was moving through the chaos, the insect cloud still roaring at the entrance, Daz somewhere in the middle of it being impossible to extract despite everyone's best efforts.

I stood with Dawnbringer still warm in my hand, watching the shape of what was left.

Jorlan and Ilvara, whatever that was — years of something between them, now arriving at its conclusion in a corrupted Underdark cave — that wasn't mine to interrupt. Two choices in one fight to hold back the strike. The eight said sensible. The eight might have been right.

I held my ground and let the thing between them run its course.
