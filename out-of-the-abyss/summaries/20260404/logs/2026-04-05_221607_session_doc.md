## Section — Thorin — Ilvara's Desperate Stand

The problem with a battlefield this big is that it doesn't care that you're a melee fighter.

I knew from the first breath of that fight that I was going to spend most of it running. The cavern sprawled in every direction — elevated walkways, vertical drops, difficult terrain that grabbed at your ankles — and the enemies who mattered most were never where I was. Ilvara had held the high ground from the start, and the heart fungus had planted itself in the worst possible position to reach, and there I was, Dawnbringer in hand, doing my best impression of a man chasing a cart downhill in bad boots.

Zalthir had opened it magnificently. He'd seized Ilvara in midair — those terrible tentacle strikes from the eldritch tattoo that still make me grateful he's on our side — and hammered her with everything he had before releasing her entirely and letting her drop. Forty feet. She hit the stone floor hard. Whatever she was before Zuggtmoy got her claws in, she was something worse now, and even the fall didn't finish her. Instead she looked up at Zalthir on his perch and did something to his mind, some bloom of supernatural fear, and suddenly our most terrifying combatant was frozen in place.

I know what it is to face something that can do that to a person. I didn't like watching it happen to him.

And then Jorlan moved.

He'd been fighting *with* us — against us — it had been hard to track, honestly. But he broke from whatever he was doing and sprinted toward Ilvara with a look on his face I recognized. Not tactics. Not strategy. Something older and worse. He was screaming her name like it was an accusation, *Dovara*, and I had him dead to rights. I could have taken the shot. Should have taken the shot.

Instead I flipped a coin. Not metaphorically — I genuinely let the roll decide it, because I did not have a clean answer and I've learned that when you don't have a clean answer you either pick one or admit it. So I admitted it. And the dice said: let him go. Let the girls fight.

I let him go.

"I like my attacks of opportunity," I said to nobody in particular, because I did and it was true and saying true things out loud is at least something.

So Jorlan charged toward his former mistress, the heart fungus was doing something large and violent to the ceiling, Zalthir was pinned by terror, Drow warriors I didn't entirely recognize were trying to push through the insect swarm Ilvara had conjured at the entrance — a tactical nightmare of her own creation, which I noted with some satisfaction — and there I was.

"I'm just sitting here watching trade blows like a tennis match."

I said it out loud. It was true. Usually I'm the one giving the blows, but the whole cavern had decided to have its crisis without me, and all I could do was take stock. This is different, I thought. This is what it looks like from here.

The hill giants had a saying about waiting. Not patience exactly — they didn't have a word for patience as a virtue, because to them everything was eventually the right size if you stood at the right distance. The saying translated roughly as: *a rock in the wrong place is just a rock, but a rock in the right place is a wall.* I thought about it while I looked at the heart fungus dominating the cavern floor, which was definitely a rock in the wrong place, and started moving.

The descent was graceless. Difficult terrain is not a dwarf's natural friend and the walkways were not designed with me in mind — they never are — but I got down to the cavern floor and I drove toward the heart fungus, which was large and terrible and, it turned out, doubly vulnerable to the thing I was holding.

Dawnbringer knows. I don't always have to explain. There's something between a sentient weapon and its wielder that skips the words, and what I felt through the hilt was something like *yes, this, here.* The radiant light came up full and clean, and I hit the heart fungus with everything I had.

It recoiled. Then it folded.

The elaborate shape of it — the limbs, the mass, the thing the GM had clearly spent real time building — just collapsed inward and went still. The cavern was suddenly, starkly emptier without it. I stood there a moment in the space it left.

"I do what I can."

Which was understating it slightly, but that's how it felt. The right rock, finally, in the right place.

The fight, though, wasn't done. Ilvara was still on her feet, and she'd kept the high ground since the start — a walkway above the cavern floor, elevation she'd used well, and I needed to close it. I looked at the bridge beneath her feet. Stone and wood, Underdark construction, not built for war.

I thought: *Is the bridge undead?*

I asked it out loud, because that's how I check my assumptions. It wasn't. But Dawnbringer doesn't need an enemy to be undead to destroy something. I hit the bridge. I hit it hard enough that it decided it was done being a bridge.

The whole structure dropped. Ilvara jumped clear, Jorlan jumped clear, and they both landed on the cavern floor below — shaken, lower now, the elevation gone. She'd lost the high ground she'd been working from since the first moment of the fight.

It wasn't elegant. The terrain itself was the target, which is not a sentence I thought I'd be proud of, but the distance had been the problem from the start, and I'd run out of ways to cross it cleanly. So I got rid of the distance instead.

A rock in the right place is a wall. A bridge in the right place is a problem.

She landed. We were closer now. The cavern felt smaller than it had. That was progress.
