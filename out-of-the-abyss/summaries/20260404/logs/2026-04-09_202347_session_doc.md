## Section — Grygum — The Fall of Ilvara

The bolt left my hand before I'd finished the thought.

That's how it works, sometimes. You reach for the decision and the body is already there, already committed — the prayer half-formed, the gold light already climbing your arm, already hungry for something to consecrate. I'd seen Ilvara standing in the wreckage of what Thorin had done to the bridge — Jorlan there too, both of them having landed with more grace than they deserved — and I'd thought, *there it is*, and then I'd thrown the Guiding Bolt, and then she was dead.

She exploded.

I want to be precise about this. She didn't fall, she didn't stagger, she didn't do anything so undignified as to simply stop. She exploded. Into a cloud of poisonous spores the color of a bruise spreading in water, dense enough to be visible, far-reaching enough to catch both Zalthir and Thorin in the radius before either of them could get clear. I watched the cloud billow outward and I noted, with the detached calm of someone who has survived the Underdark by being several feet behind whoever's in front, that I was standing far enough back to feel only the faint edge of something bitter on my tongue, and nothing worse.

"Not ideal," I said. "How big is the cloud?"

Nobody answered with specific numbers. Nobody needed to. I could see Zalthir working through the poison with the kind of focused endurance he brings to every inconvenience, treating the toxin in his blood as a tactical problem rather than a crisis. Thorin was on his feet, which was really all the assessment I needed — the dwarf who'd just carved the Big Heart Fungus out of existence with a single pass of Dawnbringer was going to survive some spores.

"I'm glad I was standing back," I said, and I meant it as a statement of tactical fact rather than relief, but honesty compels me to admit it was also relief.

I'd been checking on Daz just before the bolt flew. He'd refused extraction — which, in retrospect, was entirely in character. Nym Duskryn had told him to keep his head down, and Daz had given her a speech about solidarity and participation that was more correct than she was ready to hear. I'd been aware of that drama the way you're aware of most of the soap opera that surrounds Drow politics: with a sense that someone should be taking notes, and a quiet resignation that it would probably be me. But when I'd asked if anyone needed serious healing — asked it the way you do when you're calculating what's left against what's still coming — Daz had answered with the easy honesty of someone doing rapid internal arithmetic.

"I've got eighteen hit points of forty-four," he'd said. "I think I can take one more round of hits."

The arithmetic wasn't comfortable. Halfway down, and "one more round" isn't a plan so much as it is a bet. I noted it. I factored it. I determined that radiant damage delivered now might cost me the option of a healing word later, and then I decided that the best way to guarantee Daz survived the next round was to ensure there was no next round, and I threw the bolt.

Bahamut, apparently, agreed.

From across the cavern, the House T'sarran warrior — the one who'd spent most of the fight blaming their mage for not having Dispel Magic while Ilvara's insect plague ate them both — found his voice again, now that his voice was all he had left.

"We will meet again," he said. "Enjoy your victory for the moment."

I took a note. They would meet us again, that was probably true. They were backed by a matron mother, which meant resources, which meant persistence. But I had also personally witnessed them argue themselves into a standstill while bugs devoured them from the inside, and if that's the shape of their operational competence, I found I could be fairly sanguine about the reunion.

Asha Vandree, meanwhile, had moved from combat-adjacent positioning to her knees, palms up, face lifted toward a ceiling that was not, by any objective measure, listening but that she was addressing with full conviction regardless. She was praying. Loudly. To Lolth, with all the beatitudes and fervor of someone who had just witnessed a miracle and needed to claim it for her god before anyone else's god could.

"The evil! The undead!"

She was gesturing at something, or perhaps at everything. It was unclear. The spore cloud was still settling. Jorlan's fate remained, as far as I could tell, entirely unresolved — the man had sprinted toward Ilvara screaming her name, and now Ilvara was a cloud of fungal death, and Nym Duskryn had already asked her sister for permission to kill him, and Kaelira had said "he was always the dumber of the ones" with the complete emotional absence of someone pruning a hedge.

Thorin surveyed the aftermath with the expression of a man who had been trying to reach a fight for the entire engagement and kept finding architecture in his way.

"Nobody knows what the hell's going on," he said.

He wasn't wrong. But Ilvara was dead, and we were alive, and the spore cloud was starting to thin. I took notes on all of it. It's what I do.
