## Section — Thorin — Ilvara's Desperate Stand

The thing about a battlefield this complicated is that being the biggest, strongest person on it counts for almost nothing.

I know this intellectually. I have known it since the first time a hill giant elder sat down next to a dispute — not to resolve it, not yet, just to be there — and said nothing for two days while the argument wore itself out. The lesson was about patience, and scale. Some things you can't muscle through. Some things you just have to stand inside while they happen around you.

I hate that lesson.

Zalthir had Ilvara in the air. He'd seized her with those reaching eldritch limbs he's never fully explained to me, and for a moment it looked like the whole fight was going to resolve in about six seconds, which would have been fine. More than fine. Then he hit her again — twice, the way you don't when you're holding someone and caring about them, which is to say exactly as hard as he possibly could — and let go entirely.

She fell forty feet.

The stone caught her. She hit hard, the way Ilvara Mizzrym has never hit anything in her life, because people like Ilvara do not fall. They float. They hover at the edge of lamplight and look down at you and smile because the architecture of power has always, always put them above. And then Zalthir dropped her forty feet and she hit the cavern floor like she was anyone.

That should have been it.

It wasn't it.

She looked up at Zalthir and did something with her face, or her will, or whatever piece of Zuggtmoy's borrowed terror she still had left in her, and I watched him freeze. Just — stop. The most efficient combatant I have ever stood next to, the man who turned a grapple into an execution, standing very still with something behind his eyes that I did not want to look at directly. Frightened. He'd been frightened.

So that was that.

The heart fungus — enormous, pulsing, the thing the GM had clearly put real work into — was still operational, its great limbs slamming against the ceiling. Jorlan Duskryn broke from the melee and started running toward Ilvara, screaming her name, or a version of her name, something that sounded like love and old wounds and stupidity all at once. *Dovara.* Drow I didn't know had materialized at the far edges of the cavern, backed up against the churning insect swarm that Ilvara had conjured in what felt like spite.

I was at the center of all of it with difficult terrain in every direction.

"And I'm just sitting here watching trade blows like a tennis match."

The GM said: "This is different, usually I'm the one giving the blows, but everybody else here seems to be doing it." Which is exactly the problem, isn't it.

---

The Jorlan question was a real question. He'd come at us first — he'd been Ilvara's creature, then he'd decided he wasn't, and somewhere in that process he'd taken swings at us. And now he was running toward her with that look on his face, and my whole body knew what to do, the way your whole body knows to brace when something heavy falls toward you. Let me take the swing. Let me close the distance. I'm right here.

I rolled instead. The die said restraint.

"So, what happens if he kills her and we don't? Does that change anything? Does he get, like, first dibs on booty or something? And by booty, I mean, like—"

I heard myself say this and recognized it as the verbal equivalent of standing very still. Working out the angles. The tactical version of: *I am not allowed to just hit him, so let me figure out if letting him go is actually sensible.*

"I mean, like, they used to be Lover's Booty, I mean, like… the goods."

Nobody had a good answer. The die had said eight. I let him go.

It made me sad. I said so. I like my attacks of opportunity.

---

The bridge was Daz's idea, or adjacent to Daz's idea, or the kind of idea that Daz's ideas produce once someone thinks about the actual geometry. Ilvara had the high ground. The high ground was a bridge. The bridge was a thing that could stop existing.

"Is the bridge undead?"

It was not undead. The answer was no. But Dawnbringer and I swung anyway, and the bridge said everything a bridge says when a dwarf with a sunblade hits it hard enough, which is: *crack, crack, crack, gone.* Ilvara and Jorlan both cleared the collapse, landed below, shaken but not finished.

The high ground they'd had since the fight began was rubble.

Sometimes you can't close distance, so you close options instead. A rock in the wrong place is just a rock. A rock in the right place is a wall. Collapsing a bridge so your enemies end up on the same level as your friends: that's a wall.

---

The heart fungus was next, because Dawnbringer has opinions about abominations and I have learned not to argue. Radiant damage, double effective, against something that wanted to be a difficult multi-phase encounter. I descended the walkways, carved through the difficult terrain, got to where I needed to be.

One round.

That whole pulsing horror folded and went quiet. The cavern got smaller without it there to fill the space.

"I do what I can."

I heard the GM say something about how much time he'd spent on that icon, and I felt genuinely bad for about two seconds. Grygum said it was beautiful, which it was. Then it was gone.

---

Somewhere in the chaos that remained — the drow bickering at the tunnel mouth about Dispel Magic, Daz refusing to be carried out of the fight like cargo, Asha Vandree dropping to her knees in prayer the moment Ilvara finally came apart in poisonous spores — I stood in the middle of the mess I'd helped make and tried to figure out where to point myself next.

That's the job. Not the politics, not the drow drama, not whoever gets first dibs on Ilvara's boots. Just: point yourself at the next thing that needs addressing and go.

All I know is this map sucked, because I felt like I was running around trying to get to things the whole time.

I do what I can.
