## Section — Grygum — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The cavern had too many things happening at once. That's the only way I can describe it — not chaos exactly, because chaos implies randomness, and this had a kind of horrible structure to it. Enemy appearing, insect swarm expanding, Glabbagool taking the worst of both, Ilvara somewhere below and still moving. I catalogued it the way I catalogue most things: fast, in order of urgency, without getting attached to the version I'd planned.

I had planned things, for what it's worth.

I'd been thinking about the Big Heart Fungus since we found it — thinking about how Bahamut's radiant light moves through corruption, about what it means to cleanse something rather than simply destroy it. I had prepared considerable theological investment for that fungus. Then Thorin walked up to it with Dawnbringer and removed it from the situation in approximately one turn, and I had to let all of that go. *Boy, I had so much prepared for the fungus. Oh well.* You hold your plans lightly in the Underdark. You learn that or you don't last.

I didn't want to get in the middle of Jorlan and Ilvara. I want to be clear about that — it wasn't cowardice, it was reading the room. When a man screams a woman's name across a battlefield and breaks from a fight to reach her, that's a specific kind of drama, and inserting yourself into it rarely ends well for any party. *Couples issues,* I noted in the mental ledger I keep of things that are interesting but not immediately my problem. I moved to the back of the cavern, closer to Asha Vandree and the edge of the swarm, and I watched the insect cloud do its terrible work on everyone who got too near the entrance.

Glabbagool was in it. That worried me. The ooze had been getting hit from multiple directions, and I kept one eye on the golden shimmer of him between the chittering, poisonous dark.

Ilvara was still trouble. She'd survived a forty-foot drop because she is, despite everything, a former high priestess of Lolth and not someone who falls down and stays there. I could see her picking herself up, or hear her — the specific quality of hatred that carries across a cavern. Holy light gathered in my palm, shaped itself into something that could fly and burn and mark a target for the next strike, and I sent it at her.

It hit stone. She was still moving.

"Oh, well," I said to no one in particular. "That's a shame."

And then five more Drow stepped out of the dark.

Zalthir counted them first — he counts things on instinct, the way I take notes. Five warriors, House T'sarran by the look of them, emerging from whatever shadow they'd been waiting in directly into the outer edge of the insect swarm. You could tell from their expressions that this had not been the plan. They'd been crouching in the dark, probably for quite a while, waiting for a cleaner moment. The moment had not arrived. The swarm arrived instead.

"This is turning into the twelve days of Christmas," I said.

Zalthir said: "Five dark elves."

And from somewhere near the center of the cavern I heard exactly the tone of a man who had spent careful time building an encounter and was watching it devour itself. I chose not to make that worse.

I have a theory about well-laid plans and the Underdark. There's a story I return to sometimes — Stroud and the chess game, Protanther the Ancient Gold Dragon, four hundred years of preparation meeting a dwarf who couldn't actually play chess. The point of the story isn't that preparation is useless. The point is that four hundred years of preparation still has a blind spot. The T'sarran mage had a blind spot. It was "what do we do if the target's own boss has already filled the room with poisonous insects before we get here." A reasonable gap, in fairness. Nobody expects that.

"It's tough being a minion," I said, as the House T'sarran elite warrior took the swarm's edge in the face and kept coming anyway, which I respected even as I was hoping he'd stop.

Hand crossbows fired into the buzzing dark — at Daz, or toward where Daz had been. They hit nothing. The swarm was eating their ammunition and their nerve in equal measure. A lightning bolt came out of somewhere and found mostly cavern wall. The mage did not have Dispel Magic, which I gathered from the way the elite warrior was looking at her like she'd personally ruined his evening.

Kaelira pulled Daz northeast through the cloud, which struck me as almost loving in its efficiency — the kind of care that expresses itself as logistics. She was protecting him by moving him, which is not nothing. Whether Daz would agree was another matter.

From the back line, I had a view of all of it. Glabbagool flickering in the swarm light. Zalthir somewhere in the dark above. Thorin at the bridge, still looking for something he was allowed to hit. Asha Vandree beside me, religious fervor rising in her throat like a tide.

Ilvara still on her feet. Still a problem.

I gathered light in my palm again and watched the battlefield turn.

"If people are in need of healing," I called out, because someone had to, "we can do that this next round."

The offer hung in the air of a cavern that was about to answer it violently. Bahamut, I thought, with the particular tone I use when I'm less praying and more filing a report — *I'm doing what I can from back here. Take notes.*
