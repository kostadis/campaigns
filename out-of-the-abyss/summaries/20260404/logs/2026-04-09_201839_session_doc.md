## Section — Zalthir — Ilvara's Desperate Stand

The frightened condition is not pain. That's the thing about it — it doesn't hurt. It just takes your legs and turns them in the wrong direction, points them away from the thing you most want to destroy, and makes every step toward her feel like walking into a current. I know she did it deliberately. Of course she did. She had watched me drop forty feet of elevation on her and walk away. Frightening me was the tidiest counter available.

I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right? Alright, fine.

There was a class for this. I think it was Brother Quellin — or possibly Brother Ondrath, I'm genuinely not certain — on the management of fear-based compulsions. Maintaining tactical function under a divinely-imposed rout instinct. I remember the quality of the light in that chamber: grey, indirect, coming from somewhere above the ceiling slats. I remember that I was thinking about footwork. I do not remember what Brother Quellin said.

What I do remember is that the class existed. The information was available. I made other choices.

So. Fine. I cannot approach Ilvara. The spirit of the thing is clear enough: she is the source, she is the threat, and every instinct she has planted in me is pulling me away from her. I will honor that. Not because I have to. Because I apply rules to myself the same way I apply them to everything else — without exception and without argument, including when they work against me. Especially then.

I turned and looked at the tunnel where the House T'sarran mage was still breathing.

That I could do something about.

The T'sarran pair had arrived bristling with confidence and dissolved into bickering somewhere around the time Ilvara's own Insect Plague turned their approach into a nightmare of stinging, crawling things. Their elite warrior had been broadcasting her contempt for the mage since they appeared — *I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill* — and the mage had no Dispel Magic, which everyone now knew, which meant the warrior had to walk through the insects or not at all. The whole operation was a beautiful disaster. Two professionals undone by one of them being wrong about the difficulty rating of the job and neither of them having the resources to recover.

I had already categorized them: the warrior was angry, the mage was defensive, and neither had gotten what they came for. Competent enough to be dangerous, not competent enough to win. The kind of threat that stays in the background until it doesn't.

The mage was still in the tunnel. The condition Ilvara had placed on me pointed me toward the far side of the room. That was fine. The far side of the room was where the mage was.

I stepped into shadow and came out somewhere else.

The mage registered me for exactly long enough to understand what was about to happen. I didn't give her time to do anything with that understanding. My hands found her before she could speak. She went down hard and didn't get up.

Clean. Brief. The fight had moved her to the bottom of my list and now she was gone from it entirely.

Thorin was somewhere behind me in the chaos, I could hear it — not words, just the particular rhythm of someone physically large trying to be in three places on a battlefield that didn't accommodate it. "All I know is this map sucks," he had said at some point, "because I feel like I'm running around trying to get to things." He had said it with the tone of a man compiling evidence. And later, more simply: "I'm running around just trying to catch up with everybody."

He wasn't wrong about the map. The space was vertical in a way that rewarded patience and punished brute advance, which meant it rewarded me and worked against Thorin, and I noted that without particular satisfaction. He would get there. He usually did.

From where I was standing now, having removed the mage, I could still feel the pull of the condition — the directional pressure of it, the manufactured certainty that Ilvara was somewhere behind me and that behind was the right direction to be moving. I held my ground. Watched the fight from the T'sarran side of the room. Waited for what came next.

The condition is not pain. But it has a shape, and the shape is wrong, and I was aware of it the way you are aware of a stone in your boot — not debilitating, just present, and deeply inconvenient, and a reminder that someone else had, for a moment, put something inside my decision-making that I had not chosen to put there.

I filed that away under things to be corrected. Later.

Then Ilvara's voice cut through the noise — louder than it should have been, carrying the weight of something she was calling down rather than speaking from — and she made promises in the name of a demon lord, the kind that come before fire.

I turned back toward the fight.
