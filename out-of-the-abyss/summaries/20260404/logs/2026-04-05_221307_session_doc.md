## Section — Grygum — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The handoff found me already at the back of the cavern, and I was trying to decide whether that was strategy or cowardice.

Both, probably. I'd been tracking the whole field — Zalthir on the ceiling, Thorin at the bridge, Glabbagool somewhere in the middle of a cloud of insects that was, by any fair accounting, the worst thing in the room right now. Including Ilvara. The plague was Ilvara's own casting, which I found instructive. You don't get to be a high priestess of Lolth without learning to make catastrophic mistakes with an air of complete authority.

The insect cloud — and I want to be precise here — was not a minor inconvenience. It filled the cavern's midsection in a dense, seething mass that hit anything that passed through it, stinging and biting and doing the kind of steady damage that doesn't feel serious until it's very serious. Glabbagool was caught in it. Our gelatinous companion, who couldn't scream and couldn't run fast and had no way to explain to a swarm of magical insects that he was actually a valued member of this expedition. I watched him shudder and pulse at the edge of the bloom and felt the familiar tightening I always feel when something vulnerable can't protect itself.

I couldn't fix the swarm. Not my power, not my moment. I noted it and moved on.

What I could see was Ilvara, pulling herself upright after Zalthir had dropped her forty feet — forty feet — onto the cavern floor. He'd done something extraordinary up there on the ceiling, tentacles and strikes and sheer brute precision, the kind of assault that shouldn't come from someone who describes themselves as a monk with the same tone most people use to describe a Tuesday. She'd terrified him in response. Made him flinch, go distant, unable to close. I'd seen his face afterward and filed it under *things Zalthir will not want to discuss.*

But she was still standing. That was the problem.

I gathered the light of Bahamut into my palm — not theatrical about it, just a working gesture, the way a carpenter reaches for a hammer — and I threw a guiding bolt at her. Clean shot. Clear angle. The light scattered off the stone three feet to her left and died in the dark.

"Oh, well," I said. "That's a shame."

The battle, at this point, had opinions about my contributions, and they were mixed.

Thorin brought Dawnbringer down on the bridge, and the impact sent both Ilvara and Jorlan — still screaming her name, still making this terrible, — crashing down to the cavern floor below. The bridge shattered. The sunblade sang. Nym's lightning found Jorlan a moment later, which at least resolved the question of whether House Vandree had any feelings about him. They did not.

I took stock. Thorin. Zalthir. Daz, somewhere, doing something — probably arguing with someone about whether he needed to be protected. Glabbagool, still in the insect bloom, still enduring it.

"If people are in need of healing," I said, to whoever was listening, "we can do that this next round."

Nobody had time to answer before five drow warriors stepped out of the cavern shadows.

Zalthir said something about five dark elves and sounded almost impressed. I did not share his enthusiasm.

"This is turning into the twelve days of Christmas," I said.

Dark elves. Five of them, emerging directly into the insect bloom's edge with the timing of people who had been hiding in exactly the wrong place. They were irritated about this. You could see it — that particular expression of someone whose careful ambush has been ruined by someone else's careful ambush, and now everyone has to stand in the poison together.

"It's tough being a minion," I said, because it was, and because sometimes you have to say the thing.

The T'sarran elite warrior was pulling Daz northeast through the swarm — Daz, who I could see across the chaos was actively refusing to be pulled, because of course he was — and she was taking the insect bites on his behalf, which I respected even as the mage behind her fired a lightning bolt into the darkness and hit absolutely nothing. Over by the tunnel entrance, I could hear their argument devolving in the way all professional relationships eventually devolve.

*I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill.*

I filed this under *motivations, enemy, reconsidering.* The T'sarran drow had come here for something and were now very much in the process of not getting it.

I didn't have strong feelings about them. I had feelings about Glabbagool, who was still in that cloud. I had feelings about Jorlan, crumpled on the cavern floor, whose tragedy I had followed long enough to know it was a tragedy and not just a complication. I stayed in position, tracking the field, waiting for the moment where I could actually help rather than just miss in holy light.

"I don't want to get in the middle of it," I said, as Jorlan's fate and Ilvara's fate and the Vandree drow's increasingly complicated contractual obligations all converged in the center of the cavern. "But I'll follow up if necessary."

This is, I think, my particular theology: I hold the line, I take notes, I follow up if necessary. Bahamut presumably appreciates the efficiency. I try not to ask.

The plague of insects roared and bit and didn't stop. Glabbagool shuddered. The twelve days of Christmas continued to arrive.

I gathered the light again, and this time I waited for a cleaner shot.
