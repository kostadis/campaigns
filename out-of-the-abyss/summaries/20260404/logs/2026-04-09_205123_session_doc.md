## Section — Grygum — The Drow Reinforcements Arrive

The bickering reached me before the bodies did.

I heard them arguing in the tunnel — the specific, tetchy rhythm of people who have just watched a careful plan come apart and are already sorting out whose fault it is. A Drow elite warrior and a Drow mage, emerging from the darkness at the edge of the chamber with what I could only describe as the energy of two people who have arrived at a party that has turned into something else entirely.

"I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill."

The mage did not answer that. I noted the silence. It was the silence of someone who had, in fact, said exactly that.

"And you don't have a Dispel Magic."

"No, I don't have a Dispel—"

"So I have to go through this. I don't know what else to tell you."

*Through this* was the insect plague — Ilvara's own spell, still churning at the tunnel mouth, full of the sound and fury that spells full of insects tend to have. The irony was not subtle. I filed it under "divine humor" and watched to see what they would do.

It's tough being a minion.

I did not say that out loud. I thought it very clearly, though, at the mage in particular — standing at the edge of someone else's magical catastrophe, armed with a list of prepared spells that did not include the one that would have solved their immediate problem. I knew that feeling. I'd had moments like that. Thorin had just destroyed a Big Heart Fungus I had extensive plans for, and the file had to close before I could even open it. You prepare carefully. You hold it lightly. That's the work.

They pulled back to consider their options. There was a muttered exchange — "what if she casts Fireball?" — which told me they had been watching long enough to be afraid of what was happening in this chamber, and not quite long enough to understand it was already too late to be cautious.

Then, from a different direction, came Asha Vandree — and Asha had her own opinion about strangers.

"Who are you?" she demanded, seeing the rescuers who had come in behind Daz. Her voice carried the particular tremor of genuine religious outrage, which sounds like ordinary outrage but with better vocabulary. "You must be traitors! *Traitors to Lolth!*"

I watched Daz. Whatever fraction of his attention was not on survival or vengeance, he was already sorting this.

"No," he said, with a smoothness I privately admired — the smoothness of someone who has been lying to this particular audience for long enough to know exactly which notes to hit — "these are friends of Lolth, and they're here to help."

*Oh, yes.* Pit one weird religion against another. This is the shape of it. I already knew what it would cost and what it would buy. I was already tired of it. And it worked.

The Drow mage, meanwhile, had picked a target in the darkness and sent a lightning bolt through it. The bolt missed. It missed with some commitment — crackling past where Daz was or had been, illuminating nothing useful and scorching some stone that had not offended anyone. The mage had fired into the chaos and found only more chaos.

Daz's answer was a fireball.

I watched it bloom. These things always happen faster than you expect — one moment there are Drow guards in the darkness, and then there is fire, and then there are fewer Drow guards in considerably more pain. The mage caught the edge of it and didn't fall, but the posture changed. The posture said: the mathematics of this situation have just shifted.

The T'sarran elite warrior drew herself up. She had the bearing of someone delivering a threat she intends to file appropriately with management.

"The matron mother of House T'sarran will hear about this."

And then, with the hollow certainty of the professionally humiliated: "We will meet again. Enjoy your victory for the moment."

I took notes. House T'sarran. Matron mother. Incompetent mage. No Dispel Magic. I would remember them. Whether they actually returned to make good on that particular vow was a separate question — I had watched Zalthir, already moving, already done with the analysis, teleport across the battlefield and put a fist through the mage's remaining composure. The way Zalthir fights, it's less violence and more a formal notification that the conversation is over. *We barely knew you,* I thought. The mage went down.

Zalthir, for his part, had been dealing with something that deserved a moment of acknowledgment. Ilvara's fear effect had him pinned — and watching a monk who had just grappled someone off a ceiling and dropped her forty feet have to work around the concept of being *frightened* was genuinely, gently funny, in the way that divine irony tends to be gentle precisely because it's also correct.

He'd said it himself, working out loud through the logic of what the condition was actually requiring of him: "The spirit seems to be that I should do nothing to become closer to her, or have her closer to me. I am supposed to be trying to not be near her."

"Yeah, I mean, how do you slap?" the GM had noted.

"I'm a monk," Zalthir had said. Deadpan. Final. The complete answer to the question and also, somehow, the question itself.

So he went a different direction. He teleported toward the T'sarran mage instead — not because the mage was the most important target, but because the mage was the target available to someone who was not allowed, in spirit, to be near the thing they most wanted to destroy. He adapted. It was a different file than he'd intended to work. He closed the old one and kept moving.

I had been doing the same arithmetic on a different question. The insect plague was mine — Ilvara had cast it, but it was *in my zone,* which is not a technical description but is an accurate one. The T'sarran drow were inside it. The T'sarran drow were, as of Zalthir's intervention, retreating from it. The chaos of the chamber had produced a configuration I had not planned for and could not have.

I thought, briefly, about Protanther — about four hundred years of preparation and a natural 20 from someone who couldn't play chess. I thought about it for exactly as long as it took me to assess who needed healing, who was down, and what the most useful thing I could do was now that two of the four factions in this room had either retreated or been punched into unconsciousness.

This was turning into the twelve days of Christmas. Every round, something new arrived.

I took notes. It's what I do.
