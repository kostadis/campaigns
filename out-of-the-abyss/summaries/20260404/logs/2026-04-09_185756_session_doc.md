## Section — Thorin — The Heart Fungus and the Drow Betrayal

The heart fungus was enormous.

I'd known that in the abstract, the way you know something is large before you stand next to it. But watching its limbs extend all the way to the cavern ceiling — fifteen feet of pulsing, Zuggtmoy-stinking reach — I understood it differently. It had hit Zalthir hard enough to rattle him through the monk's defenses, and Zalthir was not a person who rattled easily. The thing was the anchor of this whole rotting mess. The priestess, the spores, the corruption spreading through Velkynvelve like damp rot through timber — it all fed back to that. Cut the root, and maybe the rest of it started to make sense again.

I needed to know what I was dealing with.

"Is there anything I need to… anything I know about the fungus in terms of, like… is it resistant to stuff or whatever?"

The answer came back: fungus, so yes, usual resistances. But radiant damage — doubly vulnerable.

I looked at Dawnbringer in my hand. The blade that never fully dims, that argues with me about darkness, that has opinions. She practically hummed. If she'd had a face she would have been smug about it.

"Oh, that's good to know," said Grygum, and I could hear genuine pleasure in his voice, the satisfaction of a man who likes when a plan has internal logic.

I was already moving.

The elevated walkways had been a frustration since the moment I hit them — a battlefield designed by someone who wanted to make a melee fighter feel stupid, all vertical distance and difficult approaches. I'd spent too long watching the action from above while other people had interesting problems. Enough of that. I dropped down into the cavern floor and pushed through the difficult terrain toward the heart fungus directly, because no enemy had come to me and I was done waiting for the invitation.

The giants I grew up with had a saying about patience — something that translated, roughly, to the idea that a rock in the wrong place is just a rock, but a rock in the right place is a wall. They'd hold a disagreement in the air for three days before anyone said a word about it. I never quite got the hang of the three-day version, but I understood the principle: wait until the angle is right, then commit completely.

The angle was right.

Dawnbringer came down on the heart fungus with everything I had. Radiant energy cracked through the blade and blazed — the kind of light that doesn't belong underground, that makes the dark flinch. Once. Then again. The massive entity buckled under the onslaught, its enormous shape wilting inward from the inside, collapsing on itself as the radiant damage ate through whatever terrible vitality Zuggtmoy had put into it. And then it was dead. Not retreating, not diminished — dead, wilted, still, where it stood.

"I do what I can," I said.

Somewhere above me, I heard the GM make a noise of theatrical grief. He had, apparently, spent considerable effort on that encounter. The party offered condolences. I felt a little bad about it, in the way you feel bad when something deserved to be a harder problem and simply wasn't. But Dawnbringer had been made for exactly this, and the fungus had been made from exactly what Dawnbringer hates. These things happen.

Then Jorlan moved.

I heard him before I saw him — a shout, raw and personal: *"Dovara!"* — and I looked up to see the scarred drow warrior break from the combat and sprint toward Ilvara. Not toward the party. Not defending any position. Toward her. She was wounded. He had decided that this was his moment.

I had a choice.

He had disengaged, which meant I could still reach him — a single attack of opportunity, the kind I enjoy making, the kind that says *you don't just walk away from this fight*. He had come at us. He had stood on the wrong side of this for most of the fight. There was a clean argument for cutting him down as he ran.

"But he attacked us first to begin with, so it's like…"

"Yeah," came the answer, "but he didn't think you guys could defeat Ilvara. Now that she's wounded, he wants to take care of her himself."

"The tides have turned," said Zalthir, dry as dust.

I stood there for a moment, holding the decision. If Jorlan killed Ilvara before we did — what happened then? Who owned the outcome? And there was the other thing, the thing I almost said out loud before I caught myself:

"So what happens if he kills her and we don't? Does that change anything? Does he get, like, first dibs on booty or something? And by booty, I mean, like…"

I paused.

"I mean, like, they used to be *Lover's* Booty, I mean, like… the goods."

Nobody stopped me. I looked at the dice. Eight. The dice said do the sensible thing.

"Alright, so 8 says I do the sensible thing. Which I guess is let them go and let the enemies… let the girls fight."

A beat.

"That makes me sad, though, because I like my attacks of opportunity here."

Jorlan ran. I let him go. Somewhere in the back of my mind, the giants were nodding approvingly at my restraint, and I was deeply annoyed about it. The satisfaction I would have taken from that attack of opportunity was real and I had just voluntarily passed on it because a dice told me to be disciplined. Moral Compass, Thorin. Growing as a person. Wonderful.

I didn't attack again after that either. Not right away. The fungus was dead, Jorlan was charging his former mistress, and something in me wanted to see what happened between those two before I put my hand into it. Drow politics had been a tennis match to me since we got here — I'm just sitting there watching them trade blows while I try to figure out which side of the net I'm supposed to be on. So I watched. Calculated. Let the chaos between them play out.

From my position on the cavern floor, I could see the broader shape of the battle: Asha Vandree somewhere above, treating Daz like a fellow servant of Lolth because of a deception I hadn't been part of constructing. The whole thing held together because Daz could spin a cover identity while on fire in the middle of a multi-faction brawl, and the priestess who should have wanted us dead was instead regarding us as complicated allies. It was an uneasy kind of advantage. Not the kind I would have chosen. But then again, I had spent a lot of time in the Underdark not choosing the kinds of advantages available to me.

Then Ilvara's voice rang through the cavern — and she was not retreating.

The sphere of insects hit like a wall of sound before it was a wall of anything else: a deafening chittering roar, a swirling mass of biting bodies that erupted at the cave entrance and caught Grygum, Daz, and Glabbagool in its radius. The cloud billowed, writhed, blocked sightlines entirely. The battlefield had just become considerably more complicated for everyone who wasn't currently standing on the cavern floor having just killed a giant fungus.

Somewhere in the chaos, Glabbagool was in it. The grey ooze took the brunt of it — a face full of swarming insects, which is not a sentence I expected to have occasion to use — and when the question came back, *is he still alive*, the answer was yes. He was still alive, and still, apparently, trying to participate in the conversation. I had seen stranger companions. I had never seen one more determined.

Grygum, steady as bedrock, dropped his concentration on the earlier spell. The primary threat was gone. No point maintaining it. He said it flat and functional, the way he says everything that's just obviously true: *now that the fungus is gone.* As if it were a bookkeeping entry. Which, for Grygum, it probably was.

Then Ilvara exploded.

Grygum's Guiding Bolt caught her — and her vulnerability to radiant damage did the rest. She didn't fall. She detonated. Her body came apart in a massive cloud of poisonous spores that billowed outward through the cavern in every direction, and I was on the cavern floor, right there, and the toxic fungal matter washed over me before I had any reasonable opportunity to be elsewhere.

I endured. But the spores that coated me were Zuggtmoy's. They carried the mark of whatever she had put into this place.

What that meant for me was a question I couldn't answer yet.
