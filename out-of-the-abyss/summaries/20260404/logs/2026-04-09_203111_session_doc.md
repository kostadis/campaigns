## Section — Grygum — The Drow Reinforcements Arrive

The first crossbow bolt skipped off the stone somewhere near Daz's ear. Then a second. Then a third.

New Drow. From the dark. Not Ilvara's people, not Asha's people — someone else's, and they'd materialized inside the insect plague like they hadn't been briefed on basic tactical intelligence. Which, as it turned out, they hadn't.

I watched them sort themselves out in the tunnel mouth — a mage, several warriors — and filed them under *problem* without removing them from *problem*. We already had too many problems. The Big Heart Fungus was already dead (Thorin, radiant damage, I had so much prepared for that thing, oh well), Ilvara was wounded and apparently praying to Zuggtmoy about a bride and mayhem, and Asha Vandree was on our side only insofar as she believed Daz worshipped Lolth. Which he did not.

This is the Underdark. Everyone is performing something for someone else. I take notes.

The mage pointed his finger at the darkness where Daz had been standing, calculated his shot, and cast a lightning bolt directly into empty air. Missed completely. The bolt lit up the cavern for half a second and then was gone, and the only thing it accomplished was illuminating the warriors beside him, all of whom had the specific posture of professionals realizing their intelligence report was wrong.

I heard one of them say — and I want to be precise about this — *"I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill."*

And the mage, to his credit, did not say anything immediately, which might have been wisdom or might have been shame.

*"And you don't have a Dispel Magic."*

*"No, I don't have a Dispel—"*

*"So I have to go through this. I don't know what else to tell you."*

I found myself genuinely sympathetic. Not enough to help them — they were shooting at Daz, whom I have opinions about keeping alive — but in the abstract, the way you feel sympathy for a man who brought the wrong tool to a very specific job. It's tough being a minion. There's a reason I keep notes before I walk into something. They clearly did not keep notes. They were now standing in an insect plague, being devoured, arguing about spell preparation.

Daz did not have similar sympathy. Daz threw a fireball into the dark.

I watched the light bloom. Several of the warriors did not survive the bloom. The mage survived it, but not well — wounded, off-balance, the kind of wounded where you're trying to calculate whether retreat is faster than death. I noted that the fireball had been very precise for a man who'd been shot at three times in the last thirty seconds. Daz has a particular quality of spite that I find tactically useful.

Then Asha Vandree saw them. Or rather, she saw the ones who came next — Kaelira's people, Nym Duskryn moving out of the dark — and her voice cut through the cavern with the particular frequency of someone who has just located a new category of enemy.

*"Who are you? You must be traitors! Traitors to Lolth!"*

I thought: here we go.

Daz, credit where it is due, did not miss a beat.

He told her they were friends of Lolth. That they were here to help. He said it with the confidence of someone who has been lying to people about exactly this for a very long time, which he has.

Asha chose to believe him. Or chose not to choose not to believe him, which in a firefight is the same thing. I filed it under *Daz: active deceptions, status: holding* and stayed where I was.

The T'sarran mage was still alive, which I noted without urgency. Zalthir had noticed the same thing. Zalthir is frightened — Ilvara did something to him, some magic, and it had grounded him in place in a way that visibly frustrated him, the way a monk gets frustrated, which is to say nearly silently. I heard him working through the geometry of it.

*"I mean, that is the way it was going, but the spirit seems to be that I should do nothing to become closer to her, or have her closer to me. I am supposed to be trying to not be near her."*

Which is a very precise way of describing supernatural fear, and also a very Zalthir way of describing it. He was acknowledging the constraint while simultaneously cataloguing exactly how much room the constraint left him. Not zero room. Different room.

The GM, at this point — I want to be honest about this — asked how, exactly, you slap someone when you're gripped by supernatural fear.

Zalthir said: *"I'm a monk."*

This seemed to resolve the question.

He teleported. Not toward Ilvara — that was the letter of the fear, and Zalthir had already noted the spirit — but across the battlefield toward the House T'sarran position. The wounded mage was still there. The warriors who hadn't been incinerated were there. They had just issued the formal threat — *the matron mother of House T'sarran will hear about this, we will meet again, enjoy your victory for the moment* — which is the kind of threat you issue when you are already leaving.

Zalthir hit the mage once.

The GM said "we barely knew you, man." 

They did not know him for long. That's accurate.

I watched the T'sarran warriors go. Their retreat had the quality of a threat with the air let out — they were backing into darkness, gesturing, invoking a matron mother who was presumably going to be very disappointed in all of them. They'd had a plan. The plan had involved a lightning bolt and an easy kill and apparently no one had mentioned the insect plague.

*The purpose of a maxim*, I heard from somewhere in my training, *is not to tell you what to do — it is to tell you what you are doing.* 

What I had been doing, for the last several minutes, was watching. Filing. Tracking who was lying to whom, which alliances were real and which were performing, where the actual threats were as distinct from the theoretical threats. Asha was now praying. The T'sarran drow were retreating. The insect plague still hummed and bit at anything in the middle of the room. Daz was covered in fungus and refusing to stand down. Zalthir had just removed one enemy from the field with a single punch.

Four hundred years, I thought — not quite to anyone, just in that direction. Four hundred years of planning and a natural twenty still breaks the board.

I took notes. It's what I do.
