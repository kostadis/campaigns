## Section — Thorin — The Heart Fungus and the Drow Betrayal

The mushroom thing hit Zalthir like a wall falling over, which is more or less what it looked like from my position — a mass of bludgeoning bulk followed by whatever poison it carried, and Zalthir absorbed most of it through sheer monk stubbornness. He's good at that. Absorbing things without showing the cost.

"Death to mushrooms," I said, because that's where we were.

Then Jorlan moved.

He'd been fighting alongside us — or close enough that the distinction hadn't mattered — and then Ilvara took a wound that finally tipped his calculus, and suddenly he was breaking formation with a single shouted word: "Dovara!" Just her name, or whatever that name meant to him, and he was gone. Disengaging. Running toward her.

The GM laid it out flat: *He's disengaging to go attack Ilvara. You get to choose. Do you want to attack him or let him go?*

Zalthir said, "The tides have turned," in the dry way Zalthir says everything dry, which is most things.

I stood there and ran the numbers on Jorlan Duskryn. He'd come at us. He'd been working with us. He wanted to kill Ilvara. We wanted to kill Ilvara. The enemy of my enemy was running screaming toward my other enemy, and I had a triggered swing waiting if I wanted it.

"But he attacked us first to begin with, so it's like—"

The GM cut in with the Underdark explanation I'd been waiting for, which was: *He didn't think you could defeat her. But now that she's wounded, he wants to take care of her himself.*

That's Drow politics. The whole thing compressed into two sentences. Someone who was your enemy becomes your ally when they think you're losing, and then breaks away from your alliance the moment you start winning, so they can steal the kill. I've had cleaner dealings with hill giants who had genuine grudges.

"So, what happens if he kills her and we don't? Does that change anything? Does he get, like, first dibs on booty or something? And by booty, I mean, like—"

The GM said he didn't know.

"I mean, like, they used to be Lover's Booty. I mean, like. The goods."

Nobody laughed. That was the right reaction. I have a certain appreciation for a joke that lands exactly as badly as expected.

The GM said it was a classic roll-the-dice moment. So I rolled.

Eight. The eight said: do the sensible thing. Which I interpreted as let Jorlan go, let whatever Drow drama unfold, let the girls fight. I set down the attack of opportunity I'd been holding.

"That makes me sad, though," I said, "because I like my attacks of opportunity."

I meant it. There's a satisfaction to a properly timed swing that you don't get from standing still and watching drow politics sort itself out like a very violent tennis match. But the eight said sensible, and I've been trying — genuinely trying — to be the kind of person who listens to what the eight says.

The one comfort was Glabbagool. In the midst of Ilvara conjuring a sphere of biting insects at the cave entrance — *Insects plague, that doesn't sound good,* Daz observed, with the tone of a man reviewing unexpected weather — Grygum asked about the ooze. Zalthir wanted to know if he was alive. The GM confirmed that Glabbagool was not only alive but had been sitting out there trying to participate in the conversation, chillaxing with his eyes, doing his best. Glabbagool always does his best. It's one of his best qualities.

I turned my attention to the heart fungus while Ilvara's insect cloud made its political statement at the entrance. The big one. The thing we'd come here to unmake.

"Is there anything I know about the fungus in terms of, like — is it resistant to stuff or whatever?"

The GM confirmed it was resistant to the usual things, the way fungus usually is, but that if I hit it hard enough it should die. And then: *It actually is doubly not resistant to radiant damage.*

Grygum said, "Oh, that's good to know."

Dawnbringer had opinions about this information. She always has opinions. We'd worked out our arrangement — her light, my darkness preferences, the compromise that keeps both of us functional — and in that moment she didn't need to say anything because radiant damage against something doubly vulnerable to radiant damage is exactly the kind of problem she was built for.

I leaped down into the difficult terrain below the ledge. The ground underneath the heart fungus was wrong the way the whole cavern was wrong — Zuggtmoy's influence spreading through the rock itself, corrupting the floor with something that resisted clean footing. I planted, swung, and let Dawnbringer do what Dawnbringer does.

The fungus wilted.

That's how the GM said it. *It wilts.* One round. Grygum said, "Oh, the giant fungus?" like he needed confirmation, and the GM said yes, the giant fungus, the one that had dominated the cavern. Gone.

"I do what I can," I said.

The GM then made a sound I recognized from the outside of difficult news, which was: *I spent so much time creating this icon.*

Grygum said it was beautiful. Zalthir said it was pretty. Grygum told the GM he'd liked it very much, using his name, with a kind of warmth that you only deploy for genuine sympathy. The GM accepted this graciously.

I didn't have much to add. There was a thing that was trying to corrupt the world, and now it wasn't anymore. That's the whole transaction. A rock in the wrong place is just a rock; a rock in the right place is a wall. Dawnbringer in the right place, against something doubly not resistant to her, is a conversation that ends in one round.

Above, behind, across the chaos of the insect cloud, Daz had apparently convinced Asha Vandree that he was some kind of Lolth-converted true believer. The GM explained this. Grygum said "Accurate," in the flattest tone available. Daz asked who exactly believed that. The GM explained it was Asha, and that what mattered was what she thought rather than what was real.

I filed that away under: Drow things I am choosing not to think too hard about.

Zalthir had taken the poisoned blow badly enough that I glanced over. He was holding together the way Zalthir holds together, which is to say completely outwardly and with significant internal cost. I thought about saying something and decided what he needed from me was not words. Sometimes what a person needs is for you to stand near them and not make it worse. I've had chicken pox. Zalthir had just taken a club the size of a cart wheel and a face full of poison, and he was still upright.

I thought, vaguely: *There, there. It'll be.*

The heart fungus was gone. Jorlan was somewhere across the cavern, running toward his revenge or his death or whatever Drow drama awaited him with Ilvara. Glabbagool had survived the insect plague and was staying close to the party, doing his ooze best. The insect cloud was still eating at the cave entrance.

I stood in the difficult terrain where the heart fungus had been, Dawnbringer still warm in my grip, and waited to see what the eight wanted me to do next.
