## Section — Daz — The Drow Reinforcements Arrive

The bolt came out of the magical darkness and found me anyway.

I had been — I will admit this privately — almost certain it would miss. House T'sarran sent a mage into the dark to hunt the most dangerous caster in the room, and I had assessed the odds the way Menzoberranzan trained me to: read the confidence, read the preparation, estimate the competence. They looked smug. They were sure of themselves. And smugness, in my experience, is the reliable marker of someone who has never had to be good at a thing because they had enough rank to avoid the consequences of being bad at it.

I was mostly right. The Shield snapped up as the bolt hit — reflex, not thought — and the magic absorbed most of what would have been a very humbling injury. But the order of those events was: bolt first, Shield second. Bravado met reality, and reality got one clean moment before I answered it.

I answered with fire.

They were still arguing at the edge of the insect swarm — Ilvara's own plague, which had become, through no design of mine, a remarkably effective wall between the T'sarran contingent and everyone they had come to kill. The mage and his warrior were bickering in the tunnel like men who had each been promised the other would handle the hard part.

*I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill.*

*And you don't have a Dispel Magic.*

*No, I don't have a Dispel—*

*So I have to go through this. I don't know what else to tell you.*

Someone muttered: *What if she casts Fireball?*

They'll get to see for just a short period of time, I thought, while they're getting flamed up.

The fireball went in empowered, into the magical darkness where the mage thought he was invisible and protected. The tunnel erupted orange. I heard the guards — not screaming, not really, more the sound of things that were briefly present and then were not. The mage survived. Barely. The guards did not. He answered my blind shot with a lightning bolt that crackled through nothing and found only air, because I had already moved.

I felt embarrassed for him.

It was somewhere in the middle of this — between the crossbow bolt and the fire and the darkness and the insects and the column of divine flame that Ilvara called down without warning and which, to be clear, hurt considerably — that Kaelira reached me.

She came from the northeast. Elite warrior, professional, the kind of economy in her movement that told you she had been doing this longer than most people live. She put herself between me and the fighting and said: "Come with me if you want to live, you idiot."

She said it the way you'd say *don't touch the stove* to a child who had already touched the stove and was currently on fire.

"If you don't want to live," she added, with the patience of someone revising a contract, "I'll still take you. But it would be much easier if you'd just come."

The rescue was unglamorous. Being dragged northeast through a swarm of biting insects by someone who had been paid to deliver me somewhere is not, in the literature of heroic acts, a distinguished chapter. I did not argue with the directional movement. I did argue with what came next.

Nym Duskryn had the bearing of someone born irritated and who had made peace with that fact. She looked at me the way Menzoberranzan's finest families looked at anything that was technically useful but aesthetically unfortunate, and she said, with aristocratic precision: "Perhaps your benefactor has a good idea. Can you please keep your head down and out of combat? We only get paid if you live."

The benefactor. The contract. *We only get paid if you live.*

Here is the thing about being told your survival is a financial arrangement: it makes you less inclined to be cooperative about it, not more. I had not survived Gracklstugh and Blingdenstone and Neverlight Grove and the death march through six varieties of Underdark nightmare in order to become someone's cargo.

"I am not going to leave," I said. "Everyone here is fighting for me without me also participating. I don't give a damn if you get paid. The best way for you to get paid, then, is to continue the fight and prevent all of us from dying."

Nym turned to Kaelira with the expression of someone completing a formal consultation.

"Can I kill your brother?"

Kaelira considered this.

"He was always the dumber of the ones," she said. "And if I understood correctly, he's covered in fungus?"

"Yes."

"Very well."

Family loyalty, among the drow, was apparently negotiable and subject to current fungal coverage. I filed this away. It would be useful information about both of them.

The city would have called my refusal sentiment. The city would have said: *you're protecting something that cannot protect you in return. You're staying in a fight when you could be extracted, because you've confused solidarity with strategy, and that is a very expensive confusion to have.* Menzoberranzan produced excellent analysts of these mistakes. It had less to say about why anyone should care.

Milo Goodbarrel, in the third chapter of his insufferably optimistic account of the dragon cult campaign, had called it *the long play* — the idea that standing with people even when it cost you something built something worth more than what it cost. I had always assumed he was rationalizing the fact that he was too sentimental to leave when leaving was the smart move.

I was beginning to think the halfling was not entirely wrong. I found this embarrassing.

Asha noticed the new arrivals before I could head her off. She went rigid in the way she went rigid whenever the world failed to match her theology.

"Who are you? You must be traitors! Traitors to Lolth!"

There was no pause. There was no calculation. The fiction was already running, had been running for longer than I could easily track, and the only thing to do was extend it.

"No," I said. "These are friends of Lolth, and they're here to help."

Smooth. Clean. Delivered with exactly the register of someone who was mildly offended at the question. How much Asha believed it and how much she simply needed something to believe in the middle of a cavern full of death was harder to say. Desperate convenience produces its own kind of faith.

The con never stops. You just roll the dice and find out how far the fiction will carry.

Ilvara, meanwhile, had decided to remind everyone she was still a problem. The prayer was quiet — a murmur about *the bride*, about Zuggtmoy, about chaos and mayhem coming — and then the column of divine fire was not quiet at all. It came down on me and Nym together, and I will say only that I had survived worse, but that the margin between *survived it* and *didn't* was closer than I would have preferred.

*Fuck that lady*, I thought. *Let's kill her.*

It was not a sophisticated strategic assessment. It was the most honest thing I had thought in several minutes, and it had the virtue of being entirely correct.

Across the battlefield, Zalthir was doing something precise and terrible with the geometry of his situation. Ilvara's fear had locked him out of closing with her — and rather than rage against it, he was working through it with the methodical calm of a man solving an equation. He considered his options. He acknowledged the spirit of the constraint. He teleported away from her — directly toward the barely-standing T'sarran mage — and hit him once with the unhurried certainty of someone who had already decided where the killing would land.

The mage went down.

Somewhere in the darkness of the tunnel, his warrior was left alone with the insects and the knowledge that the matron mother of House T'sarran was going to hear about this. He said as much, and then he said we would meet again, and then he left. The threat had the hollow ring of someone who knew they'd lost and were hoping we'd forget what they looked like losing.

The cavern settled into the particular quiet that follows when a battlefield narrows its options. Asha had dropped to her knees among the settling spores, praying in the beatitudes of a faith I was still pretending to share. The T'sarran warrior was gone. The mage was gone. Nym was alive and clearly revising her opinion of me downward.

Lots of circles, I thought. Lots of things going on. Fireballs, lightning bolts, darkness. The insect bloom still churned at the tunnel mouth. The fungal fire column's afterimage still lived behind my eyes.

I had refused to be protected, refused to be extracted, and survived anyway.

I found, against all reasonable expectation, that I was enjoying it.
