## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The first attempt hadn't worked. She'd burned her legendary action to vanish out of my grip, and I'd been left holding nothing but air and the memory of almost having her. That was the shape of the problem going into the second approach: same woman, same principle, one resource she couldn't spend twice.

I circled the thought the way I used to circle the training ring — slowly, without committing. *Last time, I tried to grab her, and then she used a legendary action to teleport away, right?*

It hadn't been a lair action. I'd called it wrong. That distinction mattered.

I turned it over. If she burned her legendary action teleporting out of the grapple, she wasn't using it for something worse. I didn't know what something worse looked like, but Ilvara had been a high priestess of Lolth before Zuggtmoy had whatever opinion Zuggtmoy had on the subject, and the general rule with high priestesses was that you did not want to find out what else they could do. So: pressure her, make her spend it, endure the reset, close again. The plan was thin. It was also everything I had, because I can only melee anyway.

*Second try is the charm, or give up on that whole plan?*

I already knew the answer before I asked. I crossed the battlefield.

---

The Eldritch Claw Tattoo is not a subtle instrument. The tentacles that extend from it don't announce themselves or ask permission — they reach, they close, they hold. I let them do what they were designed to do, tracking Ilvara across the open air above the massive heart fungus, the corrupted cavern sprawling below us in dim and spore-choked light.

The hit landed. Sixteen damage. And she was grappled.

Forty feet of open air between her feet and the cavern floor, and nowhere to go. For the first time since this encounter started, Ilvara Mizzrym was exactly where I put her.

There was a class, I think, at the monastery — Brother Quellin, or possibly Brother Vheth, I was never certain which one taught the intermediate grappling curriculum and which one taught something else entirely — about the ethical use of force against a restrained opponent. I don't remember what they concluded. I remember the light came through the east window and made everything look warmer than it was.

The conclusion I arrived at myself, hanging in the air above the heart fungus with a corrupted priestess locked in my grip, was: *now that I'm holding her, I will punch her again. Because why not.*

---

The first punch critted.

There's a feeling when everything connects — not luck, not accident, just the rare, clean sensation of technique executing exactly as it should at exactly the right moment. I felt it in my knuckles and my shoulder and somewhere further back than that. Sixteen more damage delivered at the apex of a forty-foot hang, to a woman who had made our lives a misery since before we even knew her name.

Then the second punch also critted.

Eighteen damage. I almost laughed. I didn't, because she was still in my grip and there was no reason to get sentimental about it, but the moment registered somewhere in the part of me that keeps score.

Then I let go.

Forty feet is a specific distance. It is not the kind of fall that ends a fight on its own — Ilvara was too stubborn for that, too corrupted, too sustained by whatever Zuggtmoy had done to her. But it is the kind of fall that reorganizes a person's understanding of how the fight is going. She hit the ground, and I had a moment of stillness above the chaos, and I thought: *that is the way it was going.*

---

She rose.

I don't know what I expected. Something in her core was still functional, still priestess enough to reach into the dark and find something. She looked up at me — forty feet of empty air between us — and she found me.

Whatever she did pressed against the inside of my skull and did not ask permission. Cold, insistent, wrong in a way that training hadn't prepared for. I know there was a class. Brother — I think Brother Miral, though the name might be wrong, the face is almost certainly wrong — taught something about fear. Magical fear specifically, the kind that bypasses the reasoning mind and goes straight to the part of you that is still, under everything, an animal. I wasn't listening. I remember the subject being theoretical.

It is not theoretical.

One moment I was calculating the next approach, and then I wasn't. There was only the certainty that I could not move toward her. Not wouldn't — *couldn't*. The distinction matters to a monk. We are supposed to be masters of will. The will was simply not there anymore; she had taken it.

I hung near the ceiling of the cavern, unable to descend.

*Indeed*, I thought. And then, because it was still true and the truth was still operational: she had spent her legendary action on me. She had already teleported away once. Now she'd used the terror. The legendary action was gone. The calculation still held. It had just cost me my ability to act on it.

*I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right? Alright, fine.*

The discipline is the same whether it comes from monastic training or Lolth-touched terror. You apply the rule. You do not approach. You wait.

---

What I was less prepared for was the heart fungus having a fifteen-foot reach.

I was near the ceiling. The heart fungus was on the cavern floor. These two facts had seemed, until that moment, to be incompatible with it being able to hit me. They were not incompatible. The limb came up through the dark and I caught it wrong — deflected some of it, not enough of it — and then the poison followed and I was reminded that being high above the ground does not mean you are safe, and that the cavern had opinions about my positioning that had nothing to do with Ilvara.

*Thanks, buddy*, I thought. Filed it. Returned to the problem.

The fungal minions swept off the field in a wave of insect plague, irrelevant, thirteen hit points between them and none of it mattering. I tracked their absence without mourning them. I asked the only question I had.

*How do I not be frightened?*

Wait out the end of my turn, or have someone fix it. Neither answer was the one a monk wants. Both answers were the answers available.

I held my position near the ceiling — frightened, poisoned, unable to approach the woman I had just dropped forty feet, watching the battlefield rearrange itself below me — and I waited for the cold thing in my skull to loosen its grip.
