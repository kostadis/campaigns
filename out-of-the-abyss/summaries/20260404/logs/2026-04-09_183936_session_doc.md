## Section — Grygum — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The roar of insect wings was already everywhere when I made my decision: I would not go forward. Not yet.

The swarm had expanded since Ilvara called it — thousands of chittering bodies pressing through the far end of the cavern in a mass that defied individual comprehension. I could see Glabbagool at the edge of it, the great cube pushing forward with characteristic fearlessness, absorbing the bites with a patience that was either admirable or deeply concerning. Tactically, I wanted to be closer. Theologically, I had notes.

*Stay back. Watch the approaches. Bahamut protects the shepherd who knows where his flock is scattered.*

So I moved to the rear and I watched, and I kept track.

Ilvara was still moving. That was the first thing I confirmed. Zalthir had dealt her something extraordinary — she'd fallen forty feet, I'd seen it, I'd filed it — but she'd picked herself up from the cavern floor like a woman held together by hatred and divine contamination, and she was still in this fight. The Zuggtmoy corruption running through her wasn't going to let something as trivial as a forty-foot drop close the account.

I brought holy light to my fingertips, gathered the radiant heat of it, and aimed. The bolt scattered. Harmless against stone. No mark.

"Oh, well, that's a shame."

I said it quietly, to no one. The moment had called for a decisive strike and returned only the echo of a miss. I noted the failure and moved on. That's what you do.

It was Jorlan who shifted the geometry next. He broke from the tangle of the fight with unexpected speed, screaming something — *Dovara* — at Ilvara's back, all composure gone, years of wounded pride and probably worse condensed into that single sprint across the cavern floor. I saw Thorin register the choice, saw him weigh it, saw him reach for something in his pocket and flip it.

He let Jorlan pass.

*I don't want to get in the middle of it. This is between them.* I kept my voice level when I said it to the room. *Obviously you've got some couples issues and I don't want to be between them.*

Which was true, and also: I had bigger problems to watch.

The insects caught Glabbagool at the worst possible angle — the swarm pressing in from the north, Glabbagool's gelatinous mass quivering under the assault in a way that communicated distress even without a face to show it. I wanted to call out to them. The cube had been a companion for long enough that abandoning them to a plague of enchanted insects felt like a pastoral failure. But the cavern was too loud, and Glabbagool was moving, and there was nothing I could do from here that wouldn't require crossing through the same swarm.

Then Zalthir said, almost conversationally: "Five drow. Looks like five drow."

I looked where he was looking.

Five House T'sarran warriors emerged from the eastern shadows, crossbows up, advancing in a formation that suggested someone had planned this in advance and someone else had failed to tell the insect swarm. They stepped directly into the bloom's edge. I could see, even at this distance, the moment they realized what they'd walked into — that particular stiffening, that recalculation behind the eyes.

"This is turning into the twelve days of Christmas," I said.

The five dark elves pressed forward through a wall of poisonous insects that wanted nothing more than to eat them alive. Their crossbow bolts went wide of Daz — the swarm too dense, the angle too broken — and I watched them burn resources to cover ground that was actively hostile to their presence. The T'sarran mage discharged a lightning bolt somewhere into the magical darkness on pure frustration, and it found nothing.

The elite warrior, the one who seemed to be doing the most complaining, got close enough to Daz to grab him. I could hear something that I initially thought was tactical discussion and turned out to be blame.

*I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill.*

*And you don't have a Dispel Magic.*

*No, I don't have a Dispel—*

*So I have to go through this. I don't know what else to tell you.*

I thought about the Stroud story. Not because these particular soldiers deserved the comparison, but because four hundred years of preparation still has blind spots, and here were soldiers who had clearly prepared for the wrong battlefield. Someone had briefed them on this room before Ilvara decided to fill it with a plague. Now they were improvising in poison, blaming each other, and slowly losing.

"It's tough being a minion," I said.

Thorin obliterated the Big Heart Fungus somewhere to my left — Dawnbringer finding its doubled vulnerability with the clinical efficiency of a sunblade in the hands of someone who has destroyed a lot of things — and the fungus collapsed. I had notes. I'd prepared something for that fungus. I'd had thoughts about how to approach it, what it might mean for the encounter's structure, what Bahamut might want me to observe about corrupted growth and sacred destruction.

*Boy, I had so much prepared for the fungus. Oh well.*

I closed the file.

Nym and Kaelira were extracting Daz. I could see the shape of it — the professional competence of Vandree's warriors moving through chaos with a clarity that spoke of better intelligence and better preparation than anyone else in this room. Kaelira had him. He was being moved. And I suspected, watching Daz's body language even at this distance, that he was going to have opinions about being moved.

I was right.

I couldn't hear the exact words from where I stood, but the posture was unmistakable — the planted feet, the gesture back toward the fight, the expression of someone who is not interested in being protected and is going to make that very clear to whoever is currently trying to protect him. Daz was not cargo. He had spent months becoming the feared mage of the Ember Vanguard, and he was not going to be wrapped in silk and carried out while the rest of us burned.

Good for him. Also, somebody needed to check on Glabbagool.

The cube had made it further than I'd expected. The insect swarm was thick and terrible and Glabbagool was still moving through it on something that wasn't quite courage — gelatinous cubes don't have courage in the conventional sense — but was close enough that I wanted it noted.

Then Thorin's bridge strike sent Ilvara and Jorlan both crashing, and Nym's lightning cracked through, and the wounded were scattered across three levels of cavern like the aftermath of something planned by no one.

"If people are in need of healing," I said, loud enough to carry, "we can do that this next round."

The offer hung in the air. Around me: insects, darkness, five frustrated T'sarran warriors who were going to claim victory at some point in their retreat and mean none of it, a gelatinous cube absorbing damage with admirable persistence, two drow who had once been lovers now trying to determine which of them would end the other first.

I had notes on all of it.

I kept watching, and I kept track, and I waited for the battlefield to tell me where to go next.
