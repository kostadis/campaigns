## Section — Grygum — The Fall of Ilvara

The swarm was still thick when I made my decision. Insects from Ilvara's own curse churning the tunnel air behind me, the chaos of three drow factions and whatever was left of the fungus cultists spread across the cavern below — and in the middle of it all, Jorlan screaming *Dovara* and sprinting toward the woman who had owned him.

I watched him go.

I'm amused by the Jorlan-Ilvara drama, I thought, but I think I'm still going to go ahead and attack them.

That's the honest version. The soap opera was genuinely interesting — two decades of devotion and cruelty and dependency, and here at the end he was running *toward* her, not away, because apparently that's what people do when the thing that broke them is finally breakable. I noted it. Filed it under "complicated." Stayed on mission.

But first: "Does anybody need any serious healing?"

That's the reflex. I've seen too many fights where someone goes into the final exchange already empty and doesn't come back out. You check the room before you spend what you have. Daz called back that he had eighteen hit points left out of forty-four and he thought he could take one more round. I ran the calculation. He was probably right. *Probably.* That's the kind of confidence that gets clerics busy, but the fight was entering its last phase and Bahamut doesn't reward hesitation.

I had prepared so much for the Big Heart Fungus. Positions, timing, the particular angle of approach I'd worked out for maximum effect. And then Thorin — dear, straightforward, functionally unstoppable Thorin — had walked up to it with Dawnbringer and removed it from the equation in a single turn. There was a moment of silence from the GM's side of the table that said everything. *I liked it very much, Costadis.* And I meant it. You grieve the plan, you close the file, you continue. Boy, I had so much prepared for the fungus. Oh well.

What I had left was a Guiding Bolt.

There's a version of me that would tell you I knew, in that moment, that it would be enough. That Bahamut's light had been building toward this since Menzoberranzan, since the first time we heard Ilvara's name spoken in a tone that meant *you don't survive her*. That the divine plan had us covered. I have said things like that to frightened prisoners in dark places and sometimes I even believed them.

What I actually thought was: *she's already been dropped forty feet, Zalthir has hit her twice with criticals, and she took damage from Jorlan. The math is favorable.*

I think about Stroud sometimes, in moments like this. The dwarf who couldn't play chess, sitting down across from a dragon who had been studying that board for four centuries. Protanther had mapped every line, every response, every possibility in the position. And Stroud rolled a natural twenty and won anyway. The point of the story isn't luck. The point is that four hundred years of preparation still has a blind spot — and the blind spot is the player who doesn't know what they're supposed to be afraid of.

Ilvara had been afraid of many things tonight. She had not been afraid of me.

I channeled everything I had into the bolt. Bahamut's radiance concentrating in my hands, running down the beam of light I sent screaming low across the cavern floor — not theatrical, not beautiful, just precise and sufficient and aimed at the thing that needed to end.

She exploded.

Not died. *Exploded.* Holy light hitting the Zuggtmoy corruption from the inside, and the corruption going first, and the body following, and what replaced her was a billowing cloud of poison rolling outward in every direction through the sudden silence.

"Not ideal," I said. "How big is the cloud?"

I'm glad I was standing back.

That's the whole of it, honestly. That's the cleric's summary of the moment his faith was vindicated: *I'm glad I was standing back.* Thorin and Zalthir were in the bloom, staggering, and I was at the edge of clean air because I had spent the entire fight maintaining position, tracking distances, doing what I do. The caution that sometimes reads as cowardice turned out to be the thing that kept me breathing while the woman we had been fleeing since the beginning of all this dissolved into toxic particulate.

I took notes on that too. It seemed important.

Behind me, the last drow from House T'sarran surveyed what was left — the collapsed bridge, the ash where the fungus had been, the spore-cloud rolling slowly through the cavern — and offered the tunnel wall a threat on behalf of their matron mother. *We will meet again. Enjoy your victory for the moment.* Then they were gone. I didn't bother watching them go. Their mage didn't have Dispel Magic. Their elite warrior had to walk through insect swarms. Whatever the matron mother of House T'sarran had planned for tonight, this wasn't it, and they knew it, and the retreat threat was the verbal equivalent of a door slamming on the way out. I noted it anyway. In my experience, hollow threats have a way of becoming less hollow when powerful people are embarrassed.

The settling spores caught the light strangely. Across the cavern, Asha Vandree dropped to her knees.

"The evil! The undead!"

And then she was praying, loudly, to Lolth — beatitudes rising through the poisoned air like she was trying to fill the space where Ilvara had been with something, anything, before the silence got too complete.

She had barely understood what just happened. That was fine. I understood it. I had taken notes.

Thorin summarized the situation with characteristic precision: "Nobody knows what the hell's going on."

The bridge was rubble. The fungus was ash. The priestess was spores. The drow who had dragged Daz to safety were already ghosts in the tunnels. We were standing in the middle of it, alive, victorious, breathing carefully, and I thought: *this is what faith looks like in practice*. Not the clean beam of light. Not the prayer answered exactly as asked. The standing back. The bolt that lands because the math was favorable. The cloud of poison you survive because you held your position all the way through.

Bahamut doesn't ask for certainty. Neither do I.

I took a breath of clean air and started counting my remaining spell slots.
