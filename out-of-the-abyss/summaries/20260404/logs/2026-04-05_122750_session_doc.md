## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The fight started the same way the last fight started: her floating above everything, protected by height and arrogance and whatever Zuggtmoy had given her in exchange for her soul. I studied her from below and turned the problem over the way you turn a river stone — looking for purchase, looking for the angle.

Last time I had grabbed for her and she'd slipped it. Not a lair action, the GM told me afterward — a legendary action. The distinction mattered. She could only use it once.

I said it out loud, mostly to myself: "Last time I tried to grab her, and then she used a legendary action to teleport away." A pause. "Second try is the charm, or give up on that whole plan?"

There was no third option. I can only melee. The ceiling it was.

---

The principle, once I thought through it, was almost elegant. If I hit her, she moves. If she moves, she burns the legendary action. If she burns the legendary action, she cannot do something worse with it. I was not preventing damage. I was preventing *worse* damage. A monk's entire offensive strategy, reframed as sacrifice.

"So, like, in making her teleport, I am preventing her from doing something more dangerous, potentially."

Kostadis confirmed it. He also reminded me how a monk works — *you run, run, run, and then slaps.* I told him he was pretty much right. The cavern's open air stopped looking like a problem and started looking like a runway.

There was a class on this, I think. Using the enemy's strength as constraint, not obstacle. Brother Quellin, maybe — or the one with the cracked knuckles who kept insisting that a battlefield is a conversation. I wasn't listening. I remember the smell of the monastery in the morning: stone dust and lamp oil. I remember wanting to be somewhere else. I don't remember what they said.

I went anyway. Some lessons land eventually, even if you weren't there for them.

---

The eldritch tendrils came out the moment I was in range — not my hands, not yet, but the tattoo's reach finding her first, wrapping around her before her legendary action could fire. The grapple locked. Forty feet of air beneath us both, the heart fungus enormous and pulsing below, and Ilvara suspended in my grip like something I had caught.

"Okay, 16 damage, and she's grappled."

I held her there a beat. She was light, the way dangerous things often are. I thought: *she expected me to be afraid of the drop.*

"Now that I'm holding her, I will punch her again. Because why not?"

The first fist landed clean — the strike that comes after the thought, not before it, the kind the monastery spent three years trying to teach me to produce reliably. She absorbed it. I felt the corrupted flesh give differently than flesh should, too dense, too wet, something Zuggtmoy had done to the architecture of her body that I tried not to think about.

Then the second crit.

There is a specific sound when you land two perfect hits in rapid succession on something that was not expecting the second one. The table made that sound around me — Thorin and Grygum reacting, the way they do, the way witnesses always do when something lands that cleanly. I didn't say anything. I was still hitting.

Eighteen more damage. The flurry of blows, both landing, Ilvara taking the full weight of every finger I had trained for twenty years — and she couldn't go anywhere, because I was the one holding her.

I opened my hands and dropped her.

Forty feet. The cavern floor came up to meet her with a sound that echoed.

That was the plan, complete. A clean execution. A perfect problem solved.

---

From the floor where she had landed, broken and furious and still breathing, Ilvara looked up at me.

And then she reached into the oldest part of what she was.

"Roll a DC-14 Wisdom saving throw."

The number I got back was not DC-14.

*"Well, that didn't work."*

It came in like cold water — not pain, nothing so honest as pain. More like a door closing. A sudden, total certainty that Ilvara was down there and I could not go toward her. The fear was not noise. It was not shaking or sweating or any of the things fear is supposed to be. It was simply true, the way gravity is true. She was there. I could not approach.

I hung there in the ceiling space of the cavern and assessed the situation.

She had burned the legendary action. I had gotten my hits. The plan had worked exactly as intended, and in response, the priestess of a demon lord had removed my ability to continue the fight by rearranging the contents of my mind.

Tactical exchange: even. Emotionally: she won that one.

There was a class on fear, at the monastery. The frightened condition — or whatever they called it, the mental capture, the enforced retreat. Brother Tharusk, I think, or possibly the tall one who always smelled faintly of incense. He had a very calm voice and he talked about the spirit of fear versus the experience of it: that the frightened body does not run because it wants to, but because something in it has decided, below the level of argument, that closeness to the source means death.

*I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right? Alright, fine.*

The discipline operates in both directions. I cannot find a loophole in my own nervous system. The monastery taught me that too, in a class I was apparently actually awake for, or else the lesson arrived later, deposited by some experience I can no longer identify. You work with the rule, not against it. You find what you can do inside the constraint.

I could stay in the ceiling. I could watch the shape of the fight from above. I could absorb what came next.

What came next was the heart fungus.

---

The limb extended further than I'd accounted for — fifteen feet of reach, all the way up to where I was clinging to the cavern ceiling, thinking I was above the problem.

"Oh, is — it's that tall? Okay."

The blow landed with the kind of weight that comes from something that has never needed to hurry. Massive. Bludgeoning. Poisonous undertones in the impact, Zuggtmoy's chemistry distributed into everything it touched. I deflected what I could — the practiced redirection, the monk's small savings against a very large withdrawal — and absorbed the rest.

I said nothing. The fear-lock held me in place; the fungus knew it.

*"Thanks, buddy."*

Somewhere in the cavern below, the fungal minions were collapsing. Small victories. None of them mine. I couldn't reach her. I couldn't move toward the source. I could only wait, and absorb, and let the fear run its course the way a tide runs its course — not because you've done something right, but because time passes and eventually it ends.

"Yeah, how do I not be frightened?"

"You wait a turn. Or lesser restoration."

I waited. The monk who crosses a cavern ceiling to seize a corrupted priestess in his eldritch grip stood perfectly still and counted his heartbeats until the machinery of his mind reset itself.

Below, the fight continued without me. I watched it from the ceiling. I noted the angles.

I waited for my turn.
