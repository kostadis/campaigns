## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The memory of Ilvara slipping free sat behind every calculation I made approaching this room. She had teleported out of my grip once already — a legendary action, the GM had clarified, not a lair action, a distinction that mattered because it meant she only got one — and I had spent whatever passed for the intervening time turning that failure over in my mind the way you work a knot in rope, looking for where it gives.

"Last time, I tried to grab her, and then she used a lair action to teleport away, right?"

I hadn't gotten the terminology correct, but the shape of the problem was the same: she runs, so I make running expensive. The question was whether to bother trying the same approach twice, and the answer was obvious the moment I asked it. I can only melee anyway. Second try is the charm, or give up on the whole plan. I don't know. The honest answer was that I didn't have another plan, and I had never found uncertainty to be an argument for doing nothing.

There was a class for this at the monastery. Decision-making under incomplete information — I was fairly certain it was Brother Quellin, the one who smelled of incense so thick you could practically chew it. I don't remember what he said. I remember the quality of the afternoon light through the shuttered window and wanting to be elsewhere.

The GM walked through the mechanics patiently: hit her, she teleports, I pursue. I burn her action. She doesn't use it for something worse.

"So, like, in making her teleport, I am preventing her from doing something more dangerous, potentially."

The GM confirmed it. The monk's entire logic of this fight was already in that observation — make her spend resources on survival instead of offense, accept the cost of one attack at a time, run fast enough to close the gap again. It wasn't elegant. It was arithmetic.

"You know what a monk does. You run, run, run, and then slaps."

Pretty much.

---

I launched from the floor of the cavern in a clean vertical line, passing the crown of the Big Heart Fungus, past the level of the walkways, up toward where Ilvara had positioned herself above the chaos. The Eldritch Claw Tattoo wasn't a flourish — it was a tool, fifteen feet of reach wrapped in tentacle force, and I fed it her way before she could register the angle.

"Yeah, because I'm using my Eldritch Tentacles to hit her."

Sixteen damage. She was grappled. For the first time in this encounter, Ilvara Mizzrym had nowhere to go.

I want to say something was satisfying about it. The grip closing. The look on her face, or whatever constitutes a face on someone half-eaten by Zuggtmoy's corruption. The truth is I was already thinking about the next hit. Satisfaction is for afterwards, and I wasn't confident there would be an afterwards.

"Now that I'm holding her, I will punch her again, because why not?"

The first strike was a critical hit. Thorin said something about punching Nazis — he does that, Thorin; he finds the cultural reference and drops it into the empty space — and somewhere Grygum confirmed he hated those guys. I was already through the second strike before the table finished reacting. Another critical hit. Eighteen more damage on top of sixteen on top of the first.

Fifty-plus damage in what felt like three seconds of real time and considerably less than that in Zalthir-time, because when I am inside the work, time is not the thing I am tracking.

The table was loud. I barely heard it.

---

Then I dropped her.

Forty feet. Straight down onto stone. This was, by any reasonable metric, the correct decision. I had hit her, hit her again, and now I used the grapple to turn the cavern's own geometry into a weapon. She fell.

"Roll a DC-14 Wisdom saving throw."

Well, that didn't work.

There was a class for this. Resistance to fear, mental fortitude under assault from creatures of demonic origin. Brother — I want to say Brother Tharusk, the tall one with the ink-stained hands, though he may have been the combat-instinct instructor and I am possibly conflating two different people I wasn't listening to. The point is that the curriculum existed. The information had been available. Zalthir had looked out the window and decided that the seventeen forms of non-physical combat were for monks who expected to fight things that could reach into your mind, and that was not going to be him.

It was, in fact, going to be me.

The frightened condition didn't arrive as panic. It arrived as a cold certainty, total and sourceless, that I could not move toward her. Every instinct that had carried me through the grapple and the critical hits and the drop tried to point me back at Ilvara, and the fear had already gotten there first and locked the door from the inside.

She had hit the stone forty feet below. She had reached into my mind on the way down.

"Alright, you are frightened. Which is very convenient, because you cannot now approach her. So she's protected herself against the—"

"Indeed."

What else was there to say? I had hit her harder than anyone in this room. I had burned her legendary action. I had sent her falling the full height of the cavern. And now I stood frozen above the fight, counting the turns until the condition lifted, watching everything else happen without me.

---

The Big Heart Fungus reminded me I existed.

Fifteen-foot reach. I had not registered that it was that tall. I registered it now, at close range and moving fast, when the limb connected with me at the ceiling while I was standing still and unable to pursue the thing I actually wanted to pursue. Forty-eight plus four bludgeoning, two dice of poison on top of that.

I deflected twelve of the bludgeoning.

"Thanks, buddy."

Grygum noted, with genuine appreciation, that I had rolled a one and managed to optimize around it anyway. He seemed amused. I filed his observation away and remained at the ceiling, which was, in the current arrangement of things, exactly the wrong place to be — too high to engage Ilvara effectively under the fear's restrictions, too exposed to avoid the fungus, and precisely positioned to watch the rest of my party make progress I was supposed to be leading.

The fungal minions fell around me. The insect plague that Ilvara herself had cast was eating through the T'sarran drow at the cave entrance — I could hear them blaming each other from across the room, which was at least efficient use of their remaining time. The smaller creatures with their thirteen hit points didn't survive contact with anything.

"Yeah, how do I not be frightened?"

Wait a turn. Or lesser restoration. Both options requiring something other than immediate decisive action, which was the only kind I had brought to this engagement.

I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right? Alright, fine.

I stood at the ceiling and applied the restriction against my own interest. That was the monastic training, the part I had actually absorbed somewhere between the classes I wasn't attending — not the fear resistance, not the arcane countermeasures, but the internal governance, the willingness to adjudicate the rule against yourself when it costs you. The spirit of the frightened condition was that I should not approach her. The spirit was also the letter. I worked through it myself and stayed where I was.

Below me, the fight continued without my hands in it. I watched. I counted turns. I waited for the cold thing in my mind to loosen its grip.
