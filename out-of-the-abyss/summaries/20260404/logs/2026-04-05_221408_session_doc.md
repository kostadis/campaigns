## Section — Daz — The Drow Reinforcements Arrive

The bolt came out of the dark like a punctuation mark.

Three warriors, stepping from shadow with the practiced ease of people who had done this before, crossbows already leveled. I had enough time to think *too smug, too sure of themselves* before the first one proved me wrong. It hit. It actually hit. I had the Shield up before the next breath — the magic snapping into place like a door slamming shut — but the first one had already found the gap, and I felt it, and the smugness I'd attributed to them became, for one uncomfortable moment, my own.

Bravado, meet consequence. Consequence, meet Shield.

That was when Kaelira reached me.

She was Vandree, or Duskryn, or some combination of both — I didn't have the full accounting yet, which was already bothering me. She was also between me and the crossbows, which I was prepared to find more useful than her faction allegiance. "Come with me if you want to live, you idiot," she said. The professional warmth of that address. The *idiot* delivered not as an insult but as a statement of observed fact, the way you'd say *it's raining* while already holding out an umbrella.

She pulled me northeast through the insect swarm — Ilvara's own plague, now working as accidental cover, which I found quietly satisfying. Beetles in the teeth and eyes and ears, every inch of it miserable, but the T'sarran drow were stuck at the tunnel mouth because of it, and I could hear them bickering from inside the cloud.

"I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill."

A pause. The mage's silence, which said everything.

"And you don't have a Dispel Magic."

"No, I don't have a Dispel—"

"So I have to go through this. I don't know what else to tell you."

I have sat through many post-mission debriefs in Menzoberranzan. I have watched senior house members turn on each other with the speed and creativity that only genuine institutional failure can produce. These two were amateurs at it. The blame-shifting was clumsy, the sequencing was wrong — you accuse before the crisis fully lands, not during it — and whoever had described this operation as an *easy kill* had made the foundational error of assuming the Ember Vanguard was the only variable in play. We are rarely the only variable in play.

When one of them said "what if she casts Fireball?" I almost said something helpful, which would have been a mistake on several levels.

Kaelira, having extracted me from the worst of it, deposited me in the relative shelter of the northeast while the larger battle continued to breathe fire and lightning around us. And then Nym Duskryn appeared at my shoulder.

She had the drow aristocratic affect that I recognize because I was supposed to have it myself — the sense that every person in the room is, at best, an inconvenience. She looked at me the way you look at a valuable object that has, unfortunately, developed opinions.

"Can you please keep your head down and out of combat?" she said. "We only get paid if you live."

There it was. A financial arrangement. My survival was a line item on someone's contract, and Nym's comfort with my participation in my own rescue extended exactly as far as the distance between me and danger.

"I am not gonna leave," I said. "Everyone here is fighting for me without me also participating. I don't give a damn if you get paid. The best way for you to get paid, then, is to continue the fight and prevent all of us from dying."

She looked at me for a long moment. The look of someone running a very fast calculation about whether arguing was worth the time, arriving at *no*, and filing the irritation away for later. Then she turned to Kaelira.

"Can I kill your brother?"

Kaelira considered this with genuine administrative detachment. "He was always the dumber of the ones," she said, "and if I understood correctly, he's covered in fungus?"

"Yes."

"Very well."

I have always admired how cleanly drow can settle certain questions. Jorlan Duskryn — who had, until very recently, been fighting *us* — had just been condemned in the time it takes to establish current fungal coverage. The Menzoberranzan curriculum would have approved of the efficiency, if not the specific application.

It was also, I noted, happening while I was still standing here. Not hidden. Not extracted. Present.

That was when Asha Vandree spotted Kaelira's team and went rigid with fury.

"Who are you? You must be traitors! Traitors to Lolth!"

The thing about a running deception is that it doesn't stop running just because the situation changes. You don't call a time-out. You extend the fiction, you roll the dice, you find out afterward whether it held.

"No," I said, "these are friends of Lolth, and they're here to help."

One smooth sentence. I watched it land. Asha's fury didn't exactly dissipate, but it redirected — which was enough. How much of it she believed versus accepted because the alternative was one more faction to fight in a room already full of problems, I couldn't say with certainty. The con works when people need it to work as much as you do.

Then Ilvara's prayer cut through the noise.

It was quiet, which was the unsettling part. A murmur about Zuggtmoy — *the bride, she will come* — and then the column of divine fire came down on me and Nym, and quiet was no longer the operative word.

I was still standing. Nym was still standing. The fire hadn't changed my position on the matter.

"Fuck that lady," I said. "Let's kill her."

I meant it precisely as it sounded. There was no tactical framing, no list of considerations, no alternative proposal. Just the flat conclusion of someone who has been imprisoned, chased, and is now on fire, looking at the person responsible for all three conditions.

I sent the fireball into the magical darkness — empowered, as large as I could make it, because I had been told I could not see them and my answer to that problem was to make visibility irrelevant. They would get to see for a short period of time, while they were being flamed up. The tunnel erupted in orange. I felt the heat from where I was standing. I was aware, distantly, that I was barely standing.

The T'sarran mage's own lightning bolt had already crackled uselessly through the cavern. The elite warrior had watched his mage get lit up and was doing the math on whether the matron mother of House T'sarran was going to be impressed by his survival. He made his choice, and delivered it with the hollow dignity of someone who knows they've lost.

"We will meet again. Enjoy your victory for the moment."

I have heard better exit lines. The *for the moment* was a nice touch, though — the implication that the moment was finite and theirs would come. The delivery undermined it, which happens when you're retreating through a scorched tunnel while being judged by people who just watched your mage fold.

He disappeared into the dark. His faction's promise of return went with him — filed, noted, given the weight it deserved.

The cavern settled. Asha Vandree sank to her knees among the spores and began to pray. The fighting, which had been a beautiful chaos of overlapping geometries — circles of fungus, columns of fire, pockets of magical darkness, crossbow lines and insect swarms and Zalthir teleporting sideways through fear to strike the T'sarran mage with the clean economy of someone who has identified a loophole and is living inside it — had resolved into something approaching quiet.

Lots of circles. Lots of things going on. Fireballs. Lightning bolts. Darkness.

I had refused extraction. I had refused to be a package. I had refused to be a line item in someone's contract and let the Ember Vanguard carry the weight of my survival while I kept my head down. I had, in the accounting of things, survived anyway.

I found the whole thing genuinely entertaining.

That was perhaps not the most defensible position. Milo Goodbarrel, who was optimistic about these things in ways I've never entirely trusted, would have called it *gratitude*. I suspect it was something simpler: I had been useful. I had been present. I had been, in whatever small way the fireball allows, the feared mage of the Ember Vanguard — and not the cargo.

The distinction matters. I have found it matters more than I would have predicted.
