## Section — Grygum — The Fall of Ilvara

The killing blow was always going to be mine. That's not pride — that's just how the math worked out. Ilvara was wounded, Zalthir had spent himself in the ceiling assault and landed frightened on the cave floor, Thorin was still navigating the wreckage of the collapsed bridge, and I was standing at the edge of the fighting with a clear line and a decision to make.

I noticed Jorlan first. I notice people before places — it's what I do. He'd broken from his fight with us, screaming *Dovara* like it meant something, and now he was sprinting toward Ilvara with what I can only describe as the energy of a man who has waited a very long time for this specific moment. The drama of it was not lost on me. I took a mental note. Filed it under: *interesting.* Then I made my assessment.

"I'm amused by the Jorlan-Ilvara drama," I said, "but I think I'm still going to go ahead and attack them."

Before I committed to offense, I did what I always do. I looked around at what I actually had to work with.

"Does anybody need any serious healing?"

Daz's voice came back across the cavern, flat and honest in the way he gets when he's calculating aloud: "I've got 18 hit points left of 44. I think I can take one more round of hits."

I registered that. One more round. Which meant the time for the killing blow was now, not after another exchange. The cleric of Bahamut took the measure of his party and decided the killing blow was worth more than the healing.

I have never been entirely certain what Bahamut wants from me in any given moment. I find it suspicious when people claim certainty about gods — mine included. What I have is attention, and notes, and a compass bearing that has pointed me right more times than it's failed me. The bearing said: *end this.*

I have heard the story of Stroud and Protanther's chess game enough times in my own head that it has become almost a reflex. Four hundred years of preparation. A blind spot that nobody foresaw. Sometimes the person who doesn't know what they're doing rolls a natural 20 and wins anyway. I thought of it now not because I was uncertain of my aim, but because Ilvara had been so certain of hers — had built this corruption so carefully, had twisted this priesthood into something Zuggtmoy could wear. Four hundred years of planning still has a blind spot. Her blind spot was standing at the edge of the cavern with a direct line and the light of Bahamut in his hands.

The guiding bolt left me like something that had been waiting.

A concentrated beam of radiant light crossed the cavern and struck Ilvara dead center.

She did not fall. She *detonated.*

Her body erupted outward — not blood, not flesh, but a billowing cloud of poisonous spores that rolled through the air in every direction and swallowed Zalthir and Thorin where they stood. Bahamut's light had ended a priestess of Zuggtmoy, and the answer came back as poison. Theology was never clean.

My first thought, and I am not proud of how quickly it arrived: *Not ideal. How big is the cloud?*

My second thought: *I'm glad I was standing back.*

There's a version of this moment that a better narrator would render as pure triumph — the cleric of Bahamut, light streaming from his outstretched hands, the corrupt priestess purged in radiance, the Underdark briefly less dark. I took notes on that version and filed it. The actual version had two of my companions lurching through toxic fog and Asha Vandree dropping to her knees in the settling spores, murmuring to Lolth in a voice full of genuine rapture. Boy, I had so much prepared for this moment. The theological satisfaction of it. The symmetry.

Oh well.

The House T'sarran elite materialized at the edge of the cavern — the one who'd been loudly cataloguing her mage's failure to bring Dispel Magic all the way through the insect swarm. She looked at the wreckage. She looked at the poisoned air. She made the calculation and delivered her exit line with the hollow menace of someone who knows they've lost but is constitutionally required to say something.

"We will meet again. Enjoy your victory for the moment."

And then she was gone, back into the tunnels, carrying news of the Ember Vanguard's victory to a matron mother who was going to be furious about the overhead.

The cavern settled around us. A collapsed bridge. Spores dispersing slowly through the dark air. Asha Vandree on her knees, devoted to a different faith, praying over the ruin of a woman she'd presumably served. Zalthir and Thorin staggering out of the worst of the cloud.

The battle was won. I was standing in the right place when it mattered, and I didn't take the healing when the killing blow was what the moment required.

I took notes. It's what I do.
