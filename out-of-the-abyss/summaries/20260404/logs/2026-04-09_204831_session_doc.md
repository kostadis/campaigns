## Section — Grygum — The Drow Reinforcements Arrive

The bickering reached me before the shapes did.

Two voices, sharp and accusatory, cutting through the drone of my insect plague like a domestic argument conducted at the worst possible time. I didn't need to see them to know the dynamic. One person had promised something easy. It had not been easy. Someone was going to have to answer for that.

They materialized out of the dark at the edge of the swarm — Drow warriors, and behind them a mage, already composing himself into the posture of someone who had miscalculated and was preparing to not admit it.

"I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill."

The mage said nothing. Which was its own kind of answer.

"And you don't have a Dispel Magic."

"No, I don't have a Dispel—"

"So I have to go through this. I don't know what else to tell you."

I watched the insects doing their work — the plague I'd anchored into the corridor, the one I'd placed with some care and intention before the entire situation became what it now was. The T'sarran warriors were looking at it the way you look at a river you have to ford in armor. Calculating whether the other side is worth the crossing.

*It's tough being a minion,* I thought, with perhaps more sympathy than was warranted.

Then the mage raised his hands and sent a bolt of lightning screaming through the dark toward where Daz was supposed to be.

It missed. The crack of it was enormous, the light strobing off stone, and then — nothing. No scream. No impact. Just the receding thunder of a spell that had found only air.

A beat of silence, the kind that only follows humiliation.

Daz's answer came as a sphere of fire.

I felt the heat from where I stood. The fireball bloomed in the darkness like a small sun with no interest in being gentle about it, and when it collapsed back into itself, several of the T'sarran guards simply weren't standing anymore. The mage was still there, but he was not unscathed. His careful posture was gone. He had the particular look of a man revising his threat assessment in real time.

"The matron mother of House T'sarran will hear about this."

I noted that. Filed it. *She probably will,* I thought. *And she'll hear about it from a man who walked into an insect plague without a Dispel Magic.* The matron mother, whoever she was, had chosen her subordinates with optimism rather than precision.

"What if she casts Fireball?"

Someone among them was finally asking the right question. But they were asking it after the fireball had already landed, which is a common way to ask questions in the Underdark. You learn by surviving the answer, or you don't learn at all.

On the other side of the battlefield, Asha Vandree had spotted the Duskryn extraction team — Kaelira, Nym, the whole professionally competent entourage — and her religious fervor had immediately found a new target.

"Who are you? You must be traitors! Traitors to Lolth!"

The zealot's instinct: when the situation is already on fire, light something else. I watched Daz, barely a breath of hesitation.

"No, these are friends of Lolth, and they're here to help."

Smooth. I'll give him that. Whatever complicated feelings I carry about Daz and the layer of performance that sits between him and every room he enters, the man does not hesitate when it costs something. Asha's suspicion visibly cooled, redirected, sorted back into the appropriate "ally" category. The deception held. *Oh great,* I thought, with genuine affection, *pit one weird religion against another.* It worked. It always works. I took a note.

Zalthir, meanwhile, was conducting a philosophical negotiation with his own limitations. The fear Ilvara had planted in him — that particular cold thing that wraps around purpose and squeezes — had taken charging back toward her off the table. He stood at the edge of it, visibly calculating.

"I mean, that is the way it was going," he said, with the tone of a man reading the fine print on a curse, "but the spirit seems to be that I should do nothing to become closer to her, or have her closer to me. I am supposed to be trying to not be near her."

Someone — it might have been the table, it might have been the air — asked how a monk handles this.

"I'm a monk," Zalthir said.

Which explained everything and nothing at once.

He found his answer in a different direction. Not toward Ilvara — that was closed — but toward the T'sarran mage, already wounded, already recalculating whether the matron mother would actually want a report filed. Zalthir crossed the battlefield with the specific quality of movement that makes you reconsider whether he is in fact afraid of anything, and he finished the mage with a punch that suggested the answer was: not this.

The T'sarran elite warrior looked at where his mage had been standing.

"We will meet again," he said. "Enjoy your victory for the moment."

They retreated into the dark. I watched them go and thought about the chess game — about Protanther, the ancient gold dragon, four hundred years replaying every possible move, and Stroud who couldn't play chess at all rolling a natural twenty. The T'sarran mage had prepared a lightning bolt. Had planned for Daz to be where Daz wasn't. Had not prepared for an insect plague, or a monk arriving with architectural precision while the mage was still thinking about what just happened.

Four hundred years of preparation still has a blind spot.

The matron mother was going to hear an interesting report.
