## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The grapple had failed the first time. She teleported — no, not a lair action, a legendary one — which meant she could only burn it once. The distinction mattered. I filed it away.

Below us, the cavern floor was a chaos of spores and shadow and Daz's increasingly loud opinions about who should and shouldn't participate in their own rescue. Above us, Ilvara hovered — corrupted, furious, wearing Zuggtmoy's rot like a second skin. Around us, everything was trying to kill everything else, and I had no options besides my fists and my feet.

There was a class for this situation. Aerial combat positioning, probably. The monk who taught it — Brother Quellin, I think, or possibly the one with the long scar across his nose; I genuinely cannot remember — had some system involving elevation advantage and momentum denial and the weight distribution of opponents in freefall. I did not take notes. I remember the light through the high windows of the practice hall, slanted and gold, and thinking I could be doing literally anything else.

Second try. Either it works or you give up on the plan. Except I can only melee, so: second try.

The eldritch tendrils are the one concession I've made to anything resembling magic — more extension than sorcery, the tattoo at my wrist unspooling into reach — fifteen feet of presence that moves before I do. I used them to close the distance through empty air and wrapped them around Ilvara before she could calculate the escape, hauling myself into contact, seizing her at altitude with forty feet of carved stone waiting below us both.

She was in my hands. Finally.

*Now that I'm holding her, I will punch her again, because why not?*

The first hit was clean. The second was cleaner. Two critical strikes, the kind that happen when every variable aligns — hand, weight, timing, the specific geometry of what breaks against what — and Ilvara absorbed both while suspended in my grip, the Flurry of Blows working exactly as designed. Thirty-four additional damage at altitude. The table lost its composure briefly; someone made a reference I didn't fully track, and the moment became its own kind of monument.

She was not finished. But she had felt it.

I opened my hands and let her go.

Forty feet. The sound of it carried.

She hit hard. I watched from the ceiling, still hovering on the edge of the path I'd taken up, and thought: *good.* That's one legendary action spent, one drop absorbed, and she is now substantially closer to done. The plan had worked. The plan had *worked*.

Then she looked up at me, and the fear arrived.

It wasn't gradual. It was a door slamming — one moment I was assessing angles, calculating whether I could reach her before she spent her next resource, and then there was something in my mind that simply would not let me move toward her. Not fear in any sense I recognized from drowning, or from the Underdark's pressures, or from the various things that had attempted to kill me over the preceding months. This was manufactured. Ilvara, broken and furious and halfway to dead, had reached into the oldest rooms of her borrowed power and deposited something in me that wore the shape of instinct.

*Well, that didn't work.*

She had protected herself against the one thing that had been working. Whatever she'd spent — whatever it cost her — it cost her less than another round of what I'd been doing.

*I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right? Alright, fine.*

I have to give credit where it's due. The spirit of it was clear, even if no one had asked for my compliance. I wasn't going to argue with the thing living in my skull. I stayed at altitude and I waited and I told myself this was temporary and that the choice between forcing her to spend another legendary action on me or letting someone else do damage was, mathematically, a reasonable division of labor.

The Heart Fungus had other ideas.

I had not fully accounted for its reach. I would like to say I had known it was that tall and simply chosen not to factor it in; this would be false. It filled the gap Ilvara's position had left, and its limbs came up to where I was floating with the easy certainty of something that didn't need to think about geometry, and the blow that connected was the kind that reorganizes what you thought you understood about how much structural integrity you had remaining. I deflected twelve points of it — the most I could manage against fifty-two — and the poison that followed burned through the margins I'd thought I had.

Grygum said something. I didn't dignify it.

I was pinned at the ceiling by a terror I hadn't chosen, absorbing punishment from a creature I couldn't reach, waiting for the manufactured fear to exhaust itself so I could go back to the only job I have. The monk who had controlled the first exchange — had planned the second try, had calculated the legendary action burn, had landed two criticals at altitude — was now a very expensive distraction, and not by choice.

*There was a class for fear resistance. Brother Tharusk, maybe. Or the tall one — I remember incense. I don't remember what he said.*

I held my ground in the air and waited for it to pass.
