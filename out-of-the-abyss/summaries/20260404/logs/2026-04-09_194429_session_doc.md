## Section — Daz — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The insects found me first.

That's how I would describe the opening of this phase of the engagement if I were filing a tactical assessment, which I am not, because I am writing this from memory with bits of fungus still in my hair. Ilvara's plague had transformed the center of the cavern into a column of biting, crawling misery, and I was standing in approximately the worst position relative to it — close enough to feel the edge of the swarm, far enough that I hadn't yet committed to the error of stepping fully inside. A useful liminal space for someone whose primary professional skill is observing situations without becoming them.

The cavern itself was a problem catalogue. Ilvara, corrupted and furious, had already reshaped the terrain — fungal blooms erupting from the floor to slow Jorlan's charge, divine fire called down in my general direction with the particular emphasis of someone who remembered my face from before. The Big Heart Fungus was still a presence below, pulsing. Zalthir was somewhere above, or had been — he'd struck her so hard from the ceiling that she'd fallen forty feet to the cavern floor, and even from where I stood that had looked like it settled something. It had not. She was up. She was reshaping things. She was using Zalthir's own fear against him, which I noted as professionally unpleasant.

Grygum moved toward Asha, which was the right call. Asha was volatile in the way of people whose faith is also their temper, and leaving her unattended during a battle with her former high priestess was the sort of oversight that gets you killed by an ally. Glabbagool — my gelatinous, inexplicable companion — had also joined the engagement, which I filed under *assets of uncertain classification*. Grygum sent a guiding bolt toward Ilvara. Clean intention, clean miss. The cavern ate it.

Then the eastern shadows shifted, and five House T'sarran warriors stepped out.

Zalthir counted them with the appreciation of a man who enjoys symmetry in suffering. "Five dark elves," he said. "Five Drow. That's awesome."

Grygum said, with the patience of someone who has been keeping a list of everything wrong with this trip: "This is turning into the twelve days of Christmas."

They emerged directly into the insect swarm. I watched them register this fact. I watched the moment — the very specific moment — when five professionally trained Drow warriors understood that they had timed their dramatic reveal to coincide with walking into a column of biting insects conjured by the person they had theoretically come to exploit.

The first rule they teach you in Menzoberranzan, before tactics, before theology, before the long catalogue of poisons: *know the terrain before you commit*. It is not a complicated rule. It does not require special equipment. These soldiers had been patient enough to wait in hiding, and had then walked directly into the one obstacle they could have mapped from cover.

I did feel embarrassed for them.

The embarrassment was brief, because they had crossbows, and the swarm, while inconvenient for their morale, did not fully prevent them from pointing crossbows at me. Three bolts came my direction in quick succession. I did not move. The insects, to their one contribution to my welfare, created enough interference that none of the bolts found purchase — they skipped off or were simply deflected by the chaos of a thousand wings and bodies between the shooter and the target. I made a note: the swarm was simultaneously the worst thing about my current position and the only reason I still had all my original components.

Then Kaelira reached me.

She came through the insects like someone who had walked through worse and was quietly furious about being required to walk through this, too. Kaelira was Drow — Vandree faction, Asha's people, which made her technically on the same side I was performing membership in, and her expression communicated exactly how much she had paid attention to the word *technically*. She was a professional. She had been paid to deliver me somewhere intact. She found me standing in a battle.

"Come with me if you want to live, you idiot," she said.

I had already run the numbers before she finished the sentence. The T'sarran crossbows. The insect swarm. Ilvara still active and fully capable of more divine fire. Jorlan somewhere in the mix with forty years of grievance pointed at a specific target. The list of things currently trying to end the engagement in my disfavor was not short.

And then there was the other list. Zalthir, fighting through fear Ilvara had put in him. Thorin, somewhere in the middle of it all with Dawnbringer and that particular look he gets when he hasn't hit anything recently and intends to correct this. Grygum managing Asha, managing the battlefield, managing the bleeding.

Kaelira was offering me an exit. A professional, contracted, probably correct exit.

I went with her.

Not because the numbers suggested it, though they did. Not because I had run out of options, though the swarm was making them fewer. Because the woman had come through insects and darkness and a multi-faction battlefield to find me specifically, and the second thing Menzoberranzan teaches you — after terrain — is: *know who holds the debt before you decide whether to collect it*. Someone had paid for this extraction. Someone wanted me alive enough to send Kaelira into this cavern. That information was worth more than another round of magic missiles into a target I was already watching my companions handle.

I followed her.

The T'sarran warriors, still bickering at the swarm's edge about the absence of Dispel Magic, watched me go. I heard fragments — *easy kill, you said* — and the particular silence of a mage who knows they've made a planning error and is deciding whether to admit it. Their retreat threat, when it came, had the hollowness of an announcement no one in the room was required to believe.

"We will meet again," said someone with the rank to say it. "Enjoy your victory for the moment."

Grygum would remember them. He keeps notes.

Behind me, Asha Vandree's sharp voice cut across the cavern's noise — she had spotted the Vandree extraction team and arrived, loudly, at the wrong conclusion. "Who are you? You must be traitors! Traitors to Lolth!"

I did not pause. I did not explain. I turned back just long enough to deliver the fiction in the tone of someone clarifying an obvious misunderstanding: "No, these are friends of Lolf, and they're here to help."

*Lolf.* The slight mispronunciation is always intentional. It suggests dialect, suggests sincerity, suggests someone who believes hard enough that they say the name slightly wrong. Asha heard what she needed to hear, which was confirmation, not information.

The con never stops. You just roll the dice and move.

And then Grygum found the angle.

He channeled everything into a single concentrated beam of radiant light — the kind of holy energy that the Bahamut-faithful direct with the seriousness of a man who believes, genuinely believes, that light and corruption are in opposition and that the light is supposed to win. It struck Ilvara. Struck her cleanly, at exactly the threshold her corrupted body could not absorb.

She did not fall.

She *detonated*.

The poisonous spore cloud billowed outward through the cavern — a killing stroke that was also, generously, a parting gift to everyone nearby, which included Zalthir and Thorin. Combat is never tidy. Grygum's first response, in the settling spores, was the deeply practical: *If people are in need of healing, we can do that this next round.*

The man ends a nemesis and immediately begins triage. I have genuine admiration for that. Admiration I would frame as tactical respect, if pressed.

Asha Vandree dropped to her knees in the settling cloud. Her voice came low, then building — prayers to Lolth, rapid and fervent, the benediction of someone who had witnessed something she would interpret as holy intervention. She was not wrong that something significant had happened. She had the cause and the agent entirely incorrect, but the significance read true.

The cavern went quiet.

Not silent — the insects still hummed, the spores still drifted — but the particular chaos of a battle where everyone is trying to kill everyone else gave way to the quieter chaos of aftermath, where everyone is trying to figure out what comes next.

Kaelira was next to me. The contract was still running. Somewhere in the remaining questions was whoever had paid for my extraction, and what exactly they expected in return.

I had not, in the formal sense, agreed to any of that yet.

But I was alive. Ilvara was a cloud.

For this particular moment, I was prepared to call that sufficient.
