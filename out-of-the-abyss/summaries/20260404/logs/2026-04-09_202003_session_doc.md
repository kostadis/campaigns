## Section — Grygum — The Fall of Ilvara

The bolt left my hand before the doubt could follow it.

That is the strange thing about Guiding Bolt — it is not subtle. You are not sneaking anything past the universe when you call down a column of radiant light in an enclosed cavern. You are making a statement. You are saying: *I see this thing, and Bahamut sees this thing, and we have agreed it should not exist.* I have cast it enough times to know what it costs. Not in effort, exactly. More in commitment. You cannot half-mean a Guiding Bolt.

I had not half-meant this one.

The light caught Ilvara full in the chest. I saw it land — that specific way radiant damage lands on something that has been touched by demonic corruption, less like a blow and more like an argument being settled. She had been Zuggtmoy's creature for long enough that the holy light was not just painful. It was *wrong* to her in a way that went deeper than flesh. I had time to notice this the way you notice a door swinging shut. And then she detonated.

*Poisonous spores.*

The cloud that had been Ilvara Mizzrym expanded outward in a perfect, indifferent sphere. I had been standing back — I make a habit of this, which my companions find cowardly and I find *sensible* — and the cloud reached Zalthir and Thorin before they could put meaningful distance between themselves and the explosion. I watched them absorb it. They endured. Probably because they are made of something difficult to discourage.

"Not ideal," I said, to no one in particular. And then, because the question was practical and the situation was still evolving: "How big is the cloud?"

I was already moving laterally, taking inventory. The field had gone from chaotic to merely strange. Ilvara was gone — which was the objective, and which should have felt more decisive than it did. Instead I was cataloguing: Zalthir upright, Thorin upright, Daz still breathing somewhere in the middle distance after his very persuasive argument to Nym Duskryn that he would be participating in this fight whether or not it was convenient for her contract. The House T'sarran warriors, who had spent most of the engagement arguing about whether their mage should have brought Dispel Magic (*he should have brought Dispel Magic*), were already retreating. Their final contribution to the encounter was a threat: *We will meet again. Enjoy your victory for the moment.* Hollow. I filed it anyway. Notes are notes.

"I'm glad I was standing back," I said.

I meant it both practically and theologically. There is a version of that engagement where I close to melee range and catch the full cloud when she goes. There is a version where Bahamut's protection on me means I would have survived it. I prefer not to test theories like that mid-encounter. *A hoard is always valuable; so is being alive to spend it.*

Thorin, somewhere in the chaos, offered his assessment of the political situation that had unfolded around us in the past several minutes — the lightning bolt Nym had put through Jorlan, the coin-flip restraint around Jorlan charging Ilvara, Asha Vandree dropping to her knees the instant the spores cleared and beginning to pray in beatitudes to Lolth, the retreating T'sarran faction, the Vandree extraction team having opinions about Daz's survival prospects that Daz did not share.

"Nobody knows what the hell's going on," Thorin said.

He was not wrong. But I had been taking notes. That is the best I can say for myself, most days: I had been taking notes.

I thought briefly about Stroud — not the whole story, just the image of it. Protanther, the ancient gold dragon, four hundred years of memorized chess positions, absolute certainty about every possible move. Stroud sitting down across from him, no idea how to play. Rolling a natural twenty. The dragon's face. I find the story useful in exactly these moments: right after the plans dissolve, right after the encounter reshapes itself into something I had not prepared for, right after I notice myself about to say *boy, I had so much prepared for this.* The fungus had died too early. The terrain had shifted. Ilvara had exploded rather than collapsing.

And we were still standing.

"Does anybody need any serious healing?" I asked.

Daz had already answered before I could finish the sentence, with the specific, unsentimental self-assessment of a man doing arithmetic about how much more punishment he could absorb before arithmetic stopped mattering. He thought he could take one more round of hits. I noted this. I thought he was probably right, and I thought we were probably not going to need a round of hits, and I kept the healing in reserve and watched Asha Vandree's lips move through her prayers and wondered what exactly she thought she was thanking Lolth for, given what had just happened to the woman Lolth had presumably been backing.

That is the thing about pit one weird religion against another: eventually you have to stand in the aftermath and figure out which god actually won.

I suspected the answer was *us.* Which is not the same as Bahamut, precisely, but it is close enough to make him comfortable, and I find it is generally best not to overcomplicate these things.
