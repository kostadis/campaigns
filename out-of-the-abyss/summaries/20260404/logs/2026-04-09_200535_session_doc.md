## Section — Daz — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The insects were everywhere.

Not metaphorically. Not as a tactical inconvenience to be filed and addressed. Everywhere — in the fungal dark, in the spore-thick air, crawling through the bitter edge of every breath I took. Ilvara's plague had transformed the cavern into something that didn't reward neat thinking, and I had been trying to think neatly about it for what felt like considerably longer than the situation deserved.

I was cataloguing threats. It's what I do when the variables multiply past what any reasonable person would call manageable. You make a list. You assign priority. You decide who in the room doesn't know they've already lost.

The problem was that the room kept adding names.

Five shapes materialised from the shadows at the cavern's edge — Drow, moving with the practiced stillness of people who had been waiting for what they judged to be the right moment. They wore the markings of House T'sarran. I recognised the insignia the way you recognise the shape of a threat you'd rather have seen coming: not with surprise, exactly. With the specific irritation of confirmation.

Zalthir had spotted them at almost the same instant. "Five Dark Elves," he said, with the tone of a man noting a pleasant addition to the afternoon. "That's awesome."

From somewhere in the middle distance, Grygum's voice arrived with impeccable timing: "This is turning into the twelve days of Christmas."

He wasn't wrong. The list now included Ilvara — still standing, still furious, still radiating something that made the air wrong — the fungal servants shambling through the plague zone, Jorlan conducting what could only be described as a personal grievance at volume, Asha Vandree praying with the focused intensity of someone converting chaos into theology in real time, and now five House T'sarran warriors who had emerged precisely into the insect plague they'd been hiding to avoid.

I noted this. I found it instructive.

The T'sarran crossbowmen raised their weapons. I watched the bolts leave the strings and watched them disappear into the swarm — the insects absorbing the attack with the blind indifference of something that has no concept of being used as a shield. Every bolt went wide. The plague had done that, at least. My enemy's enemies were having a terrible time, and I had contributed nothing to it. I decided to consider this a small dividend.

Then Asha Vandree caught sight of them.

She was fast. I'll give her that. Before the T'sarran warriors had finished shaking insects out of their hair, she had already arrived at a conclusion: "Who are you? You must be traitors! Traitors to Lolth!"

Here is the thing about elaborate deceptions: they require maintenance. The Lolth-convert fiction I had been running with Asha was a structure I had built carefully over time, assembled from small consistent choices and the willingness to let her reach her own conclusions. I had no intention of watching it collapse because a competing faction of Drow arrived at an inopportune moment.

I didn't pause. There was no dramatic intake of breath, no visible calculation. The con never announces itself.

"No," I said, "these are friends of Lolf, and they're here to help."

The lie was clean. It had the correct shape — confident, mildly corrective, the tone of someone clarifying a small misunderstanding rather than inventing an alternate reality. Whether Asha believed it entirely was a separate question. What mattered was that I had moved first, had defined the frame before she could, and the T'sarran warriors were too busy being eaten by insects to contradict me convincingly.

The city would have called what I'd just done *instinct*. The city would have meant it as a compliment.

Then Kaelira found me.

I had not previously registered her as part of this particular configuration of problems. She came through the swarm — braved it, actually, which told me more about her professional commitment than anything she'd said yet — and she had the expression of someone who has been paid to do a job that has become significantly more complicated than advertised.

"Come with me if you want to live, you idiot."

I respected the directness. I did not comply.

There was a specific thing happening in this cavern that I was not prepared to step away from — not because I was being theatrical about it, but because the arithmetic was obvious. The Ember Vanguard were here. They were fighting Ilvara, who had taken us in chains, who had treated people I had come to consider something like companions as property to be processed. Grygum was here. Zalthir was here. Thorin was here, doing whatever Thorin does when he decides something needs to be hit, which is usually to find the thing that most needs hitting and then hit it with Dawnbringer until the argument concludes.

Leaving was not a proposal I was prepared to entertain.

Kaelira looked at me with the patience of a person who has met difficult cargo before.

"If you don't want to live, I'll still take you," she said, "but could you — it would be much easier if you'd just come."

That was when Nym arrived. Nym Duskryn, who had apparently been dispatched alongside Kaelira as part of whatever contract had my name on it, approached with the expression of a person for whom everyone in the immediate vicinity was, at best, a temporary disappointment.

"Can you please keep your head down and out of combat?" she said. "We only get paid if you live."

And there it was. The framing that assumed I was a package, a delivery, a thing that required protecting rather than a person who was currently in the middle of a fight that mattered to him.

"I am not going to leave," I said. I was not raising my voice. There was no point raising your voice. "Everyone here — to fight for me without me also participating. I don't give a damn if you get paid. The best way for you to get paid, then, is to continue the fight and prevent all of us from dying."

There was a pause. Nym turned to Kaelira with the air of someone filing a complaint with middle management.

"Can I kill your brother?"

Kaelira considered this with the unhurried calm of a professional who has had this conversation before, in some form, about various difficult people.

"He was always the dumber of the ones," she said, "and if I understood correctly, he's covered in fungus?"

"Yes."

"Very well."

I noted, distantly, that I had just been assessed by my own rescuers, found categorically inconvenient, and provisionally condemned by one of them while the other offered a qualified endorsement. This was perhaps not the evaluation I would have chosen. But there was something in it — Kaelira's tone, Nym's disgust, the sheer professional weariness of both of them — that clarified the situation usefully.

They were here because someone had paid for me to survive. That was a transaction. My participation in this fight was something else entirely.

Milo Goodbarrel had a word for the impulse that makes you stay when leaving would be safer. He called it loyalty, and he described it as a force multiplier — the thing that makes a group of people more than the sum of their individual calculations. I had always assumed he was rationalizing. Standing in the middle of Ilvara's insect plague, covered in fungus, while two professional Drow operatives debated my fate and the T'sarran crossbowmen picked insects out of their teeth, I found myself arriving, privately and without great enthusiasm, at something like agreement.

The halfling, annoyingly, was not wrong.

I followed Kaelira. But I did not stop watching the fight.
