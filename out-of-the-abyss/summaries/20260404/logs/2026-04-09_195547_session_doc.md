## Section — Daz — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The insect swarm had been Ilvara's spell, and it had eaten the entrance to the cavern whole.

I registered this fact the way I register most tactical information — as a variable to be accounted for, then set aside. The bloom of biting chitin hung in the air like a living wall, and somewhere inside it Grygum was enduring what I was also enduring, which was the very specific sensation of a thousand small mouths finding purchase through my robes. You acclimate. Or you tell yourself you do.

What I had not accounted for was Jorlan.

He broke from the fighting with the speed of someone who had been waiting for exactly this moment — rage and old love in equal measure, which is a combination that tends to cut through tactical analysis like a blade through parchment. Grygum watched him go. I watched him go. He was screaming something I could parse as a name. *Dovara.* An old name, probably. The kind you don't use in front of people.

A wounded man settling an old score, and everyone sensible enough to step aside.

---

Then the five of them stepped out of the dark.

I counted them before they had fully resolved out of the shadows — this is not bravery, it's training; you count, you categorize, you assign. Five drow warriors, House T'sarran by the cut of their gear, moving with the precise formation of people who had been waiting for their moment and had now discovered that their moment came pre-loaded with an insect plague.

They were, to put it plainly, not having a good time.

I could hear them at the edge of audibility — the elite warrior's voice carrying the particular flat tone of someone recalculating blame in real time.

*"I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill."*

The mage had nothing useful to say in response to this, which told me everything I needed to know about the mage. They had come prepared for a precise extraction — remove the wizard, eliminate the problem, collect the reward — and instead they had walked directly into Ilvara's insect bloom and were now receiving the full benefit of her tactical vision. You had to feel something about that. I felt something between amusement and a kind of clinical embarrassment on their behalf.

*"And you don't have a Dispel Magic."*

*"No, I don't have a Dispe—"*

*"So I have to go through this."*

The city would have called this poor preparation. The city would have been right.

---

The ones who had not been reduced to bickering were, it turned out, somewhat closer to me than I had appreciated. A hand closed on my arm.

She was drow, elite warrior, moving with the efficiency of someone who has done a great deal of unpleasant work and found the most direct route through it. She hauled me northeast through the swarm — through the part that was still biting, through the part that was worse — and her expression communicated nothing except the mild professional frustration of someone whose package refused to cooperate.

*"Come with me if you want to live, you idiot."*

I want to be precise about what happened next, because I think precision is owed to the moment. I did not comply. What I did was begin to understand the play I was being inserted into, and then I began to object to it on principled grounds. These are different things.

Her associate — Nym Duskryn, aristocratic disdain worn as naturally as armor — turned to address me with the tone of someone explaining something to a servant.

*"Can you please keep your head down and out of combat? We only get paid if you live."*

Here is what I said. I want it recorded accurately.

*"I am not gonna leave. Everyone here — to fight for me without me also participating. I don't give a damn if you get paid. The best way for you to get paid, then, is to continue the fight and prevent all of us from dying."*

I meant every word of it. I want that noted too.

There was a pause in which Nym Duskryn looked at me with the expression of someone revising their estimate of a situation downward. Then she turned to Kaelira.

*"Can I kill your brother?"*

Kaelira appeared to consider this with genuine thoughtfulness.

*"He was always the dumber of the ones, and if I understood correctly, he's covered in fungus?"*

*"Yes."*

*"Very well."*

I filed this exchange under *professional assessment* and moved on. In Menzoberranzan, you learn early that family is an accounting ledger, not a bond — the House always holds the debt, and you decide later whether to collect. Kaelira had simply done the arithmetic on Jorlan and arrived at an answer. I could not call her wrong.

Milo Goodbarrel would have had something to say about this. Some optimistic framing about chosen bonds outweighing blood. I have always suspected he was rationalizing. Standing there in a cloud of biting insects, being evaluated for expiration by a drow I had never met, I was less certain than usual that he was wrong.

---

Asha was watching the newcomers.

I saw her face before I heard what she was about to say, and the calculation was immediate and automatic — she was going to call them traitors, she was going to call it out loud, and five already-irritated T'sarran warriors were going to hear it, and the entire improvised architecture of this situation was going to collapse. Asha had the religious conviction of someone who had been told exactly what the world looked like, and any deviation from that description was heresy rather than information.

I spoke before she did.

*"No, these are friends of Lolf, and they're here to help."*

The con never stops. You just roll the dice and see if the architecture holds.

It held. Barely, probably. Enough to matter.

---

The crossbow bolts came next — a few of them, loosed from the T'sarran formation directly at me. The insects took them. I want to be clear that this was not a skill on my part; the same cloud that had been grinding on my patience and my skin for what felt like a geological age turned out to be moderately effective cover, and the bolts scattered into it without finding purchase. I noted this with the private acknowledgment that sometimes the thing that's hurting you is also the thing keeping you alive, which is a principle Menzoberranzan taught with some thoroughness.

Kaelira had not let go of my arm.

*"If you don't want to live, I'll still take you, but could you… it would be much easier if you'd just come."*

The word *idiot* was implied in the second sentence even without being spoken. The exhausted professionalism of her. The complete absence of any illusion about what this operation had become.

I looked at the battlefield — Ilvara still standing, Jorlan circling, the T'sarran mage without Dispel Magic, Asha in full religious fervor, Grygum somewhere in the insect bloom doing what Grygum does, which is endure — and I made a tactical assessment.

I followed Kaelira.

Not because she had asked. Not because I intended to stay out of the fight — that particular discussion was not resolved, only postponed. But because the center of the battlefield had fractured into four separate arguments happening simultaneously, and the person dragging me northeast was, for the moment, moving in the direction that gave me the best angle on all of them.

The con never stops. You just find the next position.
