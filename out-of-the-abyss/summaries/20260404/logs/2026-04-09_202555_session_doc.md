## Section — Grygum — The Fall of Ilvara

The bolt left my hand like a prayer I meant.

That's the honest way to say it. Guiding Bolt — the textbook says it calls down radiant energy from the divine. My version always looks like someone lit a signal flare inside a cathedral. I don't know if that's Bahamut's doing or just the way I visualize it. I stopped trying to separate those things a while ago. What matters is that it flew true, and it hit Ilvara Mizzrym square in the chest, and Ilvara Mizzrym, Zuggtmoy-corrupted high priestess of House Mizzrym, longest-standing nemesis of the Ember Vanguard, exploded.

That is not a metaphor.

Let me back up, because the chaos that preceded that moment deserves its own account.

Thorin had destroyed the bridge. I want to be clear that I did not know he was going to do that, and I want to be equally clear that it was exactly the right call. The structural integrity of that span had been bothering me since we first crossed it — I took notes — and Thorin apparently reached the same conclusion through the more direct method of hitting it until it stopped existing. Ilvara and Jorlan went down. I watched them fall, and I held my breath, because people with that much combat experience do not die from falls that civilized. And indeed, they did not. Quick reflexes, both of them. Landed hard but landed functional. Drow are annoyingly athletic.

What happened next was the kind of thing I file under "I'm amused by the drama, but I think I'm still going to go ahead and attack them." Jorlan, newly on the ground floor, locked eyes on Ilvara — wounded, exposed, suddenly mortal-looking — and screamed something I can only describe as the sound a man makes when years of humiliation finally find a target. "Dovara." And he ran at her.

Nym Duskryn, who had spent this entire fight maintaining the studied aristocratic contempt of someone who expected a cleaner assignment, watched Jorlan charge his former mistress. Then she raised a hand and sent a bolt of lightning through him. Not at Ilvara. At Jorlan. Duskryn-on-Duskryn. I filed that under "interesting" and kept moving.

That was when I took stock. Old habit from training: before committing to offense, check the room. I called out across the noise — "Does anybody need any serious healing?" — because if Zalthir had been reduced to single digits while I was busy preparing a spell I wasn't going to get to use, that was information I needed before I pointed radiant energy at anything.

Daz called back, "I've got 18 hit points left of 44. I think I can take one more round of hits."

Classic Daz. Eighteen out of forty-four, and his read of the situation is "one more round." There's a version of faith that works like that — not certainty, just the calculus of acceptable risk and the refusal to stop moving. I respect it. I do not share it, but I respect it.

I was standing back. That was intentional. It always is. The tactical position of a cleric who has learned from hard experience is: close enough to reach people who are falling, far enough that you are not the person falling. I had the angle. I had the opening. Ilvara was vulnerable to radiant damage — I had taken notes on that too, somewhere between Blingdenstone and here — and she was on the ground, and Zalthir and Thorin had made her bleed.

I thought, briefly, about what I was about to do to another person. Even a person who had enslaved us, who had handed us to the Underdark, who had allowed Zuggtmoy's corruption to sink into her bones until she was more spore than soul. I thought about it for approximately half a second, which is the appropriate amount of time to spend on that thought in the middle of a fight that's been going on for longer than most meals. Then I cast Guiding Bolt.

She was particularly vulnerable to radiant damage. The GM told me this after. I knew it before — that's why I chose the spell — but there is a difference between knowing something theoretically and watching it happen.

She detonated. That's the only word. Ilvara Mizzrym ceased to be a drow priestess and became a cloud of poisonous spores. A large cloud. Moving outward in all directions, including toward Zalthir and Thorin, who had been in the middle distance and were now inside something that could kill them.

My first reaction was not theological. My first reaction was: "Not ideal. How big is the cloud?"

Because Zalthir and Thorin were in it, and I needed to know whether I was about to spend the next several rounds casting Lesser Restoration, or whether there was a faster problem to solve. Pragmatic. It's not a lack of faith; it's paying attention to the actual situation rather than the symbolic shape of victory. She was dead. That was good. My friends were in her explosion. That was the new problem.

I was glad I was standing back. I said that out loud, actually, which probably sounded callous but was just true. The radius of a poisonous spore cloud is not a mystery I wanted to solve from the inside.

They endured it. Both of them. Zalthir and Thorin pulled through the toxic aftermath by sheer stubbornness, which is a resource those two have never run short on.

What came after was the kind of scene you watch with one part of your attention while the rest of you is still running triage. The remaining House T'sarran warrior — the one who had been bickering with their mage in the tunnel about Dispel Magic and easy kills and who was supposed to handle what — looked at Ilvara's spore cloud, looked at us, made the only sensible assessment available to them, and retreated. With exactly as much dignity as you'd expect from someone who had just watched their entire plan collapse.

"We will meet again," they said. "Enjoy your victory for the moment."

I am taking notes on that too. The Matron Mother of House T'sarran will hear about this, apparently. I believe them. I also believe that the Matron Mother of House T'sarran will hear that her people arrived at Velkynvelve underprepared, got caught in an Insect Plague, bickered about spell slots, and retreated from a party that had already been fighting for thirty minutes. I am curious what notes she takes.

And then there was Asha Vandree. Who had watched all of this — the fall, the lightning, the Guiding Bolt, the detonation, the spores — and who found, in the settling dust and the poisonous aftermath, something that looked like religious revelation. She dropped to her knees. She began to pray. Beatitudes to Lolth, rising in cadence, the fervor of a priestess who has just witnessed something she can frame as holy.

"The evil!" she said. "The undead!"

Thorin, who had been navigating this entire fight from the tactical disadvantage of being the most straightforwardly violent person in a room full of drow political maneuvering, looked around at the praying priestess, the dispersing spores, the retreating T'sarran warrior, and said, with the exhausted certainty of someone who has been watching a tennis match while trying to find a clear swing:

"Nobody knows what the hell's going on."

I thought about the chess game. I always think about the chess game at moments like this — Stroud sitting down across from Protanther, four hundred years of preparation on one side of the board and a natural 20 on the other. The combat is over. The position is won. And nobody, including the person who delivered the killing blow, could have told you exactly how it got here.

Ilvara is dead. Guiding Bolt. Radiant. Final.

I took notes.
