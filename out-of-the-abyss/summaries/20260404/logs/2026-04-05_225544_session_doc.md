## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The tentacles were the right call.

I knew that before I crossed the battlefield. I knew it the way you know the shape of a tunnel before your eyes adjust — not sight exactly, more like the absence of alternatives. I can only melee. She can teleport. Last time, I closed the distance and she vanished the moment I connected, used some legendary action to blink twenty feet sideways while I stood there holding empty air.

So. Second try. Burn her reaction. Make her move. Even if I only get one strike before she disappears again, I am forcing her to spend something she only has once. That is not nothing.

*You run, run, run,* the GM said, *and then slaps.*

"Pretty much."

I did not wait to be told twice.

---

She was floating above the heart fungus — that enormous, grotesque, pulsing thing that Thorin would shortly ruin in a single turn, which I will acknowledge was impressive — and I came across the battlefield on dark tendrils, the Eldritch Claw Tattoo reaching ahead of me like a thought I hadn't finished yet. Fifteen feet of reach. I had not fully appreciated that until the GM said it aloud.

The grapple landed. "16 damage, and she's grappled."

That is the moment I flew her forty feet straight up.

I don't know that I had a clear plan for what came next. The plan was: get her off the ground, get her away from whatever protection the sphere was providing, get her somewhere she couldn't use the terrain against us. The rest presented itself naturally. She was suspended in the air, held in a grip she could not break, and my hands were free.

"Now that I'm holding her, I will punch her again," I said. "Because why not?"

The first punch landed clean. Critical. The second one also landed clean. Also critical. Two critical strikes at altitude, delivered to a creature who could not dodge, could not retreat, could not do anything except exist in my grip and absorb the damage. Forty-four combined damage, give or take. I stopped counting at some point.

Then I let go.

She fell forty feet.

I watched her fall the way you watch a stone drop into deep water — with the quiet satisfaction of a problem becoming someone else's problem, specifically the problem of the stone floor.

---

There was a class for what came next. I'm nearly certain of it. Brother Quellin — or possibly Brother Tharad, the one with the very small hands who always smelled faintly of burned wood — taught something about fear-breaking. Resisting magical compulsion. I remember the quality of the afternoon light coming through the high windows and thinking about something else entirely. I remember wanting to be on the sparring floor.

I do not remember what they said.

"Roll a DC-14 Wisdom saving throw."

"Well, that didn't work."

She was broken and bleeding on the stone forty feet below me, and she reached up anyway — not with her hands, with something older and darker than her hands — and poured supernatural terror directly into my mind. Not fear of death. Not fear of pain. Fear of *her*. Fear that annihilated the possibility of approach, that made thirty feet of empty air between us feel like the correct and necessary distance, that made the idea of closing that distance feel like walking into something that would unmake me.

The frightened condition is mechanical. I know that. It is a status effect with a duration and a saving throw. 

And yet.

"I mean, the spirit seems to be that I should do nothing to become closer to her. I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right?"

I said this to the table. To myself, mostly.

"Alright, fine."

The discipline is self-administered. That's the part people don't explain well when they describe what a monastery produces. It's not that you follow the rules because someone is watching. It's that you apply the rule against your own interest because the rule is correct, and you know it's correct, and flouting it because it's inconvenient would mean you hadn't actually learned anything. She frightened me. The frightened condition says I cannot approach her. I cannot approach her. That's the thing.

I stayed at the ceiling. Which is where the heart fungus found me.

"So you take 48 plus 4 of bludgeoning damage, and 2d6 of poisoning damage."

"I deflected 12 of that bludgeoning damage."

The deflection is something. Not enough. I absorbed the rest the way you absorb bad weather — with the resigned recognition that the weather does not care what you think of it. I had made Ilvara bleed. She had made me afraid and planted me at the ceiling like a moth on a board while the fight continued forty feet below without me.

I watched the fungal minions die one by one. I watched Thorin's Dawnbringer take apart the heart fungus in a way that made the GM briefly mourn his own encounter design. I watched Grygum's Guiding Bolt eventually solve the Ilvara problem in the most final way available.

"Yeah," I said to no one in particular. "How do I not be frightened?"

"You wait a turn."

So I waited.
