## Section — Grygum — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The cavern had already stopped being manageable before Jorlan moved. I was at the back, watching the insect plague churn near the entrance like a living fog of mandibles and noise, tracking Glabbagool's slow, desperate drift away from the worst of it. Our friend — and I mean that genuinely, the ooze had *earned* that — was catching the edge of Ilvara's conjured swarm with every inch of progress toward safety. There was nothing I could do about that at distance. I noted it the way I note everything: filed, insufficient, next.

Jorlan screamed "Dovara!" and ran.

Something in my chest decided very quickly not to be in the middle of that. I don't know what you call what those two had — whatever word the Drow use for a relationship conducted entirely in spite and survival — but it was not mine to interrupt. Bahamut had not sent me here to adjudicate someone else's longing. *I'll follow up if necessary.* That was the whole of my strategic position on the matter. I held the line at the back of the cavern and watched a man sprint toward his former tormentor with a weapon and something very much like grief, and I let him.

The chaos was already past the point where anyone was going to stay clean.

---

I tracked Ilvara when she steadied herself. She had survived the fall — forty feet of Zalthir's particular brand of hospitality — and she was still moving, which meant she was still a problem. Holy light gathered at my fingertips. I have done this enough times to know when it's ready: there's a warmth to it, a weight, the sense of something larger than me briefly lending its attention to a specific point in space.

I released it toward Ilvara.

The light scattered off nothing. Harmless into the stone. No mark, no impact, barely a trace.

"Oh, well," I said to no one in particular. "That's a shame."

And it was. But I've learned to close those files quickly.

---

Five Drow stepped out of the shadows directly into the insect bloom.

I heard Zalthir count them. Five. The number was almost impressive.

"This is turning into the 12 days of Christmas," I said.

The timing could not have been worse for them. They had clearly been waiting — patient, hidden, exactly where you don't want to wait in a cavern where Ilvara has just conjured a plague of insects that fills the air with poison and doesn't much discriminate. I had a brief, genuine feeling of sympathy for whoever had given those soldiers their orders. They had been told: *wait, stay hidden, strike when ready.* And now they were stepping into a cloud of chittering death wearing their dress armor.

How long had they been in there, waiting? Long enough to be completely wrong about how this would go.

"It's tough being a minion," I said. Because it is. I've watched enough of them discover that truth too late.

The House T'sarran mage fired a lightning bolt blindly into the dark and found nothing with it at all. The elite warrior beside him, already swearing, turned and said something about an easy kill. About Dispel Magic. The mage had no Dispel Magic. The mage had never had Dispel Magic. This was apparently new information to the elite warrior, delivered at the worst possible moment, through a face full of poisonous gas.

I watched the elite warrior weigh all available options — which were few — and step into the swarm anyway, because the alternative was admitting this had all been a catastrophic miscalculation from the start.

That's the thing about careful plans. They have blind spots. Even the ones that have been sitting in darkness for longer than they should have.

---

Thorin hit the bridge.

The strike sent Ilvara and Jorlan down together, and Nym Duskryn's lightning found Jorlan an instant later, and for one breath the battlefield rearranged itself into something almost legible. Wounded everywhere. Spores still drifting. Glabbagool still trying to get clear. The insect plague working its slow, indiscriminate damage on anyone unlucky enough to be standing in the wrong place.

"If people are in need of healing," I said, "we can do that this next round."

The offer went unanswered immediately. That's how it goes. You put it on the table and you wait for someone to either take it or prove they don't need it by continuing to stand up. I watched the cavern. I tracked the wounded. I did not presume.

---

When the opening came, I did not hesitate.

There's a precision to a well-aimed guiding bolt — not just aim, but intention. You have to mean it. You have to offer it up with something honest behind the light, because Bahamut, in my experience, doesn't respond well to mechanical gestures. He responds to you actually meaning the thing. I am uncertain about a great deal of theology, but I am fairly certain about that.

I meant this one.

The bolt struck Ilvara full and clean, and what happened next was not a death so much as a detonation. The corruption in her — Zuggtmoy's mark, years of it, deep and catastrophic — came apart all at once in a vast cloud of poisonous spores that billowed outward through the cavern like an exhaled breath. It caught Zalthir. It caught Thorin. It punished them for being close to the thing I had just destroyed.

Across the cavern, Asha Vandree's knees hit the stone and her voice dropped into the murmur of prayer.

I looked at the cloud. At my companions coughing in its radius. At the slow, dissipating outline of what had been our longest-standing enemy.

"Not ideal," I said. "How big is the cloud?"
