## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The memory of the first attempt sat behind every decision I made going into this room.

Last time, I had her. The tentacles closed, I had the grip, and then — legendary action — she simply wasn't there anymore. One moment of restraint she'd been saving, and all of it evaporated. I'd run the failure through my mind a hundred times since, looking for the version where I hold on. There wasn't one. She'd had the out prepared and she'd used it. Fine. So this time I had to make the cost of teleporting higher than the cost of staying.

I said it to the group during the approach: "In making her teleport, I am preventing her from doing something more dangerous, potentially." Which sounds almost philosophical, but wasn't. It was just arithmetic. She gets one legendary action. If she burns it to escape my grip, she doesn't have it for something worse. You spend her resource. You do it with your body, because that is what monks have. You run, run, run, and then you slap.

There was a class on this. I'm nearly certain there was — Brother Vetharn, or possibly the tall one whose name I can't recover, who smelled of lamp oil and moved like he was always slightly cold. Principles of Engagement. Expenditure of Opponent Resources. Something like that. I remember the light in that room. I remember not caring. I do not remember what was said, which is becoming a pattern in my life with a very clear cause.

I launched from the high ground and landed on Ilvara before she had finished deciding what to do next.

The Eldritch Tentacles are quiet. That's the thing about them that most opponents don't expect. No sound, no warning, just force closing around limbs and locking everything in place. I watched the understanding arrive in her expression — the calculation of her remaining options, the narrowing — and I had already thrown the first punch before she finished the calculation.

"Now that I'm holding her, I will punch her again. Because why not?"

The first hit was a critical. The second was a critical. I've trained for twenty years to put two strikes in the same moment with that kind of weight behind them, and most of the time it doesn't look like anything — the discipline is invisible, which is the point — but every so often the numbers land on top of each other and something that should require philosophy becomes briefly very simple. Thirty-four damage between two blows. The table was erupting around me. Inside the fight there was only the grip and the next point of contact and the one after that.

Then I let go of the ceiling and dropped her.

Forty feet of stone below. She hit it hard, and I came down after, and for one moment the entire fight narrowed to the distance between us on the cave floor. I had done everything right. The grip. The crits. The expenditure of her legendary action. Everything the plan had called for.

Then she looked at me.

I don't know exactly what she did. Something with fear — true fear, the kind the monastery had a word for and I had filed away as theoretical. It arrived like a cold hand reaching into the chest cavity and simply closing. I couldn't move toward her. The idea of moving toward her was suddenly something my body refused to understand.

"Well. That didn't work."

The irony — and I am fully capable of appreciating the irony — is that the fear worked precisely because I had done everything correctly. The grapple. The crits. The fall. She was afraid of *me*, and so she used the one tool that turned my own success into a cage. The better the assault, the more she needed me stopped. I had never been frightened by an enemy before. The sensation was extraordinary and I hated it.

I stood there, frozen at the edge of my own attack radius, trying to think through the geometry of this. I couldn't approach her. But she hadn't said anything about the direction I was facing. Hadn't said anything about whether I could reach the fungal servants still moving through the room. I began working through what remained available. Not rage — I want to be clear about that. The Flumph was correct: there is no noise. Just the map of the situation, redrawn for the new constraint.

I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right? Alright, fine.

The Big Heart Fungus chose that moment to swing at me. It was, I would later discover, fifteen feet tall, which I would have known earlier if I had looked at it properly when we entered the room. I had looked at Ilvara. I had been doing the arithmetic. The fungus limb connected with the section of ceiling where I was standing and delivered more force than I cared to document, and I caught twelve points of it with my forearm because I had seen it coming half a second before it arrived and that half second was the whole difference between acceptable damage and the other kind.

The fungal minions were dying. The insect plague was tearing through the tunnel entrance where the T'sarran faction had been standing, and I could hear them blaming each other about it, which I filed away as not my problem. The minions had thirteen hit points each, which is the kind of number that doesn't survive contact with what we had assembled in this room.

I stood apart from all of it. Waiting for the end of my turn. Waiting for the fear to pass — or for Grygum to remember he had lesser restoration, which felt like the faster path — counting the distance to Ilvara, watching the fight I had opened from the outside of it.

"Yeah," I said, to no one in particular. "How do I not be frightened."

It wasn't a question. I already knew the answer. You wait. You apply the rule against your own interest. You don't look for the loophole that lets you pretend the condition doesn't apply. You wait, and you watch, and you plan the next approach, and when the moment comes back around you don't waste it.

The moment would come back around. It always does.
