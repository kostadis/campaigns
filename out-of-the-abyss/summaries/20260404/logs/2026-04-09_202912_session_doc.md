## Section — Zalthir — Ilvara's Desperate Stand

The frightened condition is an interesting thing when you are a monk.

There was a class for it, I'm fairly certain. Brother Quellin — or possibly Brother Verath, the one who always had chalk dust on his sleeves — taught something about fear magic in the third year. I remember the light through the window was very good that afternoon. I do not remember anything else.

What I remember about the Ilvara fight, in the immediate moment before the condition took hold, is that everything had been going well. Perfectly, even. I had her in my grip. I had hit her twice, hard, the way you hit something you need to stop permanently, and the numbers in my mind — distance, angle, force — had all resolved cleanly. Then the magic landed, and the numbers stopped mattering, because my legs had developed their own opinions about proximity to Ilvara Mizzrym.

*I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right? Alright, fine.*

Not resentment, exactly. The condition says what it says about the spirit of the thing, and the spirit of the thing is that I should not be closer to her. I applied this against myself without needing anyone to tell me. The monastery trained me to be honest about constraints, even inconvenient ones. Especially inconvenient ones. I filed the frustration away and looked for something else to do.

This is what I was doing — finding something else to do — when Ilvara turned her attention to the ground.

I watched her let go of whatever she had been maintaining. You can see it, if you're watching for it, the small internal release of concentration, the way a spellcaster's shoulders settle differently when they stop holding a working. She dropped it deliberately. Which meant she was building toward something worse.

The fungal bloom came up around Jorlan like the floor itself had opinions. Difficult terrain. Thorin had been complaining, since approximately the beginning of this fight, about the layout of the cavern, the sprawling distances, the way every engagement required sprinting to reach it.

"All I know is this map sucks, because I feel like I'm running around trying to get to things."

He wasn't wrong. I had watched him cover the same ground three times, arriving slightly after the moment he was needed, sword already raised for something that had moved. "I'm running around just trying to catch up with everybody." This from a dwarf who had already destroyed the Big Heart Fungus in a single pass, which I mention only because he seemed not to have noticed how significant that was, being already frustrated about the geography.

The fungal bloom was Ilvara telling Jorlan to stay where he was. She had other things to do.

She called on Zuggtmoy. You could feel the register shift — this was not Ilvara expending tactical resources. This was Ilvara making a declaration, the way you speak when you're not talking to the people in front of you but to something behind them, something farther back. Promising chaos. Promising mayhem. The corruption that had been growing through her since Velkynvelve made her voice resonate oddly, like sound off wet stone, and I found myself recalibrating what I was looking at. She was not a high priestess fighting a difficult battle. She was a channel.

Then the Flamestrike came down.

Divine fire. Not conjured from ambient heat or ignited material — called down, the way Zuggtmoy's power calls things down, from somewhere else, through her. It hit Daz and Nym Duskryn, and the sound of it was enormous in the enclosed space, and the light was the wrong color, too orange and wrong at the edges, like fire that had traveled too far from where it was supposed to be.

I watched it from the far end of the cavern where my legs had decided I should remain.

There was nothing precise I could do about that. I noted it. I filed it. I looked for my angle.
