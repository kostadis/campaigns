## Section — Grygum — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The battlefield had stopped being a battlefield sometime around the third wave of enemies. Now it was just a disaster accruing interest, and I was standing at the back of it, taking notes.

Zalthir had already done the impossible thing — grappled Ilvara at ceiling height, driven his fists into her twice in the space of a breath, and then dropped her forty feet onto stone. Whatever she'd looked like at the start of this fight, she didn't look like that anymore. She looked like someone who had just fallen forty feet. I had filed this under "favorable development" and was reaching for a guiding bolt when the insect plague bloomed at the cavern entrance like something Ilvara had planted there to spite us on her way down, a roiling chittering cloud that immediately began eating everything in its radius, including Glabbagool.

I watched Glabbagool hit the edge of that swarm and felt my stomach drop. The cube is — well. Thorin invited him. He's ours now, in the way that strays become yours when you keep feeding them. And the insects did not care.

"I don't want to get in the middle of it," I said, watching Jorlan break from the fight and sprint toward the fallen Ilvara, screaming something that sounded like *Dovara*, which I was already filing under "couples issues I cannot help." "But I'll follow up if necessary."

The guiding bolt I had been preparing for Jorlan I redirected toward Ilvara instead, because Ilvara was still the actual problem, Jorlan was a soap opera, and I am a cleric of Bahamut, not a relationship counselor. The light gathered and scattered. Stone. I watched the radiance dissipate against the cavern floor and said, with as much dignity as I could muster: "Oh, well. That's a shame."

Thorin, somewhere to my left in the dark, was doing what Thorin does, which is hit things until they stop being a problem. He'd already bisected the Big Heart Fungus with Dawnbringer — one turn, double radiance vulnerability, devastating efficiency. I had, I will admit, prepared quite a lot for the Big Heart Fungus. I had thoughts. I had a theological framework for dismantling a fungal demonic growth that was a *Zuggtmoy corruption in a contested sacred space*. I had *notes*.

"Boy," I said, to no one in particular, when Thorin's sunblade finished the work and the fungus stopped being a problem. "I had so much prepared for the fungus. Oh well."

The GM — I mean, the situation — did not pause to acknowledge this loss. The situation never does.

Zalthir: "Five drow warriors. Five. That's awesome."

"This," I said, watching them materialize from the cavern shadows directly into the edge of the insect bloom, their timing impeccable in exactly the wrong direction, "is turning into the twelve days of Christmas."

The drow who were not us glanced at each other, and then at the swarm, and then at each other again. They had been hiding in the cavern walls, waiting for what they'd clearly expected to be a cleaner opportunity. They looked — and I say this with professional sympathy — extremely put out.

"How long have they been hiding there?" I asked, more or less to myself. "They might have gotten hit by the plague of insects."

They had. They were. They were very irritated about it, in the manner of elite warriors who had planned an ambush and found themselves instead standing in a poisonous insect cloud while their target fought someone else entirely. Over the sound of the swarm, I could hear the T'sarran elite warrior turning on his mage.

"I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill."

The mage apparently did not have Dispel Magic. The elite warrior made his feelings about this very clear.

"It's tough," I said, "being a minion."

I meant it charitably. I've been a minion. Most people start there. Bahamut does not hand out generalships at birth, and even if he did, you'd still have to survive your first dungeon. The T'sarran contingent had made it this far. They had arrived, on time, to a fight that had already eaten two separate waves of enemies and was still hungry. I felt, if not respect, at least recognition.

Then Nym Duskryn fired a lightning bolt at Jorlan and I returned to more immediate concerns.

The Vandree extraction team — and I use that term because apparently someone was paying them to keep Daz alive, which is either touching or alarming depending on who's writing the check — had materialized in the middle of all this with the specific energy of professionals who had not been adequately briefed. Kaelira moved through the insect cloud to pull Daz northeast, taking the swarm's poison in the face to do it. Nym looked at everything happening around her with the expression of someone who had arrived expecting a job and found a situation.

"If people are in need of healing," I said, reading the room, "we can do that this next round."

The room answered by producing more enemies.

Thorin's bridge strike sent Ilvara and Jorlan crashing down together, which was either justice or irony, probably both. Nym's lightning found Jorlan a moment later. The wounded were scattered. The spores were still coming. Glabbagool was still somewhere in that swarm.

I thought, briefly, of Stroud. Of a dwarf who could not play chess sitting down across from an ancient gold dragon who had been replaying the same game for four hundred years, and winning. I find that story deeply comforting in moments when the terrain has stopped cooperating and the plan has been dead for three rounds. Ilvara had been planning this for years. Probably longer. And here she was, on the cavern floor, having fallen forty feet, leaking light where Dawnbringer's radiance had scorched her, while her ex-lover tried to kill her and her insect cloud ate her allies and her own wounds made her glow.

Four hundred years of preparation still has a blind spot.

I gathered the light. Everything I had left, pulled into a single concentrated point. Guiding bolt. Aimed at Ilvara, who was radiant-damaged and who had fallen forty feet and who had, nonetheless, remained a problem through every single thing we had done to her. I thought: *this is the one. This is what it's for.*

It was.

The bolt found her. Bahamut's judgment, focused through a half-orc cleric with good notes and no patience left, punched through whatever was still keeping her upright and she detonated — not collapsed, not fell, *detonated* — a cloud of poisonous spores billowing outward through the cavern like her body had been storing them up specifically for this moment, specifically to catch Zalthir and Thorin in the radius, specifically to make sure that the killing blow cost us something.

Ilvara. To the last.

Asha Vandree dropped to her knees in the settling spore-cloud and began murmuring prayers to Lolth into the silence, and I stood at the back of what had been a battlefield and looked at the damage and thought: *Not ideal. How big is the cloud?*
