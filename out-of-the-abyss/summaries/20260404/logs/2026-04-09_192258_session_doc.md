## Section — Thorin — The Heart Fungus and the Drow Betrayal

Jorlan moved like a man who had been waiting his whole life for this exact moment.

"Dovara!"

The word cut through the din of the battle — the insect-roar from above, the wet sound of spores bursting, Zalthir still locked somewhere above us in whatever grip Ilvara's terror had put him in. The scarred drow broke from the melee and bolted, legs churning, heading straight for her. For Ilvara. For the woman who had thrown him away.

I felt the moment land in my chest before I understood it with my head. My ability to strike at creatures leaving my reach — it was right there. Clean. Available. I could have stopped him cold.

But.

"He's disengaging to go attack Ilvara," came the call above the chaos. "He didn't think you could defeat her. Now that she's wounded, he wants to finish it himself."

Zalthir, somewhere in his frozen misery, managed: "The tides have turned."

I stood there for half a breath, which is a long time in a fight. He attacked us first, I thought. That was the honest accounting of it. He came at us because he didn't believe we could win, and now he believed, and now he wanted his own resolution to a story that started long before we arrived in this cave. That's the full picture. That's everything.

My practical mind, bless it, found its way to the real question.

"So, what happens if he kills her and we don't? Does that change anything? Does he get, like, first dibs on booty or something? And by booty, I mean, like —"

The situation had no prepared answer for that angle of drow inheritance law. No one did.

"I mean, like, they used to be Lover's Booty. I mean, like… the goods."

Nothing. No legal precedent forthcoming. Fine.

The dice made the call. Not heroic. Not cold. Just the middle path, the sensible thing. An eight. Let them go. Let the girls fight. That's what the numbers said.

"That makes me sad," I said, because it did, "because I like my attacks of opportunity here."

I watched Jorlan sprint toward the ruined form of the woman who had once owned him, and I thought about the hill elder who used to settle disputes by sitting down, not speaking, and waiting three days for everyone to arrive at the right answer on their own. He was a massive man, that elder. He could have settled things in an instant. He rarely did. He said — and this was the whole of his philosophy — that a rock in the wrong place is just a rock, but a rock in the right place is a wall.

I did not know which one Jorlan was. I let him go.

Then Ilvara, broken and cornered and still somehow furious, reshaped the battlefield around her. A sphere of biting insects erupted at the cave entrance — not a death, but a statement. The chittering swarm filled the passage, found Grygum and Daz and Glabbagool all at once, and the roar of it was something between a scream and a grinding sound. Insects like to eat fungus, someone noted. Nobody laughed.

Glabbagool. The ooze had been sitting out there doing his best to be present, eyes drifting upward, trying to participate. The swarm found him too, and what the swarm did to him was not gentle. He was alive — we checked — but the wanting to belong had cost him something real.

"Is he alive?"

"Yes. Still alive."

Good. Good enough. Hold together, all of us.

I turned my attention downward. Below the walkways, through the difficult terrain of the cavern floor, the Big Heart Fungus pulsed. It was enormous. It was the reason for all of this — the anchor of Zuggtmoy's rot in this place, the thing Ilvara had fed herself into a vision-quest through, which: I know how that goes. I have had that exact experience and I do not recommend it to anyone.

Dawnbringer was already warm in my hand.

But first — a professional confirms before they commit.

"Is there anything I need to know about the fungus in terms of, like… is it resistant to stuff or whatever?"

Funguses are resistant to the usual things that funguses are resistant to. Fine, expected. But doubly vulnerable to radiant damage.

"Oh, that's good to know," said Grygum, and his tone said he was already thinking about what came next.

I was already moving. Dropped down through the difficult terrain — the cavern floor was uneven, stubborn, the kind of ground that punishes anyone in a hurry — but I was not in a hurry exactly. I was purposeful. Dawnbringer blazed in my grip, throwing holy light across the cave walls, and I crossed the distance to the heart fungus with all the ceremony of a dwarf walking to a job that needs doing.

The strikes landed.

Radiant light, clean and merciless, found the thing at its core. I felt the resistance — massive, deep-rooted, old — and then felt it collapse. The heart fungus wilted. Slowly and then all at once, the great architecture of corruption sagged and fell dark.

"I do what I can," I said.

One round. One sword. One anticlimactic wilt, and the elaborate encounter built around this thing folded up like a question that had just been answered.

There was a pause at the edge of things — the kind of pause that fills with mourning, if you're paying attention. Something had been constructed here. Something careful and terrible and full of presence. And then a dwarf with a sunblade walked into it.

I said nothing. I had done what I came down here to do.

The real fight — the one between Jorlan and Ilvara on the cavern floor below, the insects still shrieking at the entrance, Zalthir locked somewhere above in Ilvara's fear — that was still running. I held Dawnbringer steady and did not strike again. Curiosity, or maybe something closer to discipline. I had already let one thing play out. I made that choice twice now: watch, wait, see what the next thing is.

Somewhere above, Grygum released his concentration on the spell he'd been holding together through all of it — the fungus dead, the anchor gone, no more reason to maintain it. The field shifted again. One threat removed, the others still very much present and still very much angry.

Above the noise, Zalthir stood frozen. I could see him up there. The fear on his face was not something I had seen before, and it sat wrong with me in a way I didn't have clean words for.

"Just had him on his back or something," I said, not loudly, almost to myself. "Say, there, there, it'll be…"

It was not elegant comfort. It was the kind of comfort I know how to give, which is to say, the simple, honest, insufficient kind. He was up there shaking against something inside himself that I could not reach or strike, and I was down here with a still-bright sword and the tactical situation evolving around us both.

I stayed where I was. Watched the chaos continue. Waited for what the cave would ask of us next.
