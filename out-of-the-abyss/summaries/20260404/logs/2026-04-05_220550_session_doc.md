## Section — Grygum — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The battlefield had long since stopped being a fight. It was a disaster accruing interest, and I was taking notes from the back.

Jorlan broke first — screaming something about Dovara, which is the kind of name you scream when you are either in love or in ruins, and in his case it appeared to be both simultaneously. He sprinted past us toward the wounded Ilvara, and I watched Thorin's hand twitch toward his weapon and then — miraculously — not follow through. A coin flip, apparently. Eight said let them go. I filed that under "Thorin grows as a person" and returned my attention to the part of the room that was actively trying to kill us.

That part was the insect plague.

Ilvara had cast it near the entrance — deliberately or catastrophically, I was never entirely certain which — and it was doing its work without any further instruction from her. A roaring, chittering wall of mandibles and wings, the kind of thing that doesn't discriminate between enemies and friends and has no opinion whatsoever about Glabbagool. I could see the ooze at the edge of it, pressing forward with the particular bravery of a creature that either doesn't understand danger or understands it completely and doesn't care. Either answer made me love Glabbagool a little more and worry about him considerably.

I positioned myself at the back of the cavern. Tactical. The wide view. I told myself that was why.

What I could see from the wide view: Zalthir had already done something extraordinary to Ilvara — had grappled her from the ceiling with those tentacle tattoos and then dropped her forty feet, which is the kind of opening move that sets a tone — and she had responded by doing something to his mind that put the fear into him, the genuine article, and he was now fighting the shape of that fear as much as he was fighting her. Thorin had found the Big Heart Fungus, which I had, I will admit, been quietly preparing for. I'd had thoughts about it. Theological considerations. A whole approach. Thorin put Dawnbringer through it in one swing. "I do what I can," he said.

Boy, I had so much prepared for the fungus. Oh well.

Ilvara was still up. Still trouble. The holy light was already gathering at my fingertips — guiding bolt, the old reliable, the shot that lands like a searchlight and makes the next strike easier for whoever follows — and I sent it toward her with what I thought was reasonable confidence.

It went wide. Scattered into stone. Returned nothing.

Oh, well. That's a shame.

That's when the five drow stepped out of the shadows.

Zalthir noticed them first. "Five dark elves," he said, in the voice he uses when he's counting things instead of responding to them.

I watched them emerge directly into the edge of the insect bloom. Their timing was the worst I had witnessed in a long career of watching people time things badly, and that career begins in Menzoberranzan, which is not known for its patience.

"This is turning into the 12 days of Christmas," I said.

The GM, to his credit, said he hoped more pleasantly.

"Dark elves," I said.

What I could see that they perhaps could not: the insect swarm was already chewing through their advance, the poison washing over them in waves they had clearly not accounted for in whatever plan had brought them here. The T'sarran elite warrior was already blaming the mage. The mage did not have a Dispel Magic, which the warrior felt strongly about. A lightning bolt went somewhere dark and hit nothing.

"It's tough being a minion," I said, to no one in particular, and I meant it with something close to genuine sympathy. These soldiers had been crouching in shadow long enough to think they had good ambush position. Now they had poison gas and a dressing-down from their superior officer and a front-row view of the Ember Vanguard being more complicated than advertised.

Some of these people had not signed up for any of this.

Thorin's bridge strike sent Ilvara and Jorlan both crashing to the cavern floor in a heap that managed to be both violent and, somehow, narratively satisfying. Nym's lightning found Jorlan a moment later. The wounded were scattered. The spores were still coming. I had a round's worth of breathing room and an entire battlefield's worth of people who might need what I had left.

"If people are in need of healing," I said, "we can do that this next round."

The battle answered that offer, as battles generally do, by immediately making things worse.

But then I had the shot.

Ilvara was down, but not done — still trouble, as I had originally assessed, and the assessment had only deepened in accuracy as the fight wore on. The holy light gathered again, and this time I felt the difference. This time it came from somewhere I don't entirely know how to describe, the place where my training intersects with something I'm genuinely not certain of — Bahamut's attention, or the weight of everything we'd survived to get here, or just the clean focus that sometimes arrives when there is only one thing left to do.

I fired.

The bolt landed. Not wide. Not scattered. It landed with the full authority of radiant light, the kind that Ilvara's corrupted body could not simply absorb, and she did not simply fall.

She detonated.

A massive cloud of poisonous spores billowed outward through the cavern — which was, I noted immediately, not ideal — and caught Zalthir and Thorin in its radius, punishing my two closest friends for the crime of standing near the thing I'd just destroyed. The cloud rolled over them and I had the very particular experience of watching my triumph cost the people I cared about the breath to celebrate it.

Asha Vandree dropped to her knees somewhere to my left and began murmuring into the settling silence. Prayers to Lolth. The beatitudes of a spider goddess, offered up in the wreckage of her high priestess.

"Not ideal," I said, to myself mostly. "How big is the cloud?"
