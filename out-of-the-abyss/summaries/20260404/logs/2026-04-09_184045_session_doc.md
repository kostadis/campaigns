## Section — Daz — The Drow Reinforcements Arrive

The first bolt came out of the darkness before I had fully registered that there was darkness to come out of.

Three drow warriors, stepping from the shadows with the easy confidence of people who had already decided how this was going to go. I remember thinking they looked smug — too poised, too certain of themselves, the posture of professionals who'd been briefed that their target was soft. A mage. A wizard. Someone to be collected, not fought.

The bolt found me anyway.

I am not going to pretend that didn't sting. It stung. The shield snapped up on instinct — the spell already forming in my hand before the pain had finished registering — and I stood there for a moment with the barrier flickering around me, crossbow bolt hanging in the air just short of my face, and thought: *House T'sarran, probably. Someone's paid them well.* In Menzoberranzan they used to say: know who holds the debt before you decide whether to collect it. These people didn't seem to know who held anything.

I felt embarrassed for them, is what I felt.

Then Kaelira found me.

She materialized from the fighting like someone had specifically designed her to be unignorable — a Vandree elite warrior, moving with the kind of focused velocity that separates people who are paid from people who are paid *well*. She grabbed me by the arm and began dragging me northeast through the insect swarm Ilvara had seeded the battlefield with, and she said, without any particular inflection, "Come with me if you want to live, you idiot."

I would like to note, for anyone reading this later, that I was in the middle of assessing the tactical geometry of the encounter. I did not need extracting. I was *thinking*.

She seemed uninterested in that distinction.

"If you don't want to live," she added, still pulling, still moving, her voice carrying the exquisite exhaustion of someone who has dealt with difficult cargo before, "I'll still take you. But it would be much easier if you'd just come."

At the swarm's edge, the T'sarran mage fired his lightning bolt into the dark. It crackled through the cavern, impressive and thorough, and missed me completely. I could hear the T'sarran elite warrior, on the far side of the insect bloom, say something to his mage in a tone that suggested he had been promised this would go differently.

"I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill."

The mage had nothing for him.

"And you don't have a Dispel Magic."

"No, I don't have a Dispel—"

"So I have to go through this. I don't know what else to tell you."

The insect swarm sealed the entrance like a wall of teeth. Ilvara had cast it to punish her enemies, and it was now, coincidentally, doing excellent work on people who had come to cause me specifically a problem. There's a word for that. Milo Goodbarrel would have had a word for that — he called things like this *convenient grace*, the moment when the board rearranges itself in your favor without your having done anything to earn it. I always assumed he was rationalizing. In this instance I will allow that he may have had a point.

What I had, standing at the swarm's edge with the T'sarran forces arguing about spell preparation on the other side, was a clear line of fire into the magical darkness.

"They'll get to see for just a short period of time," I said, mostly to myself, "while they're getting flamed up."

I centered the fireball in the dark and let it go.

The tunnel went orange. The darkness burned away for one brilliant, absolute moment, and in it I could see the drow guards — four of them, the neat professional formation they'd arranged while waiting for their mage to sort out the lightning — and then I could not see them anymore because they were on fire. The mage survived it. Barely. He was still standing when the light guttered out, but the way he was standing said the rest was a formality.

That was when Asha noticed the Vandree warriors who had come in with Kaelira.

She went rigid. Eyes fixed on the unfamiliar drow who had just dragged me out of the line of fire. Her voice, when it came, had the particular timbre of someone finding a heretic at their altar.

"Who are you? You *must* be traitors. Traitors to Lolth!"

I did not pause. The con never stops — that's the only rule. You adapt or you lose the thread.

"No," I said, "these are friends of Lolth, and they're here to help."

Whether she believed me or whether she believed the alternative was worse, I genuinely cannot tell you. She accepted it. That was enough.

It was Nym who found me next. Nym Duskryn — Kaelira's associate, and quite possibly the most comprehensively disdainful person I have encountered in the Underdark, which is saying something. She looked at me with the expression of someone who has been assigned a task beneath her capabilities, and she said:

"Perhaps your benefactor has a good idea."

Then, more directly: "Can you please keep your head down and out of combat? We only get paid if you live."

I want to be clear about what happened inside me when she said that. There was a moment — very brief, very small — where I nearly did the sensible thing. Where the part of me that has been calculating acceptable risk since the first tunnel in Menzoberranzan said: *she's not wrong. You are hurt. You are their objective. Sit down.*

I did not sit down.

"I am not going to leave," I said. "Everyone here is fighting for me without me also participating. I don't give a damn if you get paid. The best way for you to get paid, then, is to continue the fight and prevent all of us from dying."

Nym looked at me for a long moment. Then she turned to Kaelira.

"Can I kill your brother?"

Kaelira considered this with the unhurried calm of someone adjusting a line in a supply manifest.

"He was always the dumber of the ones," she said. "And if I understood correctly, he's covered in fungus?"

"Yes."

"Very well."

Family, among the drow, was subject to review. Apparently current fungal coverage was a relevant factor. I filed that away.

Then Ilvara muttered something about Zuggtmoy — the bride, she said, something about chaos and mayhem, the words that come out of people who have decided to put the problem in someone else's hands. She dropped her earlier spells, raised new growth to slow Jorlan's advance, and then a column of divine fire came down on me and Nym.

Nym, I am satisfied to report, did not enjoy that.

The scorching was significant. Not the worst I've taken — the Underdark has calibrated my sense of what "worst" means — but the kind that reminds you that the margin for error is shrinking and you should probably stop giving speeches.

Zalthir was faster than the math suggested he could be. He'd been frozen by Ilvara's fear — I'd seen it, that particular stillness that has nothing to do with hesitation and everything to do with something older and less reasoned than thought — but he'd worked something out. I watched him calculate the geometry of the thing with an almost academic focus. The condition said he couldn't approach her. Fine. He teleported *away* from her instead, across the battlefield, arriving near the T'sarran mage who had survived my fireball and had made the mistake of still being present.

A monk, he said, when someone asked how.

The mage went down in one strike. That was the end of the T'sarran mage.

The surviving T'sarran warrior looked at the body of his colleague, looked at us, and offered a retreating vow with the hollow conviction of a man who would have to explain this to his matron mother.

"We will meet again," he said. "Enjoy your victory for the moment."

I watched him go and thought: you bickered about Dispel Magic at the edge of an insect swarm. You fired lightning into empty dark. You called for an easy kill and found a fireball. I do not think House T'sarran will be enjoying the debrief.

The cavern was settling. The insect swarm thinning. Asha Vandree had dropped to her knees somewhere behind me, prayers to Lolth coming in fervent, beatific fragments, the voice of someone who has witnessed a miracle and is in a hurry to attribute it correctly. My cover was still holding. The Vandree extraction team now knew I was not a package, which would complicate things in ways I was not currently in a position to calculate.

I was still standing. I had refused extraction, refused to be sidelined, and had put a fireball into a tunnel full of drow who had been *very confident* about how this evening was going to go.

"I'm enjoying it," I said, to no one in particular, surveying the smoke and the settling fungal ash and the scorched tunnel mouth where the T'sarran forces had been. "Lots of circles, lots of things going on. Fireballs, lightning bolts, darkness."

I have found purpose. I am the feared mage of the Ember Vanguard.

The irony is almost invisible.
