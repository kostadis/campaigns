## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The plan had worked once before — or almost worked, which is not the same thing. Last time, I'd closed the distance, gotten my hands on her, and then Ilvara had simply decided to be somewhere else. A legendary action. Not a lair action. I'd called it the wrong thing, and that was fine, the name didn't matter, what mattered was that she'd spent it to escape me and I'd spent nothing to make her burn it.

There was a class for this. I think it was Brother Quellin — or possibly the quiet one with ink-stained hands, I can't be certain — about tactical forcing functions. The idea that an attack doesn't have to succeed to accomplish something. I remembered wanting to be elsewhere. I don't remember anything he said. And yet here I was, forty feet above a corrupted cavern floor, applying the concept I had definitely not learned.

*Second try is the charm, or give up on that whole plan?*

I didn't say it as a question. I said it the way you say something you already know the answer to. I can only melee. The plan is the same plan. The only variable is whether she gets to escape it again.

She did not get to escape it.

The Eldritch Tentacles extended from the tattoo across my arm — fifteen feet of dark, reaching force — and I closed on Ilvara through open air. The tendrils wrapped around her before she could orient toward the threat, hauling me into contact with her, anchoring us both at altitude while the rest of the cavern stretched out below: forty feet of nothing, the heart fungus pulsing on the floor, my companions somewhere in the noise and the dark. None of that mattered. I had my hands on her.

*Now that I'm holding her, I will punch her again. Because why not?*

The first strike was a critical hit. The second was also a critical hit. I am not certain what the correct response to back-to-back critical strikes on a demonic high priestess while suspended forty feet above a cavern floor is, but the table had one, and it involved Thorin making a reference I didn't fully follow and Grygum expressing enthusiasm for punching Nazis, which I filed away as a cultural artifact I would not be investigating further. Fifty-some damage delivered at altitude, Flurry of Blows expressing itself in its purest and most satisfying form. The geometry was perfect. The execution was clean.

I opened my hands and dropped her.

Forty feet. She hit the floor hard. This was, by any measure, a successful execution of a plan.

Then she looked up at me.

Whatever she called up from the depths of Zuggtmoy's corruption, she poured it directly into the space behind my eyes — and something in my chest went cold and very wrong. Not pain. Not a wound. The functional absence of the thing that makes you move toward danger when you need to. My legs understood, with absolute conviction, that I would not be approaching Ilvara. The monk who had just dropped a demonic priestess forty feet could no longer convince his own body to close on her.

*Well. That didn't work.*

Ilvara, broken and furious on the cavern floor, had undone the entire exchange with a single legendary reaction. Everything I'd built in the last thirty seconds — the grapple, the altitude, the drops — she'd answered it by reaching into a part of my training I had apparently not spent enough time on.

There was a class for this too. Resistance to fear. I think it was Brother Tharusk — or possibly someone else entirely, there was more than one monk who tried to teach the subject, I was not listening to any of them. I remember the quality of the light. I remember wanting to be in the training yard instead. I do not remember the technique for when a corrupted drow priestess reaches into your mind and teaches you what terror actually feels like.

*I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right? Alright, fine.*

I said it to no one in particular, working through the spirit of the thing myself. The fear was telling me to stay away. The fear was, technically, tactically correct in this specific moment. I applied the rule against my own interest and worked the problem from where I was: not there, not useful in the way I'd been useful ten seconds ago, waiting.

The heart fungus filled the gap. Its limbs had a reach I had not adequately estimated, and one of them connected at the cavern ceiling where I was floating, having nowhere better to be. The impact was enormous — the kind that doesn't just register as damage but as information, the body urgently updating its understanding of what the current situation costs. I deflected twelve points of it. Twelve out of considerably more than twelve.

"Wow," said Grygum, with the gentle warmth of a man who genuinely enjoys watching the party's most precisely optimized fighter get hit by a fungus.

I held my position at the ceiling. Frightened, absorbing punishment from something I couldn't reach, waiting for the cold grip of supernatural terror to loosen enough to let me think straight and move.

*How do I not be frightened?*

Wait it out. Or someone reaches me with a lesser restoration. Those were the options.

I held. I counted. I watched Ilvara on the floor below, the fight continuing around her, and I waited for the part of me that knew how to walk toward danger to come back online.
