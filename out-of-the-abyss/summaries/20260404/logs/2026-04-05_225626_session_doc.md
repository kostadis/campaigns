## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

The first time I tried to grab her, she vanished. One moment she was there — the next, she'd used whatever dark privilege a legendary creature keeps in reserve, and I was holding empty air forty feet above the cavern floor.

I filed that away. Not frustration. Just data.

There was a class, at the monastery, about legendary creatures and their particular geometries of power — the way they move through a fight differently from ordinary things, the reactions they hoard and spend like currency. I think it was Brother Quellin who taught it. Or possibly the tall one with the ink-stained hands. I'm genuinely not certain. I remember the quality of the light in that room — cold, coming from the left — and I remember being elsewhere in my head, working through a footwork sequence, not listening. I know the class existed. I know the information was available to me.

I was not paying attention.

So: second try. The same plan, because the only alternative was standing at range and watching everyone else do the work, which is not something I do. I can only close distance and hit things. That's what I have. The question was whether hitting her was worth burning her reaction, forcing her to spend that vanishing trick again — and then the logic shifted. *So, like, in making her teleport, I am preventing her from doing something more dangerous, potentially.* That was the reframe. Not eating the cost. Setting the price. She gets one legendary action. Every time she spends it escaping me, she's not spending it on something that ends one of us.

A monk runs, and then slaps. The run was already happening.

I crossed the cavern on the Eldritch tendrils — the tattoo reaching out ahead of me, dark extensions of will finding her before I arrived — and wrapped around Ilvara, and *held*. Sixteen damage and a grip that doesn't negotiate.

For a moment, the most dangerous creature in this room was dangling forty feet off the ground, and I was the one holding her there.

The logic of the next few seconds was simple enough that I didn't need to deliberate. *Now that I'm holding her, I will punch her again — because why not?*

The first hit was a critical. I felt it the way you sometimes do — not luck, not close, but the body moving perfectly into a space that opened exactly as I expected. The kind of punch that lands before she can decide to not let it. Then the second one, same register, the same clean certainty. Eighteen more.

She was still in the air. Her feet were nowhere near the stone.

Then I let go.

The fall was forty feet. The landing was loud. The table around me erupted with something that took a moment to parse as joy.

I noted this. Filed it. Returned to watching.

Ilvara hit the ground broken and bleeding, and I was already calculating the next approach when she reached into whatever was left of her power and did something to the inside of my head.

*Roll a DC-14 Wisdom saving throw.*

Well. That didn't work.

I want to be precise about what happened, because imprecision about threat vectors is how people die: it was not pain. It was not anything I could locate in my body. It was a conviction, total and sudden, that approaching her would end me — that whatever was wrong with this creature would be worse up close, that the distance between us was the only thing keeping me intact. The fear was not proportional. Fear rarely is. But it sat in me like certainty, and certainty is very hard to argue with in real time.

*Indeed,* I thought. Which is exactly the word for it.

The elegant part — and I can appreciate the elegance even from inside the problem — is that she'd protected herself against the one thing she couldn't outrun. She couldn't vanish again. She was on the ground and hurt. But now I couldn't take a single step toward her.

She'd turned my weapon into the instrument of my removal.

There was a class for this too, I'm fairly sure. Brother Tharusk, maybe, or whoever taught resistance to magical fear — the techniques for holding your own mind against something pressing in from outside. I have the same certainty about not having listened that I have about everything else from that period. The light in the classroom. The shape of the doorway. Not the content.

I was paying for that now, pinned to the ceiling by nothing more than my own terror, watching the fight continue below me while the heart fungus registered my predicament and treated it as an invitation.

The strike hit like the ceiling had come down on me. Fifty-some feet of cavern compressed into a single moment of impact — bludgeoning and something fouler moving through it, the poison settling into the spaces between my deflection and my endurance. I turned away twelve points of the impact. The rest I absorbed with my body, and my body had opinions about this that I noted and set aside.

I was still on my feet. Barely. One more round like that and I would have different opinions.

The fungal minions died around me — they didn't have much to offer, they never do — and I stood at the cavern ceiling, frightened and depleted, watching my companions dismantle the fight I'd opened, waiting for the fear to stop being true enough to move me.

*I am supposed to be trying to not be near her. Right? Alright, fine.*

The discipline holds even when it costs you. That's the point of it.

I held my ground. I held my breath. I waited for the cold grip of it to loosen beat by beat, until I could think straight again, until the calculus of the room returned and I could start running the numbers on what came next.
