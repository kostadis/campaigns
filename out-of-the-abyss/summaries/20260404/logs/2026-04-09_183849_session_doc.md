## Section — Thorin — The Heart Fungus and the Drow Betrayal

The handoff finds me already in motion, already committed — which is exactly where I prefer to be.

Jorlan moved first.

One moment he was fighting alongside us — or near us, anyway, which in drow politics constitutes a formal alliance — and the next he had broken entirely, shoving through the battlefield toward Ilvara with something wild behind his scarred face. I heard him before I understood him: *"Dovara!"* Not a battle cry. Something older than that. The sound a man makes when he has waited years for a particular moment and suddenly sees it arriving.

She was wounded. Zalthir had seen to that — had grappled her, punched her, and dropped her forty feet, in that order, with the particular thoroughness of someone who takes combat personally. And now Jorlan, who had served her, loved her, been discarded by her, and apparently never stopped counting the debt, saw his opening.

I saw mine too.

There's a discipline I've developed — not naturally, I won't claim that — that comes from carrying a weapon that has opinions. Dawnbringer has never once suggested I lower my guard, but she has, over months of shared dark and shared purpose, made me aware that *can* and *should* are not the same word. Jorlan was within range. The right motion, the familiar arc, and he wouldn't reach Ilvara at all.

But.

"He didn't think you guys could defeat Ilvara," someone said, "but now that she's wounded, he wants to take care of her himself."

*The tides have turned,* Zalthir observed, with the tone of a man filing information rather than expressing a feeling.

I stood there with the weapon live in my hand and worked through it the way the hill giants taught me to work through things: not fast, not dramatic, just methodically. What does it change if Jorlan kills her? What does it change if he doesn't? Does he get first claim on whatever she's carrying? And by *booty* — I said this out loud, because I say things out loud — by booty, I mean like, the *goods*. The magical items. The practical spoils. Not the other thing. Though they had history, so presumably that was booty he'd already claimed, and that wasn't what I meant, and I noticed the conversation stopping, and yes, I kept going anyway.

Nobody had an answer. The GM spread his hands. *You decide. He's running away. This is a classic roll-the-dice moment.*

Well. When a man tells you it's a dice moment, you roll dice.

Eight.

Eight says I do the sensible thing.

I lowered my arm and let Jorlan Duskryn run past me toward the woman who had thrown him away like a broken tool, carrying in his chest whatever years of humiliation compress into when finally given a direction to travel. I watched him go. I did not love this decision. I want that on record. I like my attacks of opportunity. I have *built around* my attacks of opportunity. Watching one die unused in my hand is its own small bereavement.

But eight says sensible. Eight says let the girls fight.

That made me sad. I said so.

No time to mourn it. The cavern decided it wasn't done being complicated: Ilvara, broken and furious on the floor, conjured an insect plague at the cave entrance — a sphere of biting, shrieking darkness that hit Grygum, Daz, and Glabbagool like a wall. The air stopped being air. I could hear the swarm from where I stood, this grinding, layered sound of ten thousand small mouths.

And somewhere in the middle of it, Glabbagool — who had been, as best I could tell, just *watching*, doing his best to follow along, his pseudopod-eyes oriented toward the action with the attentive look of someone trying very hard to be included — absorbed the full force of the insects without being asked.

He survived. Of course he survived. He's a gelatinous cube. But I felt the injustice of it anyway.

I had already asked about the fungus before the insect plague landed. Something in the tactical accounting had told me: *the thing in the middle of the cavern is the thing you're most equipped to deal with.* So I'd asked — is there anything I should know? Resistances, vulnerabilities, what am I working with?

*Doubly not resistant to radiant damage.*

I had Dawnbringer.

There are moments in the Underdark where everything aligns — the right tool, the right target, the right moment to stop watching political drama and do the thing you are physically built to do. I leapt down from the elevated walkways, hit the cavern floor, and pushed through the difficult terrain toward the heart fungus. The ground was wrong — uneven, corrupted, the kind of footing that rewards stubborn momentum over elegance. I have stubborn momentum. I am largely constructed of it.

Dawnbringer lit the way.

I struck. Radiant light cracked through the fungal flesh in a way that felt, honestly, surgical — like the weapon knew where to find the thing that held it together, and went there first. The heart fungus recoiled. Then it shuddered. Then it wilted, the whole elaborate structure of it collapsing inward, like a building whose foundation had decided it was tired.

One round.

*I do what I can,* I said.

I meant it without vanity. The fungus had been enormous, had clearly taken considerable preparation to construct and place, had probably featured in some tactical plan that Ilvara had designed with genuine care. And now it was gone because Dawnbringer is very bright and fungus is very flammable to righteousness, and I happened to ask the right question at the right time.

There is a saying the giants had — it doesn't translate cleanly, but it runs roughly: *a rock in the wrong place is just a rock, but a rock in the right place is a wall.* I had spent this whole battle feeling like a rock in the wrong place, watching drow politics unspool around me like a very slow, very poisonous rope. The Jorlan situation. Asha Vandree's prayers. Daz performing his Lolth-follower routine for an audience that genuinely believed it. Me, standing in the middle of all of it, understanding perhaps a third of the layers in operation at any given moment.

*I'm just sitting here,* I'd thought, watching Jorlan sprint toward Ilvara, *watching them trade blows like a tennis match.*

But the fungus was the thing I was built for. That part, I understood.

With it dead, and Jorlan locked in his private war somewhere across the cavern, I held my ground. Dawnbringer was still warm in my hand, still radiating — she doesn't fully dim, and I've stopped fighting that — and I watched what happened between those two, the ex-lover and the ex-mistress, and did not raise my arm.

Two choices in one scene to withhold the strike.

Eight said sensible. Watching said the same.

Neither of them was easy for a dwarf who built his whole fighting style around the moment the opening appears.
