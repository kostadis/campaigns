## Daz — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The battlefield had become a taxonomy problem.

I run them through the list while ducking a crossbow bolt that misses me by what feels like courtesy: Ilvara, wounded and venomous, holding the center. Glabbagool surging forward, which I appreciate in a general sense. Grygum angling toward Asha. Jorlan — Jorlan, who we'd watched barrel through our lines screaming his former lover's name — apparently now someone else's problem. The Big Heart Fungus: already gone, carved apart by Thorin in the time it takes most people to decide whether to act. And the insects. Always the insects. Ilvara's plague fills the middle distance with biting, stinging, churning noise, a wall of biological interference that had begun to feel personal.

Five new drow stepped out of the shadows.

Zalthir's voice, dry and somewhat delighted: "Five dark elves. That's awesome."

Grygum, quieter: "This is turning into the twelve days of Christmas."

I agreed, privately. House T'sarran markings — I clocked that before the first bolt cleared the bowstring. A rival house, then. Not Vandree, not Mizzrym. Someone who had apparently decided the chaos of this particular cavern represented an opportunity worth pursuing. The tactical reasoning was not without merit. The execution, however.

Three crossbow bolts came at me. All three ate insects instead of wizard. The swarm, for once, earning its misery by absorbing the shots that Ilvara's spell had apparently been too generous to warn these newcomers about. I watched the T'sarran elite warrior turn to the mage with an expression I recognized from every failed operation I had ever observed from a comfortable distance.

*I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill.*

The mage had no good answer. He also, it emerged, had no Dispel Magic. The elite warrior processed this information with the grim resignation of a professional who has been let down by every supporting structure in his professional life.

I should have felt threatened. Instead I felt something closer to embarrassment on their behalf.

They looked a little too smug, too sure of themselves — the posture of people who had planned an ambush and not yet updated their confidence to reflect that the ambush was actively being digested by magical insects. They would update. The insects were thorough. But in that moment they still had the bearing of professionals arriving to collect, and the shots went wide, and I noted it the way you note a poorly shuffled deck: the tell is in the hands.

Grygum was already offering healing. Thorin somewhere behind me, navigating what he'd called the worst map in the Underdark. Asha Vandree praying to Lolth or preparing to pray or preparing to accuse someone of not praying — the volatility of her devotion made her difficult to predict on a round-by-round basis, which was the primary management problem I had been handling since before the fight started.

Then Kaelira stepped out of nowhere and walked directly into the insect cloud.

Professional. I'll give her that. She came through the swarm — taking hits, grimacing, not stopping — and arrived at my position with the expression of someone who has had simpler assignments and is not sure what she did to deserve this one.

"Come with me if you want to live, you idiot."

She waited a beat, perhaps in case I had missed it, then elaborated with the careful patience of a woman who has said this line before: "If you don't want to live, I'll still take you, but could you — it would be much easier if you'd just come."

The first rule of the House, as the training goes: *know who holds the debt before you decide whether to collect it.* Kaelira was holding something — a contract, a commission, someone's standing order to extract me from this cavern, someone who had paid and expected delivery. I did not know who that was. I did not know what I would owe them for it. Both gaps in my information seemed important.

What I knew was this: I was upright. I was functional. The T'sarran bolts had missed. The insects were, in their horrible way, protecting me. Ilvara was still in the room, and Ilvara was the one who had put the collar around my neck, and I had not come this far and burned this many spell slots to be carried out before the accounting was finished.

I had one other problem, which was Asha Vandree, who had already looked at the T'sarran drow with that edge in her eyes that precedes accusations. Zealotry is readable, once you've catalogued enough of it. She was three seconds from calling someone a traitor to Lolth. I had watched her manage Jorlan's abrupt defection from our engagement with religious gravity. Fresh unknown drow appearing from shadow would read to her as sabotage, infiltration, the work of enemies of the Spider Queen.

The con never stops. That's not a principle; it's just a fact about cons.

Before she could speak, I told her they were friends of Lolf. Here to help. Said it smoothly, said it fast, said it with the absolute confidence of someone who has never once doubted that Lolf was watching. The adaptation cost me nothing — it never does, when the groundwork is already laid. I just extended the fiction another room.

I filed Kaelira's look away for later. The dice were rolling. We'd see where they landed.

What I was not going to do was leave.
