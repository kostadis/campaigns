I clocked the room before I clocked the people. Tables cleared from breakfast and not yet reset, a statue of Milil standing quiet in its alcove, two waiters stacking linen at the far end. That's how you read a place. The furniture tells you what the night was. This one was mid-shift, nothing finished, everybody waiting on the next thing.

Daral and I walked in. The two folding linen both knew him. One said hello like he meant it. The other rolled his eyes and said hello like it cost him something.

"Popular dude," I said.

Three of them noticed me crossing the floor and went still in the way people go still when they think they're about to get asked questions they'd rather not answer. Good. That's the terrain. You don't chase the ones who look comfortable.

"So, we go start interviewing them," I said. "Where were you on the night of, and all that."

The first one's name was Irony.

"Are you being ironic?"

"No." Flat. Then a little tired around the edges. "But I, you know, I really hate it when people make that joke, oh, I'm so sorry, this compulsive need to always tell the truth."

That landed for me harder than she probably expected. A person who can't lie. In the Underdark that'd be a death sentence, but up here it's the most useful thing in the building.

"Well, I appreciate that," I said. "I am investigating a crime, so somebody who always tells the truth is definitely a very useful thing." And then, because I can't help myself, "Hatred always lies."

"No, Sprig doesn't — Sprig doesn't always lie."

Wrong joke. I held up a hand. "Regardless, that was another joke, I apologize."

She gave me the supper instead. The Deadwinter feast started at the five o'clock bell — twelve plates, beetroot soup, fried carp, smoked mackerel, on and on, spiced wines and ales, honey, green tea. Served by three junior avowed. Her, Orrin, and Sprig.

"Okay, so, okay, so we can interview Orrin," I said, "and see if Orrin." I kept the names. Three servers in the room means three sets of eyes, and a room with three pairs of eyes doesn't keep a secret well.

Everybody at that supper was tense and quiet, she said. Everybody except Alkrist. He was cheerful, going on about some new project — *Tales of War by the Generals That Waged Them*, first-hand strategy from warlords the world over.

*Daz would've gone at motive there. Anyone pushing a project that'd never get funded? Pull that thread.* So I let her keep talking, and the thread came loose on its own — somebody named Teles scoffed that Alkrist's project sounded about as likely to succeed as the Fheminor Scrivenbark program, whatever that was.

"I think — was it, who was in a good mood again? It was —"

"Alkrist."

Kept that. The cheerful one in a room full of tense people. Janussi, meanwhile, praised the direction, called it the kind of knowledge that benefits civilization. And when the keeper asked everyone to show more solidarity, Daral muttered under his breath — *Oh, you've been right behind one of us all night.* I noted Daral had teeth in him after all.

Then Sylvira Savikas stormed out. After that, Kazryn took it on herself to hand each gift to its great reader. Janussi unwrapped his, thanked his secret donor, and left, throwing the wrapping paper in a wicker basket by the door on his way out.

"Oh, so this was a Secret Santa type of thing, situation," I said.

Most people drifted off after that. They were about to scrape the dirty plates when Bookwyrm told them to carry the leftovers to their dorms and enjoy the night instead.

"Was anybody sick from eating the leftovers?"

"No, no, nobody was sick. Drinking too much, but not sick."

If you're worried about the food, she said, you should talk to Sprig. She tasted all of it.

"Like, is that part of her job, or is she just, like, tasting the food?"

"Well, yes, we have a poison taster!"

*Zalthir would frame this one flat: if the only mark was Janussi, the taster's collateral. She'd go down at midnight too and nobody connects it. So either someone kept her off the wrong plate on purpose, or she got lucky. Either way, whoever'd care enough to protect her is a name worth having.* I tucked that away.

I asked if anybody'd been particularly friendly to Sprig. Irony rolled her eyes. "I don't know, but these avowed, sometimes they're, like, they're a little bit too interested in the junior avowed."

"Wink, wink, nudge, nudge, okay."

I went at the book before I went at Sprig, because the book is the whole thing. "Like, how the book got tampered with, right? Somebody could have unwrapped it and rewrapped it." So I asked her, before I'd even talk to Sprig, whether she'd seen anybody messing with the gifts under the — "non-Christmas Christmas tree." In this kind of group it'd all be books anyway, right? Just a pile.

"Yeah," she said. "Or wands, or magical artifacts."

Sprig gushed. Twelve delicious dishes, the work of the Candlekeep chefs, and how she'd enthusiastically volunteered to taste every one to make sure none were poisoned. The chefs told her it wasn't necessary. She insisted. Daral lit up at that — said it was the kind of initiative that should be commended, a best practice going forward.

"Concern at all," I asked her, "with the fact that you were the poison taster, and it turns out somebody was poisoned during that meal?"

Daral cut in low — they don't know she was poisoned. Only we know that.

"Okay, so yeah," I said, "we don't want to tip our hand here." Radical transparency has a time and place, and a murder investigation in a fortress full of suspects isn't always it. I'm learning that. It costs me something to learn it.

Late that night, Irony said, she'd left her dorm for the kitchens to grab cookies and run into Sylvira Savikas searching for something by candlelight. *What was she hunting for? Did she say?* No — Sylvira only pointed at her own throat, too hoarse to speak, and Irony helped her find a pot of honey and bade her goodnight. One in the morning.

I took a hard look at Daral. Tried to read whether there was anything conniving under all that helpfulness. There wasn't. The man was scared out of his skin — kept glancing at Dawnbringer like if there were any way to kiss the sword's feet without it being weird he'd already be down on the floor. Not the killer. Just terrified.

Then Orrin Glass marched up. Third waiter, deaf in one ear, a bit of an oddball — the kind of man who's been standing in a corner all his life because nobody bothers to talk to him.

I asked the thing that was bugging me. "I wanted to know — is anybody really looking after Sprig? Because, like, why didn't — you know, who'd care if the poison taster lived or died?"

Wrong tree, came the answer. That's *Irony* — Irony who'd been trying to thwart the advances of one of the great readers. "It's not for me to say, but since she threw me under the bus —"

"You know, we are — it's a murder investigation, cut her some slack."

So it came out: Teles Ahvoste, one of the great readers, making passes at her.

"At Irony."

Yes. And I should talk to Orrin, who notices everything even if he hears nothing.

*Zalthir would've caught it before I did: Irony can't lie — but she didn't answer. She pointed us at Sprig. "Can't lie" isn't "has to tell." She steered us instead of speaking false. Worth remembering how she dodges.*

"She didn't lie," somebody said. "Can't lie is one of those ambiguous things. Can't lie *well*."

"I mean, let's go talk to Orrin, I guess." I kept it simple with him. "Yeah, I mean, let's start with, like, what does he see? Did he see anybody messing with the gifts under the tree, et cetera, et cetera. What does he recall about the night?"

He made one interesting comment about Alkrist — that Alkrist seemed to drop a small scrap of paper he'd been holding toward the candle the moment two junior avowed walked in.

"Okay, so maybe he's a suspect then also."

"Yes."

"We're getting more suspects instead of fewer, just to be clear."

And then Orrin gave me the one that closed the door. Something out of the ordinary, he said: the string tied around Janussi's Deadwinter gift had clearly been cut and retied.

That was it. That was the wall in the right place. *Daz's conclusion, and it's the right one — the string was cut and retied. Whatever happened to that book happened under the tree.*
