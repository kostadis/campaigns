I clocked the room before I clocked the people. Long tables cleared from breakfast and not yet reset, the linen half-folded, a statue of Milil standing quiet in his alcove with a brass chalice in one open hand. Two waiters stacking cloth at the far end. The furniture tells you what the night was. This one was mid-shift, nothing finished, everybody waiting on the next thing.

Daral and I walked in. Both waiters knew him. One looked up, saw him, and said hello like he meant it. The other looked up, saw him, rolled his eyes, and said hello like the word cost him money.

"Popular dude," I said.

Daral didn't hear it. He'd stopped dead in front of the statue, staring at the chalice like it had bitten him. "Oh my goodness! We used it for the Midsummer Wine! Th-th-there might be something important in it!"

"To what chalice is this?"

"Look, the lead chalice wine is very important because it hides things from scrying, the magic that finds what's hidden. Perhaps somebody hid something in it." His hands were flapping. "How could I have not thought about this?"

I held up a hand. "Wait, wait, wait, I wanna understand. Okay, so this is a dining hall." He pointed at the statue, which is a god. "I imagine everybody has their own chalice." There's a statue of him, Daral said, and a chalice. "Statue, a chalice, because why the hell not, right?" I let that sit. Giants taught me you don't rush a thing just because it's standing in front of you. "Trust statues, they come alive."

And then Daral remembered the part that mattered: the Milil chalice was lead-lined.

*Lead-lined.* That's not nothing. Lead blocks the kind of magic that finds things. A person who wanted to hide something where no scrying could reach it would pick exactly this cup. Zalthir would've said it first, flat. I clocked it, then walked the rest of it backward out loud.

"Okay, so everybody — you have a party, things get a little out of hand, somebody puts a lamp shade on their head, somebody steals the chalice from the god that's lead-lined, because that's a good idea, and it's part of the whole sort of revelries. Is that what I'm hearing?"

Y-yes, said Daral. Yes, that's where it must be! He started toward it. "But I am but a scholar!"

I stepped in front of him. I didn't want a scholar's nervous hands anywhere near a cup that might be the murder weapon. "Hey, back off, dude." He looked relieved, actually, worried there might be a ward on the thing, something that goes off when the wrong hand reaches in. Fair. "Look, if something bad happens to me, like, you know, I'm not sure that Dawnbringer's gonna take care of you. Just be aware."

The terror on his face was complete.

I went to the alcove and lifted the chalice out of the statue's open hand. Looked inside. Light still reaches in fine. What stops a finding-spell doesn't stop a man's eyes.

There was a heart in it. A heart, and a blood-stained meat cleaver.

"Wait, wait, a heart?"

Janussi's heart, Daral said. They stole his heart, because they didn't want anybody to be able to bring him back and ask him what happened.

I looked at it a long moment. A heart in a god's cup, tucked behind lead so no spell could find it. Somebody took it out of a man so nobody could ever ask the man anything again.

"I left it in Waterdeep."

Yes, Daral said.

"Everyone does." I kept my face flat. "Especially — can we reunite the heart with the body and actually do a whole sort of, like." Wake the dead long enough to speak. Put the man back together enough to ask him who did this. I let it trail. We could try it later.

In a world like this the ground keeps moving. Bodies you can rewrite at will. You can't trust a face, you can't trust a body, you can barely trust a heart in a cup, and somewhere in the middle of me going on about all of it, the thing landed. Whoever planned this knew there was a chalice that was lead-lined. That's not knowledge a stranger has. Everyone at that dinner handled the cup, sure, but somebody handled it later, after the meal, when there was no reason to. The lead doesn't just hide the heart. It narrows the door. Anyone who saw a person at this cup late is a witness and a window both. An inside job. The cup proved it.

I set the heart down gentle and looked over at the two waiters still folding linen at the end of the hall.

Three of them noticed me crossing the floor and went still, the way people go still when they think they're about to get asked questions they'd rather not answer. Good. You don't chase the ones who look comfortable.

"So, we go start interviewing them," I said. "Where were you on the night of, and all that."

The first one's name was Irony.

"Are you being ironic?"

"No." Flat, like she'd been bracing for it. "But I, you know, I really hate it when people make that joke, oh, I'm so sorry, this compulsive need to always tell the truth."

That stopped me. A person who can't lie. In the Underdark I'd have paid in gold for one of those, and here was one stacking linen.

"Well, I appreciate that," I said, and I meant it. "I am investigating a crime, so somebody who always tells the truth is definitely a very useful thing." Then she mentioned Sprig, and I couldn't help it. "Hatred always lies."

"No, Sprig doesn't — Sprig doesn't always lie."

"Regardless, that was another joke, I apologize." Two name jokes is my limit anyway.

Once she got going she couldn't stop, like she'd been holding it. The Deadwinter supper started at the five o'clock bell. Twelve plates, beetroot soup, fried carp, smoked mackerel and on down the line, spiced wines and ales, honey, vodka, green tea. Served by three of the junior avowed: her, Orrin, and Sprig. Everything out on big platters, passed and shared, drinks poured from carafes. Everybody tense and quiet, except Alkrist, cheerful, going on about some new project, *Tales of War by the Generals That Waged Them*, first-hand strategy off warlords and commanders from all over.

Motive next. Daz would've gone at it first: anybody here pushing a project that'd never get funded? I pulled the thread, and it came loose on its own. Teles had scoffed, Irony said, called Alkrist's project about as likely to succeed as some Fheminor Scrivenbark program. She didn't know what that meant. Janussi praised it the other way, the kind of knowledge that benefits civilization, and reminded the room that deciding which branches of knowledge to fund was their privileged responsibility. And when the keeper asked everyone for solidarity, Daral had muttered under his breath: *oh, you've been right behind one of us all night.* The scholar had teeth in him after all.

Then Sylvira Savikas stormed out. After that Kazryn Nyantani took it on herself to hand each gift to its great reader. Janussi unwrapped his, thanked his secret donor, and excused himself, dropping the wrapping paper in a wicker basket by the door on his way out. The paper turned up later in his office, not here; I keep the two from blurring. Most folks drifted off. Bookwyrm told the waitstaff to carry the leftovers to their dorms instead of scraping every crumb into the bin.

"Was anybody sick from eating the leftovers?"

"No, no, nobody was sick. I mean, drinking too much, but not sick."

Then she said the thing that turned my stomach a little. If I was worried about the food, I should talk to Sprig. Because Sprig tasted all of it.

"Like, is that part of her job, or is she just, like, tasting the food?"

"Well, yes, we have a poison taster!"

Turn it over flat, the way Zalthir would. If the only mark was Janussi, the taster's just collateral. She tastes the poisoned dish, she dies at midnight too, and nobody ties the two together. So either somebody kept her off the wrong plate on purpose, which is a name worth having, or she got lucky. But the food and drink were all shared, so the delivery's hard to aim unless a server was in on it. As far as we know it was the book. A dozen ways to kill the man and they picked the slow, roundabout one. One reason to skip the food is if you didn't want the taster dead. Which puts her friends, if she's got any in the inner circle, on the board.

Irony rolled her eyes. "I don't know, but these avowed, sometimes they're, like, they're a little bit too interested in the junior avowed."

"Wink, wink, nudge, nudge, okay."

She had one more. Late that night she'd left her dorm for the kitchens, after cookies, and run into Sylvira Savikas searching for something by candlelight. Sylvira only pointed at her own throat, too hoarse to speak, and Irony helped her find a pot of honey and bade her goodnight. One in the morning.

"That was actually her, somebody wearing her face, right? Because they didn't talk."

"Right. This is the second person who's provided evidence that she's not. She didn't talk. And she was using a lantern, even though she's a tiefling."

That landed clean. A tiefling doesn't need the light. Which means she wasn't looking for something. She was leaving something — the heart and the cleaver, set down where the lead chalice would swallow them past the reach of any far-seeing magic. The honey was the excuse for when Irony walked in.

I went at the book before I went at Sprig, because the book is the whole thing. Somebody could have unwrapped it and rewrapped it. So I asked Irony, before I'd even talk to the taster, whether she'd seen anybody messing with the gifts under the — "non-Christmas Christmas tree." In this kind of group it'd all be books anyway. "Yeah," she said. "Or wands, or magical artifacts."

Sprig gushed. Twelve delicious dishes, the work of the Candlekeep chefs, and how she'd enthusiastically volunteered to taste every one to make sure none were poisoned. The chefs told her it wasn't necessary. She insisted. Daral lit up at that, said it was the kind of initiative that should be commended, a best practice going forward.

"Concern at all," I asked her, "with the fact that you were the poison taster, and it turns out somebody was poisoned during that meal?"

Daral cut in low. They don't know she was poisoned. Only we know that.

"Okay, so yeah," I said, "we don't want to tip our hand here." Radical transparency has a time and place, and a murder in a fortress full of suspects isn't always it. I'm learning that.

I took a hard look at Daral while I had him close. Tried to read whether there was anything conniving under all that helpfulness. There wasn't. The man was scared out of his skin. Kept glancing at Dawnbringer like if there were any way to kiss the sword's feet without it being weird he'd already be down on the floor. Not the killer. Just terrified.

I'd been chewing on the taster all along, so I asked the thing that bugged me. "I wanted to know — is anybody really looking after Sprig? Because, like, who'd care if the poison taster lived or died?"

Wrong tree. That's *Irony*, came the answer — Irony who'd been trying to thwart the advances of one of the great readers. It came out slow: Teles Ahvoste, one of the great readers, making passes at her. And I should talk to Orrin, who notices everything even if he hears nothing.

I marked how the answer arrived. Irony can't lie, but she hadn't answered me either. She'd pointed us at Sprig instead. "Can't lie" isn't "has to tell." She steered us rather than speak false. Worth remembering how she dodges.

"I mean, let's go talk to Orrin, I guess." I kept it simple with him. "Did he see anybody messing with the gifts under the tree? What does he recall about the night?"

He made one interesting comment about Alkrist: that Alkrist seemed to drop a small scrap of paper he'd been holding toward the candle the moment two junior avowed walked in.

"Okay, so maybe he's a suspect then also."

"Yes."

"We're getting more suspects instead of fewer, just to be clear."

And then Orrin gave me the one that closed the door. Something out of the ordinary, he said: the string tied around Janussi's Deadwinter gift had clearly been cut and retied.

That was it. That was the wall in the right place. The string was cut and retied. Whatever happened to that book happened under the tree.
