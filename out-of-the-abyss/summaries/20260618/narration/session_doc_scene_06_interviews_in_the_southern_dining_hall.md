The room still smelled like breakfast. Long tables shoved to one side, cleared and not reset, and a Milil statue tucked into an alcove — God of Bards, open hands, a brass chalice cupped between them like he was waiting for somebody to pour. Two waiters stacking linen. One of them looked up, saw Daral, and said hello like he meant it. The other one looked up, saw Daral, rolled his eyes, and said hello like the word cost him money.

"Popular dude," I said.

Daral didn't argue. Three of them watching us, I clocked — wondering, probably, which of them was getting questioned and which of them got to keep folding napkins. Fine. That's the job.

"So, we go start interviewing them," I said. "Where were you on the night of, and all that."

The first one's name was Irony.

"Are you being ironic?"

"No!" Sharp, like she'd been bracing for it. "But I, you know, I really hate it when people make that joke, oh, I'm so sorry, this compulsive need to always tell the truth."

That stopped me. A person who has to tell the truth. In the Underdark I'd have paid in gold for one of those, and here was one stacking linen.

"Well, I appreciate that," I said, and I meant it. "I am investigating a crime, so somebody who always tells the truth is definitely a very useful thing."

Then she mentioned Sprig, and I couldn't help it. "Hatred always lies."

"No, Sprig doesn't — Sprig doesn't always lie."

"Regardless, that was another joke, I apologize." She let it go. Good. Two name jokes is my limit anyway.

Once she got going, she couldn't stop, like she'd been holding it. The Deadwinter supper began at the five o'clock bell. Twelve plates — beetroot soup, fried carp, smoked mackerel, and so on down the line. Fruit compots, spiced wines and ales, honey, vodka, green tea. Served by three of the junior avowed: her, Orrin, and Sprig.

"Okay, so we can interview Orrin and see if Orrin —"

Everything came out on big platters, she said. Set on the table to be passed and shared. Drinks poured from carafes. Everybody tense and quiet, except Alkrist — cheerful, going on about some new project, *Tales of War by the Generals That Waged Them*, where he'd gather first-hand strategy off warlords and commanders from all over.

*Daz would've gone at motive first — was anyone here pushing a project that'd never get funded? That's the kind of thread he pulls. So I pulled it.*

Teles had scoffed, she said. Called the project about as likely to succeed as some Fheminor Scrivenbark program. She didn't know what that meant. Janussi praised it the other way — the kind of knowledge that could benefit civilization — and reminded everybody that deciding which branches of knowledge to fund was their privileged responsibility. And when the keeper asked the room for solidarity, Daral had muttered under his breath, *oh, you've been right behind one of us all night.*

"Who was in a good mood again?"

"Alkrist."

A keeper, she said. A dragonborn. "I'm just saying, he's not on our suspect list, I don't think, but okay."

Then Sylvira Savikas had stormed out, and after that Kazryn Nyantani decided it was time for the gifts under the evergreen tree and took it on herself to hand each one to the right great reader. Janussi unwrapped his, thanked his secret donor, then excused himself — dropped the wrapping paper in a wicker basket by the door on his way out.

"Oh, so this was a Secret Santa type of thing, situation."

Most folks made their excuses and left. Bookwyrm told the waitstaff to take the leftovers to their dorms instead of scraping every crumb into the bin.

"Was anybody sick from eating the leftovers?"

"No, no, nobody was sick. I mean, drinking too much, but not sick."

"The wrapping paper, is it still around?"

"I think so?"

"It'd be something worth looking at. I would handle it very carefully, because we don't know how the poison would spread." *The paper turned up in Janussi's office, not here — I keep the two from blurring. When we get to it, handle it careful. No telling how the poison rides.*

Then she said the thing that turned my stomach a little. If I was worried about the food, I should talk to Sprig. Because Sprig tasted all of it.

"Like, is that part of her job, or is she just, like, tasting the food?"

"Well, yes, we have a poison taster!"

"Is she the poison taster?"

"Well, yes!"

"I didn't realize that was her role. Okay, good to know."

*Zalthir would turn it over the flat way he does. If the only mark was Janussi, the taster's just collateral. She tastes the poisoned dish, she dies at midnight too, and nobody ties the two together. Either somebody in the administration kept her off the wrong plate on purpose — that's a name worth having — or she got lucky.*

"Yeah, but it sounds like all the food and drink was pretty shared," I said, "so it would have been hard to be targeted in the delivery unless one of the servers was in on it." *Was there ever a plate that was only his? If it was all shared, the delivery had to be the book — or a server standing in the right place.* "I mean, as far as we know, it was the book, right? We don't know for sure, but Alpine's point to the book."

*And he'd keep pressing: a dozen ways to kill the man, food, drink, any of them, and they picked the book. One reason to skip the food is if you didn't want the taster dead. Which puts her friends, if she's got any in the inner circle, on the board.*

Irony rolled her eyes. "I don't know, but these avowed, sometimes they're, like, they're a little bit too interested in the junior avowed."

"Wink, wink, nudge, nudge, okay."

"I don't know, you might want to talk to her, I don't think I should talk about that."

But she had one more. Later that night, she'd left her dorm for the kitchens, after cookies, and run into Sylvira Savikas searching for something by candlelight.

*What was she hunting for by candlelight? Did she say?*

"No, I said hello to the great reader, but the tiefling only pointed at her throat by way of reply, suggesting she was too hoarse to speak. I helped her find a pot of honey and bade her goodnight." A beat. "One in the morning."

"That was actually her, somebody wearing her face, right? Because they didn't talk."

"Right. This is the second person who's provided evidence that she's not. She didn't talk."

"Very suspicious that, like, she wouldn't talk, and, like, yeah."

"And she was using a lantern, even though she's a tiefling."

That landed clean. "And then maybe the whole honey thing was just an excuse because she was discovered, right?"

*Looking for something, lantern in hand — and a tiefling doesn't need the light. Or not looking. Leaving. The heart and the cleaver, set down where the lead chalice would swallow them from any divination. The honey was the excuse for when Irony walked in.*

"Yes," Irony said. "For example."
