## Section — Grygum — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The insects hit like a wall.

One moment the cavern was loud with the usual sounds of desperate combat — feet on stone, Dawnbringer's radiant crack, Ilvara's howling about Zuggtmoy and "the bride" and whatever theological score she was settling even while people were trying to kill her. The next, the air was just *moving*, a rolling roar of wings and mandibles that filled every gap between the stalactites and pressed into mouths and ears and the back of the throat. Ilvara had called it up from somewhere that didn't bear thinking about, a sphere of biting, chittering catastrophe that swept the cave entrance and didn't much care who was standing in it. Grygum was standing in it. So was Daz. So was Glabbagool.

His first thought, if he's honest, was not tactical.

His first thought was: *Glabbagool.*

"Oh, how about Glabbagool?"

The ooze was attempting to participate in the battle with the particular determination of a creature who has recently decided that belonging to a party means contributing, and was taking twenty-six points of insect damage for the privilege. He survived it, which is more than some of the fungal minions managed. The fungal minions were dissolving into paste at the edges of the swarm — Ilvara's own creatures, which felt like some kind of theological statement about what Zuggtmoy's blessings were actually worth. Grygum filed that under *ironic* and turned his attention to the situation.

The situation had developed a new wrinkle.

Jorlan Duskryn, fury running hot enough to see from across a cavern, had broken from the party entirely and was charging toward the woman who'd discarded him once his face stopped being acceptable. Whatever Grygum might have expected from an ambush on a corrupted high priestess, a lovers' quarrel conducted in active combat had not been high on the list. He watched Jorlan move and felt the shape of the thing clearly: this was between two people who had unfinished business that predated the fungus, the demon lords, the Underdark, and quite possibly recorded history.

"I don't want to get in the middle of it," he said, "but I'll follow up if necessary."

The DM, with the weary look of someone who had designed this encounter to go differently, confirmed his read: *yeah, this is their couples issues, not ours.* Bahamut's teachings were clear enough on the subject of other people's romantic grievances. You stayed out. You kept moving. You followed up if necessary. Grygum kept moving, maintaining what felt like the appropriate diplomatic distance between himself and whatever Ilvara and Jorlan were working out at sword-point.

Then Thorin found the Heart Fungus.

This is a thing Grygum will think about later, probably during a rest cycle when there's nothing better to occupy him. The Heart Fungus was *impressive*. Even as a thing trying to kill them, it had a kind of grandeur — massive, strange, the kind of encounter piece you could see someone spending real effort on. He had already begun composing, in the back of his mind, a small suite of tactical observations about it. He had opinions about approach vectors. He had *notes*.

Dawnbringer does not care about notes.

The radiant light cracked out through the fungus like morning through a smashed window, and then the fungus was simply gone, collapsed in on itself before it could demonstrate any of the things it had presumably been built to do. The table experienced a brief moment of collective mourning. Grygum, who genuinely meant it, offered: "I know, it was beautiful. I liked it very much, Gustatus." He had, in fact, liked it very much. He also had a guiding bolt ready and an entirely different set of tactical plans that were now obsolete.

"Boy," he thought, "I had so much prepared for the fungus."

He closed the file.

In its place, Ilvara.

Battered from Zalthir's aerial violence — the grapple, the forty-foot drop, the precise and devastating flurry of blows delivered with Zalthir's customary emotional register of someone ordering lunch — she was still moving, still calling down divine fire, still muttering her fragmentary prophecies about Zuggtmoy's bride and the chaos to come. Grygum cast his guiding bolt and missed, which was annoying, and she reshuffled the battlefield in the space the miss created: fungal growth slowing Jorlan, concentration dropped, divine fire falling on Daz and Nym Duskryn with the particular targeting logic of someone who had decided to take as many people with her as possible. Thorin brought down the bridge. She leapt clear.

He was readjusting when the cavern offered its next gift.

"Just when you thought everything was under control..."

"Are there 3 more unnamed creatures?" Grygum asked, because optimism is a spiritual discipline and three would have been manageable.

"5 Dark Elves, it looks like?" Zalthir said, with the flat affect of a man cataloguing weather. "5 Drow? That's awesome."

Grygum looked at the five drow emerging from the shadows directly into the lingering cloud of poison they apparently had not known was there, watched their expressions cycle through *confident ambush* and arrive rapidly at *oh no*, and said: "This is turning into the 12 days of Christmas."

The DM expressed hope that it would be pleasant. Grygum, with the cadence of someone who had been in too many corridors with too many enemies to believe in pleasant anymore, sang: *Dark Elves.*

"It's tough being a minion."

The five new arrivals were already catching poison. The insect swarm was making everyone's life miserable. A drow mage fired a blind lightning bolt into the chaos and hit nothing. Daz answered with an empowered fireball that lit the tunnel orange and converted several of those carefully placed guards into a solved problem. Things were improving, in the way that things improve when enough of your enemies are on fire.

"More fungus among us?" Grygum tried, as the shape of the next threat emerged from the smoke.

"No, no. No, it's different this time."

Different. Right. He adjusted his read, pivoted his notes, and — because at some point all the tactical preparation either pays off or it doesn't — lined up Ilvara.

She was still trouble. She was, in fact, flagrantly, howlingly, still-invoking-Zuggtmoy trouble. The Jorlan drama was ongoing. The minion accounting had grown so complicated that he'd briefly assumed the collapsing grey ooze servitors belonged to the party, because at this point in the journey the companion manifest had gotten genuinely difficult to track. "We used to have so many people traveling with us," he had noted, with sheepish honesty, "I forgot."

"I'm amused by the Jorlan-Ilvara drama," he said, "but I think I'm still going to go ahead and attack them."

He cast the guiding bolt. It hit.

Ilvara Mizzrym, former high priestess of Lolth, corrupted instrument of Zuggtmoy, architect of their captivity and a great deal of subsequent suffering, did not die with dignity. She died as a biological hazard — a puff of poisonous spores dispersing into the cavern air, Zuggtmoy's blessing rendered into its most honest form at the end.

Grygum's first observation, delivered with the flat pragmatism of a man who had just ended the session's primary antagonist and was already assessing consequences:

"Not ideal. How big is the cloud? I'm glad I was standing back."

He had, in fact, been standing back. He had taken notes. It's what he does.
