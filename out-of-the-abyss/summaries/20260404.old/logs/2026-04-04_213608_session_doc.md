## Section — Zalthir — The Confrontation with Ilvara

He announced it like it was barely worth saying.

*I'm just gonna try annoying her then.*

That was the plan. That was the whole plan. Ilvara Mizzrym, high priestess of House Mizzrym, Zuggtmoy's beloved instrument, corrupted vessel of a demon lord of fungal nightmare — and I was going to *annoy* her. The eldritch tendrils of the tattoo were already unreeling from my arm before the words finished leaving my mouth.

The cavernous space above the heart fungus was dark and vast and exactly the kind of silence I prefer to operate in. I crossed it fast, using the reach of the tentacles to close the distance before she had a chance to process what I was doing. The first strikes landed. Sixteen damage, the grapple secured.

*She's 16 damage and grappled, right?*

She was. I was holding onto her. That was the important part.

I do not, as a rule, enjoy monologue. I leave that to Daz, who has the cheekbones for it. What I enjoy is the moment when a thing is in your hands and you have not yet decided to let go of it. That moment. That is the thing I came to the monastery for, even if I did not know it when I arrived, even if I spent the first three years thinking I was there to learn to fight.

I dragged her up. Forty feet. The stone floor dropped away beneath us, and the enormous obscene mass of the heart fungus shrank below, and Ilvara was — for a moment — entirely subject to gravity and to me.

Then the dice agreed with me. Twice.

*Crit.*

I do not celebrate at the table. It is not my custom. But the second one surprised even me, and I recall something warm and undignified moving through my chest that I chose not to name.

*Oh, wow. Another crit.*

Forty feet up, gripping a corrupted high priestess, delivering consecutive critical strikes — and then I let her go. That was the point. The drop was always the point. The grapple was just delivery.

She hit the ground.

And then she looked up at me.

I have trained for years to read danger in the environment before I read it in the faces of creatures. The shape of the tunnel, the angle of a weapon, the particular quality of silence before something moves. I am rarely caught by what happens *after* the decisive action.

But Ilvara Mizzrym, bleeding, furious, struck from forty feet of free fall, still had something left. She reached into whatever dark place Zuggtmoy had hollowed out inside her and she *pushed* — not against my body, not against my grip, but against the part of me that the training does not fully reach.

*Roll a DC-14 Wisdom saving throw.*

Well. That didn't work.

The fear was immediate and specific and entirely unwanted. Not a general dread — not the crawling ambient horror of the Underdark that you learn to fold into background noise. This was targeted. This said: *she is down there, and you will not approach her, and you will not approach her, and you will not approach her.* Repeated. Insistent.

*You are frightened. Which is very convenient, because you cannot now approach her.*

*Indeed.*

I am a monk. My entire tactical vocabulary is built around being adjacent to the problem. Range is something other people deal with. Range is Daz's whole personality. I hit things. I grapple things. I drag things into the air and then drop them from inconvenient heights. All of that requires *proximity.*

And Ilvara's legendary reaction had, in one economical move, made proximity impossible.

I stayed where I was. Forty feet up. The frightened condition is not subtle — it is not a suggestion. I turned it over, the way you turn over a rule that has been applied against you, looking for the seam where the letter and the spirit diverge.

The spirit seems to be that I should do nothing to become closer to her.

Right.

*Alright. Fine.*

I am supposed to apply the rule honestly, even against my own interest. That is the monastic training operating at the level of self-adjudication, or so I was told during the years I spent thinking I was there to learn to fight. I spent a lot of time dismissing lessons that turned out to be useful. I am still working through the implications of that.

So I stayed up. I did not approach.

And the heart fungus, which had apparently been paying attention, chose this moment to remind me that fifteen feet of reach is a very generous number when the thing reaching lives on the floor and I have been hovering at the ceiling being useless.

It found me. The blow was enormous — the kind of damage that lands differently when you have no immediate recourse, when you cannot immediately close back in and return the favor. I deflected twelve of the bludgeoning. The rest I accepted. The poison damage I noted and filed away beside the indignity.

*Thanks, buddy.*

I said it quietly. I doubt anyone heard. It felt like the appropriate response — dry, clipped, and entirely sincere in its lack of sentiment. The heart fungus had earned its acknowledgment. It had done exactly what the situation called for, which is the most I ask of anything.

I remained where I was. Frightened. Grounded by something I could not approach. Watching from the ceiling while the rest of them worked.

It was not where I intended to be.
