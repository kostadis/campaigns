## Section — Grygum — Chaos in the Fungal Cavern

The insect plague announced itself the way truly bad ideas do — all at once, everywhere, with no warning that would have changed anything.

Ilvara's sphere of swarming, chittering darkness bloomed at the cave entrance like a living bruise, and within seconds I was in it. The sound alone was enough to make a person's skin crawl before the actual biting started. It caught Daz, it caught me, and it caught Glabbagool — who had been, in his earnest gelatinous way, simply trying to participate in the conversation.

My first thought was tactical. My second thought was *oh, how about Glabbagool?*

He took twenty-six points of damage from a cloud of insects that did not know or care that he was a sentient being who had chosen, with genuine moral agency, to join our cause. He absorbed it. Survived it. I noted the number with the part of my mind that tracks debts and obligations, because Glabbagool's survival in this fight had cost him something real, and that matters even if no one else was writing it down.

Then Jorlan Duskryn broke from the line and charged.

He was shouting in Drow — her name, probably, or something close to it. The scarred face, the burning eyes. I had the full context by this point. Ilvara had discarded him when the scars came. He had spent however long since then being discarded and furious about it, and now she was here and he was going to do something about it. Lovers' quarrel. Zuggtmoy corruption. Combined for maximum tactical inconvenience.

I noted the situation. Filed it. Arrived at my position on it quickly: *I don't want to get in the middle of it, but I'll follow up if necessary.*

Bahamut's wisdom, applied to Drow relationship drama. You take the tools where you find them.

The thing about interpersonal catastrophes is they require a certain kind of witness — someone close enough to intervene if it tips fatal, far enough away not to catch a stray emotion. I moved past Jorlan at what I calculated was the appropriate diplomatic distance. Whatever was between him and the woman who had humiliated him was not mine to carry. What was mine was everything else in this cave, and there was plenty.

Thorin, meanwhile, had a question about the big heart fungus.

"Is it resistant to stuff?" he asked, which was the right question. I told him it wasn't resistant to swords, which was my contribution to the scholarship. The DM added that it was doubly vulnerable to radiant damage, which was Dawnbringer's entire wheelhouse. Thorin descended through the difficult terrain, reached the fungus, and hit it once.

It died.

The elaborate construction — the careful placement on the battle map, the icon the DM had clearly spent time creating — collapsed in a single round of radiant fury. I watched it happen. The sun-blade did not know what the DM had planned for it, and did not especially care.

*Boy, I had so much prepared for the fungus. Oh well.*

I told him it was beautiful. I meant it. "I liked it very much, Gustatus." The DM had made something worth appreciating, even briefly. The accounting of what we spend time on doesn't stop being meaningful just because the outcome was fast.

My Guiding Bolt missed Ilvara entirely. She used the moment well — fungal growth to slow Jorlan, concentration dropped, divine fire called down on Daz and Nym. Thorin struck the bridge beneath her feet and it cracked and fell and she leaped clear, because of course she did. People like Ilvara do not simply fall.

Then the reinforcements arrived.

"Just when you thought everything was under control..." the DM said, and five drow stepped out of the cavern shadows.

I looked at them. I looked at the insect plague still churning through the air between us.

"This is turning into the 12 days of Christmas," I said.

The DM expressed hope that it would be more pleasant. I sang *Dark Elves* to the appropriate tune. The five drow were already irritated that they were about to walk into the poison. They had been hiding, hoping not to deal with any of this, and now they were dealing with all of it. That is the condition of being a minion. I noted it. "It's tough being a minion." You don't always choose the catastrophe you're summoned into.

The drow mage fired a blind lightning bolt that found nothing. Daz answered with an empowered fireball that lit the tunnel orange and solved the guard problem entirely. Then the question of what our *current* minions were took a moment of genuine accounting.

"If anybody is too saddened by the loss of any of our minions," I offered, "I do have a Revivify spell I could use to bring one of them back."

The DM reminded me those weren't our minions. They were Ilvara's.

"We used to have so many people traveling with us," I said. "I forgot."

It was sheepish honesty and I stand by it. At some point in the Underdark you lose track of whether the bodies belong to your column or theirs. I take notes. I had apparently missed one.

More fungus appeared. I asked if it was more fungus among us. The DM clarified it was different this time. Things were different. I accepted this.

And then Ilvara was still there, still standing, still a problem — battered from a forty-foot drop, howling something about Zuggtmoy and the bride, about chaos and vengeance, the words of a woman who had traded herself for power and was now watching the investment collapse. She once again howled her desire for vengeance. I noted the drama, appreciated the commitment, and concluded that radiant damage was the appropriate response.

*Ilvara seems like she's still trouble*, I thought, which was perhaps the year's most significant understatement.

The Guiding Bolt found her this time. I am a cleric of Bahamut. Radiant damage is not an afterthought. The light struck true, and Ilvara — the former high priestess, the corrupted servant of Zuggtmoy, the woman who had dumped Jorlan Duskryn when his face got inconveniently scarred — did not simply fall.

She detonated.

The spore cloud was massive and immediate and thoroughly indiscriminate. Zalthir and Thorin caught the edge of it and staggered. I had been standing back, which was either tactical wisdom or a coincidence of positioning, and I will accept credit for either interpretation.

"Now that the fungus is gone," I said, dropping concentration, "I'm gonna drop concentration, so that spell disappears."

The insect plague dissipated. The House T'sarran elite warrior surveyed the wreckage, calculated her odds, promised we would meet again, and withdrew — the matron mother would hear about this, apparently. I noted it. Favors flow in both directions; so do threats.

And in the settling spores and the sudden comparative quiet, Asha Vandree went to her knees.

She began to pray. The words were Lolth's. I listened without comment, because I am not in the business of telling people which god to thank when they're alive and shouldn't be. I took my own moment of quiet gratitude in a different direction — Bahamut, who presumably noticed, and Glabbagool, who was definitely still alive, and the simple accounting fact that we had all survived something that was specifically designed to kill us.

A hoard is always valuable. A blessing from Bahamut.

The spores drifted down like very poisonous snow, and I stood in them, and I waited to see what the silence would ask of us next.
