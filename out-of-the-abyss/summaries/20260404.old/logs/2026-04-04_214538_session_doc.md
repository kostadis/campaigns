## Section — Grygum — The Fall of Ilvara

The bolt was already building in my hand when I made the decision.

Let me back up. For most of this fight I had been in observation mode — which is a polite way of saying I was watching Jorlan Duskryn and Ilvara Mizzrym tear at each other like competing bad ideas, and finding it genuinely entertaining. You don't often get to watch your primary antagonist and a lover's grudge collide in real time. There's a whole drama there — she discarded him when he got the scar, he's been carrying that wound ever since, and now here they both are at the bottom of a ruined bridge with Zuggtmoy's rot flowering through everything she touches. I took mental notes. It's what I do.

I'm amused by the Jorlan-Ilvara drama, I thought. But I think I'm still going to go ahead and attack them.

The entertainment of watching enemies tear at each other is free. But Bahamut does not, in my experience, favor spectatorship. I have never once received a divine message that said *wait this one out, it's interesting.* He is more of a *you see the weakness, now act* kind of god. And Ilvara was still standing, still murmuring pieces of prophecy about Zuggtmoy and the bride and all the chaos to come — the kind of muttering that sounds unhinged until it turns out to be accurate, and I have seen enough of this Underdark to know better than to dismiss it. She was already something other than the woman she had been. The corruption had been working on her for a long time.

The bolt was already building.

Thorin had already toppled the bridge, sent both of them crashing to the cavern floor — classic Thorin, total commitment, Dawnbringer still humming with residual radiance the way a tuning fork hums after you strike it. Zalthir had been up and down and around, doing what Zalthir does, the entire fight a series of surgical moves delivered with the emotional register of someone sorting correspondence. But Ilvara was still here. Still muttering. Still Zuggtmoy's.

There is a version of Guiding Bolt that I think of as administrative — you point it, it works, enemy takes radiant damage, case closed. And then there is the other version, the one that happens when everything has led to this particular moment and the light in your hand feels like it has been waiting. I have seen enough corrupted things in this Underdark — the egg, the grove, the Pudding King's kingdom of rot — to know what it looks like when something has been claimed entirely by a power it should never have touched. Ilvara was not being aided by Zuggtmoy anymore. She *was* Zuggtmoy's work, walking around in a drow priestess's bones.

The bolt left my hand.

It lanced across the cavern floor in a concentrated column of radiant light and found her where she stood.

She did not die quietly. She detonated. Her body ruptured outward in a massive toxic bloom — a cloud of poisonous spores billowing through the cavern in every direction, the demonic corruption venting itself all at once in the only form it had left. Zalthir and Thorin staggered in it. I was already standing back.

Not ideal, I thought, immediately practical again. How big is the cloud?

I was glad I was standing back.

That is the whole thought. That is the entire reflection on delivering the killing blow to a demonically corrupted high priestess of Lolth in the middle of the Underdark: *not ideal, how big is the cloud, glad I was standing back.* Bahamut gets the credit for the radiance. I get credit for the positioning. We understand each other, Bahamut and I.

The cavern settled into a thick, spore-choked silence. The heart fungus was already a collapsed ruin — Thorin had seen to that in one devastating, almost insulting round, while I stood there with everything I'd prepared for it and nowhere to deploy it. *Boy, I had so much planned for the fungus.* The bridge was rubble. The poisonous bloom was already beginning to thin and drift. And somewhere in the settling quiet, Asha Vandree dropped to her knees and began murmuring prayers, her voice the only sound left — words to Lolth, rising from the wreckage, already reshaping what she'd witnessed into something her goddess could use.

Whatever theology she was building out of this moment, I filed it under *watch this space.*

The last House T'sarran warrior looked at all of it — his mage dead, his guards incinerated, Ilvara nothing but settling spores and a fading prophecy about a bride — and stepped back toward the tunnels without urgency. "We will meet again," he said. "Enjoy your victory for the moment."

I took note of that too. A threat delivered without heat is always more credible than one made in anger. I'll want to remember that name. The matron mother of House T'sarran would be hearing about this. I believe I should find out who that is before she finds out who we are.

My first real question, once the cloud was thin enough to breathe through, was about Glabbagool.
