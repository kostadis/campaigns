## Daz — Daz Under Fire

The first bolt hit my shield ward before I'd consciously decided to cast it.

That's the thing about Shield — it's a reaction, which means the body decides before the mind does. One moment I was watching three Drow warriors step out of concealment with the particular ease of people who think they've caught something interesting in their sights. The next moment arcane force was already flaring around me, and the bolt that would have found my ribs clattered away into the dark instead. My hand was still raised. My breathing had not changed.

I spent a moment noting the look on their faces. That specific expression — the slight recalibration when the target doesn't go down. I find it useful to catalogue.

*Three Drow warriors. Crossbow formation. Tactical positions chosen in advance. Someone prepared for this.*

The cost was one spell slot. The benefit was that I was still standing. These are acceptable terms.

I had been standing in the open because the alternative was cowering behind cover while my companions fought around me, and I had already resolved — some time ago, in a conversation with Nym Duskryn that I'm not sure she understood was a conversation — that I was not going to do that. Nym's position is that I am a package. A delivery. Something to be kept intact until she receives payment. Kaelira had actually grabbed my arm at some point, eyes blazing with the kind of urgent sincerity that suggests she's either genuinely concerned or has been told to perform concern, and said — *come with me if you want to live, you idiot* — which I thought was somewhat overstated, but I understood the sentiment.

Nym was more direct. *Can you please keep your head down and out of combat. We only get paid if you live.*

I find transactional arguments interesting when they're offered as though they should be persuasive to the person whose life is being treated as currency. The logic runs: *Daz, you should hide, because we get paid when you live.* The invisible premise is that I should care about whether Nym gets paid. I don't, particularly. What I care about is that Thorin and Grygum and Zalthir are in active combat with a corrupted high priestess and a field of biting insects and what appears to be an inexhaustible supply of Drow reinforcements, and the idea of standing in a shadow while that happens is — I want to find a precise word — *unacceptable.*

So I told her: *I am not going to leave. Everyone here is fighting for me without me participating. I don't give a damn if you get paid. The best way for you to get paid is to continue the fight and prevent all of us from dying.*

There was a pause. Nym turned to Kaelira. *Can I kill your brother?*

I assume she meant Jorlan, not me. I chose not to clarify.

What had just happened on the battlefield, while I was establishing these principles, was that Ilvara had cast Insect Plague. Which is — I want to be accurate about this — a genuinely significant spell. The kind of spell that reshapes terrain. A swirling column of biting, stinging insects filling a twenty-foot sphere, scouring everything inside it. The fungal minions, who had thirteen hit points between ambition and circumstance, ceased to exist. Glabbagool — our sentient, gentle, luminously good-natured ooze companion who had been, from what I could tell, simply trying to participate in the ambient conversation of the battle — took twenty-six points of damage and survived. I find the fact that Glabbagool survived reassuring. He doesn't deserve what this cavern keeps throwing at him.

And then, just as the smoke from the fungal deaths was still settling, five more Drow stepped out of the dark.

Zalthir counted them with the tone of someone who has been told dinner will be delayed again. Grygum observed that this was turning into the twelve days of Christmas. I looked at the five new arrivals and performed a rapid assessment: armed, positioned, and immediately furious, because Ilvara's own Insect Plague — the spell she had just cast to devastate our side of the field — was now swirling around *them* as well. They had been hiding in the affected area. They stepped out into it.

I want to be balanced about this. These were soldiers following orders, and being stung to pieces by your own mistress's magic is genuinely unpleasant, and they had not chosen to be born into a society where that was an occupational hazard. But they had just fired crossbow bolts at me. Three of them. In a coordinated volley. With crossbows.

I was prepared to be balanced about it later.

The question of what spell to use is, in the end, always a question of geometry. You are managing a volume. You are thinking about who is inside it, who is outside it, and how much of what you need is inside relative to the damage you're prepared to accept on the edges. An Evoker — and I am an Evoker, trained in Menzoberranzan where the destructive arts were considered the only magic worth taking seriously — learns to carve exceptions into area spells. The ability is called Sculpt Spells, and it means I can reach into a Fireball and pull specific individuals out of harm's way, leaving the fire to do its work on everything else in the radius.

I identified my allies. I identified my targets. I cast Fireball in a shaped exclusion around everything I wanted to keep.

The cavern went very bright for a moment.

The Drow warriors who had been irritated about the Insect Plague were no longer irritated about the Insect Plague.

While the light was fading, I registered that Asha Vandree was somewhere in my peripheral vision. This is the problem with mid-combat, when you're managing threats on three axes — you can't give full attention to the secondary problem. The secondary problem, in this case, is that Asha believes I am a devout worshipper of Lolth who has somewhat unorthodoxly accepted a team of surface-world companions as tactical assets. She believes this because I told her so. She has not asked follow-up questions, which suggests either that she finds the explanation sufficient or that she is more interested in Ilvara's death than in my theology. Either outcome serves.

When the five Drow arrived and Asha's immediate instinct was that they must be traitors, I said, without any particular hesitation: *No, these are friends of Lolth, and they're here to help.*

There is a quality of statement that is so completely confident that it skips over the step where someone decides whether to believe it. You simply assert the thing. You use the target's own categories against them. Asha is a Lolth-devotee; she has a ready-made slot in her worldview for "Lolth-aligned drow who are here to help." I put the Duskryn sisters in that slot and moved on. The lie barely costs anything when you're already committed to the architecture.

Grygum, at some point in this sequence, observed that the situation was "accurately described" by Asha's characterization of me as an insane mage who had converted to Lolthism. He meant it kindly. Or as kindly as Grygum observes anything, which is with the steady, clear-eyed precision of a man keeping accurate records.

*What's important? That she thinks it, or that it's real?*

I know what's real. I know what I've left behind and what I haven't and what Menzoberranzan made me and what the Underdark has cost me and what I believe now, privately, about slavery and free people and the basis of any society. I know all of that. But right now, in this cavern, Asha thinks I serve Lolth, and that belief is load-bearing.

So we let it stand.

Then Thorin reached the Heart Fungus.

I had been peripherally aware of the Fungus — the centerpiece creature, the battlefield anchor, the thing that had been pumping spores and fungal reinforcements into the fight. Thorin had been trying to get to it across a sprawling, complicated cavern layout for most of the encounter, and watching him navigate that frustration had been like watching a very determined hammer trying to cross a room full of furniture. When he finally reached it and brought Dawnbringer down —

The fungus wilted. One round. Radiant damage against something with deep vulnerability to radiant damage. The cavern filled briefly with the smell of cooked mushroom.

Thorin said: *I do what I can.*

The GM — and I say this with genuine sympathy — mentioned that he had spent a great deal of time on the icon for the Heart Fungus. Grygum said it had been beautiful. Zalthir agreed it had been pretty. There was a brief, sincere memorial for a carefully crafted encounter piece that lasted approximately six seconds of game time. I found I did not have anything to add, because Dawnbringer had already moved on, and so had the fight, and there were still Drow warriors in the cavern who had fired crossbow bolts at me and then been Fireballed and might need to be addressed again, and Ilvara was still somewhere in the dark, howling about Zuggtmoy and the bride and what was coming.

We were not done. But the geometry of the battlefield had shifted, and I was still standing, and the shield had held when it mattered.

The con was still running.
