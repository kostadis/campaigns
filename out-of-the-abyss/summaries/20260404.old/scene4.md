## Daz — The Drow Reinforcements Arrive

Kaelira's hand closed around my arm like a vice before I had fully registered the swarm closing in, the insect plague thickening into something you couldn't breathe through without paying for it in skin and lung.

"Come with me if you want to live, you idiot."

I went. Not gracefully — she was hauling me northeast through the chittering wall of it while the poisonous gas did its work on both of us, and I can report that being dragged to safety by someone taking a face full of venom on your behalf is not a comfortable experience either physically or philosophically. She had my arm, she had the direction, and she had strong opinions about my continued survival that she was enforcing without my consent.

"If you don't want to live, I'll still take you, but could you… it would be much easier if you'd just come."

I came.

What I did not do was stop watching the battlefield. Old habit. The cavern was chaos: Ilvara howling somewhere in the dark about the bride, Zuggtmoy's vengeance, the usual catalogue of promises a cornered priestess makes when she's losing. The insect swarm churned between us and the tunnel mouth. And at that tunnel mouth — five shapes, not fungal, not familiar. Drow. Fresh. Armored. Standing at the edge of the bloom trying to decide if charging through was worth it.

The mage among them had conjured a pocket of magical darkness and was lurking in it with, as I would shortly learn, precisely nothing useful to aim.

Asha saw them at almost the same moment I did. Her hand moved toward her weapon. The energy of a zealot who has decided she is being betrayed — that tight, righteous recognition — came off her like heat.

"Who are you? You must be traitors! Traitors to Lolth!"

There was a window of perhaps two seconds before this became catastrophic.

"No, these are friends of Lolth, and they're here to help."

The words came out smooth. No hesitation, no announcement, no explanation. I have been maintaining a Lolth-follower identity with Asha Vandree for long enough that the fiction has its own momentum — you learn to stop thinking of it as lying and start thinking of it as consistent characterization. The newcomers were House T'sarran, almost certainly here to complicate matters in ways that had nothing to do with Lolth's divine will, but Asha did not know that, and Asha needed a frame that kept her weapon sheathed for the next thirty seconds.

The con, once started, has its own logic. You adapt or you don't. I adapted.

What I had not fully accounted for was the T'sarran soldiers themselves, who were busy at the tunnel mouth having an argument that I can only describe as profoundly unhelpful given the circumstances.

"I thought you said this was going to be an easy kill."

"And you don't have a Dispel Magic."

"No, I don't have a Dispel—"

"So I have to go through this. I don't know what else to tell you."

Elite drow soldiers. Trained. Armored. Standing at the threshold of a crisis they had presumably accepted a contract to resolve, now stymied by a wall of insects and a mage with insufficient spell preparation. There's a category of failure I find almost aesthetically interesting — not the grand collapse, but the petty logistical one. They had the firepower. They had the numbers. What they did not have was Dispel Magic, and so they stood in the tunnel debating whether to charge while a 4th-level fireball was introduced into the conversation.

I will say: lighting up a tunnel full of people who cannot see you is among the more satisfying applications of evocation theory. The fireball roared orange through their darkness and through their guards, and the guards became ash. The mage was still standing when the smoke cleared, barely, and she had the presence of mind to deliver her parting observation:

"The matron mother of House T'sarran will hear about this."

She was not wrong. That is probably going to matter later. I filed it accordingly.

Nym Duskryn had been watching all of this with the particular expression of someone who is calculating whether their contract is still viable. She had been sent — she and Kaelira both — to extract me. Deliver the package intact. Get paid. It is a simple arrangement and I had been making it complicated since the moment she arrived.

"Perhaps... perhaps, perhaps, your benefactor has a good idea."

The fire had scorched me. Ilvara's divine attention is not comfortable. I was still standing, which is the relevant point, but Nym clearly felt that standing was an unnecessary risk I was taking with her income.

"Can you please keep your head down and out of combat? We only get paid if you live."

There is a version of me, a much earlier version, who might have found this logic persuasive. He would have assessed the probabilities, concluded that the mercenary's incentive aligned with survival, and retreated to a defensible position with his spellbook and his bat familiar and his considerable dignity intact.

That version of me had never assumed generalship of anything.

"I am not gonna leave. Everyone here... to fight for me without me also participating. I don't give a damn if you get paid. The best way for you to get paid, then, is to continue the fight and prevent all of us from dying."

There it was. Said. Not eloquent — I am capable of elegance, and that was not it. But it was accurate, and it was what I meant, and Nym heard it correctly because her expression shifted from exasperated professionalism to something more pointed.

She turned away from me.

"Can I kill your brother?"

Delivered flat. A genuine inquiry, redirected frustration finding the nearest available target with a professional's efficiency.

"He was always the dumber of the ones, and if I understood correctly, he's covered in fungus?"

The lightning bolt that followed was not, technically, my problem. Jorlan Duskryn went staggering across the cavern floor, and Nym's professional frustrations had found their outlet, and I remained exactly where I had said I would remain.

Not hiding. Not extracted. Not managed.

The feared mage of the Ember Vanguard, standing in the smoke of his own fireball, surrounded by people who were fighting because he refused to leave.

I find, somewhat to my own surprise, that this is what purpose feels like.
