The air changed first.

It thinned in a way that had nothing to do with depth — it simply became something else. Something with weather in it. Something that moved without being asked to. The passage out of Blingdenstone's tunnels opened onto the Silver Marches in full mid-spring afternoon, and the sky landed on me like a physical thing, like a hand pressed flat against my face.

I did not stop walking. I noticed the sensation, catalogued it, filed it under *adjustment required*, and kept moving. My eyes needed a moment they were not going to get. The light was everywhere — not the bioluminescent smear of fungal colonies, not the cold directed gleam of Dawnbringer, not the faerzress shimmer that had haunted the deep corridors for months. This was light that came from above and had nowhere else to be. It hit stone and grass and ox-flank and refugee and all of us with the same indiscriminate abundance, and none of it asked permission.

The monastery, I thought — almost despite myself — had a curriculum for this. There was a class about returning to surface conditions after extended underground travel. I believe it was Brother Tharusk. Or possibly the one after him. I don't remember the name clearly because the light through the shuttered window of the instruction room was more interesting than whatever was being said. I am paying for that now, in the currency of squinting.

Eldeth said her goodbyes at the mouth of the exit. She had earned them. I watched her go with the same attention I give to anything that has been a known quantity and is ceasing to be — a neutral inventory. She had been capable. She had been, at moments, more than capable. The word I would not use was *good*, because good was imprecise and I was not in the habit of imprecision.

We turned south.

The land told its own story without asking us to listen. The Silver Marches had been swept by something — the evidence was everywhere if you read it correctly, and I read it the way I read tunnels: shape first, then cause, then threat assessment. Burned hamlets. Timbers still leaning in the postures they'd died in, blackened and rain-softened, too far gone to call ruins, not far enough gone to call landscape. A standing stone broken at the base and tipped into the earth at an angle that said weight and intention rather than erosion. A Tempus shrine with whatever consecration it had once held scraped or beaten away, left as hollow architecture. Barbarian hordes, I decided. After the War of the Giants. The land had absorbed it the way land absorbs everything — not recovered, not healed, just quieted.

There was a refugee family on the road. Three generations, hand cart, one ox. No grandfather. I counted the generations and noted the gap without marking it further. They were heading south. So were we.

Grygum slowed near the Tempus shrine. I watched him register the defilement, perform the small internal calculation of a cleric confronted with desecration that is not his god's desecration, and arrive at the reasonable middle position: a brief reverence, a muttered blessing, the genuflection of someone who doesn't like seeing sacred things mistreated regardless of which name they were sacred to. He was a practical man under the theology. I had noticed this repeatedly and not mentioned it once, because it was not my business and was not a threat.

Thorin did not respond to the shrine in any particular way. He was present. His grapple with whatever the months underground had done to him was ongoing and I was not going to name it for him.

When I was asked whether I had anything to offer — the shrine, the hamlet, the moment — I considered the question. The honest answer was that I was still learning to look at the sky without wanting to look away from it. That I was calibrating. That the surface was enormous in a way the Underdark was not, because the Underdark was enormous like a labyrinth is enormous — directional, navigable, ultimately legible — while this was enormous like a ceiling without a ceiling, and something in me that had been working in close quarters for months did not yet have the appropriate category for it.

I said I was supportive. It was accurate. It was also the only thing worth saying.

The road continued south. The family's ox moved at the pace of an ox. I matched my pace to the landscape rather than to my preferences and watched the sky from the side of my vision where it was less immediately insistent.

Glabbagool was in the bag of holding. He had, at some earlier point — I had been there; I had watched — looked up at an open sky and demonstrated curiosity rather than retreat. I had no words for him in that moment, no shared language, the Stonespeaker Crystal being Daz's instrument. But I had noted the response. Filed it. What I remembered was that Glabbagool had come through the ooze crisis, the Pudding King, the bag, the months, and had still looked up.

I found that admirable in a way I was not going to say out loud.

The Silver Marches stretched south. The light did not diminish. My eyes, eventually, would adjust. That was what eyes did — they adjusted to conditions, they stopped fighting what was simply true about the environment, and they began to function. The monastery had a saying about this. I believe it was Brother Heslin who said it, or possibly the tall one who always smelled of incense — I was not paying close attention during that particular lesson either — but the shape of it was something like: *the first step to moving through a new terrain is to stop expecting it to be the old terrain.*

My eyes adjusted. The sky remained enormous. I kept walking south.
