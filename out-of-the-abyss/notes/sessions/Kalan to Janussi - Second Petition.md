# Second Petition for a Chamber of Evidentiary Practice

*Kalan Strongbranch, Gatewarden, to Janussi, Keeper of Tomes*

**Subject:** Second petition for an evidentiary chamber  
**Disposition requested:** Authorization and modest funding

Keeper Janussi,

You described my earlier proposal as “solipsism by lesser minds”—a discipline devised by those unable to recognize truth when a sufficiently learned person places it before them.

I have considered the criticism. I remain unable to accept it.

The proposal does not claim that truth is unknowable. It claims that every means by which we know a thing possesses a corresponding means by which we may be deceived.

A witness may be honest and remember an illusion. A corpse may answer accurately and have mistaken the face above it. A truth-working may prevent a lie without compelling an answer. A divination may be blocked, redirected, or asked the wrong question. Matter may be planted. Memory may be altered. An aura may identify a working without identifying its author.

Learning reduces these dangers. Rank does not abolish them.

**Rank is not an evidentiary root.**

I therefore renew my petition for a one-year trial of the Threefold Practice. In any death, disappearance, theft from the restricted collections, or suspected violation of the wards, the inquiry would distinguish three roots of evidence:

1. **Matter:** bodies, wounds, residue, handwriting, locks, fibers, prints, components, ledgers, and other physical traces.
2. **Memory:** living witnesses, animal reactions, contemporaneous notes, familiar reports, and testimony recovered from the dead.
3. **Working:** active magic, residual auras, damaged wards, divinations attempted, divinations obstructed, and every spell employed by investigators upon the scene.

No consequential finding would rest upon one root where magical interference is plausible. Where identity is disputed, all three would be sought.

This requires procedure, not philosophy.

I request two adjoining rooms beneath the Sea Warden’s Tower; one alchemical examiner; two copying clerks; lead-lined cabinets; paired seal-dies held separately by the Gatewarden and Lorekeeper; and sufficient warding for twelve evidence lockers. The estimated first-year expense is 1,840 gold pieces, declining by nearly half thereafter.

Every person entering a secured scene would be named. Every spell cast upon a body or object would be recorded with its caster, target, time, and exact result. Samples would be divided before examination. One portion would remain unworked. No examiner—including a Great Reader—would retain sole custody of evidence they interpreted.

You objected that these requirements would delay competent officers.

They would.

So do locks.

The inconvenience is the safeguard.

Our present practice assumes that a sufficiently senior Avowed can observe, interpret, preserve, and report without introducing error or interest. That is not a method. It is confidence in a person. Candlekeep exists because confidence in persons is not enough. We preserve texts precisely because memory, authority, and tradition fail.

We should not become less rigorous when the subject is a body rather than a book.

You also objected that no procedure can defeat every conceivable magic. This is true and irrelevant. A door need not resist every possible creature before it is worth closing.

The useful question is not whether one clue can be forged. It is what time, access, knowledge, and power would be required to forge several independent clues without causing them to contradict one another.

I have tested this proposition against the respondent in the Chambers of Lost Lore. Its answers were indirect, as they often are, but it could provide no example of a single deception that survives every independent channel without cost or residue. It observed—correctly, I think—that perfect concealment is merely another name for power sufficient to govern the investigation itself.

That answer troubles me more than it comforts me.

If the proposal is rejected, I ask that the rejection and its stated grounds be entered into the Gatewarden’s permanent register. If it is accepted, I will assume personal responsibility for the trial and submit its results to hostile review after one year.

I do not ask you to trust my theory.

I ask you to make it possible for those who follow us to discover when we were wrong.

**Kalan Strongbranch**  
Gatewarden of Candlekeep  
Keeper of Secrets
