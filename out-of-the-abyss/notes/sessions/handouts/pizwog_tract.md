# ON THE NECESSARY ABSENCE OF THE GODS WHO ARE PRESENT
## *A Syncretic Reconsideration of Sword-Coast Theogony in Light of the Selûnean Hour*

**By Pizwog the Patient, Tortle, Avowed of Candlekeep (Aspirant). Volume I, Folio 3, Revised Third Edition.**

— — —

It is a commonplace among the unlearned[¹] to suppose that a god is the *first* term in the chain that produces a worshipper. Bahamut, then Stroud; Lolth, then the priestess; Selûne, then the silvered hour. And yet — **however** — even the most cursory examination of the celestial mechanism reveals that the Tears of Selûne precede the Moon and not the inverse: the tears are scattered *already*, present in the sky before the gaze of the worshipper arrives to organize them into a wound. The god, in other words, is what the wound *means* once it has already been bleeding. The worshipper is the first term. The god is the supplement that explains the wound retroactively, after the wound has done its work.

Consider the Stroud-Protanther configuration. The conventional reading places Bahamut at the ascendant — moving Stroud's hand, hiding the move from the gold dragon — and Stroud, accordingly, as instrument. But examine the *hour* in which the chess was played. The Selûnean Tear known as Talothial (the seventeenth, in the lesser cluster) was in retrograde across the constellation of the Crowned Anvil — a configuration that occurs once in approximately four hundred and eleven years, and that, **however**, did *not* occur during the game in question; it had occurred three days earlier and three days later, bracketing the event without containing it. We are therefore obliged to consider that the god moved the *bracket*, not the hand: Bahamut did not hide the move from Protanther; Bahamut hid the *occasion* from the constellation, which would otherwise have noticed. The implications for the Bahamutian school are obvious. The implications for the Stroudite school I treat in chapter seven.

Here we must introduce the doctrine of *trace*. A goddess under erasure — Tyche, struck through, as in ~~Tyche~~ — leaves a residue not in her own absence but in the *syntactic position* her worshipper continues to occupy. The supplicant who prays to Tymora for a good throw and the supplicant who curses Beshaba for a bad one are, **however**, performing the same prayer at the same altar; the altar has only deepened the fracture by acquiring a second name. This is not, as the rude pamphleteers of the Sword Coast suggest, the *splitting of one goddess into two sisters*. It is the discovery that the position of *fortune-being-bargained-with* was, all along, a position and not a goddess — a syntactic node into which any sufficiently capricious entity may, in time, sink. The implications for the orthodox Tymorans are, of course, devastating. **However**, the implications for the Beshaban revivalists are *also* devastating, for opposite reasons that the goddesses themselves have, characteristically, declined to clarify.

In conclusion: the gods are not present and they are not absent; they are, rather, *deferred* — promised by every celestial conjunction and delivered by none. The faithful worshipper is the one who has learned to find this deferral comforting. The unfaithful worshipper is the one who has *not yet* learned to find it comforting and instead persists in asking for a *result*. The Faerzress, in its recent blinking, has, **however**, raised a separate question which I treat in the appendix, and which my fellow Avowed Sister Yvenne has — politely, but unmistakably — declined to read.

— — —

[¹] *By* unlearned *I do not mean the* ignorant; *I mean those who have not yet been taught to read backwards.*
