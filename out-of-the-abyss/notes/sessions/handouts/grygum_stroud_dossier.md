## II. The four schools — one polemic each

### a. The Stroudite reading (establishment position)

> *Bahamut, in his patience and in his judgment of the prides of
> his children, hid the move from Protanther for four hundred
> years and gave it to Stroud kin-of-Ortho when the Cult of
> Tiamat threatened the Realms. The dragon was prepared in every
> variation his pride could imagine. The dwarf was given the
> variation his pride could not. The win is grace. The alliance
> that followed is the work the grace was for. To read the match
> any other way is to flatter the dragon at the expense of the
> god.*
>
> — From a sermon delivered by **Sister Halena Brassmark** at the
> Temple of Bahamut in Triboar, the second year after the match.

### b. The Protantherist reading (humility-by-choice)

> *Let us not insult Protanther by claiming his loss was hidden
> from him. He is a gold dragon and a council elder; he reads
> chess as he reads weather. He saw the move. He chose to let
> himself be moved by it. The teaching is in the choosing —
> a great being humbling himself to a mortal who arrived not by
> wisdom but by inheritance. The win is real. The reading is
> Protanther's. To call it Bahamut's is to take the work out of
> the dragon's hands.*
>
> — From an essay by **Master Ulvian Crest,** wandering scholar of
> draconic ethics, third year after the match.

### c. The Skeptical reading (mundane upset)

> *Stroud is a charming man and a poor chess player. Protanther
> is an ancient dragon whose four hundred years of mental
> rehearsal had drift, as four hundred years of anything will
> have. The dwarf made a move that had not been considered, not
> because it was hidden by a god, but because it was outside
> the analysis tree of a being who had been considering only the
> moves a serious player would make. After the loss, Protanther
> needed an alliance against the Cult of Tiamat that he could
> not credibly demand from a position of pride. The Bahamut
> reading is the political cover. The dragons are not lying.
> They are theologizing the necessary. We do them no favor by
> calling it grace.*
>
> — From a private letter by **the philosopher Daerlin Voss,**
> circulated in three copies. Vareth has the second. The first
> was destroyed by the recipient.

### d. The Reconciled reading

> *Both Stroud's win and Protanther's reading were sincere. The
> match was a moment when something happened that neither the
> players nor the witnesses fully understand. The win is real;
> the theology is honest; the meaning remains contested because
> it is contested in itself. To pick a school is to close a
> door that was not built to be closed. We are three years from
> the event. Read again in twenty.*
>
> — Inscribed on a scroll left at the door of the Bahamut shrine
> in Beregost, unsigned. Vareth attributes it tentatively to the
> council dragon **Illeurath.**

Vareth's marginal note: *"I have not yet decided. Don't take that
as guidance. I am paid to keep the question open."*

## III. The unsigned sting

*[A copy of an annotation found on the back of one of Stroud's
earliest post-match letters to Candlekeep. The hand is not
Stroud's. The ink is darker than the original letter. Written
within the past three years.]*

> *"He let you win, old friend."*

**Unsigned.** Vareth has been trying to identify the handwriting
since he discovered the annotation. He has not succeeded.

The phrase **"old friend"** most plausibly refers not to Stroud
but to **Ortho** — Stroud's ancestor, Protanther's actual old
friend, whose unfinished game Stroud finished. If the writer
intended Ortho, the annotation reframes the win as a continuation
of an already-friendly relationship rather than a divinely-
engineered humbling.

**Suspect pool** (Vareth's narrowed list, all unconfirmed):

- **Protanther himself** in mortal-form visit to Candlekeep
- **One of the council dragons** — most plausibly
  **Otaaryliakkarnos (Eli),** given his sarcasm about Protanther's
  speeches
- **A Stroud party member** from the Tyranny of Dragons events
- **Another descendant of Ortho** — a Stroud cousin or rival who
  reads the win as inheritance, not divine grace
- A messenger or Avowed (lower probability)

Vareth: *"Take the copy. Show it to Stroud if you wish. He has
not seen it. I have not sent it to him. I leave that to you."*

## IV. The Yartar-tavern letter (the disappointed)

*[A letter from a young Bahamut acolyte who pilgrimaged to find
Stroud in the past year. Vareth has marked it as one of three
letters from the disappointed file he wishes Grygum to read.
Excerpted.]*

> *I came to Yartar with the songs in my head and the chess
> problem on a folded paper in my belt. I asked at three taverns
> before I found him. He was at the fourth. He saw me looking,
> and he waved me to his table without asking who I was, and he
> bought me a drink that he could not afford.*
>
> *He talked for an hour. He told me about the move six times.
> Each time he told it slightly differently. He laughed at the
> same parts each time. He made me play it through on the table
> with cups for pieces. He let me win the cup-game and laughed
> about that too.*
>
> *I left thinking he was not what I had expected. I was
> disappointed. I walked back to my inn and I sat on the bed and
> I tried to write you this letter the next day, and I did not
> know how to begin.*
>
> *I think I have it now.*
>
> *He is not what I expected. He is also not less. I think the
> story is bigger than the man, and that is allowed.*
>
> *I am still a Bahamut acolyte. I am still here. I do not know
> what to do with what I now know about my hero. Perhaps that is
> the work.*
>
> — From **Acolyte Fenny of Triboar,** to Brother Vareth, dated
> last summer.

Vareth's marginal note: *"Read this one when you are tired. Not
when you are bright. It needs the right light."*

## VI. A representative Stroud letter

*[Vareth's pick: not the earliest-tightest, not the latest-most-
mythologized. The one that captures the man.]*

> *Brother Vareth —*
>
> *I have been told you are collecting the writings around our
> match, which is a kind thing for you to do, and I am grateful.
> Let me give you the version I would tell my grandmother, if
> she had lived to hear it.*
>
> *Protanther was kind. I want that on record. He was a gold
> dragon and he was a kind man. When he saw I was Ortho's, he
> did not ask me to prove it. He polymorphed into a young
> blonde human in light blue clothes — I think he chose the
> colors so I would not be afraid — and he conjured the chess
> set out of the air, which I will tell you was the most
> beautiful thing I have ever seen.*
>
> *I made my move and he made his and I made mine and so it
> went. I was sweating. I will admit that. I was not raised to
> play chess against gold dragons.*
>
> *And then I made the move that won.*
>
> *I want to be honest with you, Brother. I do not know if it
> was Bahamut. I will say it was, because Protanther said it
> was, and Protanther knows more about his own loss than I do.
> But in the moment, what I felt was not grace. What I felt was
> Ortho. As if my grandfather's grandfather was standing behind
> me with his hand on my shoulder and finishing the game he
> started. The grace, if it was grace, came through Ortho. I
> did not feel it from above. I felt it from behind.*
>
> *I am writing this five months after the match. I have
> already told it a thousand times. I will tell it a thousand
> more. Every time I tell it I get something a little wrong,
> and every time I correct myself, I have already decided which
> wrong version is mine.*
>
> *Forgive my handwriting. The mead at the inn is good and the
> light is poor.*
>
> *— Stroud, kin of Ortho, dwarf of [clan name TBD by GM]*

Vareth's marginal note: *"He is not a fraud. He is not a saint.
He is the man who finished the game. I have read every letter he
has written to this library and I am still not sure which school
to belong to. I am offering you the question. The schools will
not be settled before you are old. Pick the one you can carry."*
