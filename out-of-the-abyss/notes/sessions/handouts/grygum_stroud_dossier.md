# The Stroud–Protanther Dossier — Grygum's take-home packet

*Compiled by Brother Vareth at Grygum's request. Annotations in
his hand. To be read in the order Grygum prefers.*

## I. The chess problem — *Tessarra's Lock*

The pattern Stroud delivered. Named after **Tessarra Vaeleth,**
elven chess theorist of pre-Spellplague Myth Drannor, whose
*Studies in the Geometry of Endgames* catalogued the position
centuries ago. Chess scholars across Faerûn treat Tessarra's
catalog as foundational; the *Lock* is taught as one of the great
geometric mates.

### The position (set up on a board)

```
    a   b   c   d   e   f   g   h
 8  .   .   .   .   .   .   bK  .
 7  .   .   .   .   wN  .   bP  bP
 6  .   .   .   .   .   .   .   .
 5  .   .   .   .   .   .   wQ  .
 4  .   .   .   .   .   .   .   .
 3  .   .   .   .   .   .   .   .
 2  .   .   .   .   .   .   .   .
 1  .   .   wK  .   .   wR  .   wR
```

**White (Stroud):** King c1, Queen g5, Knight e7, Rook f1, Rook h1.
**Black (Protanther):** King g8, Pawn g7, Pawn h7.
*(Other pieces irrelevant — geometric core only.)*

**White to move. Mate in two.**

### The move

**1. Qxh7+!!** — Queen takes pawn on h7, **sacrificing the queen,**
with check.

Black's only legal response: **1...Kxh7** (the king must capture —
the rook on f1 controls f8, the only escape square, so the king
cannot move; capture is the only way out of check).

After **1...Kxh7**, the king is checkmated *immediately:*

- **g6** — controlled by the white knight on e7
- **g7** — blocked by black's own pawn
- **g8** — controlled by the white knight on e7
- **h6** — controlled by the white rook on h1 (now a clear file)
- **h8** — controlled by the white knight on e7
- The king cannot capture the rook (six squares away). No black
  piece can block.

### Why Protanther's 400-year preparation missed it

The geometry of Tessarra's Lock was always there; what Protanther
had not anticipated was **Stroud's willingness to spend his queen
for a single pawn.** His analysis tree had no branches in which
his opponent voluntarily gave up the most valuable piece. Tessarra
wrote about this exact problem centuries ago. The pattern is *old.*
The application is what surprised the dragon.

Vareth's marginal note: *"I have shown this position to seven
chess scholars of various Realms. Two call the queen sacrifice
the *most* obvious move once you know Tessarra. Three call it
brilliant. One calls it unsporting. One refused to comment. The
geometry is real; the reading of the geometry is the three
schools."*

## II. The three schools — one polemic each

The match has produced three coherent readings, organized by
**worldview** about who has agency in the universe. They cannot
all be right at the same time.

### a. The Stroud school (humanoid-centric)

> *Brother Vareth —*
>
> *I am told you collect the writings around our match, and I
> cannot in good conscience let you build a library on this
> subject without the **primary source.** Let me correct the
> record. Again.*
>
> *The position when I sat down was Tessarra's Lock geometry —
> please consult the diagram I have included on the back, and
> please copy it correctly into your archives this time, I noticed
> a discrepancy in your last filing. Protanther had been turning
> this position over for **four hundred years,** which I will say
> twice because no one seems to absorb it the first time: **four
> hundred years.** You'd think that would be enough.*
>
> *Here is what I saw that he did not. Tessarra wrote about the
> Lock geometry centuries ago — I am a dwarf and I read my chess
> theory, you may consult Annotation 7c of my previous letter
> (filed last Greengrass; you may have it under "Stroud —
> annotated commentary, vol. III"). The point is: he had the king
> moves locked in three different lines, but in each line he had
> also assumed I would do the **expected** thing. I did not. I am
> a dwarf and I do not always do the expected thing, particularly
> when I am sitting across from a polymorphed gold dragon and
> would rather not be there much longer.*
>
> *I have heard the various theories that have grown around what
> I did. Let me address them.*
>
> ***The dragon-folk** want to say Protanther chose to lose. I
> respect Protanther. He did not choose to lose. He polymorphed
> into a young blonde human in light blue and **conjured a chess
> set out of the air** — does that strike you as the behavior of
> a being planning to throw the match? He was there to win. He
> prepared for four centuries. He lost because I was **better than
> him in that one moment.** Take it like an adult.*
>
> ***The pious folk** want to say Bahamut hid the move from him.
> I have great respect for Bahamut, who I now know took an
> interest in my work, and I have great respect for the priests
> who teach his ways. But I would gently note that Bahamut did
> not move my hand. I have hands. I moved them. If Bahamut
> whispered anything to me, the whisper was **go and beat this
> dragon, Stroud, you can do it.** And I did. I believe in divine
> encouragement. I do not believe in divine intervention here,
> because I was there, and I was the one moving the pieces.*
>
> *So credit where credit is due, Brother. To me. There. I have
> said it. You may file this under "primary source — author
> insists, please catalog accordingly."*
>
> *Yours in continuing exasperation that this still requires
> explaining,*
>
> *— Stroud, son of Strakkin, of clan Stoutshield, kin of Ortho,
> chess champion of the Council of Dragons (3 years running,
> undefeated)*
>
> *P.S. The "Stroud Gambit" name was my idea. The opening it
> refers to is **not** what I actually played. I want this
> corrected in the chess literature.*
>
> *P.P.S. Acolyte Fenny of Triboar was at the inn last summer.
> I remember her. She was pleasant. I hope she is well.*

— *On file at Candlekeep, **post-match year 3.** Stroud's most
representative letter; Vareth's pick after reviewing the prolific
3-year archive. **Stroud is the chief advocate of the Stroud
school by virtue of being its loudest exponent.***

### b. The Dragon school (draconic-centric)

> *Let us not insult Protanther by claiming his loss was hidden
> from him. He is a gold dragon and a council elder; he reads
> chess as he reads weather. He saw Tessarra's Lock geometry.
> He saw the queen sacrifice. He chose to let himself be moved
> by it.*
>
> *Consider what we know. Protanther polymorphed into a kind form
> — a young blonde human in light blue, chosen so the dwarf would
> not be afraid. He conjured the chess set as a gift. He
> recognized Stroud as Ortho's kin and asked him to finish a
> game four hundred years older than Stroud's grandfather's
> grandfather. **These are not the gestures of a being trying to
> win.** These are the gestures of a being honoring an old
> friendship across a bloodline.*
>
> *The teaching is in the choosing. A great being humbling
> himself to a mortal who arrived not by wisdom but by inheritance
> — that is grace, but it is **draconic** grace. The win was
> Protanther's gift. To call it Bahamut's is to take the work
> out of the dragon's hands. To call it Stroud's brilliance is to
> miss the gift entirely.*
>
> — From an essay by **Master Ulvian Crest,** wandering scholar
> of draconic ethics. Third year after the match. Vareth has the
> sole copy in his archive; published copies were withdrawn by
> the printer under pressure from Sword Coast Stroudites who
> objected to its framing. **The unsigned sting** *(see section
> IV)* belongs to this school.

### c. The Bahamut school (theological / divine)

> *Bahamut, in his patience and in his judgment of the prides of
> his children, hid Tessarra's geometry from Protanther for four
> hundred years and gave the seeing of it to Stroud kin-of-Ortho
> when the Cult of Tiamat threatened the Realms. The dragon was
> prepared in every variation his pride could imagine; the dwarf
> was given the variation his pride could not. **Tessarra's Lock
> is centuries old. The pattern was always there. The grace was
> in the moment of seeing it.***
>
> *The strongest evidence is the dragon's own testimony. At the
> council, Protanther stood and named the loss as Bahamut's. He
> did not need to. He could have demanded the alliance against
> Tiamat from a position of pride. He did not. He spoke of being
> humbled, of having missed the move, of his ally Ortho's kin
> having been given what he could not see. **The dragon vouched.***
>
> *Those who read the silence after — three years now of Protanther
> declining further public discussion — read it as reverence. The
> testimony was the gift Bahamut required. Silence after is the
> dignity of not adding to what was given.*
>
> — From a sermon delivered by **Sister Halena Brassmark** at the
> Temple of Bahamut in Triboar, the second year after the match.
> The most-circulated Bahamut-school text. Cited heavily by the
> three other living Stroud-Inspired half-orc Bahamut clerics.

Vareth's marginal note: *"I have not picked a school. I am paid
to keep the question open. Take this as the absence of guidance,
not its inverse."*

## III. The disappointed (the most poignant beat)

*[A letter from a young Bahamut acolyte who pilgrimaged to find
Stroud last summer. Vareth has marked it as one of three letters
from the disappointed file he wishes Grygum to read. Excerpted.]*

> *I came to Yartar with the songs in my head and the chess
> problem on a folded paper in my belt. I asked at three taverns
> before I found him. He was at the fourth. He saw me looking,
> and he waved me to his table without asking who I was, and he
> bought me a drink that he could not afford.*
>
> *He talked for an hour. He told me about the move six times.
> Each time he told it slightly differently. He laughed at the
> same parts each time. He made me play it through on the table
> with cups for pieces. He let me win the cup-game and laughed
> about that too.*
>
> *I left thinking he was not what I had expected. I was
> disappointed. I walked back to my inn and I sat on the bed and
> I tried to write you this letter the next day, and I did not
> know how to begin.*
>
> *I think I have it now.*
>
> *He is not what I expected. He is also not less. I think the
> story is bigger than the man, and that is allowed.*
>
> *I am still a Bahamut acolyte. I am still here. I do not know
> what to do with what I now know about my hero. Perhaps that is
> the work.*
>
> — From **Acolyte Fenny of Triboar,** to Brother Vareth, dated
> last summer.

Vareth's marginal note: *"Read this one when you are tired. Not
when you are bright. It needs the right light."*

## IV. The unsigned sting

*[A copy of an annotation found on the back of one of Stroud's
earliest post-match letters to Candlekeep. The hand is not
Stroud's. The ink is darker than the original letter. Written
within the past three years.]*

> *"He let you win, old friend."*

**Unsigned.** Vareth has been trying to identify the handwriting
since he discovered the annotation. He has not succeeded.

The phrase **"old friend"** is the load-bearing tell. After the
council match, "old friend" most plausibly refers not to Stroud
but to **Ortho** — Protanther's *actual* old friend, whose
unfinished game Stroud finished. **The annotation is a
Dragon-school text:** if accepted, it asserts the loss was a gift
from Protanther to Ortho's bloodline, *not* a divinely-engineered
humbling and *not* Stroud's brilliance. Both the Stroud school
and the Bahamut school are invalidated.

**Suspect pool** (Vareth's narrowed list, all unconfirmed):

- **Protanther himself,** in a moment of private acknowledgment
  that contradicts his public Bahamut-school speech. His
  polymorph-form visits to Candlekeep are not unprecedented.
  *(If true: the dragon's public testimony was diplomatic; the
  dragon's private truth is the Dragon school.)*
- **One of the council dragons** — most plausibly
  **Otaaryliakkarnos (Eli),** given his sarcasm about Protanther's
  speeches.
- **A Stroud party member** from the Tyranny of Dragons events
  who knows something Stroud is omitting from his own retelling.
- **Another descendant of Ortho** — a Stroud cousin or rival who
  reads the win as inheritance, not divine grace or individual
  brilliance.
- A messenger or Avowed (lower probability).

Vareth: *"Take the copy. Show it to Stroud if you wish. He has
not seen it. I have not sent it to him. I leave that to you.
For you specifically — a Bahamut-school cleric — this annotation
is a threat to your faith. If accepted, your faith dissolves into
a four-hundred-year friendship between a gold dragon and a
dwarven lineage. Sit with it before you pick a school."*
