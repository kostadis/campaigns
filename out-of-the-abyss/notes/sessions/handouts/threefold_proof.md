# THE THREEFOLD PROOF
## On Evidence, Identity, and Reasonable Doubt in a World of Magic
A memorandum for Readers, Watchers, magistrates, and those unfortunate enough to be handed a body before breakfast

Set down for the instruction of investigators who have been told, by some confident apprentice, that a single spell should settle the matter.

## PREFACE

It is often asked how evidence can matter in a world where faces may be borrowed, memories altered, bodies transformed, doors crossed without opening, the dead questioned, and gods petitioned for answers.

The question usually assumes that magic destroys investigation.

It does not.

Magic destroys naïve investigation.

A world with magic has more ways to observe an event and more ways to counterfeit each observation. It has speaking corpses and edited memories, truth-compelling circles and carefully literal answers, divinations and anti-divinations, visible disguises and invisible changes of form. The result is not a world without evidence. It is a world in which no single channel deserves sovereignty.

The thesis of this memorandum is simple:

Proof is not a thing that cannot be forged.

Proof is a pattern whose independent parts are too costly, too contradictory, or too numerous to forge together.

The discipline of investigation is therefore the discipline of convergence. Matter, memory, and magic must be made to disagree with the lie.

## I. THE ERROR OF THE PERFECT WITNESS

The inexperienced investigator imagines that somewhere among the spellbooks there must be a working called Find the Murderer.

There is not.

There are spells that reveal a school of magic, hear a corpse's recollection, constrain deliberate falsehood, observe a distant room, expose surface thought, locate an object, consult a power, or recover a life. Each answers a particular question under particular conditions. None converts the world into a disinterested magistrate.

This matters because an answer can be accurate and still fail to prove the proposition for which it is offered.

A corpse may report what it perceived without knowing who wore the face it saw. A truthful witness may sincerely remember an illusion. A suspect within a truth-working may omit, evade, misunderstand, or describe an altered memory. A divination may answer the exact question asked while leaving the fatal assumption untouched. An aura may identify the kind of working without identifying its caster.

Magic is an instrument. Instruments have ranges, resolutions, operators, calibration errors, countermeasures, and attack surfaces.

The first rule of magical evidence is therefore the same as the first rule of every instrument:

Write down what it actually measured.

## II. WHAT EVIDENCE IS

Evidence is not an object that carries certainty inside itself. Evidence is an observation that changes the relative plausibility of competing accounts.

A footprint does not say "the murderer stood here." It says that a foot of some described shape pressed this surface under some described conditions.

A fingerprint does not say "this person killed the victim." It says that a ridge pattern consistent with a particular hand was transferred to a particular surface, unless the mark was planted, copied, preserved from an earlier contact, or produced by a form that shared the pattern.

A magical residue does not say "this wizard cast the spell." It says that a working of a certain character affected this place or object, unless the aura was masked, imitated, displaced, or left by an item, scroll, construct, duplicate, or third party.

The possibility of a counterfeit does not make a clue worthless. Bread may be poisoned; this does not make bread a myth. The relevant question is not whether some magic could fabricate the clue. In a sufficiently large library, some magic can fabricate almost anything.

The relevant questions are these:

What magic would be required? Who possessed it? What access did it require? What else would that intervention disturb? Which independent observations would it also have to explain?

If an advocate may defeat every fact by saying "a wizard did it," then no wizard can ever be convicted either.

A competent magistrate asks: which wizard, by what working, with what opportunity, and at what cost?

## III. THE QUESTION OF FINGERPRINTS

The lines upon the fingers and palms are useful evidence in Faerûn for the same reason they are useful anywhere: they are numerous, persistent, readily transferred, and sufficiently variable to permit comparison.

Their value, however, is often misunderstood.

### What a fingerprint can establish

A clear print can associate a particular physical hand with contact upon a surface. A partial print can exclude some hands and remain consistent with others. Several prints in sequence may help reconstruct how an object was held, opened, turned, or carried.

At a place such as Candlekeep, careful Readers could discover ridge individuality, preserve prints with fine powder or alchemical vapor, and compare a questioned mark with a voluntarily or lawfully obtained exemplar.

No industrial revolution is required. The practice needs observation, records, patient comparison, and an institution wealthy enough to employ someone who enjoys patient comparison.

### What a fingerprint cannot establish

A print does not reveal when the contact occurred unless the surface or surrounding deposits provide a chronology. It does not reveal why the object was touched. It does not distinguish innocent access from criminal access. It does not identify an unknown person without a prior exemplar or registry.

Faerûn has no universal archive of every hand. A print usually answers, "Did one of these known people touch this?" It does not answer, "Which creature anywhere in the Realms touched this?"

### The magical attack surface

Gloves remain effective, a fact sometimes overlooked by apprentices who have read too many spellbooks.

Cleaning can remove transferred material. A lifted or molded print can be planted. A preserved object may carry an innocent mark from days earlier. A glamour that alters appearance without altering substance may leave the wearer's true prints, making fingerprints especially useful against visual disguise. A genuine transformation may alter the hand itself. A duplicate, simulacrum, shapechanger, or magically constructed body may create a harder question: which body made the mark, and what relationship does that body bear to the legal person accused?

These weaknesses do not make fingerprints useless. They define the proposition the print can support.

A fingerprint is evidence of contact by a physical pattern. It is not a certificate issued by the soul.

## IV. THE THREE EVIDENTIARY ROOTS

For practical inquiry, evidence may be divided into three roots: Matter, Memory, and Working.

### Matter

Matter includes bodies, wounds, blood, hair, footprints, fingerprints, fibers, tool marks, poison residue, components, broken locks, displaced dust, documents, ledgers, containers, meals, ash, and every other stubborn thing that remains after people begin telling stories.

Matter is often called mundane evidence, as if mundane meant weak. This is foolish. A glamour may persuade an eye while failing to persuade a cat's nose, a floorboard, a narrow boot, or the stitching on a discarded cloak.

Matter's great strength is that different materials respond differently to interference. Its weakness is that matter can be cleaned, moved, planted, transformed, conjured, repaired, destroyed, or innocently present.

The investigator must establish sequence. Which injury caused death? Which was staging? Which stain lies over which dust? Which lock was opened before the ward failed? A spectacular wound may be less important than a quiet poison delivered hours earlier.

### Memory

Memory includes living witnesses, animal behavior, familiar reports, a corpse's answers, written notes made near the event, and the later recollection of those who survived.

Witnesses are sensors with motives. They have limited positions, incomplete attention, prior expectations, loyalties, fears, species-specific senses, and memories that change even without magic.

Magic enlarges the problem but does not invent it. Illusion can deceive perception. Enchantment can redirect action. Memory can be altered. A witness may be truthful about an experience that never occurred as remembered.

This makes unusual witnesses valuable. A cat may not understand murder, but it may distinguish a familiar person from an unfamiliar scent or movement. A rat may know which creature entered a pantry. A corpse may remember the attacker's last words. None supplies a verdict. Each samples a different channel.

### Working

Working includes active spells, residual auras, broken wards, teleportation traces, planar disturbance, summoned creatures, exhausted divinations, magical components, altered objects, and the recorded results of controlled spellcasting.

Magical evidence is powerful because it can reveal facts unavailable to ordinary senses. It is dangerous because investigators are tempted to confuse rarity with certainty.

A spell's result must be recorded with the same care as a physical test: who cast it, upon what, under which conditions, using what focus, after what prior magic, with what possible interference, and with what exact words or perceptions returned.

The working is not merely what was discovered. The working itself becomes part of the scene.

## V. WHY THE FAMOUS SPELLS DO NOT END THE MYSTERY

### Speaking with the dead

Questioning a corpse is testimony, not resurrection and not judgment. The witness is limited to what it knew, perceived, believed, and chooses or is able to express through the working. It may not recognize a disguised killer. It may have died without seeing the decisive act. It may be hostile. Its answers are bounded.

The use or deliberate exhaustion of such a working can itself become evidence. If someone hurried to question a body first so that later investigators could not, the important clue may be the consumption of access rather than the answers obtained.

### Zone of truth

A truth-working constrains deliberate falsehood. It does not manufacture knowledge, compel speech, prevent evasion, correct altered memory, or force a witness to adopt the investigator's definitions.

"I did not kill him" may be sincere from the person who supplied the poison, hired the blade, created the duplicate, or believes the victim died later from another cause.

A truth-working is most useful when the proposition has been carefully defined in advance and the witness is required by a legitimate procedure to answer directly. Even then it authenticates sincerity under the spell, not objective reality.

### Reading thoughts

Surface thought reveals current cognition. It may expose fear, recognition, rehearsal, or a concealed association. It can also reveal intrusive thoughts, planted memories, deliberate mental discipline, irrelevant shame, or terror of being falsely accused.

Thought is evidence of thought.

It is not a transcript of the world.

### Divination and scrying

Divinations are questions addressed through a constrained channel. The answer may be limited by the power consulted, the formulation of the question, the time horizon, interference, wards, competing wills, or the difference between what will happen and what might happen after the answer is heard.

Scrying observes from a position. A position has a field of view. It may show a figure without proving identity, an act without revealing coercion, or a room already staged for observation.

The more important the case, the more likely an adversary has considered the obvious question.

### Detection magic

Detection can classify an active aura or reveal that magic is present. It rarely signs the caster's name beneath the result. A scroll, item, duplicate, dominated intermediary, summoned creature, or masked aura may separate the working from its apparent author.

The investigator should ask not only, "What magic is here?" but "Who could cause this magic to be here without being here themselves?"

## VI. COUNTER-FORENSICS

A magical society does not merely develop better investigation. It develops better crime.

The common families of counter-forensics are concealment, substitution, alteration, displacement, contamination, and obstruction.

Concealment hides a person, object, aura, thought, room, or route.

Substitution presents the wrong face, body, voice, signature, aura, object, or creature as the relevant one.

Alteration changes memory, form, material, injury, handwriting, or residue.

Displacement moves the actor or evidence without traversing the expected path.

Contamination adds innocent traces, planted traces, duplicate residues, false witnesses, or too much magical noise.

Obstruction blocks divination, consumes a limited opportunity, destroys a body, uses shielding material, breaks the chain of custody, or forces investigators to act before their preferred examiner arrives.

Every countermeasure has requirements. It must target some channel. It consumes time, access, skill, components, knowledge, or institutional authority. It may solve one problem while creating another.

A disguise may fool the servant and alarm the animal. Cleaning may remove blood and disturb the dust beneath it. Teleportation may avoid footprints and leave an arcane discontinuity. Lead may defeat a search and advertise that the hider expected a search. A forged note may lure the victim while introducing handwriting, paper, ink, and delivery history.

The competent criminal attacks the expected test.

The competent investigator changes tests.

## VII. CHAIN OF CUSTODY IN AN ARCANE AGE

When evidence can be altered by touch, word, gaze, spell, familiar, summoned hand, or remote effect, the history of the evidence becomes evidence.

The purpose of chain of custody is not bureaucracy for its own sake. It is to preserve an account of every opportunity for alteration.

A sound Candlekeep protocol would require the following:

Observe before touching. Record the position, condition, temperature, illumination, odor, and visible aura of the scene before any investigator handles it.
Name every entrant. A scene without an entry ledger is already a scene with an unknown suspect.
Make a mundane record before magical examination. Sketches, measurements, written descriptions, and sealed impressions survive after an aura fades or a spell changes the object.
Separate collection from interpretation. The person who gathers a trace should not be the only person who decides what it means.
Divide samples. Keep at least one portion unworked so a second examiner can test it by a different method.
Seal twice. Use an ordinary physical seal and an independently registered arcane mark or ward. Each protects against a different class of interference.
Record every spell used on the scene: caster, time, target, components, observed result, failed result, and any known secondary effect.
Question witnesses separately and early. Later agreement may reflect conversation, enchantment, fear, loyalty, or honest contamination of memory.
Preserve failures. A blocked divination, an exhausted corpse, an absent aura, or a ward that returns no answer may be evidence of planning if the obstruction itself can be established.
Disclose capability. Investigators, examiners, and custodians should record their own relevant magic, transformations, familiars, and conflicts of interest.

The central principle is redundancy. Mundane and magical seals should not be controlled by the same person. Duplicate samples should not be examined by the same method. A diviner should not be told which answer the appointing Reader hopes to receive.

An institution cannot remove interested will from the world. It can arrange competing interests so that manipulation becomes harder to conceal.

## VIII. THE PROBLEM OF IDENTITY

Magic separates concepts that ordinary law often treats as one.

A face, body, voice, mind, memory, soul, true name, legal name, divine allegiance, and continuous personal history may point to the same person. They need not.

An investigator must therefore state which identity claim is being made.

"The witness saw Sylvira" is not one claim. It may mean:

The witness saw a body with Sylvira's appearance.
The witness heard Sylvira's voice.
The witness recognized Sylvira's habitual movement.
An animal reacted as it normally reacts to Sylvira.
The body carried Sylvira's physical traces.
The mind presented memories associated with Sylvira.
A divination returned Sylvira's name.
A soul or divine power recognized the being as Sylvira.
The legal person Sylvira arranged the act through another body.

These propositions are related. They are not interchangeable.

This is why small inconsistencies matter. A familiar face carrying an unnecessary lantern, using the wrong hand, choosing an unfamiliar phrase, smelling wrong to an animal, leaving the wrong footprint, or failing to know a private habit may reveal that appearance and identity have separated.

Identity in Faerûn is not a fact discovered by one test.

It is a bundle of continuities.

## IX. THE RULE OF UNSHARED FAILURE

The Threefold Proof does not require that every case produce matter, memory, and working in equal measure. It requires investigators to seek channels whose weaknesses do not coincide.

Two witnesses who discussed the event are not independent. Two divinations from the same patron are not independent. A familiar and its master may be two observers but one interested source. A print and a blood trace may both be defeated by planting material from the same stolen object.

The rule is this:

Trust most the agreement of channels that would have to be attacked differently.

No serious judgment should rest on one evidentiary root when magical interference is a plausible part of the case.
Two roots may establish a working conclusion when their failure modes are genuinely independent.
Three roots should be sought when identity is contested, the accused has access to transformation or memory magic, or the penalty is irreversible.
Two examinations performed by the same caster, deity, familiar, item, or institutional chain are not independent merely because two spells were named.
A magical result should have a mundane companion whenever possible. A mundane trace should not be discarded merely because an unspecified spell could theoretically counterfeit it.
The weight of a pattern depends on the cheapest plausible account that could fabricate the whole pattern, not on the most extravagant magic anyone can imagine.

This is not mathematical certainty. It is disciplined reasonable doubt.

The impossible standard—evidence that no magic could ever counterfeit—would acquit every sophisticated criminal and condemn only the poor.

The useful standard asks whether a concrete alternative account can explain the whole record without multiplying unsupported miracles.

## X. RIGHTS, POWER, AND THE POLITICS OF PROOF

Magical investigation is not merely a technical practice. It is an exercise of power.

Compelled truth, intrusive thought-reading, divine consultation, searches by familiar, forced transformation, and postmortem interrogation all raise questions of consent, privilege, religious authority, and institutional abuse.

A spell may be accurate and still be unlawful. An unlawful spell may produce a lead without producing admissible proof. A suspect may rationally refuse a mental search because every person possesses thoughts unrelated to the charge that would destroy a reputation, reveal a patron, expose a lover, or endanger another life.

Wealth also changes the threat model. A common thief may own gloves. A guildmaster may hire a memory-worker. An archmage may use intermediaries, duplicates, remote workings, private sanctums, and protections unknown to the examiner.

Procedure creates legitimacy precisely because power is unequal. The more powerful the accused, investigator, temple, or appointing authority, the more important independent custody and adversarial review become.

The question is not only whether the evidence is true.

The question is who was permitted to make it true for the court.

## XI. WHY CANDLEKEEP STILL NEEDS INVESTIGATORS

Candlekeep possesses an extraordinary concentration of spells, experts, records, wards, familiars, alchemical reagents, historical precedents, and people capable of explaining at length why another expert is wrong.

This does not make investigation instantaneous. It makes the contest sophisticated.

The obvious divination is obvious to the murderer. The rare spell requires a rare caster whose attention has other strategic value. Senior mages have institutional loyalties, political exposure, private secrets, and reasons not to place their minds or divine patrons at the disposal of every inquiry. Some workings destroy or contaminate the evidence they examine. Others return answers that require interpretation by the same institutions under suspicion.

Candlekeep's greatest forensic asset is therefore not a spell.

It is the ability to compare records across disciplines.

An alchemist identifies the poison. A healer sequences the wounds. A Watcher reconstructs access. A scribe compares ink and handwriting. An animal offers a sensory discrepancy. A diviner documents an obstruction. A Reader finds the procurement ledger. An investigator notices that the accounts cannot all be true at once.

No one clue solves the case.

The contradiction does.

## XII. A FIELD METHOD FOR MURDER INQUIRIES

When approaching a suspicious death, investigators should resist the urge to begin with the most impressive available spell.

Begin with sequence.

Determine what caused death, what occurred before death, what occurred after death, and which feature of the scene appears designed to attract attention. The dramatic injury is often staging. The quiet delivery mechanism may be the murder.

Then establish access.

Who could reach the victim, obtain the material, learn the wards, enter the room, use the relevant disguise, exhaust the obvious divination, or move the evidence afterward? Access is distributed across people and institutions; a complex scene may record more than one actor.

Then separate identity channels.

Record appearance, voice, scent, movement, touch, knowledge, aura, body, and legal agency as distinct observations. Do not allow a witness's confident name to erase the details by which the recognition was made.

Then map countermeasures.

For each missing result, ask whether it is naturally absent, accidentally destroyed, or deliberately blocked. An obstruction is useful only when its existence and purpose can be distinguished from ordinary failure.

Finally, seek unshared failure.

Do not ask for ten clues that all depend on sight. Ask for one material trace, one independent witness channel, one institutional record, and one magical observation whose weaknesses do not share a single convenient explanation.

For each significant clue, ask:

What proposition does this clue actually support: presence, contact, sequence, identity, intent, access, or guilt?
What ordinary process could have produced it?
What known magical process could have produced or altered it?
What would that intervention require in time, access, power, components, knowledge, and risk?
Which other observations share the same failure mode?
Which independent channel could test the same proposition differently?
Who benefits if this clue is believed, and who had the opportunity to arrange that belief?
What negative result or obstruction is present, and is the obstruction itself authenticated?
What conclusion remains if the most dramatic clue is removed?

## XIII. THE CANDLEKEEP PRESSURE TEST

The present Candlekeep inquiry is a useful demonstration without requiring a judgment about any named suspect.

The investigators face several distinct kinds of propositions:

What killed the victim?
Which injuries belong to the killing and which to later staging?
Who possessed or could obtain the delivery mechanism?
Which person entered, and which identity channel did each witness actually perceive?
What magic was used in the room, and does it identify an actor or merely an event?
Which records show access, appointment, procurement, or motive?
Which divinations were blocked, exhausted, redirected, or made unreliable?
Which observations were made by creatures not fooled through the same senses as humanoid witnesses?
Could one person produce the entire scene, or does the evidence imply a murderer, a later visitor, and a cover-up with different purposes?

Fingerprints can contribute to this inquiry. They might associate a known hand with a book, vessel, blade, safe, latch, or hidden object. Their absence may suggest gloves or cleaning. Their presence may be innocent, planted, transformed, or chronologically irrelevant.

They should be collected.

They should not be worshiped.

The same is true of every other clue.

## CONCLUSION

Magic does not make evidence meaningless.

It makes evidence adversarial.

Every observation exists inside a contest among people who can deceive senses, alter memories, move bodies, mask auras, consult gods, bind spirits, and anticipate procedure. But deception is not free. It must act through a channel, satisfy a mechanism, consume an opportunity, and coexist with the rest of the world.

The investigator's task is not to find the one incorruptible witness.

There is no such witness.

The task is to assemble witnesses that can be corrupted only in different ways, then ask whether the accused could plausibly have corrupted them all.

A world of magic can lie.

The lie still has to coordinate.

That is where evidence begins.
