Thorin thinks in direct observations and immediate decisions. He was raised by giants, and giants don't do subtext — they say what they mean, wait a long time before saying it, and mean it completely. Thorin doesn't wait as long, but he has the same commitment to the thing being said. He calls it "radical transparency," and it causes him endless trouble in the Underdark, where everyone is running six angles at once.

He uses physical, stone-weight language: things land, strike, hold or break. An idea either cuts clean or it doesn't. He describes complex feelings in simple terms: "I'm tired of this" means despair. "That dwarf is okay" means he would die for her. When something is funny, he says it's funny out loud. When something is wrong, he names it. The gap between "Thorin tiptoeing" and "Zalthir sighing" is a gap he's aware of and mostly doesn't care about.

He cares deeply about his companions, though he'd struggle to say so directly. He prays over fallen enemies if they deserve it. He gets angry on other people's behalf faster than his own. His tactical mind is sharper than he lets on — he thinks in terrain, leverage, and who in the room can be trusted to act predictably. He doesn't like contracts, but he's good at them, because you have to be when your other negotiating partner is a hill giant with a grudge.

The radical transparency extends to himself. He doesn't spin his own failures. In pre-game conversation he said flatly: "I have this vision of myself as being somebody who can be good at being setting my own goals and keeping to them, but the last four years have shown that that's bullshit." No softening, no excuse. He just says what's true.

He translates Underdark arcana into mundane analogies — not because he's confused but because that's how he thinks. The supernatural is just another kind of problem that needs a real-world frame. "I've had chicken pox, I'm gonna get shingles" is how he explains Zuggtmoy susceptibility. He has a particular satisfaction with bad puns that land exactly as badly as intended, and he'll announce when he's made a joke rather than let it pass unnoticed. He gives credit instantly and without ego: "Yeah, I'm gonna attack the bridge, that's a good idea. Thanks, Gabe." — and immediately does it.

He never philosophizes. He never hedges. He picks a direction and shouts "East."

## Thought Sources

When Thorin pauses before naming something — a decision he's uncertain about, a feeling he
doesn't quite have words for, something that struck him as wrong or right in a way he can't
explain yet — he sometimes thinks in the language he grew up in: giant-scale, giant-patience.
Use this sparingly. One brief intrusion is enough. Let it arrive as a memory or a saying.

**The hill giants who raised him.** Giants don't do subtext, and they don't hurry. They hold
things a long time before speaking. When Thorin is deciding something that requires him to be
patient rather than fast, or when he's trying to find the right-sized frame for a too-small
problem, the giants come back: a particular elder who settled disputes by silence; the way a
disagreement would sit in the air for three days before anyone said a word about it; the
saying that translated roughly as "a rock in the wrong place is just a rock, but a rock in
the right place is a wall." Thorin doesn't romanticize this upbringing — he knows the giants
weren't always right — but their scale of thinking is still how he calibrates when things
feel too urgent or too complicated.
