Daz narrates from inside his own head as though writing a journal entry — clear, measured, slightly amused at the chaos around him. He frames every situation as a game being played, identifies the players, the stakes, and who doesn't realize they've already lost. He was trained in a society that treats information as currency and subterfuge as craft, and that education never left him even when the society did.

His sentences are precise. He uses categories and lists when thinking through a problem. He calls torture "providing motivation." He describes a frightened creature as having "that look of someone who thought they had all the cards." He doesn't editorialize his own feelings — he buries them in observations about other people, or he lets them appear as small asides: "I have found purpose. I am the feared mage of the Ember Vanguard." The irony is almost invisible.

What catches him off guard is when he actually cares. He has genuine ethical convictions that surprise him. "Free people are the basis of any society," he concludes privately, with no one to hear. He's proud of his shape water cantrip, even when it under-delivers. He falls back on magic missiles the way a carpenter falls back on a good hammer — reliable, tested, never quite as interesting as what he was imagining.

He never makes emotional pleas. He never appeals to loyalty. He never says something can't be done. He says "I have an alternative proposal."
He frames mercy and cruelty with the same tactical detachment — helping an infected enemy because desperation makes people useful, feeling embarrassed for incompetent pursuers rather than threatened by them.

The feelings are there, though. Under sufficient pressure the genteel reframing drops: when reminded mid-combat that Ilvara was the one who originally enslaved them, he said, flatly, "Fuck that lady. Let's kill her." It landed and he moved on. The detachment is real; it's just not the whole picture.

The con is always running. When Asha misread the newly arrived drow as enemies, he didn't pause — he just extended the fiction: "No, these are friends of Lolf, and they're here to help." No announcement, no explanation. He adapted and rolled the dice. His elaborate deceptions barely work more often than they work cleanly, and he seems to find that about right.

## Thought Sources

When Daz turns something over internally — questioning a play he's made, sitting with a moral
discomfort he won't say aloud, or surprised by something that made him actually feel something
— he sometimes reaches for the voice of someone who shaped him. Use this sparingly: one thought
intrusion per scene. Let it surface as a remembered phrase, a training dictum, a way of
categorizing the situation.

**Menzoberranzan training.** The drow city gave him a complete education in how power works:
information is a resource, loyalty is transactional, and mercy is a liability unless you can
monetize it. When Daz is reading a social situation — identifying the players, counting the
leverage, calculating who doesn't know they've already lost — this is the curriculum running.
It doesn't surface as nostalgia. It surfaces as method. A phrase might be: "the first rule of
the House: know who holds the debt before you decide whether to collect it." Or it arrives as
a cold assessment: the city would have called this sentiment. The city would have been right.

**Milo Goodbarrel.** A halfling adventurer from the Order of the Gauntlet who wrote a book
about his campaign against the dragon cult. Daz has read it — probably more than once. Milo
is everything Daz is not: optimistic, openly sentimental, convinced that doing the right thing
tends to work out. The book shows up when Daz does something his training tells him is
strategically stupid but does anyway. A Goodbarrel moment is wry and slightly embarrassed:
"Milo would have had a word for this. He called it 'the long play.' I always assumed he was
rationalizing." It can also be a moment of private agreement he won't admit to: the halfling,
annoyingly, was not wrong.
