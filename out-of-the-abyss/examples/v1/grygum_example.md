# Grygum — The Kitchen and the Arrival of Demogorgon

I took the kitchen because it was the right choice, not because I'm a cook. A mess hall full of Drow soldiers waiting to be fed is, as any sensible orc will tell you, also a mess hall full of Drow soldiers who are watching the cook instead of watching the doors. I filed that away under useful.

Imbros liked my show. That surprised me. I'd expected contempt or indifference, but the Drow has his vanities like anyone else, and apparently watching an orc toss mushrooms through the air and hum a tune while two deep gnomes rob him blind is entertaining enough to hold the attention of six soldiers. Topsy caught my eye once while a knife slid behind her back. I tossed another ingredient overhead and kept moving.

I took notes. Undercommon: I don't speak it, but I can read a room. The Drow who ran the kitchen, Imbros, corrected me once when I reached for a knife to sharpen it. I could tell from his tone and the specific look Suushar was giving me that the words he was saying had more texture than "don't touch that." Suushar translated afterward, helpfully omitting every insult while somehow communicating all of them. That's a particular skill.

Later, when we were standing at the docks of Sloobludop and the chanting reached its pitch, I was already watching the water. I'd been watching the water since the moment the blood hit the Darklake. That's what the teachings of Bahamut call attentiveness — noticing what is actually happening instead of what you hope is happening.

What was actually happening was that the Darklake was beginning to foam.

"Not to worry," I thought, because something in me still believes that Bahamut has the architecture of events in hand and that the creature foaming up from below was probably just a large manta ray, a very large manta ray, nothing more than that.

Then the tentacles came out.

I know what Demogorgon is. Gorg'Bahamut sat me down once when I was young and explained it the way a teacher explains fire: you understand that it will burn you before you understand anything else about it. Two heads. The Abyss given a face. He destroys things not because destruction is useful to him but because it is his nature, the way that warmth is the nature of the sun.

I shouted his name. Nobody needed me to name it, but I named it anyway, because someone in the group needed to say what the thing was so we could all agree that we were running.

We ran.

As we reached the boat, I heard the screaming from behind us — first the Kuo-Toans, then the structures collapsing, then something that wasn't quite a sound at all. Demogorgon doesn't care who you worship. He doesn't care what your divine plan is.

I took notes. Stool was in my arms. Thorin was at the oar. The Darklake was dark, and for the first time in days, that felt like a mercy.
