# OotA End-Game Extension: "The Fall of the City of Spiders"

A design rationale for an alternative end-game to *Out of the Abyss*, meant to be shared with other DMs running the campaign.

## What this document is

A structural substitution proposal for the end-game of *Out of the Abyss*, covering roughly Chapters 11–17 of the published module. It keeps the core premise — demon lords loose in the Underdark, Vizeran DeVir's ritual as the plan — but relocates the finale inside Menzoberranzan, raises the political stakes, and tightens the mechanical logic of the ritual.

Written for DMs who have already read the module at least through Chapter 17 and are evaluating an alternative before committing. It is not a replacement for the module; it is a set of substitutions you can drop onto a preserved foundation.

We want feedback. See **Open questions for DM feedback** at the end.

---

## 1. Orientation: what the original module does

### The end-game arc

Chapters 1–10 are self-contained and largely unaffected by this extension. The end-game begins at Chapter 11 (Gravenhollow) and resolves in Chapter 17 (Against the Demon Lords).

- **Ch. 11 (Gravenhollow).** The party meets Vizeran DeVir, an ancient drow archmage opposed to Lolth and the Orthodoxy. He believes Gromph Baenre summoned the demon lords by accident via a Faerzress-powered spell. He proposes an alliance.
- **Ch. 12 (Araj).** Vizeran's tower, far from Menzoberranzan. The party accepts his plan; he assigns component runs.
- **Ch. 13–14 (Wormwrithings, Labyrinth).** Purple worm egg, goristro heart, angel feathers, demon lord blood.
- **Ch. 15 (City of Spiders).** Infiltrate Menzoberranzan; recover Gromph's grimoire from Sorcere.
- **Ch. 16 (Fetid Wedding).** Deal with Zuggtmoy / Juiblex / Araumycos; potentially collect timmask mushrooms.
- **Ch. 17 (Against the Demon Lords).** Vizeran assembles the Dark Heart. The party chooses where to place it. Vizeran returns to Araj and performs a 9-hour ritual. The Dark Heart radiates a Faerzress summons; all demon lords in the Underdark are pulled to its location; they fight each other to the death; the party engages the weakened survivor.

### The ritual's mechanism

Chapter 17's ritual does not directly banish demons. It summons them, forces combat, and relies on 5e's default rule for non-native fiends: *when a demon's physical form is destroyed anywhere other than the Abyss, its essence returns there and reforms over time.* The module is terse about this because it assumes the DM knows the rule.

So the ritual's mechanical job is:

1. Summon every demon in the Underdark to one point (Faerzress-powered).
2. Force them into combat with each other (*"enraged by being forcibly summoned yet again"*).
3. Attrition reduces them to one survivor.
4. Party kills the last standing demon lord.

MM-default banishment-on-death does the rest.

### The revenge subplot

Vizeran's stated motivation is ending the demon crisis. His hidden motivation, disclosed via his apprentice Grin Ousstyl in Chapter 15, is that he wants the Dark Heart planted in Menzoberranzan specifically — to have the demon lords destroy the city in vengeance against House Baenre and the Orthodoxy. The module tells the party "the talisman can be placed in any location" and flags Vizeran as untrustworthy in *Loose Threads*.

## 2. The extension: seven structural changes

### Change 1 — Vizeran moves into Menzoberranzan

**Original:** Vizeran operates from Araj, a distant tower. The party travels to and from him.

**Extension:** Vizeran hides inside Menzoberranzan in an anti-divination-shielded sanctuary, likely the ruins of House DeVir. He never leaves. The party operates out of his hideout for the entire end-game.

### Change 2 — The Tripod of Ruin

**Original:** The Dark Heart is one talisman placed at one site of the party's choosing.

**Extension:** The Dark Heart is a "receiver" paired with three wired sites in Menzoberranzan:

| Site | Role | Mechanical goal |
|---|---|---|
| Narbondel (the Clock) | Beacon | Etch runes turning the city's heat source into a demon-baiting signal |
| Great Rift (the Lungs) | Worm stampede | Drop pheromone reagents to pull the Wormwrithings population into the city |
| Pool of Baenre (the Heart) | Portal | Pollute the High Matron's communion pool to open a Faerzress rupture |

The party "wires" each site across the mid-end-game chapters. Each site is a distinct infiltration with its own Heat cost.

### Change 3 — The Living Circuit is a cage

**Original:** Vizeran is the ritual's caster. He performs it from Araj and emerges with three levels of exhaustion.

**Extension:** Vizeran is physically bound to Menzoberranzan's ley architecture as a **containment cage**. His lifeforce, projected through the Dark Heart, seals every demon summoned by the Tripod inside the city. Demons cannot leave. Vizeran cannot leave. When the last demon inside the cage dies, the cage's purpose is served and Vizeran dies with the seal resolving.

This is the load-bearing substitution. It does four things at once:

- It gives the original's "forced rage" handwave a specific mechanical driver: rivalry + proximity + no-exit produces the slugfest, not spontaneous frenzy.
- It makes Vizeran's insistence on Menzoberranzan structurally necessary to his plan (the cage is welded to *this* city's ley lines; "any other location" isn't an option for him).
- It preserves MM-default banishment-on-death as the termination rule.
- It converts the kill-Vizeran choice into a genuine branch (see Change 4).

### Change 4 — Kill-Vizeran becomes a deliberate trade, not a failure state

**Original:** Vizeran survives Ch. 17 (exhausted but alive) and is a post-campaign loose thread. Killing him during the finale is essentially outside the module's design space.

**Extension:** The party can kill Vizeran during the ritual. The Tripod's components are anchor-independent, so the beacon, portal, and worm stampede still fire. The city still falls. Demons still engage each other — rivalry plus game-theoretic necessity (every surviving rival becomes exponentially harder to kill later) drives the fight without ritual compulsion. But the cage collapses, and **wounded losers flee rather than fight to the death.**

The finale converts from "kill the weakened sole survivor" to a **hunt**: pursue multiple wounded demon lords through the collapsing city and surrounding Underdark before they reach redoubts. The Faerzress trap keeps them Prime-bound (they cannot voluntarily plane-shift home), so every escapee becomes a permanent post-campaign threat. This also strongly motivates the optional Vhaerun post-arc (see Change 7).

This makes distrust of Vizeran an actionable choice at the table, not a plot-derailing mistake.

### Change 5 — Daz as Daz'issin DeVir

**Original:** The PCs are swept into the plot by circumstance. Vizeran's relationship to them is transactional.

**Extension:** One PC (Daz) is Daz'issin DeVir, the vaulted-memory heir of the destroyed Fourth House. Vizeran had the memories surgically excised by a Sharran priestess decades ago ("Mercy of Oblivion") and placed Daz in Velkynvelve as a time capsule. Daz doesn't know he's DeVir — the cover personality is genuinely his.

Daz is mechanically required:

- **Ritual calibration:** DeVir blood-signature unlocks at least one Tripod node.
- **Access:** DeVir wards and vault-paths only respond to valid lineage memory patterns.
- **Political leverage:** an authenticated DeVir survivor can force a legal fracture under Menzoberranzan's annihilation-liability precedent against House Do'Urden.

The reveal runs on a four-stage ladder (Ambient Signals → Material Evidence → Conflicting Testimony → Hard Proof at the West Wall Ruins crest). At Stage 4, the player chooses whether to claim, reject, or burn the DeVir name. That choice shapes the post-ritual political landscape.

**This change requires a specific kind of player** — one who wants deep backstory integration. The rest of the extension works without it, but the political-thriller texture thins out.

### Change 6 — Heat and Social Masquerade

**Original:** Menzoberranzan infiltration (Ch. 15) is chapter-scoped. Discovery is a narrative beat, not a systemic pressure.

**Extension:** Two overlapping systems govern the end-game tempo.

**Heat** — a five-tier city-alert state the party's actions move up:

| Tier | Description |
|---|---|
| Ghost | No one knows the party is in the city |
| Rumors | Increased patrols in the Braeryn; "undesirables" questioned |
| Purge | The Orthodoxy begins "Night of Shattered Glass" — safehouse raids |
| Lockdown | City gates sealed; constant scrying |
| Extermination | Baenre Inquisitors actively hunt the party |

Tier transitions require a proximate cause the party can name post-hoc. Heat doesn't drift up from narration alone.

**Social Masquerade** — in noble districts, Daz plays "Master" and the rest of the party plays retinue unless they deliberately break cover. Scrutiny NPCs probe the masquerade with house-protocol tests that can collapse it.

### Change 7 — The Blink event and the Vhaerun post-arc

**Original:** Gromph's accidental Faerzress spell is the crisis's cause. Post-campaign continuation is "Into the Demonweb Pits" (Ch. 17 *Loose Threads*).

**Extension:** A separate event — the **Faerzress Blink** — preceded Gromph's spell and radicalized Vizeran from reformist ("prune the system") to anti-recurrence absolutist ("fire"). Who engineered the Blink is its own four-stage reveal ladder, with candidate authors being the Orthodoxy (internal purge tool), Vhaerun (external god), or Gromph himself (side effect).

If the players chase the Blink investigation past Stage 3, the **Vhaerun post-arc** activates at level 20+: a four-act epic-tier continuation about controlling the archive that holds the Blink authorship proof. It's optional by design. Tables that don't want epic-tier content can stop at OotA closure.

## 3. What's preserved from the original

The extension is structural substitution on a preserved foundation. Deliberately unchanged:

- **Ch. 1–10.** The escape arc is left intact.
- **The Gauntlgrym alliance (Ch. 8).** Bruenor's faction network translates cleanly into the extension's coalition asset framework.
- **The demon lord bestiary (Appendix D).** All demon lords can appear.
- **MM-default banishment-on-death.** The cage explicitly relies on it.
- **The Dark Heart component list (Ch. 17).** Components fold into the Tripod wiring.
- **Gromph Baenre as upstream cause.** The Blink complicates but does not replace his role.
- **Grin Ousstyl.** Still the party's inside source on Vizeran's true intent.
- **Jarlaxle.** Still a cross-faction wildcard; fits the Heat system naturally.

## 4. Why these changes: root problems in the original

Above, each change has its own local rationale. These are the broader observations that drive the substitutions:

### Geographic sprawl

The original end-game bounces the party across six or seven locations in five chapters. Each transition costs narrative momentum. Consolidating into Menzoberranzan roughly halves scene resets and lets the city accumulate weight over time.

### The forced-rage handwave

*"Enraged by being forcibly summoned yet again by a ritual, the demons fly into a frenzy once they see each other"* is a single sentence doing load-bearing work. The demon lords in the Underdark have been demonstrably *not* killing each other on sight for the entire campaign — Demogorgon hunts the Darklake, Zuggtmoy runs a wedding, Juiblex plans to consume Zuggtmoy's domain on his own timeline. The cage substitutes rivalry-plus-proximity-plus-no-exit for "frenzy on contact" and produces the same termination outcome with a specific, defensible driver.

### The mechanical ambiguity of ritual placement

The original says *"the talisman can be placed in any location."* This makes the moral dilemma real, but it also collapses the villain subplot if a player says "let's put it in Sloobludop." In the extension, the cage topology is welded to Menzoberranzan specifically. Vizeran's Menzoberranzan preference is mechanically necessary to *his* plan; the party's choice is whether to enact his plan at all, not where.

### Thin political stakes

The original's finale kills Demogorgon (probably). Menzoberranzan survives unless the party actively dooms it. The political order of the Underdark changes little. The extension binds the finale to the end of the Orthodoxy's hold on Menzoberranzan, ties one PC's lineage to the lever that pries it loose, and gives the party a three-way choice about what survives — a bigger lift, and a more campaign-defining outcome.

### Kill-Vizeran as dead letter

In the original, killing Vizeran is something the module handles in *Loose Threads*, not at the table. The extension makes it a live Stage-4 decision with distinct mechanics and aftermath. Players who distrust Vizeran can act on it without torpedoing the session.

## 5. Tradeoffs and costs the extension accepts

**More DM prep.** Heat, Social Masquerade, and the three-site Tripod require more preparation than "gather components, plant at one site, roll initiative." A DM running this cold will struggle.

**Requires one committed player.** Daz's lineage arc needs a player who wants deep backstory integration. A rotating-PC table, or a party where no one wants that role, should skip Change 5. The rest of the extension still works, but the political-thriller texture thins.

**Higher political literacy demand.** Players track factions, Heat state, proximate causes, and safe-room protocols. Tables that prefer combat-centric tempo will find the mid-end-game phase too talky.

**The cage mechanism is load-bearing and must be made visible.** If the DM doesn't communicate the cage's function by Stage 4 of the Vizeran Disclosure ladder, the kill-Vizeran branch loses its meaning. The reveal has to actually run, not just exist in the doc.

**The hunt-branch adds a 1–2 session tail.** If the players kill Vizeran, you're committing to extra sessions pursuing escaped demon lords. Not strictly a cost, but not free either.

**The Vhaerun post-arc can be a trap.** Optionality still shapes expectations. Some tables will see it as bait and feel obligated. Name it optional early and repeat.

**This extension does not rewrite the spine of Ch. 17.** Demons still die via MM default. The summoning is still Faerzress-powered. A DM who reads the original carefully will recognize most of the parts. That is intentional — the goal is a minimum-invasive substitution, not a replacement.

## 6. Open questions for DM feedback

These are places we are genuinely uncertain and would benefit from other DMs' experience:

1. **Daz's identity reveal pacing.** The four-stage ladder works on paper. Does it hold up across 4–6 sessions of actual play, or do stages compress and collapse into single scenes?
2. **Heat system calibration.** The tier transitions need proximate causes the party can name. Is "you killed the scrutiny NPC" enough cause to jump Rumors → Purge, or should tier jumps require multiple independent causes?
3. **Cage visibility.** The cage is a driver the players need to understand by Stage 4. Is "Grin Ousstyl tells them" sufficient disclosure, or does it need an in-game demonstration (e.g., a lesser demon arrives early and visibly cannot leave the city)?
4. **Coalition asset finale.** We replaced "fight alongside allies" with faction-provided strikes (intel, access, sabotage teams). Cleaner in writing; harder at the table. Has anyone else tried this substitution?
5. **Kill-Vizeran branch playtime.** The hunt phase is designed as a satisfying trade, but it tacks a 1–2 session tail onto a long campaign. Is that acceptable, or does it overshoot the party's stamina?
6. **Vizeran's self-sacrifice landing.** His "I die with the seal resolving" beat depends on the players having come to understand and maybe respect him. If the ladder runs clean and they see him as a tragic fanatic, it lands. If it runs messy and they see him as a manipulator, it's anticlimactic. What's the reliable tell that it's going to land?
7. **Vhaerun arc fit.** Does the level-20+ continuation feel like a natural extension, or like bait? We've designed it as optional, but optionality still shapes expectations.

## 7. Quick reference: original vs. extension

| Element | Original | Extension |
|---|---|---|
| Vizeran's base | Araj (distant tower) | Hidden sanctuary inside Menzoberranzan |
| Ritual location | Any battlefield the party chooses | Menzoberranzan specifically (cage welded to ley lines) |
| Dark Heart | Single talisman at one site | Receiver paired with three Tripod sites |
| Ritual mechanism | Summon + forced rage + MM banishment | Summon + cage + rivalry-driven fight + MM banishment |
| Vizeran's fate | Survives with exhaustion; post-campaign loose thread | Dies when the cage's purpose is served; canonical |
| Kill-Vizeran branch | Implicit failure state | Explicit hunt phase; 1d3+ escaped demon lords as post-campaign threats |
| PC identity integration | None required | Daz as Daz'issin DeVir (four-stage reveal, claim/reject/burn) |
| Infiltration tempo | Chapter-scoped (Ch. 15) | Continuous Heat system across entire end-game |
| Political outcome | Demon crisis ends; Menzoberranzan mostly survives | Menzoberranzan falls; Orthodoxy ends; Daz chooses DeVir's fate |
| Post-OotA epic | Demonweb Pits hook (Ch. 17 *Loose Threads*) | Vhaerun / Blink authorship arc (optional, level 20+) |
| DM prep load | Moderate | High |
| Player commitment | Standard | One PC needs backstory buy-in; table needs political literacy |
