# Chapter 26 Journey to and into the Lost Tomb of Khaem. 
## 7th day of 1st tenday of Myrkhul, 1495 
### Grygum 


The boat is sunk. My mending skills are in high demand. And if I want to not end up on a ship, it's best to keep it secret from myself. 


## 9th day of 1st tenday of Myrkhul, 1495
### Daz


At first, I thought this adventure was about escape. About finding a way home. But now that we have been wandering through the world, I realize it’s an opportunity to learn and explore. 


My whole life, I was a Drow of Menzoberanzaan. Trapped in the formalities of ritual and expectations, trying to avoid the predations of the Cult of Lolth and the machinations of the Noble Houses. 


I never saw the Underdark for what it is, a place to explore and learn. And now I know this journey not as an escape to another place, but instead as an escape from a previous worldview. My journey is about discovery, appreciating the diversity of ecosystems, life forms, and civilizations, and learning. 


For two days, Eldeth has been hiding our tracks for two days, where all we could see when we could see was the dark passages. Serith insisted he knew where we were going. I was hoping to see new things. 


We turned down yet another passageway, and the air grew thick with a familiar, subtle hum, the Faerzess. 


Ahead, there was no longer the rough-hewn stone, but an area suffused with the arcane glowing magic of the Faerzess. Fist-sized chunks of quartz crystals erupted from the wall, shedding a dim ten-foot radiance. 


Before, this would have been yet another obstacle, another exotic backdrop to our desperate flight to safety, but now I wanted to experience and explore. 


“Let’s collect some of these crystals and look around.” 


In a corner were two desperate humans. I stare at them and quickly surmise they are escapees from the Drow. 


“Who are you? And back off!” shouts a male human. He holds a crystal as a weapon. It’s too pathetic. 


They insist they are escapees of the Drow; I concur, but Zalthir, who is curiously unobservant, scoughs.


The male, dressed in rags and starving, explains that his name is Fuurm Coppernose, or Copper to his friends, who had lost his sister and went looking for her in the Underdark. 


I look at him and wonder if he has any skills as a rogue, a fighter, a wizard, or a cleric, but he explains that he is just a farmer. 


I am astonished that he is alive and question how he escaped. 


Baedora, the woman who is dressed in rags as well, and is equally starving, explains, “That it was demons that attacked. The Drow fought back. We ran. Figured we were more likely to stay alive away from Drow and Demons.” 


We have collected so many strays that one more won’t harm us. 


And so I invite them to join our group. 


Before we leave the cave, we take as many crystals as we can. To hide our tracks, we smash the cavern and the crystals, as if some monster had attacked, not escaped slaves who took crystals that shone in the darkness. 
## 2nd day of the 2nd tenday of Myrkhul, 1495


### Grygum 


Three more days in the dark. Was it three more days? I think it was. And finally, the winding passageway opened, and the air grew heavy with the subtle, earthy scent of the damp loam and something indescribably sweeter. 


There were the towering stalks and caps of fungi, a veritable forest of the subterranean. Mushrooms of all kinds and of all types, stretching up into the unseen ceiling, some glowing faintly with a soft, bioluminescent light, others a muted, earthy brown. We were making good progress, moving at a steady pace since Eldeth had done well to hide our tracks in the faerzress crystal cave we'd just left. Stool, my new myconid friend, was curled up contentedly against Thorin's back, probably still reliving his 'good idea' to ride on the dwarf. It was, for a moment, almost peaceful.


Then, it hit. A sudden, piercing shriek that ripped through the heavy air and vibrated through the very rock beneath my feet. Not one, but many. Shriekers. The kind of noise that sends a jolt of adrenaline through any sane creature, screaming "danger!" and "dinner is served!" to anything else that might be lurking in the tunnels. My first instinct, honed by years of getting into and out of trouble, was to find cover or to run further into the fungal maze.


But then a thought sparked, a mischievous, practical thought. We had just gathered a small collection of those glowing quartz crystals from the previous chamber. I half-expected a surge of spores, some defensive blast, or perhaps even a confusion spell, something bizarre and dangerous to grapple with. For a moment, I even thought, "I was gonna have to survive throwing a crystal at the shrieker".


But no. The crystal sailed through the air, a small, glowing arc, and clattered against a broad fungal cap. And the shrieking... didn't change. It was a bit of an anticlimax, really. Just shrieking. No grand magical effect, no sudden silence. Just continuous, irritating noise. Still, at least it meant the fungi weren't actively harmful themselves, just loud alarms for bigger, hungrier things. 


## 3rd day of the 2nd tenday of Myrkhul 
### Grygum 


Another cave full of crystals. And we collect more. Eldeth is worried; she’s reasonably convinced we have thrown the Drow off our tracks, but every time we mess with the environment, we make it easier for them. 


I tell her not to worry. 


Jimjar shouts, “Want to make a bet?” 


And so I turn to him, “Wanna bet that these crystals will be useful?” 


He looks at me, confused. He’s not used to other people making the bet. 


But he smiles and says, “Yes, I do.” 


## 5th day of the 2nd tenday of Myrkhul 
### Zalthir


My dragonborn scales feel rough against my skin, constantly reminded of the grime and the chill that permeates everything. We've evaded the Drow, thanks to Eldeth's shrewdness, reducing their pursuit to a mere whisper behind us. Fuurm and Baedora chat nervously, their bellies full and their spirits somewhat lifted. Jimjar, of course, has already started a new round of bets, probably on how many steps Thorin takes before tripping. My vow of silence, of speaking only when there's truly something to say, feels easier with each passing mile.


Then, it happens. A soft, feminine voice, distant yet clear, rings out in my mind, "Hello? Is someone there? Oh, please, I need your help! I have been trapped in the dark for so long... so very long. Please, won't you help me? Won't your help to free me?"


My senses immediately sharpen. This isn't the first time strange voices have echoed in these depths, but this one carries a distinct plea. It’s also unnervingly direct, pinpointing the direction of a tomb entrance. I glance at the others, confirming they've heard it too. "Is that the direction we were going anyway?" I ask, my voice a low rumble, ever the pragmatist, seeking confirmation that this isn't some new diversion. 


Serith nods yes, this is the way. Of course, it does. Nothing in the Underdark is ever truly straightforward, yet here, a trapped voice leads us exactly where we intended to go, The Lost Tomb of Khaem.


We arrive at a large double door. The stone is ancient, hinting at a lost era, a common theme in this alien landscape. Inside, a chill descends, and my eyes adjust to a faded grandeur. A stone diorama stands to the right, depicting a Netherese noble, Brysis of Khaem, surrounded by her retinue, flaunting wealth and power. To the left, a sprawling mural covers the wall, illustrating fantastic floating cities.


I know the lore of the Netheril Empire – a civilization of immensely powerful magic users, whose cities defied gravity, only to fall catastrophically when one of their leaders tried to seize all magical power, tearing the very fabric of reality. It's a stark reminder of unchecked ambition, a cautionary tale against hubris. My monastic training emphasizes balance and inner peace, not the dangerous pursuit of absolute external power. It makes me wonder what kind of sorceress Brysis was to be buried here. 


We descend a dusty staircase. Grygum, with his keen perception, takes the lead, carefully testing each step. On one landing, I note a Netherese Calendar Stone, a relic of a time long gone, its purpose unclear. The stairs finally give way to a shrine, its floor littered with shreds of dusty tapestries.


I focus, drawing on my monk's discipline, seeking patterns, anomalies in the dust, and then I speak, "Proof here of a lower power." 


What an absurd conclusion that immediately grates on my own rational mind. How dangerous to be so easily misled by my own mind, in a place where observation is key to survival.


Grygum, thankfully, is more successful. He notes recent damage but no tracks in the thick layer of dust. He then recalls old tales of “floating undead creatures that typically rest in tombs”.


Yes, I agree, a "lost tomb" implies something more than just undisturbed dust and cobwebs. It implies guardians, dangers, and perhaps, the very undead Grygum speaks of. I remember Fargus Rumblefoot's warning about a "secret room" or "false tomb" here, adding another layer of treachery to this place. Daz checks an altar, finding nothing of arcane significance, another dead end for now.


There is a passageway south and one going east, so we proceed East. The room we enter contains four stone sarcophagi. Markings of robed human figures in repose adorn their lids. We inspect them, looking for traps, for anything unusual. And then, Grygum points it out: the northeast sarcophagus rests on hidden stone rollers. A possible secret, a hidden passage. This is it.


I move into position, my hands forming the familiar gestures. I call forth the shadows, plunging the area around the sarcophagus into a sphere of magical darkness. Daz won't be able to cast Magic Missile within it, but that's a calculated risk. Thorin, strong as an ox, leverages the darkness, pushing the heavy sarcophagus aside. The stone grinds, revealing... a hole. The true tomb, perhaps. We are finally here. My instincts prickle.


A chill emanates from everywhere at once. Rime creeps across surfaces, and my breath plumes before me in the suddenly frigid air. A moan, pained and distant, rises with the wind, and I see a figure detach itself from the shadows and glide toward me—weightless, formless, undead.


Daz


The sarcophagi stand, silent and imposing, lining the room like morbid sentinels. Zalthir, ever the master of shadow, had just blanketed the area in his magical darkness. I appreciate the tactical advantage it offers, though it always makes me momentarily blind until my own Drow eyes adjust to its particular brand of void. Thorin grunts, a familiar sound of effort, as he shoves the heavy northeast sarcophagus lid aside.


A gasp from someone, then a sudden, chilling presence. From the newly opened space, a form coalesces from the gloom – translucent, malevolent, undeniably undead. A Spectre. My instincts, sharpened by a lifetime of navigating the treacherous currents of the Underdark and the fleeting horrors of Velkynvelve, scream danger. Then, another, and another. My mind quickly registers the threat: four Spectres now hover, incorporeal and menacing. "No, you must save me!" a soft, feminine voice cries out in my mind. It's the same voice from when we entered the tomb, trapped, pleading. It adds another layer of urgency, a distraction from the immediate threat before us. These are Brysis's retainers, then, still bound to her ancient tomb.
It's my turn. I assess the swirling forms, their spectral claws already reaching. With a familiar incantation, I conjure the shimmering darts of pure force. Magic Missile. Two bolts streak through the darkness, finding their mark on one Spectre. Another bolt follows, striking a second Spectre. Each hit is satisfying, a clean impact on an ethereal foe. They might be immune to a host of things, but raw magical force isn't one of them.


Then, their icy touch. One Spectre lunges at me, its chilling form passing through my defenses. A jolt of cold, raw energy courses through me, necrotic damage, and I feel my very life force diminish. Before I can fully process it, another Spectre attacks, a more potent drain this time, twice as much necrotic damage, and my strength wanes further. My body screams for respite. "I'm gonna need a long rest here pretty soon," I mutter, the words barely audible, a desperate plea for recovery. This is not the clean, surgical combat I prefer. This is a battle of attrition, and my life force is the price I pay.


Grygum, in his devotion, tries to turn them, his holy symbol raised, but the Spectres resist, their wills too strong, or perhaps their existence too ancient to be swayed. Zalthir, ever the whirlwind, moves his sphere of darkness, then his fists become blurs in the gloom, punching and pummeling. He kills one, then another. "I will punch the ghost!" he declares. I can almost hear Pelek, our ghost, correcting him. 


With the last Spectre vanquished by Zalthir's relentless strikes, a momentary quiet descends, broken only by our heavy breathing. My mind is already racing, calculating. "Is this, would this be a good time for us to take a… Long rest. By chance?" I inquire, hopefully.


My mind races through tomes I studied, drawing on my arcane knowledge, and I remember that many such creatures will return after being destroyed when the central evil force that created them remains.  


If we rest, we will find them here when we come back. 


And although they may just stay here, they may also pursue us. 


Before we go down the dark passageway, we open the sarcophagi. Each sarcophagus contains mummified remains, the tattered remnants of ancient burial garments, and treasure of Netherese origin.  


They are worth a great deal as gold, and perhaps even more to the right collector. 


### Grygum


We descend, Thorin taking the lead, his broad shoulders easily pushing through the confined space. The air grows heavier, thick with an ancient stillness. We enter a new chamber, and the faint light catches murals on the walls, depicting grand scenes, probably of the Netherese sorceress Brysis of Khaem and her lost cities. A gilded sarcophagus gleams against one wall, clearly the focal point, and I notice the subtle shimmer of an invisible stone chest near its foot. Predictable. An actual tomb wouldn't be without its grand pronouncements and its hidden treasures.


Then, a cold, empty feeling washes over me, a familiar touch of undeath. From the sarcophagus, a figure rises, ethereal and malicious. A Wraith. Its voice, resonant with ancient menace, fills our minds, not with the pleas of the previous Spectres, but with a chilling declaration: "Your deaths will free me from this prison, and how my victims will serve me even in death!”


I remember that a wraith, when it kills, turns its victims into specters, and I shudder. 


Daz contemplates a Fireball, but then remembers that they are resistant to fire. “Magic missiles it is,” and the bolts of power are precise and undeniable, but the spectral form endures.


The wraith glides forward, a silent, menacing presence, and attacks Thorin. But the dwarf's armor and the grace of Bahamut protect him; the attack misses. Then, in a move I find utterly infuriating, the Wraith hovers back into its sarcophagus. "Coward," I think, a dry amusement stirring within me. Retreating into a stone box is hardly a display of power.


Zalthir moves. Ever the tactician, he casts his magical darkness, spreading it to cover the entire room. The already dim tomb plunges into an even deeper, magical gloom. I hear him move, and then move the sarcophagus. Just as the lid creaks aside, the soft, feminine voice from earlier, the one that first called to us, speaks again, "In the sarcophagus! I can help you!" 


So, the trapped entity, the true owner of the voice, is inside this sarcophagus.


Is it the Wraith or is it something else? 


Zalthir looks in.
