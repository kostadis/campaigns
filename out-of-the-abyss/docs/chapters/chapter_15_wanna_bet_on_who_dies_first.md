# Chapter 16 Wanna Bet on Who Dies First?


## 9th day of the 1st Tenday of Myrtul 1493
### Grygum


The stone giant with the two heads burned and wavered but did not fall. Bahamut’s magic did not kill him. 


A flash of flame engulfs the giant from two duergars that have come to join the battle. 


Startled, Grygum notices that the fire comes from a device they are holding. It is a tube that connects to a bottle on their back. At the front of the tube, a small flame sits outside the tube. He realizes that the flame is produced from some kind of magic concoction lit by the tiny flame when the duergar operator pulls a lever. 


“Don’t kill him! There’s something wrong with this giant! Let’s try and subdue him,” shouts Thorin. 


A few bolts of magic streak from Daz’s hands. 


Grygum looks at Daz, and then looks at the giant, and thinks, “Well, I guess Daz chose to weaken his spell purposely.” 


Grygum hears from within the darkness the sound of Zalthir’’s fists hitting the giant and the blade of Thorin, but still, the giant stands. So Grygum rushes in to strike with his mace. 


But the Giant stands. 


The Duergar, unconvinced by Thorin’s entreaties, kill the giant. 


Grygum watches as a distraught Thorin whispers prayers for the dead. 


\*pop\*


Grygum looks up, and the enlarged Duergars have shrunk to their standard size. 


### Thorin


Although Thorin is unfamiliar with the patterns of behavior of the Duergar, the madness of a stone giant is unheard of. The Stone Giants don’t move. They are taciturn to a fault. And so he needs to learn more. 
He turns to one of the Duergar, “Are there more crazy people about?” 


The Duergar stares at him, confused. “Crazy?” 


Thorin remembers that the Duergars are literal people but is uncertain how to continue. 


The guard sensing that asks, “Someone who does not follow orders and engages in anti-social behavior, but does it in a way that will bring them no profit and certain doom?” 


Thorin breathes a sigh of relief, “Yes.” 


The Duergar turns to the others on his patrol, briefly discusses, and says, “We have noticed that there have been significantly more incidents of this kind of behavior. As a result, the work of the militias has increased.” There is a pause, “And we know of no underlying cause. Although a clan leader may have information they have chosen not to share with us.” 


Before Thorin can ask a follow-up question, another Stone Giant appears, flanked by Duergar, armed with curious stone weapons. 


The Stone Giant stares at Thorin, whose body and blade show that he fought and perhaps killed the two-headed giant. Thorin remembers that Stone Giants are not talkative and may take a long time before they initiate a conversation as they ponder all possible things.  He begins the discussion by asking about some stone giants he has heard of. 


The Stone Giant doesn’t smile, “No. But then again, I don’t know many. Who are you?” 


And Thorin explains his curious relationship with giants and how he got here. 


The Stone Giant pauses, then speaks, “I am Dorhun, and this is Rihuud. We were both apprentices to Stonespeaker Hgraam. Did you kill him?” 


Thorin explains how he tried not to kill but subdue him and that it was the Duergar. The Stone Giant confirms his story and sighs again. 


“We have qualified immunity if a Stone Giant is rampaging through our city,” one explains. 


The Stone Giant sighs. 


Then he says, “I ask you to visit me at Cairngorm Cavern before you leave the city.” Turning to the Duergar with the stone weapons, he says, “Stone Guards, you are to let them through.” 


The dwarf watches how the Duergar gives the Stone Giant the briefest of scowls, showing how much they don’t want to give this non-Duergar permission to visit their city. 


## Zalthir


Zalthir is trying hard to protect himself from being burned alive, crushed by a giant, or arrested. 


And he’s feeling good about his chance when he hears a voice and a body materialize behind him.


“I gotta get me some of that ability,” he thinks. 


A Duergar appears right behind him. Zalthir immediately notices the scars on his ears, four notches that don’t look self-inflicted. 


“I am Gartokkar Xundorn, and these are my Duergar.” 


Just then, a few other Duergar approaches to see what is happening. Gartokkar flashes a bade of some kind, and the other Duergar move away quickly. 


Zalthir thinks, “And that, too.” 


“I am the Custodian of the Flame, the chief of the Keepers of the Flame, who tend to Themberchaud’s needs.” 


Zalthir stares, confused. 


“The furnaces of Gracklstugh must be kept hot, and instead of using a large amount of kindling, we use a fire dragon that burns stone hot that then is used to keep the furnaces running.” 


Zalthir hears Daz ask while holding down a snigger, “So he’s a lighter?” 


Gartokkar is a Duergar, and he is literal and misses the sarcasm, “Yes. The Keepers of the Flame tend to the dragon’s needs. And we are the most prized of the Deepking’s advisors. We need someone like you.” 


Zalthir hears Grygum’s joke, “We do have some kindling here.” 


Gartokkar laughs. “ A joke. I have not heard a good joke in so long.” 


Then, he becomes serious. “Your skill set would be of great value to the city of Gracklstugh. If you were to come to the Keepers of the Flame headquarters and do me a service, I could give you safe passage through the city. And as long as you did nothing that the Deepking would disapprove of and would not require me to do too much, I could keep you out of the prisons.”


There is a moment of hesitation, “The offer expires tomorrow. And if you don’t accept the offer, I cannot promise we will be so friendly when we meet next.” 


Gartokkar disappears. 


## Daz


The story of this dragon intrigues Daz. 


“How does a dragon end up turning into a lighter? What’s in it for him? Red dragons are not exactly known for their willingness to serve. They want to build a hoard or something. A dragon that flies around lighting fires is odd.” 


His friends look at him, and he looks back. 


“Well, we should find out what we can do before we help him. Perhaps we can go to the brewery?” 


And so, from just outside the Blade Bazaar, Daz and his odd collection of travelers arrive at the Darklake Brewery.


The Brewery is closed to non-Duergar, and Daz is disappointed until Grygum, who sneaks a quick peek, says that inside is a pretty dour place where people talk only in hushed whispers. 


Just outside is a Drow and Halfing, and they are drinking beer. Daz and Grygum approach them. 


The halfling looks at Grygum, “You must be new.” 


“I’m just glad you see an orc and don’t run off scared.’ 


The halfling laughs, “I am in the Underdark. I see so many scary things. You are the least scary thing I have seen this week.” He pauses, “You won’t turn invisible?” 


He laughs and, after being introduced, asks, “You haven’t gotten in trouble with the law?” He looks at them and says, “Of course not; otherwise, you would be in the Overlake Hold and not hear talking to me.” 


“Say, you wouldn’t be the people who took down the crazed giant?” 


Daz acknowledges that. 


“Good for you.” In a conspiratorial tone, he says, “Did any of the factions approach you for a job? You would be crazy to join them, and they are trying to kill each other. It all began with the Deepking’s madness.” 


Daz describes the madness of the merchants, and the halfling shrugs, “Yes, that kind of madness. However, in Deepking’s case, the laws are more arbitrary. We’ve always had laws in Gracklstugh, but they made sense. Now, they don’t. It’s as if he is detached from reality.”


The halfling again whispers, “You must have met the Keepers of the Flame? They are in a pickle, what with the Gray Ghosts and the Crown.”


“How do you know so much?”


“I’m an outsider. And what do I know? Not very much. And as long as I stay an outsider, no one considers me a threat. And I can share what I know with those not in the game. And you are not part of the game, nor am I.” He pauses, searching for a phrase, “What is that expression? It's -- Not relevant to the game.” 


Daz wonders, “Can you come with us to the meeting with the Keepers of the Flame?” 


“No, I won’t. That might make me appear like a player in the game. I like to watch. I would avoid such encounters and actions. Although, it might be impossible to stay alive, anyways.” 


At that, Daz asks about the dragon. 


“Yes, the Red Dragon. The city has had one for ages. The red dragon keeps the stones hot enough for the furnaces to burn. Are you wondering about its horde? The keepers of the flame give him a horde, and they pamper him. The problem, and you’re right to wonder, is that Red Dragons dislike playing second fiddle. And so, at some point, the Dragon discovers it’s not in charge. Then, the Keepers of the Flame have to kill the dragon. There is also the problem of the dragon becoming more powerful over time. So the Keepers must kill and replace the dragon with a new one.” 


Daz is about to ask why but remembers how Dragon eggs can be purchased and hatched. 


At this point, the Drow sitting next to the Halfling splutters, “Look, friend, I am from Menzoberranzan. The backstabbing and politicking are nothing compared to the Duergar. I mean, we will do it with style and finesse. Here, it’s just slaughter. No torture, no speeches, no art, and I mean no style. It was just a brutal struggle. They just engage in violence for violence’s sake. It’s disgraceful if you ask me.” 


At this point, Daz wonders if they should interview the various factions to find out which one they should join or whether they should join any of them. 


### Thorin


Thorin downs his Darklake Brewery Stout, burps, and then sees a group of Duergar with curious stone weapons approaching. 


“I am Errde Blackskull, captain of the Stone Guards on the lake, and I must ask that you come to my office so we can talk.” 


Daz stares at her, “Are you threatening us?” 


Thorin grabs his sword and wonders where exactly they will run to because it’s not clear how they can beat the entire stone guard, but the effect Daz was hoping for is achieved. Errde is taken aback and smiles, “Of course not. It is a friendly conversation.” 


Thorin, worried that some faction might think they are joining with the Stone Guards, shouts, “It’s just a friendly meeting. Not that kind of meeting!” 


The Halfling smiles and touches his finger to his head as if to signal that Thorin has made the right move. 


A few minutes later, they are in the Overlake Hold, the local guard house. Errde brings them to a sitting room. Like everything in this town, it is austere. The chairs and table are made of stone. There is no color—the light is grey. And the room is just cold enough to make one feel they are deep underground. 


A subordinate brings some pastries and some warm liquid to drink, and after they have eaten, she speaks. 


“I have been tracking a particular Duergar named Droki. He lives in the West Cleft District, where the Derro live. If we were to go there in force, it would provoke a violent reaction. So we need someone who isn’t us to find him.”


Thorin is tempted to say yes, but Daz holds his arm, “Of course, but we would need remuneration for all of that plus our time.” 


“You want me to pay you?” 


“It makes it sound dirty, more like helping us help you.” 


Thorin is unconvinced. He is tempted to work for the Stone Guards. After all, they are friends of the Stone giants, but the warnings of the Halfing worry him. 


“Are we helping you? Or the city?” 


Errde’s face suddenly darkens. “There is something very wrong going on in my city. Some kind of corruption eats at the heart of the Deepking’s court. Droki is the key to uncovering that corruption. He is linked to the Council of Savants and maybe even elements of the Clan Lairds. Discovery of this conspiracy will earn me great favor with the Deepking. And when that happens, I swear you will get free and safe passage out of the city.”


Thorin pauses, “Look, we want to know if this is for the Stone Guards or the City?” 


“It is my job to protect the city from corruption. So yes, it is for the city.” 


She pauses, and Thorin notices that there seems to be something odd about her, “Do you have any proof of corruption?” 


Daz asks, “What is corruption?”


Thorin stares at him, confused, but remembers the previous encounter with the Duergar. 


Errde smiles, “ Things were not proceeding according to the rules of the market, of the bazaar. It starts with not obeying the rules. Then it proceeds to grift, then chaos, and then destruction.” 


Daz smiles, “Yes, there was much of that going on.” And he repeats the behaviors at the Blade Bazaar. 


Thorin is confused, and Daz mutters, “In this authoritarian, strict-as-rules city, what we saw is corruption.” 


Errde smiles, “You have found me proof!” 


Thorin says, “Yes!” 


“Excellent. As a reward, I offer you 175 gold pieces of equipment from our armory. When you bring me Droki and the proof I need, then I will give you 175 more.” 


She then smiles very evilly, “And I can offer one more thing.” 


Thorin looks at her, “If a particular group of Drow were to come looking for some escaped slaves, I could tell them that they are not here. As you think about which group to work with, remember I am the one that can guarantee your freedom.”
