# Chapter 49 Out of the dark and into the darkness.


## Daz — The Mercenary Contract
The two of them hadn't lowered their weapons so much as they'd decided we weren't worth the effort. I filed that away. There's a specific kind of professional contempt that tells you more about a situation than any interrogation would.
Nym spoke first, in that tone Menzoberranzan produces naturally — the one that sounds like courtesy and isn't.
"Daz, I understand why your concern for your safety existed."
I appreciated the past tense. It did a lot of work.
The pieces assembled themselves quickly, the way they do when you've been trained to read a room before you've fully entered it. Two Drow. Duskryn name. Hired in Menzoberranzan. Hired to protect me — specifically, *me* — from Ilvara's House and from the T'sarran goons we'd just finished burning. Someone had paid for this. Someone who knew I was here, knew who was hunting me, and knew enough to contract professionals rather than handle it directly.
That last part was the interesting part.
I'd been running a list in the back of my head since Velkynvelve — the kind of list you keep when your life depends on knowing who wants what. On that list: entities with motive to see me dead. The list was substantial. Entities with motive to see me alive and sufficiently protected to require hired blades from Menzoberranzan — that list had, until approximately this moment, been empty.
I asked the obvious question. You always ask the obvious question, because the answer is rarely the obvious answer.
"What is the terms of your contract, just to make sure that I... am alive, or that I won this particular battle?"
Nym's expression didn't change. "Our terms of our contract were to make sure that the other drow factions didn't get you killed."
Kaelira gestured toward the scorched remnants of the extraction contract the party had produced — the document that had apparently been at the center of all this — and addressed me with the brisk precision of someone who had already decided the conversation was almost over. There were two threats named, she said. House Mizzrym. House T'sarran. Those were the parameters. They'd been prepared to handle the T'sarran contingent personally if it came to that. It hadn't.
"We hoped that you'd be smart enough to avoid Ilvara... well, we could have handled House T'sarran and whatever goons they threw at you. But you were able to do it quite on your own. Bravo, bravo, bravo."
Three *bravos*. I have found, as a general principle, that a third *bravo* is never sincere.
I pressed the logical extension of the argument, because that's what you do when the other side thinks they've finished the transaction.
"If you haven't eliminated all the other factions, and I go and get myself killed in the next 15 minutes, and you guys go and collect on the bond, and then your employer finds out — it's not gonna be a good day for you."
The sisters exchanged a look. A brief one. The kind that means *we talked about this already and the answer is still no.*
When the subject of Asha came up — our own Lolth cultist, devotedly genuine in her faith and thoroughly deceived about mine — Nym dismissed it with a kind of elegant indifference that I found, privately, somewhat reassuring. A window into what they actually thought of the threat assessment.
"If Daz can't protect himself from a low-level cult initiate of Lolth, then quite frankly, whoever paid for his survival deserves to lose his money."
And then, with equal efficiency: "You could probably kill her in a few seconds."
I chose not to confirm or deny this. What I said instead was: "Hey guys, thanks for the help."
Thorin, to his credit, tried to retain them. He asked what it would cost. Nym's answer was three words and they landed like a portcullis.
"That is a fee... that is a price you cannot afford."
No elaboration. No negotiation. Just the statement of a fact as though she'd assessed our resources at a glance and found them inadequate — which, to be entirely fair to her, she probably had.
Then Kaelira said the thing that made me go still.
She was looking at the contract. Looking at the numbers. And then she looked at me, and there was genuine puzzlement in it — not contempt, not theater. Actual, professional confusion at an anomaly that didn't fit the pattern she'd been given.
"That is an excellent question. You don't belong to any noble house, your background is a complete mystery, you suddenly show up, and all of a sudden, we're contracted to protect a nobody. Do you know how much money that required us to get, for us to do? A lot. Like, they interrupt you. A lot."
A nobody. The word landed cleanly, without malice. It was the professionally accurate term. I had no house. No lineage with the right names. No claim on any of the structures that give a Drow life its currency in Menzoberranzan. And yet someone had paid *a lot* — her emphasis, not mine — to keep this nobody breathing.
I said what I said, because there wasn't much else to say: "So then maybe there's more to me than meets the eye?"
Kaelira's response was sharp and not unkind. "Yes, yes, yes, that's what every orphan thinks. Do you have daddy issues? Do you have mommy issues? Every drow thinks there's some noble things. For all we know, this was a game being played by the houses."
And when I held the line, because I couldn't quite let it drop: "Perhaps, but we're not paid to think that hard."
Right. They weren't. That was my problem.
I will say this about Kaelira Duskryn: she had the manner of someone who had seen through a great many self-mythologizing idiots and wasn't interested in entertaining another. It is, in certain lights, a useful quality in a professional. In the context of someone disassembling the question of why I was apparently worth a significant expenditure to some unknown party in Menzoberranzan — it was less convenient.
I said what I actually believed, which is a thing I do less often than I probably should: "I literally have no idea why there's all this focus on me."
The party fell into the kind of quiet that follows a statement that everyone believes. Thorin had that look he gets — the one where he's deciding whether to charge at the problem or stand very still and wait for it to explain itself. Grygum was probably filing away some theological observation about mysterious dispensations. Zalthir, characteristically, was simply watching.
The sisters departed. Professionally. Without ceremony. The contract was fulfilled; they were done.
I considered what remained. Someone in Menzoberranzan had spent generously — *they interrupt you, a lot* — to ensure that a male Drow apostate with no house affiliation and no apparent strategic value survived long enough to do whatever it was I was apparently supposed to do. They had done this anonymously. Through cutouts. In a manner that ensured even the hired blades didn't know who was paying.
In Menzoberranzan, that level of insulation around a financial transaction means exactly one thing: the payer has significant reason not to be associated with the purchase. Which means the purchase is either embarrassing, illegal, or dangerous to be seen making.
Protecting me was one of those three things to someone powerful enough to fund it.
"You know what would help?" I said. "To audit their books."
No one laughed. Grygum looked thoughtful, which is his equivalent.
I sat with it privately, the thing I wasn't going to say aloud: *for all you know, the reason this all got started was because they all decided to be friends with the wrong drow.*
The thought had a particular shape. Elegant, in the way that retrospective clarity sometimes is. The whole of it — the capture, the escape, Velkynvelve, the months of running — threading back to a single point. Me. A nobody. A nobody that someone in Menzoberranzan had interrupted their schedule over, more than once.
Milo Goodbarrel, that relentlessly hopeful halfling whose book I'd read too many times to admit, had a phrase for the sensation I was experiencing. He called it *standing at the edge of the question that changes everything.* He said you could always recognize it because it made you feel very small and very important at the same time.
He was, annoyingly, not wrong.


## Zalthir — The Fate of Asha Vandree
The moment Asha Vandree declared herself in charge, something in me went very still.
She said it with the absolute confidence of someone who had just survived something she shouldn't have — chaos, screaming, close calls that she'd already reframed as divine favor — and that confidence was the problem. *Excellent! Now that I am in charge!* The words landed in the space between all of us like a stone dropped into still water, and I watched the ripples. Grygum's expression shifted: not offense exactly, more the particular look of someone who had decided, quietly, to do something about it. Daz's face was carefully neutral in the way that meant he was calculating very quickly. Thorin was already moving.
I was watching Asha.
I've read rooms like this before. The Garden Shadow had a saying — or possibly Brother Quellin did, the one with the dusty robes who taught something about pressure points of social authority, a class I attended for approximately four sessions before concluding I'd rather be running drills — that the most dangerous person in a room isn't the one with the blade drawn. It's the one who has already decided they've won. Asha had already decided.
Grygum tried. I'll give him that. He leveled something at her — a directed burst of divine light that should have taken her off her feet — and it missed, and she turned the miss into a declaration.
"I am blessed by Lolth!"
*Sure,* I thought. *Let's see.*
"I certainly don't like condescension, regardless of—oh," Grygum said, the 'oh' arriving at the exact moment the bolt went wide. He recovered quickly: "Let's put a period after that 'now that I'm in charge' thing."
Thorin put a period on it in a different way. He crossed the distance and got his hands on her — properly, a full grapple, one hand clamped over her mouth before she could invoke anything else. It was effective. It was also not a solution.
Nym watched this unfold with the expression of someone observing a very slow cart roll into a ditch.
"When attacking a cleric of Lolth, Thorin, one does not just simply grapple them." She managed to make *grapple* sound like a mild obscenity. "One takes your blade and cuts their heads off. Are you well?"
"I also put my hand across her mouth so she can't talk, so..." Thorin said.
Nym looked at him for a long moment.
"It's amazing that you people have made it this far."
She wasn't wrong. That was the irritating part. A Lolth cleric with her mouth uncovered is a Lolth cleric who can pray, command, curse — and a grapple, however competent, doesn't hold forever. We had Asha contained for exactly as long as Thorin's arms held out, which in practical terms meant we had a few seconds to decide what we were doing.
I looked at Daz. He was still calculating. I looked at Grygum, who had the expression of someone who would prefer not to kill anyone and was trying very hard to think of an alternative. I looked at Asha, who even now, hand over her mouth, was watching us all with eyes that said she was already composing the prayer.
There was a class for this. Decisive action under moral uncertainty — the ethics of necessary violence, or something like that. I think it was Brother Harren, the one who never sat down, who taught it. I don't remember what he said. I remember it went on for a long time.
"I'm making a call," I said.
I stepped in.
The Eldritch Claw Tattoo doesn't announce itself. One moment I was behind her, the next my hands were something else — longer, reaching — and the strikes came in sequence, each one placed with the kind of precision that the monastery's combat drills were actually good for, the part of the curriculum I had paid attention to. It was quick. Asha Vandree did not get another word out.
The silence after was the particular kind that follows a decision you can't walk back.
Then Kaelira started clapping. Slow, deliberate, the kind of applause that is also a critique.
"Well done, well done, well done!" She sounded genuinely pleased, or at least genuinely amused. "See, he wasn't completely defenseless."
Thorin, still holding what had been a problem until a moment ago, said: "I could have taken her out if I wanted to."
"Of course," said the Drow who'd responded, and the flatness of it was its own verdict. "And that is precisely the problem."
"I don't like killing people if I don't have to," Thorin said. "I mean, you know..."
He trailed off. I didn't fill the silence. He wasn't wrong either — that's the thing about both of them. Nym and Kaelira's calculation and Thorin's reluctance and Grygum's preference for alternatives and Daz's careful neutrality — all of it was reasonable. All of it was true.
I just happened to also be true.
Someone had to make the call. I made it. That's all.
I didn't look at the body.


## Grygum — Looting the Drow Outpost
The keys were the first thing I noticed. A jailer's ring, iron and heavy, each key worn smooth from years of use. I turned them over in my hands and felt the particular weight of irony settle in my chest.
"You know, they would have been very useful in the very beginning," I said, mostly to myself. "These are the very keys that had you imprisoned."
Daz looked over from where he was already cataloguing the rest of the room. "We've come a long way."
He said it lightly. He meant it seriously. We both knew.
The payroll was next — a chest of coin, Velkynvelve's operational budget rendered in gold and silver. A hoard is always valuable, as the maxim goes. I took notes. I didn't touch it yet; I wanted to understand the room before I started carrying things out of it.
Then I saw the other chest.
Confiscated prisoner belongings. Ilvara had been carrying it. Of course she had — she was exactly the sort of high priestess who would keep trophies, who would drag along evidence of her ownership of people the way some clerics keep relics. I opened it, and the contents were exactly what you would expect from someone who understood that stripping a prisoner of their possessions is the first act of stripping them of themselves.
"If there was anything that was missing from the very beginning," I said, not quite finishing the thought, "like your tooth, Thorin."
Thorin looked up. Something shifted in his face — recognition, and then something quieter beneath it.
"Oh, yeah. I want my tooth back."
He reached in and found it. He held it for a moment without saying anything. Then:
"Very sentimental."
Very sentimental. That's one word for it. Another word is: she had been carrying his tooth around the Underdark. She had collected it like a curiosity. I filed that under Ilvara, and closed the file, because she was already dead and I had killed her, and whatever she had been carrying was ours now.
We also found a water-damaged journal from a prisoner we would never meet — someone who had written things down and then, presumably, stopped being able to. I held it carefully. The pages were swollen and the ink had run in places, but the handwriting was still visible in the drier sections, the marks of a person who had kept records right up until they couldn't. I took note of that too. It's what I do.
The flask of Overbright brandy I received with considerably more personal interest.


Asha Vandree's body was next.
I should say something pious here about the dignity of the dead. My mentor Gorg'Bahamut would have wanted me to. But Asha Vandree had been a cleric of Lolth who had also become, in some capacity, a cleric of Zuggtmoy, and in the end she had been indistinguishable from the rot she served. So I looted her with the efficient respect one extends to a theological problem that has been resolved.
The holy symbol of Lolth. Chain mail. Prayer beads. And a sealed wax pouch.
Zalthir took the Drow cloak she'd been wearing — the fine spider-silk kind that drinks shadow — and I did not begrudge him that. I had the wax pouch, and I suspected it was the more interesting item.
I was right. I was also, immediately, very tired.
The seal broke cleanly. Inside were pages of close, careful script — the handwriting of someone who had once been methodical, who had trained as a theologian and brought that training to bear on a question that should not have been asked. I read far enough to understand what it was, and then I looked up.
"It's basically a long theological exploration of why Zuggtmoy and Lolth — and claiming that Lolth and Zuggtmoy are the same thing," I said. "It's very tedious and boring."
Tedious and boring. That was me being diplomatic. What it actually was, was the written record of a mind finding its way toward the conclusion that two incompatible things were secretly one thing — the kind of reasoning that starts from a desired destination and works backward. Clerics of Lolth have been doing this kind of theological contortion for centuries; what was unusual here was the destination. Not just *we serve the Spider Queen.* Also: *and the Demon Queen of Fungi is the Spider Queen, so the rot in everything sacred is also sacred.* It was very well-argued. It was also completely mad.
"Is this pre-craziness or post-craziness, do you think?" I asked.
No one answered with any certainty. I went back to the pouch.


Ilvara's prayer book was still on the body when we got to her.
"You know, I'm the cleric," I said, "so I'll examine it gingerly."
Gingerly was the right word. A prayer book belonging to a high priestess of Lolth who had been actively subverted by Zuggtmoy was not an object you wanted to open carelessly, the way a letter from a diplomat who has recently defected is not an object you open over your breakfast. You approach it with the assumption that the text will tell you things you may not want to know.
It told me things I did not want to know.
The main text was a standard Lolth prayer book — the kind of thing they give to promising young acolytes in the outer sanctums of any major drow religious house. Formatted prayers. Liturgical responses. The hierarchy of supplication laid out in careful descending order. Ilvara had presumably carried it since early in her career, which meant it was old, which meant she had been using it as a devotional object for years before something began to change.
The marginalia told the story.
"It's just a prayer book," I said, turning pages slowly, "but you notice it has Zuggtmoy marginalia — the margins in a different hand — since it shows the corruption timeline. So basically, you can see, like, she was normal, and then things started to go sideways."
A different hand. That detail mattered to me more than any of the specific words. Someone else had written in her prayer book. Or — and this was the reading that settled in my stomach like cold stone — she had written in it herself but in a state so altered that the handwriting had changed. Two hands because two minds had been working the same pen. The early entries were neat and formal and exactly what you would expect. Then, gradually, around the margins, a second voice crept in. Less structured. More associative. The sentences didn't always end. Some of them were questions that answered themselves with other questions.
I had seen something like this before, in a completely different context — a merchant I once knew who had been Tongued by a bardic curse, whose correspondence from that period was eerily similar: coherent on the surface, and then underneath the coherence, something working against the grain of itself. I had taken notes then too. I was taking notes now.
The corruption timeline was legible if you knew how to read it. Ilvara had been normal — a functioning, ambitious, politically dangerous high priestess — and then something had found the edge of her and started pulling. The marginalia documented the pull. She had not resisted it. I could not tell from the text whether she had known she wasn't resisting or whether she had genuinely believed she was still moving in her own direction. That ambiguity was the most troubling part.
I thought, briefly, of Stroud sitting down across from Protanther. Four hundred years of preparation. Still had a blind spot. Sometimes the thing that undoes you isn't the opponent you've been studying — it's the thing you didn't know to look for.


The sealed orders from House Mizzrym were more immediately practical, and I was almost grateful for them.
I read them aloud, more or less: "Where are you? What have you been doing? Respond — kind of thing. You've gone missing. Followed by more frantic: where are you, where are you? Didn't you read the previous memo? What's up?"
Daz was paying attention in the way he pays attention to things — still, cataloguing, already working the implications.
The letter from Neverlight Grove completed the picture, in a grim sort of way. A missed meeting. Political correspondence that had gone nowhere, because the person it was addressed to had gone somewhere else — somewhere a Demon Queen's influence had been a stronger pull than any political alliance.
I put the letters with the other items. They were someone's records of a relationship that had dissolved without anyone on one end knowing why. House Mizzrym would learn eventually, I supposed. We had helped with that.


The last item was on Ilvara herself.
"The Living Serpent Head Scourge sounds..." I paused, considering the thing, which was exactly what it sounded like — a scourge whose head was an actual living serpent, or had been, in some intermediate state between alive and not. "It sounds like a great museum piece."
Someone laughed, or made a sound adjacent to laughter.
I meant it, partly. There is a tradition, in certain temples I have visited, of displaying items that are terrible in origin and remarkable in craft, on the theory that acknowledging the craft is not the same as endorsing what the craft served. A great museum piece. Bahamut's treasury is full of things taken from hoards that should not have existed. You document them. You label them carefully. You do not pretend they aren't what they are.
I set it aside with the other items. The prayer book. The wax pouch. The sealed orders. The serpent scourge. A cleric's catalogue of the things a high priestess of Lolth becomes when something older and stranger finds the seam in her theology and begins to work.
I had so much I could have said about any of them. I took notes instead.


## Thorin — Looting the Fallen and the Heart Fungus
The heart of it was still warm.
The fungal mass sat at the center of the chamber the way a problem sits at the center of a bad situation — obvious, unavoidable, radiating something wrong in every direction. Most of it was dead now. We'd done that. But the core hadn't collapsed the way the outer layers had, and when I pressed into it to see what was holding, my fingers found something that wasn't fungus.
Smooth. Dense. Fist-sized. The weight of it was wrong for something grown — too even, too finished, like a stone that had been worked by a hand that knew what it was doing. And the sensation coming off it wasn't heat or cold exactly. More like standing next to a door that opens into a room you don't want to know about. Necromantic, I decided, the same way you decide the smell coming from the back of a giant's larder means something died in there a while ago. I didn't need a name for it. It was worth something to the right buyer, and I was not going to leave anything useful in this cavern, so I pocketed it.
Behind me, Grygum was dealing with the platform.
Ilvara had built herself something elevated, which made a certain kind of sense — High Priestesses don't stand at floor level, they stand at the level where everyone has to look up at them. But the fungal growth that had crept up the stone was still pulsing with Zuggtmoy's reach, slow and rhythmic like breathing, and Grygum was not the sort to leave corruption breathing. He opened his mouth, and the acid came out clean and decisive, and the fungal matter that had spent months becoming something wrong collapsed into ash in the time it takes to exhale. The smell that followed was scorched rot, which is not pleasant, but it was still better than the alternative. Better burned than spreading.
The bundle was underneath.
Leather-wrapped, neat in a way that the rest of the chamber wasn't. Inside: incense cones, a silver bowl small enough to fit in one hand, and a cloth that had been stitched with two sigils — Lolth's web worked into the eight-fold geometry of Zuggtmoy's mark, one woven inside the other. I looked at it for a moment. I've carried stranger things out of stranger places in the last several months, but this one sat differently. It wasn't equipment. It was evidence. It was Ilvara's madness made physical — the thing she'd built her rituals around while she was becoming something that served two masters and understood neither.
I wrapped it back up and moved on.
The guards hadn't survived, which was expected, and I moved through the bodies the way you move through anything unpleasant — efficiently, without dwelling. Gold, short swords, a long sword, a steel whistle. The dead soldiers of House Mizzrym would never collect their pay. I didn't pray over them. They'd come here doing what they were told by someone who had already lost her mind, and I had nothing to say to them that wasn't just "better luck in another life." Sometimes that's the whole eulogy.
The mage was different.
I found the signet ring first — House T'sarran, the seal unmistakable on the face of it. Then the wand: obsidian, a captive spider preserved in amber at its tip. Then the documents. They'd been partially burned, which meant someone had tried to destroy them and hadn't quite managed it, which meant the information in them was exactly the kind of thing worth knowing. Daz took one look at the signet ring and did what Daz does — processed it in about three seconds and came out the other side already thinking about what it could be used for. The ring was proof. It was also a key.
The documents were worse. What was left of them, in the partially legible pieces that the fire hadn't finished, confirmed what we'd been trying to piece together since the mage had arrived with her mercenary escort. There had been a contract. Extract Daz alive and unharmed. House T'sarran behind it.
I turned that over in my mind the way you turn a stone over to check the underside. Somebody had spent a significant amount of gold to ensure that the elf standing to my left made it out of this cavern breathing. Somebody had wanted him badly enough to hire professional Drow mercenaries and route the contract through a house with enough reach to make it stick. And the mage who'd been carrying those orders was now dead on the floor with a T'sarran ring on her finger.
We showed the documents to Nym and Kaelira.
Nym had the kind of face that gets more expressive the more irritated she becomes, and right now she was very expressive. Kaelira was the opposite — still and professional, reading the burned pages with the focus of someone who was paid to be good at exactly this. She examined, she considered, and then Nym said what Nym was thinking:
"That is an excellent question. You don't belong to any noble house, your background is a complete mystery, you suddenly show up, and all of a sudden, we're contracted to protect a nobody. Do you know how much money that required us to get, for us to do? A lot. Like, they interrupt you. A lot."
Daz looked at her with something behind his eyes that I couldn't quite read. He let a beat pass, and then he said: "So then maybe there's more to me than meets the eye?"
Which was, I thought, the most restrained thing he could possibly have said in that moment.
Nym was not moved. "Yes, yes, yes, that's what every orphan thinks. Do you have daddy issues? Do you have mommy issues? Every drow thinks there's some noble things. For all we know, this was a game being played by the houses."
Kaelira said, without looking up from the documents: "Perhaps, but we're not paid to think that hard."
Which was the most honest thing anyone had said in this chamber all day. I found myself respecting it more than I probably should have.
I had the necromantic pearl in one pocket. I'd already turned over the question of what it meant — something this finished, this dense, at the core of a corrupted fungal mass that Ilvara had been using as a ritual anchor. A rock in the wrong place is just a rock. A rock in the right place is a wall. The pearl wasn't in the wrong place by accident. Someone had put something like it there, or something like it had grown there because the conditions were exactly right for it. Either answer was a problem that needed a name.
But that wasn't the only thing I was carrying out of this room.
When we'd first found ourselves hanging in nets at Velkynvelve, Ilvara had made a point of collecting trophies. It was the kind of thing a person does when they want their captives to understand the power relation clearly — she'd taken my tooth. A chip broken off in the capture, displayed in her chest like a piece of evidence of what she could do. I'd thought about that tooth on and off during the months since. Not constantly. Not dramatically. But the way you think about a debt that hasn't been settled yet.
It was settled now. I had the tooth back. And I had the pearl that had been buried in the heart of whatever she'd turned this place into.
Two objects. One that marked where we started, and one that marked how far the corruption had spread by the time we got here.
I didn't say any of that out loud. Thorin Giantfriend, radical transparency, except when the thing being said wouldn't add anything to the next ten seconds. We still had a chamber to clear, a party to account for, and the smell of scorched rot hanging in the air like a question that hadn't finished resolving itself.
I looked around the chamber once more, checked my pocket, and moved on.


## Daz — Reflections and the Road to the Surface
The question had been sitting in the middle of our camp like a stone none of us wanted to trip over, and eventually someone kicked it.
Was it all of us they wanted? Or was it me?
I turned it over the way I turn over most things that unsettle me — analytically, at arm's length, with the careful distance of a man cataloguing someone else's problem. The Duskryn sisters had been hired through cutouts. The contracting party was unknown even to them. They had come specifically for me, through layers of intermediaries arranged with the kind of deliberate opacity that costs serious coin to maintain. That is not how you extract a prisoner of incidental value. That is how you extract a target.
The honest answer — the one I kept arriving at and then setting aside — was that I had no idea why.
I am not, by any meaningful measure, a person of consequence. I am a male Drow apostate from Menzoberranzan who studied magic, held private opinions that would have gotten him killed if voiced aloud, and eventually ended up in Velkynvelve's cages alongside a dwarf raised by giants and a dragonborn who doesn't talk much. My lineage is not distinguished. My House, if I ever truly had one, is not significant. I do not possess anything worth this level of orchestrated attention.
And yet.
I said as much to the group — that I was, genuinely, confused. That powerful Drow houses do not spend this kind of resource on nobodies. That the gap between what I know about myself and what someone apparently believes about me was large enough to be, frankly, concerning. And then I did what I always do when a problem has no clean solution and I need the room to stop looking at me like I should already have one.
"I think," I said, keeping my voice very even, "that we should investigate this together. Whatever we find — whoever is behind this, whatever they think I'm worth — we split the proceeds."
Audit their books. Understand the ledger before anyone decides whether to collect the debt.
Nym and Kaelira regarded me with the specific expression that Drow reserve for people they find simultaneously interesting and beneath consideration. Nym — or possibly Kaelira, I was still calibrating them — made a sound that was almost a laugh.
They had opinions, the sisters, about mysterious orphans who arrive at adulthood convinced they have noble blood waiting somewhere. They had seen this particular performance before, apparently. The tone was sardonic in the way that only people trained in the same school of thought as me can manage — not cruel, just precise. An accountant's skepticism applied to genealogical pretension.
I did not argue the point. I don't know that I could have. The uncomfortable truth is that they were not entirely wrong to be skeptical. Every minor Drow functionary with a grudge and too much ambition imagines himself the dispossessed heir of something. I have, privately, catalogued that possibility and found it embarrassing. Whatever the answer to this question turns out to be, I prefer it to be something more interesting than inherited blood I didn't earn.
I filed the question where I file most things that don't have immediate answers — in the part of my mind that I return to at inconvenient moments — and let the conversation move on.
It moved, with startling speed, to Dawnbringer.
Someone had raised the possibility — I believe it was raised tactically, as these things usually are — of using the sunblade in some manner of purification ritual. It was a reasonable suggestion on its face. Dawnbringer is a weapon of radiant power. Ritual purification is within a reasonable interpretation of that portfolio.
Dawnbringer disagreed.
"I'm not some kind of magical wizard thingy that purifies things. I am a high-quality sunblade. I kill things. I will not be treated like a religious symbol to be used and waved over corrupt—"
The blade did not finish the sentence, which I think says something about the depth of its feelings on the matter.
I have made my peace with the fact that we travel with a sentient weapon that has opinions. It is, in context, one of the more manageable elements of our current situation. I noted the outburst, noted Thorin's expression — the particular patience of a man who has had this conversation before and expects to have it again — and watched.
Thorin reached, unhurriedly, for the scabbard.
"Do I need to show you the scabbard again?"
There was a pause. I have observed that the Vaultmaster Locking Scabbard functions as a remarkably effective negotiating tool when Dawnbringer's rhetoric outpaces its better judgment. The pause had the quality of something reconsidering its position.
"I suppose…" Dawnbringer began, and then, with the dignity of someone granting a significant concession: "I suppose I could do you this one. I could do this solid for you."
I made a note — mental, as always — that the scabbard was doing more diplomatic work than it was probably designed for, and that Thorin was a more patient man than I would have been in his position. I also noted that the exchange had the comfortable rhythm of repetition. They had been here before, the dwarf and his sword. They would be here again.
Around me, the road to the surface waited. I had an unanswered question about my own identity sitting unresolved in my chest, a pair of Drow mercenaries who found me either fascinating or ridiculous, and a sunblade that had just been persuaded to participate in something it found philosophically objectionable.
Some things you audit. Some things you simply carry.
We moved on.


## Zalthir — Emergence into the Overbright
The tunnel mouth opened like a held breath finally released.
I had stopped counting the steps somewhere around the third hour of climbing — not because I lost track, but because counting had started to feel like superstition, like I was measuring the distance against the possibility of failure. The air changed first. It thinned in a way that had nothing to do with depth; it simply became something else. Something with weather in it.
Then the light hit.
I had been thinking about this moment, intermittently, for months. Not with anticipation exactly — anticipation implies you trust the thing to happen. More the way you might think about a theory you have not yet been able to test. The Overbright was a concept I had been carrying through Velkynvelve and Gracklstugh and the Darklake and Blingdenstone like a stone in a pocket, something I reached for when the tunnels pressed too close. I had stopped trusting it would be real.
It was real.
The light that came through the tunnel mouth was not like Dawnbringer's light, not like faerie fire or bioluminescence or any of the things we had been navigating by for however many months it had been — I genuinely cannot tell you how many months it had been. It was not a light that came from a source. It was a light that simply *was*, filling everything from every direction at once, and what I felt when it touched my face was a kind of violence, or close to it. Not pain. Just the body registering that something fundamental had changed, the same way a deep-water creature might register the pressure dropping.
My eyes managed it. Everything else took a moment.
The others emerged around me. Thorin first, which was correct — he always led from the front, always had, it was as close to instinct as that dwarf ever got. Grygum next, blinking with something that looked less like discomfort and more like relief, the earnest half-orc having what appeared to be a genuine spiritual moment with direct sunlight. Daz came through last, the way Daz always came through last, already observing before he was fully present, already composing the analysis before his feet had cleared the threshold. The rest of our moltley crew followed, Eldeth, Spiderbait, and the rest of the folks who had survived this journey.

I stood and let the light happen to me. I put my cloak in a bag. I knew that Drow cloaks disintegrated in the sunlight.
There was a class for this. Some form of material impermanence — I think it was Brother Quellin, or possibly the tall one whose name I never quite caught, the one who always had chalk dust on his robe. The transience of tools. The attachment to implements. Something about a bowl and an empty hand. I remember thinking that whoever designed the monastery curriculum had a great deal of opinions about things that were not fighting, and I was there to learn to fight, and the rest of it would work itself out.
It had not, in fact, worked itself out.
Grygum had opened the bag of holding.
I should clarify: Grygum had opened the bag of holding just enough, and Glabbagool — our companion, our gelatinous ward who was now a grey ooze, the most improbable relationship any of us had formed in the Underdark, which was saying something given the competition — had peered out. There is no other word for it. He peered. He had not been in sunlight before. None of us were entirely sure what Glabbagool's eyes were, from a structural standpoint, but they were wide and present and sweeping across the surface world with an attention that made my own response to the Overbright seem, by comparison, measured. He took it in the way a child takes in a first ocean, the way the sheer scale of a thing produces a silence that is not quite awe but is adjacent to it.
I watched him watch the sky.
Whatever he made of it, I did not have the vocabulary to ask. The Stonespeaker Crystal was Daz's. But I noted the response. Filed it. Glabbagool had survived Blingdenstone, the ooze crisis, the bag of holding, and however many months of Underdark travel, and his first reaction to the surface world was curiosity rather than retreat. I found that admirable in a way I was not going to say out loud.
We stood in the Overbright for a while, longer than was probably necessary from a tactical standpoint, and nobody suggested moving immediately. That was unusual. We were a group that moved quickly, that had learned to move quickly, that treated stillness as the thing you did when stillness was the only remaining option. But nobody moved. Thorin stood with his face at an angle that suggested he was calculating the sun's position relative to the year. Grygum was doing something with his hands that was probably prayer and probably sincere. Daz was, I suspected, already drafting a journal entry.
The weeks that followed blurred in the way that recovery blurs — not forgetting, exactly, but the edges going soft. We had months ahead of us before the next thing began, and the party spread toward its separate concerns the way a held breath spreads into air, each of us expanding slightly into the space the Underdark had compressed out of us.
The decision about what to do with the months was largely Daz's call, framed as a group discussion but with Daz's particular analytical gravity already pulling it toward a conclusion before the conversation began. He did this without meaning to. The conclusion — Candlekeep, the Great Library, research into House T'sarran and Jimjar's disappearance and whatever the various cutouts and hired Duskryns were ultimately pointing at regarding Daz's own history — was reasonable. Candlekeep had information. We needed information. The logic was sound.
Thorin, characteristically, was thinking about Dawnbringer. Not what to do with it; he would never give it up, and the sword would never go quietly. But what it had been through. Months of darkness, months of the deep, a blade whose nature was radiance forced to endure the complete absence of the thing it was built to carry. He had mentioned something about a specialist — the phrase *sword whisperer* had come up, delivered with the particular dwarvish seriousness that Thorin applied to things he actually cared about, which meant he believed this person existed and should be found. I had no opinion on sword whisperers. I noted the category and moved on.
Grygum had pamphlets on his mind. This was entirely consistent. Grygum was the only one among us who had accumulated a list of things he wanted to read, and Candlekeep was going to be, for him, something between research and pilgrimage. He would look up the lore of Bahamut. He would pick up pamphlets. He would be genuinely delighted by the availability of pamphlets. This was Grygum.
Someone raised the question of the gold. We had more of it than we had started with — which was not saying much, given that we had started with nothing — and the debate that surfaced was the familiar one: invest it in something that might grow, or spend it on what would keep us alive. The logic for adventuring gear was simple enough that even I could lay it out without needing a meeting: we were people who went into danger. Gear reduced the consequences of danger. Business ventures required infrastructure we did not have, stability we had not earned, and a calendar that the demon lord situation was unlikely to respect.
We agreed on the gear.
I stood at the entrance to the surface world, squinting at a sky that went farther than anything I had navigated in months, and I thought: this is where we were going.
We had made it.
Now there was everything else.
