# Chapter 46 Fungus Among Us

---

## Daz — The Spider's Blessing

The plan had three moving parts.

First: my familiar. A small spider, real enough, convincing enough if you don't look too hard at the absence of divine ichor. Second: Dancing Lights, shaped not into floating globes but into something grander — a silhouette, towering and feminine and terrible, the suggestion of eight eyes and infinite patience. Third: my nerve.

I've run more complicated cons with worse materials.

The spider scurried back across the fungal floor toward me on cue, and I was already weaving the lights around it — not frantically, not with the showy flick of wrists that amateur illusionists love, but with the quiet precision of someone who has thought this through to the fourth step. The luminescence built. Condensed. The shape of the Spider Queen herself, reduced and reduced and *reduced* until it shrank into that small skittering body and seemed to pass directly into mine. Then: darkness. The performance was seamless.

I know what I'm doing.

Behind me, somewhere in the fungal shadows, Glabbagool was doing what Glabbagool does. Which is to say: failing at the very simple task of being a rock.

I had given him clear instructions. Motionless. Silent. A feature of the cavern. And he had, apparently, decided that rocks sometimes needed to narrate their own invisibility, because he started *talking*. I heard the reminder that invisible rocks do not speak. I heard — I am fairly certain I heard — the small wet sound of him opening his eyes, which then began tracking left and right with the particular energy of someone who absolutely cannot help himself.

"Glabbagool," I said, without turning around, without breaking the posture of a man recently vacated by a goddess, "I'm taking care of this. It's fine."

It was fine. It had to be fine.

Asha Vandry was watching me with those wide, fractured eyes — the look of someone who has spent so long in paranoid devotion that suspicion has become its own kind of faith. The spectacle had landed. I could see that. What I could not yet determine was exactly *which* way it had landed.

She spoke.

"You're a wizard. A wizard… a wizard who prays to Lolth?"

Not awe. Suspicion, clean and immediate. The question of someone who has been deceived before and has filed the memory carefully. I felt, for a fraction of a second, the specific sensation of a gamble that could go either way.

I leaned in. Not physically — just into the role, into the space the performance had opened.

"I am… you are not speaking to the wizard of this body. I… he… this body has communed with Lolth. And I… I am speaking through this body."

Conviction carries what evidence cannot, if you commit to it fully enough. I committed.

Then she surprised me.

"Aha," Asha said, and her voice had the bright, brittle quality of someone who has just solved a puzzle, "you are trying to test me, to see if I was one of those fake followers of Lolth, who are willing to believe every charlatan magic user that is somehow being possessed by the power of Lolth."

I took a moment to process that.

She had not seen through the deception. She had reframed it entirely — had taken my con and reclassified it as a *divine test of her discernment*, which she believed she had now passed. The paranoia hadn't broken my gambit. It had built her a completely different story in which she was the clever one.

There are players who don't know they're at the table. There are players who think they're winning. And then there is Asha Vandry, who had somehow managed to be both at once.

I recovered. This is what you do. You don't argue with the story someone has already decided to believe — you find the thread that connects their version to yours and you pull it.

"There are many imitations," I said, and let the words settle, "but as you saw, I came to you in my smaller form, and led you here. You should know the truth."

The spider. Ground it in the spider, in the thing she had actually witnessed with her own eyes before any question of wizards or possession had entered her mind. A deft redirect — I was pleased with it — anchoring the claim in observable fact rather than asking her to simply take my word for a goddess.

And then the floor dropped out a second time.

"Oh, dear goodness," Asha breathed, and I watched her face change again — not into belief, not into suspicion, but into a third thing entirely, "have you two been infected by the spores, possessed by the crazed magic of Zuggtmoy? convinced of the bride's power? Is this another trick of Elvara's?"

She was not calling me a liar. She was asking whether I was a victim. Whether the lights and the spider and the quiet reverence were not deception or divinity but *infection* — the particular madness of a fungal god's influence playing tricks on a compromised mind.

I stood in the dark of the Underdark with a gelatinous cube behind me failing to be a rock, and I considered the specific difficulty of selling a lie to someone whose paranoia keeps generating better explanations than the truth.

## Zalthir — The Deception of Asha Vandry

There is a particular kind of silence that drow society uses as a weapon. Not the comfortable silence of empty stone corridors, not the productive silence of a hunter waiting for movement — the silence of deliberate erasure. Of being present in a room and being treated as furniture. I have spent enough time among my own kind to recognise it immediately. It never gets easier to absorb.

Asha Vandry noticed me the moment Daz beckoned and I stepped out of the shadow where I had been standing. I could see the calculation cross her face — male, surface-trained, drow enough to identify but not drow enough to acknowledge. She filed me away and returned her eyes to Daz. Not a glance afterward. Not a word in my direction for the duration of the conversation. Every question, every demand, every withering assessment delivered squarely to Daz, as though I were a piece of equipment he had brought along. A weapon he'd sheathed. A pack he'd set down.

I have noticed this before. I will notice it again. I noted it, set it aside, and watched.

Daz had already threaded us this far into the deception, which was impressive. Asha's opening had been a pointed thing — *"Have you two been infected by the spores, possessed by the crazed magic of Zuggtmoy? Convinced of the bride's power? Is this another trick of Elvara's?"* — the kind of question designed to see what shape your panic takes. Daz didn't panic. He gave her something more interesting: doubt worn as sincerity.

"I really do believe that I have," he said. "Perhaps there are some spores within me, but I… it feels… I feel it. And by the way, I've convinced others to join me."

The pause before *it feels* was good. The hesitation wasn't a crack — it was a texture. It made the conviction sound lived-in. I would have let her sit with that pause a moment longer, but Daz had his own tempo and it was working.

Then Glabbagool nearly ended everything.

The ooze had, apparently, opinions about the negotiation. It began to form the shape of an utterance — which is not something you want from what is supposed to be an inanimate ooze familiar — and Daz caught it with the ease of someone who has managed Glabbagool before.

"Glabbagool, I'm taking care of this, it's fine."

An ordinary sentence. The kind of thing you say to a dog that has started moving toward the table. Asha processed it as — I assume — some mild eccentricity of a spore-addled convert. The cube subsided. I did not visibly react. Somewhere behind my eyes, I filed it away in the archive of things I have witnessed that I will never be able to explain.

Asha began to talk about Ilvara, and the temperature of the room shifted. This was not careful diplomacy anymore. This was something older and sharper.

"Ilvara, who was once a high priestess of Lolth, has become seduced by this abomination, this idea that Lolth wishes to be married. There is something foul and stinking about her, and then all of those allies she seems to covet have turned into fungal creatures that are mindless. We need to kill her. We need to butcher her\! We need to send her to an unearthly grave\! I fear that her disease will spread."

Hatred and theology, wound together so tightly you couldn't separate one from the other. Asha's conviction about Lolth's will was absolute and cold, and the fury underneath it was not cold at all. She wanted Ilvara dead with a fervor that had been marinating in this cavern for gods know how long. I believed every word of it. The useful part wasn't the emotion — it was the intelligence hidden inside it. Ilvara's spores. A mushroom artifact. The specific vectors of infection. Asha had been studying this the way I study a tunnel entrance, and she had handed us the map without realizing she'd done it.

When Daz asked what they'd get for solving her problem, I watched Asha's posture tighten.

"The gratitude of Lolth," she said.

"I already feel the gratitude of Lolth," Daz said. "I have that."

I did not smile. But I noted it.

There is a specific kind of bold that is indistinguishable from foolishness until it works, and then it looks like mastery. Daz was operating in that register. Asha's response was to question the nature of his conviction — *"That is not the kind of conviction a follower of Lolth shows"* — and Daz didn't flinch.

"It's the kind of conviction this follower of Lolth shows."

She could have decided to kill us all at that moment. That was a real possibility. She didn't. Which told me something about how desperate she actually was.

That desperation became explicit when Daz pushed on the obvious question.

"How do you propose to kill her if you're not gonna get within 20 feet of her?"

A beat. Then: "I was not going to kill her. That is what you… where you come in."

The honesty had an almost refreshing quality. She needed us because she couldn't do this alone. Whatever power Asha held, she was not willing to test it against Ilvara's spores at close range, and she had been sitting in this cavern, waiting for a solution to walk through the door. We were, apparently, the solution.

She made this clear in her own way: "I thought you had strong allies, who had a tactical awareness and understanding how to use their magic for destructive purposes."

She was still talking to Daz. She was gesturing vaguely in the direction of the rest of us — which I presume included me, standing here, a person — but the words went to Daz. I considered pointing out that I was standing directly in front of her. I decided that would not advance anything useful.

Daz handled it smoothly, explaining that none of us were specialists in fungal corruption, and that asking before blundering in was the sensible approach — not ignorance, caution. Asha considered this.

"Well put, well put," she said. "This was just a test." A pause. "Don't touch me."

That last line. The flinch in it. Whatever confidence she projected, whatever command she assumed over the conversation, Asha Vandry was afraid of contamination in a very specific and personal way. She had been in proximity to Zuggtmoy's influence long enough to make her paranoid in a direction that felt earned.

We withdrew to confer. The terms were clear enough: Ilvara dies, the mushroom artifact is destroyed, the infection does not leave this cavern. Asha had confirmed the critical detail — the Heart Fungus itself wasn't the vector. The spores were Ilvara's. The artifact was the other risk. Contain both and the job was done. The reward was everything Ilvara had accumulated, which, given that she was a high priestess who had been running an operation in this cavern, was probably worth the attempt.

Grim arithmetic. We did the calculation and arrived at the same answer.

I spent the hour before the assault doing the thing that was useful and quiet and required no discussion. I placed a zone of Darkness behind our position — not at the entrance, not anywhere Ilvara might notice the edge of it and understand what was coming. Behind us. A fallback. A place to pull someone if the line broke. Nobody asked me about it. That was fine. Rope you've coiled is still rope.

The scene with Asha Vandry ended the way all negotiations end when neither party trusts the other but both parties need the same thing: a tentative and mutual willingness to proceed. She had not addressed me once. I had watched everything.

I knew where the shadows fell in the cavern ahead. I knew the shape of what we were walking into. That was enough.

## Grygum — Planning the Assassination of Ilvara

The plan began, as most plans do, with me laying out what I had.

"It's a… the spell is Glyph of Warding," I said, because someone had asked and I'd been waiting for the right moment to explain it. "You inscribe it in a particular place, and then when somebody triggers it by moving across it, it does its thing." Simple. Elegant. Impersonal in the way that traps have to be impersonal, because if you think too hard about who's walking into it, you hesitate on the inscription.

Then I kept going, because the moment called for it. I'd been carrying these spells like coins in a purse I hadn't spent yet, waiting for exactly the kind of situation where spending them right could change everything.

"So I also have another spell I haven't had a chance to use yet called the Dust of Suleiman," I said, and I'll admit there was something satisfying about laying it on the table. "Which I can cast at a 120-foot distance. It creates a 20-foot sphere, and anything inside this sphere takes 20 points of damage per turn."

Daz was paying attention. I could tell.

So I kept going.

"Finally, I now have Phantasmal Killer."

"What's that?" Daz said. "Sounds fantastic."

It does sound fantastic. That's part of why I like it. "120-foot range," I said. "It says you tap into the nightmares of a creature you can see and create an illusory manifestation of its deepest fears. They become frightened if they fail a wisdom saving throw, and if they fail the saving throw in each turn — and it can last up to a minute — then they take 4 die 10 psychic damage."

A spell that reaches into something's mind and pulls out what it's afraid of. Bahamut didn't teach me that one, exactly, but I don't think he'd object. Some fears are just.

Then came the part I'd been dreading saying out loud.

"So these powerful spells I've been talking about… they're all 4th level, and I only have one fourth level slot. I can only cast one."

Three keys. One lock. The planning was, in a sense, just a very elaborate way of deciding which door we most needed to open.

I took notes in my head, the way I always do. Three options. One shot. Everything downstream of that decision.

---

I'm not too proud to ask for help when the terrain is unfamiliar. The politics of this cavern — the power structures, the hierarchy of what needed to die in what order to make the rest of it possible — that was Asha Vandry's territory, and I knew it.

"Well, that would be good," I said. "Does Asha have any ideas?"

Asking a contemptuous drow priestess for tactical input is not the most comfortable thing I've done in the Underdark. But it's what the situation required.

Asha looked at me the way you look at someone who's just suggested something obvious. "Well," she said, and I could hear the slight effort it cost her to engage with us at all, "that's an obvious one — use it on the big heart fungus."

"Oh," I said. "I was thinking about using it on Ilvara."

She stared at me for a moment. Then, with the practiced precision of someone who has spent years cultivating disdain: "I thought you had strong allies, who had a tactical awareness and understanding how to use their magic for destructive purposes."

There it is. The sting of being corrected by someone who hates you — made worse by the possibility that she's right. I took the note. I filed it. I did not say anything I would regret.

The Heart Fungus was the key. That was her point, and she wasn't wrong. The fungus was how Ilvara kept control over the whole area. Kill the fungus and you don't just hurt Ilvara — you dismantle the architecture of her power. The Dust of Suleiman, which I had been imagining pouring down on Ilvara directly, was actually more valuable as infrastructure demolition.

She was right. I hate when that happens.

---

Then came the question of who could safely touch what.

"Basically pointing out," Asha said, her voice shifting to the register of someone delivering information they find tedious, "that if Thorin and Grygum grab it — I think they were the two that went to visit, what's her name, right? — yeah, you two probably don't want to be near it, but everybody else is fine."

Whatever we'd picked up in Gracklstugh in the Whorestone Caverns when we visited the pocket dimension, had left a marker on us. The mushroom artifact could exploit it. Good to know before I walked up and tried to destroy it with my hands.

Zalthir, bless him, raised the broader concern about spores. He'd been thinking about it — we all had.

Asha's response was theatrical. "Oh, no, no, oh, oh, oh — you think that you'll get infected by some spores and turned into Zuggtmoy demon spores and become mindless creatures that are under the whim of Ilvara?"

"That was on the list, sure," Zalthir said.

"You worry about these things a lot."

"Yes," Zalthir said.

"We do, yeah," I agreed.

Because we had seen what that looked like. Zalthir gestured toward Daz. "I mean, — look at him\! He's already infected. I don't want to be infected."

There's nothing more clarifying than having a visual aid. The spores weren't theoretical. We'd seen what happened when someone stopped worrying about them.

Daz smoothed it over, as Daz does — explaining that our caution came from unfamiliarity with mushroom-related side effects, and that asking for expert guidance was tactically sound rather than ignorant. Asha was, apparently, briefly impressed.

"Well put, well put," she said. "This was just a test. Don't touch me."

The relief on Daz's face was worth something. The absurdity of Asha trying to pretend she'd been testing us rather than ranting was worth more.

Then she delivered the actual intelligence — through Daz, not through me, because I had apparently lost her respect at some point and she was going to make sure I knew it.

"My dear boy," she said, and then looked at me with contempt, and then turned to Daz. "Tell the Dragonborn that the big heart fungus will not actually cause any fungal disease, other than the usual poisoning and pain and suffering and breathing difficulties. The particular spore illness only originates from Ilvara's spores and from her mushroom."

She paused.

"I believe."

I believe. Two words that undermined everything she'd just said with such authority. I stored them carefully. *She believes.* Not *she knows.* Two very different categories of information, and I've been in enough bad situations to know the difference matters.

---

While we were deep in the logistics, Asha let something slip that I hadn't expected: genuine exasperation.

"The only three people who are no longer captured by her spores are myself and Jorlan," she said. "Me, because I have diligently kept the faith to Lolth, and have rejected all of these claims of the bride heresy, and Jorlan, because apparently he really hates Ilvara for something or other."

And then she kept going.

"It's very awkward, really, to be stuck with these two. I mean, they used to have an affair, it used to be this whole thing, and then she broke up with him, and then he was bitter because she broke up with him, and now I've had to be in the middle of these two for the last few months. Do you have any idea how aggravating that is?"

I couldn't help it. "Oh, man," I said. "These relationships are so complicated."

"I agree\!" she said, with a warmth she'd shown none of us since we'd met her. "Like, you would think that in the midst of the abyss with the demons, everyone could get over their relationship issues, but no, no, no, no, no — Jorlan still is complaining that Ilvara… Ilvara this and Ilvara that."

There's something very human about that. Very drow, I suppose. The world is ending and someone's still nursing a broken heart in the middle of it. I took notes. Not on the tactics — on Asha, who was more person than I'd given her credit for, even if she refused to speak to me directly.

---

Glabbagool nearly ended the meeting early.

At some point, Asha noticed the blinking eyes emerging from the ooze, and the question of cover story became suddenly urgent. Thorin solved it with characteristic practicality.

"Will Glabbagool be offended if we refer to him as a familiar? I think that's probably the easiest way to just explain it away," he muttered. Then the dwarf introduced Glabbagool as Daz's familiar.

Asha considered this. Her expression shifted from alarmed to — and I watched this happen in real time — impressed. "An ooze familiar," she said, looking at Daz now with something approaching respect. I could tel that she's now even more impressed with Daz, for securing the services of an ooze familiar."

Glabbagool blinked. Probably didn't mind. I made a mental note that if we survived this, Glabbagool deserved something for the indignity of being called a familiar, even if he didn't seem to mind.

---

The spore servants were the last piece I needed to understand.

"What manner of creatures be they?" I asked.

"They are the drow of our party whom have totally succumbed to Ilvara's power," Asha said. "There were those who foolishly believed Ilvara's claims about how the bride was Lolth's further manifestation, and who embraced the spores and the ecstasy that came through them."

People. Hollowed out. Not monsters dragged in from somewhere else, but people who had made a choice — a terrible choice, maybe a seduced choice — and were now walking around as the consequence of it. The glyph I was about to inscribe would not know the difference between them and anyone else who crossed it. I noted that too.

---

The glyph went down on the bridge.

I spent the necessary time on the inscription. Stone holds runes differently than wood — you have to press harder, commit earlier. While I was working, I heard myself say: "I don't know how to inscribe a glyph that will only kill the drow that we don't like."

Which is the honest problem with a blunt instrument. The glyph doesn't have opinions. It just detonates.

And I remember that we had picked Asha over Jorlan.

"Okay," I said. "And then, I guess the trigger is — anyone approaching from the side that Jorlan is on."

Not a perfect solution. A workable one. Jorlan was not our ally, even if he hated Ilvara. We'd made our choice between the two of them, and the glyph would reflect it. I finished the inscription and straightened up. The bridge was now a threshold. Everything on the far side of it was already committed to a detonation it didn't know about yet.

---

Thorin was working through the problem of his own usefulness, which I respected.

"What do you all think the best thing for me to do is? Because I'm kind of a brawler, and it sounds like we're not really going to get close."

Daz talked through the phases — the opening volley, the depletion of spell slots, the point at which the brawlers would need to go in. Thorin thought about his darts. "Yeah, my darts will only — are not great for range. This is… I gotta figure something out."

"Are you good with other ranged weapons?" I asked. "I can lend — I can lend you my light crossbow."

It's a small thing. But everyone needs a way to contribute when it starts. I handed it over and hoped he wouldn't need it.

---

We watched Ilvara from the corridor near the pools.

She was on an elevated platform. Corrupted. The fungal growth had changed her in ways that were specific and terrible — the scourge still in her hand, but everything else altered. She was preoccupied with the mushroom token. She didn't know we were watching. The Heart Fungus pulsed between us and her, enormous and obscene, the mechanism of her control.

I looked at her and I thought: Phantasmal Killer reaches into nightmares. What does she dream about? What would an illusory manifestation of her deepest fears look like?

Then Zalthir said: "I can try to grab Ilvara and drop her into that, such that you're hurting both of them, and then the rest of you can fireball or whatever from there."

And there it was. The final shape of it. Zalthir teleports in, grapples, drops her into the sphere of damage. The Dust of Suleiman burns the Heart Fungus. Daz fireballs the overlap. Thorin holds the line with my crossbow and whatever else he has. The glyph waits on the bridge for whoever tries to reinforce her.

One 4th-level slot. I'd made my choice. The Dust of Suleiman on the fungus, because Asha was right and the infrastructure was more important than the satisfying image of Ilvara's nightmares eating her alive.

I took notes on the whole plan, the way I always do. Not on paper. In my head. Every piece placed, every spell accounted for.

Now we just had to survive the execution.

## Thorin — The Battle of the Heart Fungus

The plan was always going to start without me.

That's not self-pity — that's just terrain. Ilvara is sixty feet up, Zalthir has wings, and I'm standing on a bridge over a pit that smells like the inside of a dying thing. The smart play is ranged. I know this. I made peace with it before the fight started. Mostly.

I watch Zalthir vanish — just gone, teleported straight to her — and then there's the crack of his fists finding purchase, once, twice, thirty-one damage in the space of a breath. That's the opening salvo. That's the plan working. I note it, feel the familiar surge of wanting to be in the middle of it, and then she melts.

She just *melts*. Into the floor. Into the fungus. The grapple breaks like it was nothing, and she reforms somewhere else in the cavern screaming, and I think: *okay. Different kind of opponent.*

The Heart Fungus has been sitting in the middle of the cavern this whole time, pulsing, and I have been treating it as terrain. That's a mistake I revise immediately when it unleashes a cloud of spores that hits Zalthir so hard he has to climb ten feet straight up just to breathe. The thing has opinions about us being here. Fine. It can get in line.

Then the spore servants come up out of the ground — four of them, former Velkynvelve guards, shambling and wrong — and now the cavern is full in a way that starts to feel personal. Ilvara is rebuilding the math of the fight in real time. She's fast. I respect it. I don't like it.

I don't have a ranged option worth mentioning. Dawnbringer is a sword, and I am a dwarf who was raised by giants, which means my relationship to range is "how far can I throw this rock." So someone lends me a crossbow, and I take it, because I'd rather be in the fight badly than out of it entirely. I hold it like a man who knows exactly how to hold a sword.

And then I see Jorlan.

He's across the cavern, bitter-eyed, nursing whatever wound his pride has taken since Ilvara discarded him. He's armed. He's furious. He's also standing exactly twenty feet from the Glyph of Warding that Grygum spent real patience setting into the stone of the bridge.

Jorlan doesn't know about the glyph. Jorlan just needs to take about four steps in the right direction.

I put the crossbow down.

I have been thinking in terrain and leverage since before I could name those words for what they were. Giants negotiate through position — you stand where you stand, and the ground under your feet says everything your mouth doesn't need to. Jorlan is a man who has been humiliated by someone he loved, left broken on the far side of that love, and he's been stewing in it so long the rot has gone all the way through. That makes him predictable. Rage that personal has a vector.

I just have to give him a target.

"Come get it, idiot\!"

It's not eloquent. Giants aren't eloquent, and neither am I, and neither is this situation. What it is, is *direct*.

Jorlan charges.

He comes straight onto the bridge, straight onto the glyph, and the detonation is immediate and total — 21 damage, one spore servant gone to nothing, another barely holding itself together, and the Heart Fungus itself shuddering from the force of it. The bridge holds. The math shifts.

I didn't expect it to work that cleanly. I'll take it.

Now I have Dawnbringer in hand — the real hand, the right hand, the one that knows what it's doing — and Jorlan is on the bridge in front of me, wounded and furious and still standing, which is a problem I can work with. The blade catches some ambient light from somewhere and turns it into something brighter, something that has no business existing this deep underground, and I close the distance.

The first hit lands true. Fourteen damage, and I roll through the Sap, put him off-balance, give him disadvantage on whatever's coming back at me. Jorlan staggers. He doesn't fall.

The second swing goes wide. I know it the moment the blade leaves my hands at the wrong angle — too high, overcommitted, Jorlan's AC is 18 and I'm rolling a 2 and there's nothing in the world that fixes a 2\. The Precision Attack doesn't find it. Jorlan is still there, staggered but not broken, which means he's still angry, which means we are not done here.

Then a spore walker climbs up behind me, because of course it does, and the first swing misses clean — the thing reaches up and just swings into nothing, which is the best that can be said about it — but the second one connects. Five damage. A glancing thing. Thorin, son of the Ember Vanguard, flanked by a dead man's fungal corpse while the fight rages in all directions around him.

I adjust my footing.

Behind me, deeper in the cavern, Grygum is grinding the Heart Fungus down with something that sounds like the word *Suleiman* and delivers twenty force damage per turn like a promise. The thing shudders. Good. Let it shudder. And then Daz opens up with a Fireball shaped so precisely around his allies that it catches Ilvara and her precious mushroom icon in the center of it, 24 damage worth of fire finding her even through her save, and the icon smolders.

The mushroom is burning. Ilvara is bleeding. The math has changed.

Jorlan is still in front of me, and Dawnbringer is still in my hand.
