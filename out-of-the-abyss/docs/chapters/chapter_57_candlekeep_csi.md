# Chapter 53 Candlekeep CSI 


## Daz — Investigation of the Keeper's Body

There is a particular comfort in a corpse. It does not lie. It does not run a counter-play. Everything it knows is written on it, and all I have to do is read.

Janussi, Keeper of the Tome, was laid out before us, and the early findings were already on the table. The heart was gone — torn out, not for any wound it would cause, but as a guard against any working that calls the dead back to answer. Whoever had done this had not wanted Janussi answering questions on the other side of the veil. The ribcage was smashed, something axe-like, and Grygum was the one who pressed on whether the cut was jagged or clean. *Is the wound jagged? Does it seem like it was cut out, or is it magical.* A fair question. The answer was the interesting part.

The blows had been precise. Too precise, too close together, for a man fighting back. No spray across the room — no blood pressure left to throw it. Janussi had been dead before his chest was opened. Whoever did this had not been in a rush. He had not been trying to kill. He had been desecrating a corpse at his leisure, with nowhere to be. I logged that. *The first thing to read is the tempo of the killer.* This one had time.

And then the nose. A whack to the nose, off-center, the kind of thing that happens to a face that meets the floor.

"Anyone want to take a closer look at the body?"

Grygum went first and announced his findings the way you announce bad weather. "I can barely tell which end is which." Thorin muttered something about having the second-worst eye for detail in the room. A low bar, apparently, and they were both under it.

So I stepped up. "Let me look."

I crouched beside the Keeper, and because the moment seemed to ask for it, I gave it the full performance. Steepled fingers. A long, considering pause over the body, the kind a man does when he already knows the answer and wants you to watch him arrive at it.

"Hmm. It would appear…" I let it hang. "Body is dead."

I did say it was a performance. The good part came under it.

Clotted blood under the nose. Smeared bloodstains on the right sleeve of the tunic — the *right* sleeve, the working hand. And blotches of midnight blue across the cuff. I knew that blue.

"Wasn't the blue ink — the ink they used in the books?"

It was. Janussi had been taking notes when this happened. Writing hand, fresh stains. A man interrupted at work.

But there was something else on him, and it was the something else that mattered. The tip of his left forefinger was blackened. Not blue. Darker than blue. And the same dark smudge sat dead-center on his lower lip.

"Like, Dumbledore style?" Zalthir asked. "Or, like—"

"With ink?"

No. Not ink. That was the entire point of the long look — not that the finger was stained, but that the stain was *wrong*. Darker than anything in his inkwell. Zalthir, ever the auditor of other people's competence, pushed on it — *our great detective took his long, careful look and still can't tell us if it's ink?* No. That was exactly what I could tell. The skill wasn't in identifying the substance. It was in knowing, with certainty, what it was *not*.

And the gesture wrote itself across the body. Finger to a page, finger to the lip — turning pages, wetting the fingertip the way readers do without thinking, a hundred times an hour, never once considering that the page might be the thing reading *them*.

"Do we think this black stuff might be poisonous?" Zalthir asked.

I thought it the likeliest answer. But thinking it and proving it are different things, and we had no one who could read the substance — until Zalthir, of all people, found the angle. "Wouldn't Glabbagool have ingested, like, all sorts of different kinds of materials and poisons and goo in the Underdark?"

Well played. An ooze is, if nothing else, a lifetime of taste-tests. We gave him the substance. He gave it his best. It wasn't enough — a thin first effort, then a second pass with help, and still nothing he could name. But even his failure carried a verdict: there was *something* about the black, something that mattered. When even a creature that has eaten the Underdark can't place a poison, you've learned the poison is worth being afraid of.

More surfaced. The tongue, blackened at the tip — ingestion confirmed twice over now. And a chip in one of his front teeth, the edge jagged. Recent. Not an old break worn smooth, but a fresh wound.

Thorin worked it like a problem. "So either that was related to him being murdered, immediately as part of the murder, or it was something we need to investigate — how did he chip the tooth recently?" Fair logic, but it carried its own answer: if the black smear was poison, why would a tooth chip belong to the murder at all? Two different stories were trying to live in one body.

Zalthir asked if anyone could use a working to find the chip. We didn't need magic. It was right there — a few feet from his face, sitting on the floor where it had landed when the man went down. He had fallen forward. Face-first. Chipped the tooth on the way down. And then someone had come in, after, and rolled him onto his back to take the heart.

"And also broke his nose," Grygum said.

That was the loose thread, and Grygum pulled it. His read of the wound came clean — sharp work this time — and the nose had broken *before* death. Bruising doesn't form on a corpse; there's no living tissue to swell. Zalthir worked the same logic from the other end, confirming it with Grygum twice over: *you don't bruise after you're dead.* So the broken nose belonged to the living man, not the desecrated one. Within the evening — Janussi had eaten dinner with his face intact and his teeth whole. Whatever happened, happened in the narrow window between that meal and his death.

I ran the sequence one more time. A man at his books, wetting his finger to turn a poisoned page. The black climbing through him while he read on, suspecting nothing. A fall, forward, hard — a broken nose, a chipped tooth on the stone. And then the patient hands arriving after, in no hurry at all, rolling the body and opening the chest to make certain he stayed gone.

The body had told us everything it knew. It always does. The only question left was who had been certain enough to want him silent on both sides of death.

## Grygum — Forensic Investigation of the Keeper's Chamber

Daz worked the body over carefully. He had the eye for it, and the recent education — Milo's murder mystery, fresh in his head — and when his focus snapped clean on the corpse he started naming things faster than I could write them down.

The keeper's left forefinger was blackened. So was the center of his lower lip, and the tip of his tongue.

"Like, Dumbledore style?" Zalthir asked. "Or, like…"

It wasn't a tattoo, it wasn't a wound. The man had been reading a book, smudging his fingers on the ink, and licking the smudges off his lip. A reader to the end. I noted that and felt something I didn't examine too closely — a clerk dying mid-sentence in his own library. There are worse exits.

Then the tooth. A blackening on the tip of the tongue and a chip in one front tooth, jagged, recent. Thorin turned it over out loud — either part of the murder, immediate, or something we needed to chase. Zalthir asked, half-joking, if anyone could find the missing thing by magic, and the chip turned up a stride from the keeper's face. Which told its own story: he fell forward, hit hard enough to break the tooth, and then someone came in afterward and rolled him over to take his heart. Died on his face. Got turned over by his killer. The sequence had a horrible tidiness to it.

That was where I came in, because Daz's reading and Zalthir's logic only got us to the moment of death. I had the part before it.

"And also broke his nose," I said.

I looked closer. This is the kind of thing I know — what a body does when the heart stops, and what it can't do. The bruising around the broken nose held its color, dark and settled, the way bruising only sits when blood was still moving when it formed. My focus narrowed and held, and the picture came clear and certain: this happened before he died. Not the fall. Earlier.

"Okay, so his nose was broken, he was killed, smashed on the face."

Zalthir caught the implication a half-step behind me. "Grygum, you've got the healer's eye, right? How does bruising work if he's dead? Like, I would assume you don't really."

"You don't — you don't bruise after you're dead."

The dead don't mark. The body has to be defending itself to make a bruise, and a corpse defends nothing. So the bruise wasn't from the killing blow. Somewhere between dinner and the moment his heart was carved out, someone had struck this man in the face hard enough to break the bone. A struggle. An assault. An hour of his evening I could suddenly account for, even if I couldn't see the face that did it. I filed it. A bruised nose into a timeline — it's what I do.

The neck told us more. The impression of a chain on the back and sides, and beneath it two broken lead links. Something had hung there, and someone had torn it off him. A locket, a necklace — gone, snapped at the chain. I have catalogued a great many sacred spaces in my time, and there is a particular grief in an empty setting where something belonged. The links were worn where it had hung. Whatever it was, he'd carried it a long time.

The room itself kept volunteering details. A silver pedestal by the desk, an ebony pipe finely carved, cherry tobacco. Pretty, until Zalthir's instinct landed it — tobacco smoke would hide the smell of poison. Of course it would.

Then the safe. A box of metal as wide as a man's reach, sunk into the tower wall, its door standing open.

"Oh, is anything inside?"

Empty pouches scattered in front of it, sapphires loose and dazzling, scraps of parchment naming spells. Daz's mind clicked again — material components, every pouch a spell — and he murmured, "a little bit of this, a little bit of that," pleased with himself. But one sapphire was missing. One parchment. "Somebody is trying to build a sapphire spell," he said, "a spell that requires a sapphire."

And here is where the room stopped making sense as a robbery. Because still inside the safe were four sealed pouches, each with its sapphire — and beside them a figurine of a raven, a potion of supreme healing, another of water breathing, five diamonds each worth a fortune, a heap of platinum and gold, a small treasury left untouched.

"In other words, score what they left them."

A small fortune, untouched. Whoever did this came in, took one sapphire, and walked past a king's ransom.

"I mean, well, that can eliminate some former adventurers," I said. The greedy don't leave the gold. This was a man who needed exactly one thing.

Zalthir checked the lock. Open, but undamaged. No forced entry. The killer had the combination, or had no need of one.

Daz drifted to the armchair next, and read the wounds in it the way he'd read the wounds in the man. Three circular impacts, horsehair stuffing scattered, two holes punched clean through the chair back. He studied it and pronounced it: darts of pure force. Somebody had been furious. But not at the keeper — those darts never miss their mark, Thorin pointed out, and the man bore no such marks. Somebody had aimed three bolts of force at a chair.

"Why would you loose three darts of force at a chair?" Daz asked. Nobody had an answer. I wrote it down anyway.

Last, the statue. Three feet of marble, a hooded bearded old man holding a candle aloft, toppled on the floor near the armchairs and the tea table.

"Shake the candle," Thorin said.

Tadric, who'd been hovering and guarding the scene, gave him a look.

"I don't want it to wake up and attack us," Thorin explained. "I've had, you know, had bad experiences."

It worked on poor Tadric exactly as you'd fear. He tried not to look at the candle and then could not stop looking at the candle, and finally turned to Thorin. "Are you sure this — is it true about candles?"

"I mean, you just never know when a statue's gonna wake up," Thorin said, kindly, and I do believe he meant it kindly. "When there's magic with the statue, who knows what's going on with the candle. But, I mean, chances are we're gonna be okay. Just stay around us and we'll protect you."

The statue had simply fallen, it turned out. The Great Reader A'lai Aivenmore had knocked it over in shock when he found the body at dawn.

"How heavy is it?" I asked. "Is it something that you could bump into and knock over?" Three feet of marble. Yes — a man could stagger into that and bring it down, if he'd just walked into a room and seen what we were standing in. I believed it. A scholar discovers a murdered keeper with his heart cut out and reaches blindly for the wall and the wall is a statue. That, at least, was the one honest accident in the room.

Everything else had been deliberate. I closed the file in my head, for now. Someone had struck him, broken his nose, waited or returned, killed him, rolled him over, taken his heart and one sapphire and a locket from his neck — and left the gold.

I took notes either way.

## Zalthir — Clues in the Ink and Parchment

The desk again. We came back to it. Not because anything was new — because we hadn't yet read it in the order it happened.

In the middle of the desk sat a spellbook. *The Discoveries.* Beside it, blue ink, fresh enough that it had a sequence to it. There was more than one color on those pages, and I wanted the newest one isolated before anything else. "There were multiple… colors of ink," I said. "There's… the blue one is the newer one. He was writing something, but what was the blue text?"

Musings on unfamiliar spells. Notes on how they might be cast — a wizard cataloguing things he'd seen others do and never managed himself. The sort of thing a careful man writes when he is trying to understand something larger than he is. I have a passing respect for that. I let very few classes at the monastery reach me, but the ones I let in, I let in completely.

What mattered was the timing. He'd been struck in the face, returned here, and written. Then he'd died. "Well, no, but he got punched in the face, came back here, was making notes, and then someone killed him," I said. "I'm curious… trying to understand what he was writing at the time of death." The room reads better when you ask what a man was doing in his last unguarded minute than when you ask who he was.

The blue ink on *The Discoveries* had been laid down after the blue ink on another book. *The Golden Ass.* "So that's the last thing he was writing?" Confirmation. The spell research was the final thing under his hand.

"The product placement notes," I said. A homage to some other adventure, a book you could go and buy if the mood took you.

"Oh, the mystery," Grygum said. "You have to go buy the other adventure." He found this funnier than I did. Most things, he does.

Then the second book complicated itself. *The Golden Ass* was a comedy. Janussi had been writing down jokes he found funny — the man had a sense of humor he'd evidently kept private. "No, wait," I said. "The blue ink captures both notes on obscure spells and jokes?" The same hand, the same fresh ink, two registers entirely. Jokes first. The ink on those had dried. Then he'd flopped over into the spell research. "So he was noting down jokes, and then flopped over to spell research." Yes. And somewhere in the seam between the two, the comedy book had walked out of the room.

That was the absence. The notes proved the book had been here — you do not annotate a thing you do not hold. And now it was gone, and it was the only thing gone. A room tells you most by what it is missing. The Garden Shadow had a saying about that — or possibly Brother Tharusk did, the tall one who always smelled of incense; I wasn't listening to either of them closely. The point survived anyway: count what should be present before you count what is.

Thorin had been turning the black residue over. "What color was, like, on his fingers and mouth, was it black or red?" Black. Definitively not the blue ink — Grygum had already settled that, checked it twice and certain. So the black on the man's mouth came from somewhere the blue ink did not.

A gift tag surfaced from the wastebasket as we were leaving — almost behind the desk, in a cramped, sour hand. "Wait, is that describing the type of writing?" Thorin asked. It was. "It's, like, grumpy?" I said. Grumpy. Cramped. The script of someone who resented the act of writing it. *To Janussi, read it and weep, you old snob, a proper Dead Winter gift, your loving rival.* Thorin lit up. "Okay, so that supports my theory. Some sort of rival gave him a gift, but it was sort of like a bitter thing."

There was one thing I wanted closed before we left it. The smoke. "Just to double check, do we know where the tobacco smoke was coming from?" Janussi had loved the stuff — kept his chamber thick with it, medicated his failing lungs with the smell of it, lived inside a haze on purpose. A man who fills his own room with smoke as a habit. Which means a man who would not notice if, for one night, the smoke was working for someone else.

"The one thing that tobacco smoke would do is hide the smell of poison." It wasn't a leap. It was the absence again, read forward. You don't fill a room with smoke to kill a man — you fill a room with smoke to keep him from smelling what's already there.

"Okay, so yeah, there's definitely some sort of poison going on," Thorin said, "and there's no way for us to figure out definitively, we've already sort of blown our chances." Probably. The black on the pages of a book that was no longer in the room. The smoke that hid the smell of it.

"If it really is a comedy book, there's probably not arcane knowledge or anything else there," Thorin went on. "There's something written in the book that wasn't part of the original book — or maybe he's using the book and the notes to do some sort of cipher. Some sort of replacement cipher, and there's, like, some sort of code that the book ends up being the key to. Am I reaching too deep here?"

"I like the way you're thinking," Daz said.

I said nothing to that. The poison and the cipher could both be true. The missing book carried both. We had read the room down to what it was willing to give. The rest of it lived somewhere else, and we'd have to go find it.

## Thorin — The Rivalry Revealed

We came out of the murder chamber and the air in the hall was better — not good, but better. Fembris was hovering the way assistants hover when they've decided you're the people who fix things, which is its own kind of pressure. He wanted to be helpful. Fine. Helpful I can work with.

Grygum and I both opened our mouths at the same time. We've been doing that a lot lately — months in a tunnel will teach two people to think along the same line whether they want to or not.

"have any enemies, right?" I said, and then caught that Grygum had started too. "I'm sorry, but I didn't mean to stomp on you."

"Oh, I was gonna say, does he have any rivals?" Grygum said. Then, dry: "Same question."

Same question. That's the thing about the two of us. He gets there with compassion and I get there with a hammer, but we get to the same door.

Fembris didn't even have to think. "Oh, that'll be Daral Yashenti. Those two have been at each other's throats for years." A beat, and then the part that landed clean. "He's the one who called you sellswords."

And there it was. A name with weight to it.

I knew the name the second he said it. Sell swords. Said it like he was wiping something off his shoe. I despise this whole place a little — not Candlekeep, Candlekeep's fine, the books are fine — but the *culture* of it, the scholars who keep grudges the way a dragon keeps gold, sitting on them for years, polishing them. In the Underdark everyone runs six angles at once and at least there it's honest about being a knife fight. Up here they smile and they file the grievance and they wait. Daral had been at Janussi's throat for *years*. Whatever was rotting in that man, it had gone all the way through by now. I've learned to read that. Rage that's had time to set is a vector. It points somewhere.

"Hmm, well, I already don't like him," Grygum said.

"Right?" Fembris agreed. "He's the one who was very, you know, that not seemed, very impressed with you."

Not impressed with us. The man who looked down his nose at the sell swords is now the sell swords' prime suspect. I didn't say it out loud, but I'll admit there was a particular flavor to that. Giants would have appreciated it. A rock in the wrong place is just a rock. But a rock in the right place is a wall, and Daral Yashenti had just walked himself into being the wall.

We worked Fembris a while longer, and after we'd talked to the cat — long story, the cat had things to say — Fembris coughed up something else, the way he kept coughing things up once he got going.

The locket was missing. Off Janussi's neck. And that knocked something loose in his memory.

"But Fembris, sets… By golly!" he said, to himself, the way nervous people do. "Well, you know, the one thing that Janussi… Janussi held one of the two keys of the, of the Hightower Library."

Daz's head came up. You can always tell when Daz has fit two stones together — he goes still first, then he talks. "So could that be the key to the library that was around there? Did somebody… that might explain why that stuff got deleted off the library, or off that book that no one should have touched. Someone snuck in."

"Yes, well, you never know, but…" Fembris said. "so what you know is that Janussi held one of the two keys." And then, because he was the kind of man who couldn't stop being useful once he'd started: "that the other key is heralded by the gate warden, Kalan."

Two keys. One around a dead man's neck, gone. One with a gate warden. That's the terrain now — not bridges and pits, just keys and grudges, but it works the same. You find out who can stand where, and then you find out who shouldn't have been standing there.

Grygum, meanwhile, was already three steps down the practical road, which is where he lives. "One thing we need is somebody who knows what should be here, so they can tell us if anything's missing."

Right. Because Daz could guess at the locket and I could guess at Daral, but a guess isn't a wall. You need someone who knows the room cold.

"Let me go over that one more time," Grygum said, slow, lining it up the way he does. "go find, say, the housekeeper, and ask her, what is missing or out of place."

That's the move. I'm not interested in the politics of who hated whom and how long — that's noise, that's the part of this place I can't deal with. But *what's missing from the room* — that I can stand on. That's ground.

Hollypocket. The housekeeper. We'd go find her, and she'd tell us what shouldn't be there and what should and wasn't.

"Alright," and we were walking out, Fembris trailing, asking if we were all done.

Not yet. Not even close.

## Grygum — The Housekeeper's Testimony

Hollypocket's apartment sat below the Keeper's tower — modest, but with room to breathe, and warm in a way the chamber above us had not been. An elderly gnome charged with looking after the dead man's every need, from his morning tea and pastry down to emptying his chamber pot. She offered us seats. She offered us herbal tea. I took notes.

"They've been arguing like alley cats day and night, but I never thought one of them could murder him! He was such a nice man! A little stiff, maybe, but such a nice man! Murdered by his own colleagues! May the gods watch over his soul!"

"Wait, by his colleagues?" Zalthir said.

By his colleagues. That was the thing about a place full of scholars — they would shed blood over a marginal note and call it discourse. Janussi had spent his last morning in a series of one-on-one meetings with the Great Readers, clearing business before Deadwinter Day. At least half of them, Hollypocket announced, had ended in explosive shouting matches. Then she leaned in. "I do hate gossip, but if it's necessary for the investigation…"

"I mustn't," Zalthir said gravely, "but for the investigation."

"Oh, well, yes, of course, for the investigation!"

This is the thing I have learned about grieving witnesses. They do not want to be interrogated. They want permission. Give a frightened person a reason to tell you everything, and they will hand you the whole catalogue of the dead — every slammed door, every overheard word. So we let her.

Kalan Strongbranch first. "I was bringing the keeper his morning tea when I passed Kalan Strongbranch on the steps. He had a face like thunder, nearly knocked the tray out of my hands." A face like thunder. I wrote that down.

Then Teles Ahvoste, around the ninth bell. The meeting soon descended into a shouting match, and though she swore she wasn't listening — "especially as I wasn't listening" — she had heard one word clearly. "I did hear Teles shout, *hypocrite*, at poor Janussi before he stormed out."

Then Daral Yashenti, around the eleventh bell, nursing a hangover. Nothing new, she added — *he drinks way too much.* She happened to be dusting near the door and chanced to overhear some very unscholarly language. "You're not fit to be the candle keep's keeper, Daral shouted at poor Janussi," and then something about being *nothing but a glorified book peddler, whatever that means.*

But the bickering was nothing, she said, compared to what came next. "That devil woman! She gives me the heebie-jeebies!" Sylvira Savikas. Raging, screaming that Janussi was a traitor, the two of them sounding for a moment as though they had come to blows, before she stormed out and nearly took the door off its hinges.

A devil woman. I filed it, then I had to refile it — Sylvira is the Curator of the Infernal Fortress. A woman who studies devils. Not an epithet. A job function.

"Good clarification," Zalthir said.

Then the evening. Daral came back at half past ten, drunk as a skunk, pounding on the door until Janussi let him in, another loud argument, then the slam and the great reader running back down the stairs. "I'd never seen him run away so fast."

"Well, this definitely sounds like somebody we need to talk to," I said. A scholar who drinks, picks two fights with the victim in one day, and flees the second time — even Bahamut, who has rarely volunteered an opinion on anything, would not call that nothing.

She turned to offer the tea around again. Thorin declined — "No thank you, I'm good." Zalthir accepted with rather more enthusiasm than the situation strictly required. "Yes, I love… Absolutely!"

Then she told us about the midnight thud. She'd just settled in for her beauty sleep when the bell tower rang, and she thought she heard a thud upstairs. She'd have gone to look, but Janussi didn't like to be disturbed after eleven. She never slept before eleven, you see, in case the Master needed something — that was how she knew he had been alive until the thud.

"Nothing more," Thorin said.

It landed wrong. The word *thud* and the word *nothing* sitting next to each other reminded her, all at once, what the thud had been. She started to cry. These relationships are so complicated — a whole life reduced to one sound through a ceiling. The three of us closer to her did what we could to settle her; Daz, I noticed, was paying the weeping woman no attention whatsoever, off somewhere in his own ledger. I do what I can. We got her steady.

And then, steadied, she gave us the thing I will be thinking about for a while.

"Now that you mention it, maybe I was dreaming, but at some point in the night, I woke up, and I swear I could see that devil woman's — Sylvira Savikas's — face glowing red in the candlelight. She looked at me for an instant, and then suddenly she was gone. Must have been around 2 or 3 in the morning, but I… I don't know."

"You saw her going up or down?" Zalthir asked.

"She was gone, she was going down." Then, quieter: "But what was really odd was not that Sylvira was there in the middle of the night — I've seen her in the middle of the night before — but that she was carrying a lantern. She's a tiefling, and she can see in the dark without any light at all."

A tiefling carrying a light she had no use for. The candlelight that lit her face had not come from a candle at all. I wrote it down slowly. That one mattered.

"Hmm," said Daz, suddenly listening after all.

"That explains the candle," said Zalthir.

She added one more thing, almost to herself. "I was never sure but what the keeper saw in her, but he certainly incurred her wrath that morning." And then the locket — Janussi carried it always, all the time, never took it off. He carried it all. I underlined that one twice.

## Daz — Interrogating the Tower Cat

The interview began the way most interviews do — with someone explaining why this couldn't possibly work, and me proving otherwise.

Zalthir wanted a way to talk to the cat. Sound enough. The animal had been in the chamber; the dead don't volunteer alibis, but a five-year-old housecat with opinions might. Grygum admitted he couldn't afford a potion — clerics of Bahamut, it turns out, run on a tighter margin than their theatrics suggest. So I put the obvious solution where it belonged: in my own column.

"I think I can talk to the cat," I said.

"Oh, can you?" Zalthir, surprised. He is rarely surprised. I keep a small, private tally of the times.

"Talking to beasts, yeah. It's part of what the stone speaker crystal grants me." A useful thing, the Stonespeaker. I had been carrying it as a curiosity. Now it was a witness chair.

"Well, that saves me money on potions," said Grygum, relieved — someone else had just taken an expense off his account. At least we have that.

The cat introduced herself, after a fashion. Queenie — five years old, sleet-grey, and entirely uninterested in being disturbed. "I did not order any more ticklers!" she announced, stretching. "Any good ticklers are the only good… and good ticklers are… tickles are only the good reason for disturbing my beauty sleep."

A negotiation, then. I have run a few of those. Establish what the other party wants and provide exactly enough of it to keep the conversation yours. "I'll give Queenie a little tickle," I said, and tickled. Information is currency. So, evidently, is scratching behind the ears.

She warmed. They always do, when you find the lever.

What she gave us, once warmed, was worth more than the price. She had settled into Janussi's armchair for a nap, she said, "only to be rudely awakened some thirty minutes later by our Sylvira Savikas, this is in the morning, blasting the armchamber with a lance of unerring force light." Thorin dug in — *what was going on with those bolts of force, do we know why* — and the answer arrived dressed as gossip: Sylvira the tiefling, and Janussi, an on-and-off-again relationship. A lover's quarrel, then, opened with a flung working of force. I have known worse opening arguments. I have made worse opening arguments.

She woke from her afternoon nap around eight, after supper, and watched the evening's guests arrive in order — first Fheminor, *the short-legged one*, and later Daral, *the stinky breath one*. There is a precision to how cats categorize people that I find I respect. No titles. No politics. Just the relevant data: gait, smell.

Then Daral's part. Too much shouting, she said, so she retreated to the corridor — "when I later saw him run downstairs with a hypnotic block."

A hypnotic block.

Something in the phrasing snagged. I have spent my life learning to notice when a description is doing work the describer doesn't understand. The detail sat wrong, and the wrongness was the point — *the first rule is to read the witness who doesn't know what they're a witness to.* I pressed.
