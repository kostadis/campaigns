# Chapter 45 The Wreckage of a Lie


## Daz — The Deception Completed
The spider was already back on my wrist before Asha had finished watching the light die. That part had gone well — the Dancing Lights threading down like a column of divine attention, absorbed into the familiar, absorbed into me, blinking out. Clean. Considered. I'd been genuinely pleased with myself.


Then she spoke.


"You're a wizard. A wizard… a wizard who prays to Lolth?"


Not wonder. Appraisal. The flat, practiced appraisal of someone who has lived her entire life in a society that treats information as currency and lying as a primary language, and who has therefore developed, out of sheer professional necessity, an excellent ear for both. I filed the tone away. She wasn't convinced. She wasn't unconvinced. She was running the numbers.


So I leaned in.


"I am… you are not speaking to the wizard of this body. I… he… this body has communed with Lolth. And I… I am speaking through this body."


I shifted registers. I let the sentence stumble, then recover. I was trying to sell her something a drow cleric would recognize — the specific texture of divine intrusion, the grammar of a body that is briefly not its own. I've read enough. I've observed enough. Being a drow, you pick some stuff up.


It didn't work.


"Aha," she said. "You are trying to test me, to see if I was one of those fake followers of Lolth, who are willing to believe every charlatan magic user that is somehow being possessed by the power of Lolth."


She hadn't disbelieved me because the illusion was poor. She'd disbelieved me because she had seen this exact con before — probably multiple times, probably from people more practiced than I am, probably in considerably higher-stakes circumstances. I felt something I will not call embarrassment. I will call it a recalibration event.


The divine possession angle was closed. I pivoted.


"There are many imitations," I said, "but as you saw, I came to you in my smaller form, and led you here. You should know the truth."


I was threading a needle — the familiar's earlier appearance as the spider, reframed. Not fraud, but mystery. Not possession, but presence. If she couldn't accept what I was claiming, perhaps she could accept that something strange was happening, and that strange things sometimes arrive in wizard-shaped containers.


She was quiet for a moment. And then she began building her own frame.


"Oh, dear goodness, have you two been infected by the spores, possessed by the crazed magic of Zuggtmoy? Convinced of the bride's power? Is this another trick of Elvara's?"


I want to be precise about what happened in that moment: she had discarded my lie and replaced it with a different explanation that she found more coherent. The divine claim was dead. But Asha Vandree was not walking away. She was doing what intelligent people do when confronted with something that doesn't fit — she was finding a frame that let her keep the useful parts and discard the implausible ones.


A deluded magic user was more believable to her than a divinely-touched wizard. Fine.


I could work with deluded.


"Perhaps my prayers have been answered through this… confused vessel," she said — almost to herself, the way people do when they're granting themselves permission to proceed.


She'd seen through me and chosen to keep me anyway. In drow terms, that is more or less a letter of reference.


"I really do believe that I have," I said. Leaning into it now. "Perhaps there are some spores within me, but I… it feels… I feel it. And by the way, I've convinced others to join me."


I brought the party forward. Madness with followers is not simply pathetic — it is tactically interesting. It suggests either genuine power or a sufficiently compelling delusion, and from Asha's position, the practical difference between those two things was probably small.


It was at this point that Glabbagool attempted to pass for a rock.


The eyes gave him away. They drifted left. They drifted right. They did this with the unhurried curiosity of a creature that has never needed to pretend to be geological before and doesn't fully understand why it's being asked to start now. I looked at him. He looked at me. Or at least, several of his eyes moved in my general direction.


"Glabbagool," I said, with what I hope was a tone of patient authority rather than suppressed desperation, "I'm taking care of this. It's fine."


No deception manual I have ever studied contains a chapter on managing an ooze during active negotiations. I am adding one mentally, in real time.


Asha, to her credit, did not run. She assessed. She accepted Glabbagool as a bound familiar — the grey-ooze creature, strange and drifting and thoroughly uncategorizable, fitting quite neatly into the profile of a deluded mage who nonetheless had genuine and unusual power. It may have helped more than the Dancing Lights. I will not dwell on this.


Then she began to talk about Ilvara, and the information was worth everything the con had cost.


The hatred in it was real. Not political calculation. Not tactical positioning. Something that had been building for a long time in close quarters with a corrupted superior, and was now coming out in full.


"Ilvara, who was once a high priestess of Lolth, has become seduced by this abomination, this idea that Lolth wishes to be married. There is something foul and stinking about her, and then all of those allies she seems to covet have turned into fungal creatures that are mindless. We need to kill her. We need to butcher her! We need to send her to an unearthly grave! I fear that her disease will spread."


I listened. Only she and Jorlan remained uninfected — Asha through devotion, Jorlan through hatred, which is its own kind of immunological theory. Twenty feet of maintained distance. Separate food. The mushroom artifact from Neverlight Grove was the mechanism of corruption. The Big Heart Fungus was the primary weapon, would need sustained damage, would not die quickly or easily.


Then, because I am who I am:


"How do you propose to kill her if you're not gonna to get within 20 feet of her?"


A fair question. Asha had a ready answer.


"I was going to kill her. That is what you… where you come in."


The plan, such as it was, had always required someone else to do the lethal part. She had survived long enough to find useful lunatics willing to close that distance for her. I could not fault the logic.


"If I and the crew that is with me were able to take care of this small problem for you," I said, "what would be in it for us?"


"The gratitude of Lolth."


"I already feel the gratitude of Lolth. I have that."


I delivered it without a beat's pause. The dryness of it, I think, may have done more for the deluded-believer angle than anything else I'd attempted — it had the specific texture of a man who genuinely believes his spiritual compensation is already sufficient and is asking a practical follow-up question. Which, I realized afterward, was the most accurate version of the character I had played all evening.


She pushed back.


"That is not the kind of conviction a follower of Lolth shows."


"It's the kind of conviction this follower of Lolth shows."


I didn't flinch. Didn't qualify. Didn't offer an explanation. Steadiness, in my experience, does more work than eloquence — eloquence suggests you've been thinking about what to say, and steadiness suggests you've never needed to. The negotiation stayed alive.


She offered Ilvara's magical items as additional payment, with the caveat that they might be contaminated. I noted that caveat. I filed it.


The intelligence was solid. The alliance was provisional but real. Asha Vandree had seen through my lie, built her own explanation out of the wreckage, found it workable, and decided that a spore-maddened wizard with followers and a gelatinous familiar was, on balance, the best available instrument for killing someone she needed dead.


She wasn't wrong.


## Thorin — Tactical Preparations
The mushroom thing — I want to be clear about the mushroom thing — the fates looked at Grygum and looked at me specifically. Both of us. The artifact sitting there like a dare, and the warning coming down with actual gravity: *do not touch it*. Grygum and I had both been where that road goes. I didn't need the long version. Neither did he. We both just said nothing, which for us was the equivalent of a blood oath.


So I watched the others work through the plan instead.


The crossbow came first, because I'm not going into something like this with nothing that reaches past ten feet. Grygum had a light crossbow. I asked him for it. Simple transaction, no drama — he handed it over and I checked the mechanism the way you check anything borrowed before a fight: pull the string, sight down it, make sure the person who owned it before you wasn't sloppy. He wasn't. That dwarf is okay.


Then the party spread out the options on the table, or whatever the Underdark equivalent of a table is when you're crouched in the dark with a drow informant and a borrowed crossbow. Glyph of Warding. Dust of Suleiman. Phantasmal Killer. Fireball. Grygum had one 4th-level slot. Daz had one 4th-level slot. Those are not renewable resources in the next twenty minutes, so every piece of this had to land somewhere it mattered. I listened. I watched who spoke with certainty and who spoke with hedging. Zalthir said he could teleport directly to Ilvara — or grab her and move her somewhere unpleasant. I stored that. Teleportation as a weapon. Thorin files that away in the part of his brain labeled *useful people.*


Asha laid out her terms, and I heard them clearly. She would not come in at the start. Once we initiated, she would hold the perimeter — anything coming from outside, she handles it. Which is fine. It's useful. But I know what it is. She's drow. She's going to fight this fight on her own schedule, at the angle that suits her. I don't hold that against her. I'd be lying if I said it didn't register.


Then Grygum took the bridge.


Jorlan's side. He moved to the crossing and started inscribing the Glyph of Warding into the stone itself, and I watched the whole process with the specific attention of someone who needs to *not step in that spot* when things start moving fast. The trigger: any hostile creature crossing. Invisible once finished. Patient in the way only traps can be patient, because they don't get tired. It went down into the rock and disappeared, and the bridge looked like a bridge again.


I noted the position. I noted it carefully.


And I looked at Jorlan across that bridge, and I thought about weight and leverage and who in this room can be trusted to act predictably. Jorlan is not complicated. Jorlan is a threat with a location, which is the best kind of threat because it tells you exactly where to aim.


I have a borrowed crossbow. I have a position. He doesn't know we know he's there.


*The goal the whole time*, I thought. *Eyeing the prize.*


## Zalthir — Zalthir Opens the Fight
The distance across the cavern was a problem. Then it wasn't.


One moment I was reading the geometry of the space — the elevated platform, the sightlines, the gap between us and her — and the next I was already there, adjacent, inside the radius where a priestess stops being an authority and becomes a target. She hadn't called that. She hadn't dictated anything. I stepped through the dark and arrived before she'd finished deciding what to do, and that moment of her not-deciding was exactly what I needed.


I drove the weapon in. Sixteen. The grip locked.


Grabbing a drow priestess of Ilvara's caliber felt like grabbing a live coal — you know going in that it's going to cost you, you do it anyway because the alternative is worse. But the grapple held. She was in my hands, her movement mine to constrain, and suddenly the battlefield looked different. Not good. But different in a way I could work with.


The second strike came with her pinned and her options narrowed. Advantage. Fifteen more. Thirty-one total. She was hurting and she knew it and I could feel that knowledge in the tension of the hold — the way resistance shifts when something goes from confident to afraid.


Then I looked at the cavern floor. At the Dust of Suleiman area. At the rough arithmetic of: priestess plus that zone equals someone else's problem.


It was a brutal plan. It was also economical. You let the battlefield do the killing when you can. I'd spent enough — I wanted to spend her.


I started moving her toward it. Fly, carry, drag if I had to.


She screamed.


Not at me. The GM's exact words: *at herself.* And I caught that — I filed it, the way you file things that tell you something true. She was furious at herself. Which meant I'd actually hurt her, actually threatened something she thought couldn't be threatened, and the scream was the sound of that particular illusion breaking.


Then she was gone.


Between one heartbeat and the next, she came apart into the fungal matter beneath her feet — flowed through it like water pouring into a crack, like smoke running back into the dark — and reappeared somewhere else on the cavern floor. The grapple didn't break. It simply stopped having anything to hold. My hands closed on nothing. She was out of reach and already putting distance between us, and the scream still echoed off the cave walls like she'd left it there on purpose.


I hung in the air.


Out of actions. Out of bonus actions. Out of everything I'd had when I arrived on that platform thirty-one damage and one tactical calculation ago.


Below me, the battlefield was still happening. People were still fighting, or dying, or doing things that required me to have something left to give, which I didn't. I had altitude and a view and the specific exhaustion of a plan that almost worked.


*She's angry at herself,* I noted, and filed it away, and watched.


## Grygum — The Heart Fungus Retaliates
The cloud didn't drift. That was the first thing I noticed. Normal smoke drifts. Normal gas disperses. This hung in the cavern air like it had decided to stay — twenty feet of spore-thick poison, sitting in the middle of the battlefield as if it belonged there, as if it had always belonged there, as if *we* were the interlopers in its home.


Which, to be fair, we were.


I'd been watching the Big Heart Fungus since we entered this chamber. That's not a metaphor. I took notes. It's what I do. And what I had written in my head about that particular organism was: *not scenery*. The moment it detonated that cloud across the cavern floor, every instinct I had confirmed the notation.


Zalthir took the full brunt of it. I watched the dragonborn's body register the toxin — not a dramatic collapse, nothing so clean as that — just a subtle wrongness in how he moved, a falter in the stride of someone who had been, until thirty seconds ago, pressing a meaningful advantage over Ilvara. The poisoned condition settling over a person looks different depending on who they are. On Zalthir it looked like frustration wearing a physical costume.


He disengaged and went straight up. Ten feet. Clear of the worst of the cloud, hovering in that particular way of someone who has made the tactically correct decision and is not happy about it.


"I'm out of actions and bonus actions," he said, from somewhere above the spore line, "so I guess I'll just kind of look on and be confused."


The dry resignation in his voice said everything. I didn't laugh, but I noted it. The battlefield had fragmented faster than any of us had planned for, allies pulled in three different directions by pressure we hadn't fully mapped before we walked in. That's how these things go. You plan for the drow commander. You don't always plan for the floor itself to become an enemy.


And then Ilvara called them up.


Four shapes. Rising from the cavern floor with the patient, purposeless movement of things that are no longer choosing to move — they are simply directed. Former Velkynvelve guards. I recognized the posture of soldiers whose bodies had been repurposed, the fungal colonization of Zuggtmoy's influence made visible through Ilvara's particular corruption. These had been drow with names and duties and probably opinions about their assignments. Now they were just pointed at us.


Zalthir said, from his aerial vantage point, "Those poor guards. They've been through quite a lot."


Then he reflected on Valen. Not out loud, really — it was more in the quality of the silence that followed. I felt the thought land in the room anyway. Valen, who had been one of them. Who could have been one of them, differently categorized. These bodies on the cavern floor had once been exactly what Valen was, and somewhere between then and now, they had lost the thread entirely.


Soldiers. Now weapons. The difference between the two is sometimes just time and circumstance, and I filed that thought away under *things not to dwell on mid-combat*.


Jorlan was moving, and I saw the angle.


The Glyph of Warding on the bridge was still live. We'd placed it with exactly this kind of chaos in mind, and Jorlan was close enough, distracted enough, *angry* enough that he might not be tracking the floor carefully. I calculated this in approximately one second, which is about as long as tactical decisions get in a room that is actively trying to kill you.


"Come get it, idiot," I called across the chaos.


He ran toward the trap.


The glyph detonated. One spore servant ceased to be a problem. The others staggered in a way that suggested they had not enjoyed the experience. Jorlan himself was not having his best moment.


"Kaboom," I said, because it seemed warranted. Then: "I didn't expect it to work."


That last part was honest. The tactic had been a gamble — you call a man an idiot and invite him toward an explosive ward and you are making a bet on ego overriding tactical awareness, which is sometimes a safe bet and sometimes gets you killed. This time it paid. I took note of the conditions that made it pay, in case they became relevant again.


With the servants disrupted and Jorlan compromised, I turned my attention back to the thing that had started all of this. The Big Heart Fungus. Still standing. Still anchoring the far end of the battlefield. Still the reason Zalthir was hovering above the fight instead of in it.


I committed the Dust of Suleiman and cast it directly onto the fungus. This was not a small resource. I made the decision anyway, because the creature had already demonstrated what it was willing to do to us and I had no interest in finding out what it would do next if left unaddressed.


Then Daz's shaped Fireball hit — Ilvara and the Zuggtmoy icon both, simultaneously — and the two effects overlapped in a way that exceeded what either spell would have done alone. The cavern went briefly and magnificently wrong in all the right ways.


"That worked better," I said, surveying the damage — servants staggered, Jorlan reeling, Ilvara absorbing punishment she hadn't budgeted for, fire and spore and disruption filling a space that had, ten minutes ago, been merely dangerous — "than I had any right to expect."


The fungus was still standing. The fight was far from over. But the battlefield had shifted in our favor, and I noted the specific combination of factors that had produced that outcome: bait the ego, trust the glyph, commit the resource, let Daz do what Daz does.


Bahamut may or may not have been watching. I took notes either way.


## Thorin — The Glyph Detonates
The Glyph was Grygum's idea, or at least Grygum's mouth. The placement was all of us. We'd laid it on the bridge like a welcome mat, and then the whole job was just: don't stand on it yourself, and wait.


Grygum called out. "Come get it, idiot."


I will say this for Jorlan: he committed. The drow didn't hesitate, didn't slow — he came across that bridge like a man who had already decided the outcome. You have to respect that, a little, even when the outcome is about to be wrong for him. There's something almost giant-brained about that kind of certainty. Giants move like that. Big, direct, total confidence in the thing they've already decided. The problem is, giants are usually right about the terrain.


Jorlan wasn't right about the terrain.


The Glyph fired.


Force and light erupted outward in a sphere, the kind of detonation that doesn't care who's in the way. Twenty-one damage blooming through the cluster all at once — through the spore servants he'd brought across with him, through Jorlan himself. One servant came apart completely. Just gone. The others staggered; I watched one barely hold together, listing like a wall with one stone pulled from its base. And Jorlan took the full force. He hadn't cleared it in time, hadn't been fast enough, hadn't seen it coming the way you can't see it coming when you're already running at what you think is your prize.


Grygum said, "Kaboom!" And then, with something like genuine surprise underneath it: "I didn't expect it to work."


That's the thing about a plan that comes together. You stop expecting it, almost. You get tactical, you do the work, you lay the trap in exactly the right place — and then somewhere between placing it and watching it detonate, you forget to believe in yourself. Or maybe you just don't want to be disappointed. I understood it. I felt the same flicker of it, the half-second of *oh. Oh, that worked.*


It worked.


I didn't stand there appreciating it. I was already closing on Jorlan. The explosion was still ringing through the cavern when Dawnbringer came down — crit, the blade's light cutting into him hard and clean, 14 damage landing exactly where it needed to. He'd taken the full force of the Glyph and now this. The second swing went wide; he shifted at the last moment and I carried past him. Doesn't matter. I pressed the Sap after it, using the momentum wrong to do something right — shoving him off-balance, leaving him on the back foot, disadvantage waiting for him the next time he tried to hit back.


One of the servants wasn't finished with me. Legendary action, a swing that went so wide it almost circled back around to being impressive — natural 1, nothing — and then the follow-up connected for 5. Glancing. A reminder.


Fair enough. I noted it. We weren't done.


## Daz — Concentrated Fire
The Dust of Suleiman lands and the battlefield reorganizes itself in my head.


This is the part I'm good at. Not the talking, not the diplomacy, not whatever Thorin does when he walks directly into the emotional centre of a conflict and somehow makes it worse and better simultaneously. This part. Reading the board. Identifying the highest-value targets. Calculating acceptable losses against projected return.


The priorities are clear: Ilvara, and the icon. In that order, or really in no particular order — they're the same problem wearing different faces. Asha was explicit enough about what's riding on this that I haven't been able to fully file it away the way I prefer to file things. It keeps surfacing. The stakes are not abstract. The stakes are Asha, and everything she told me, and the fact that a Zuggtmoy-aligned symbol of office left standing is not a symbol — it's a seed. You don't leave seeds.


So. Fireball. Cast at fourth level. Eight dice.


I have used Fireball the way some people use a favourite coat — comfortable, reliable, something I reach for when precision matters less than coverage. But coverage is not what I need. I need placement. Ilvara is elevated, which is poor positioning on her part and convenient positioning on mine. The icon is beside her. Both targets, one sphere, and the geometry cooperates if I'm exact.


Zalthir and Thorin are inside the problem zone.


This is what Sculpt Spells is for. I carve them out. The fire will know the shape of them by the time it arrives and it will go around — not because fire cares, fire doesn't care about anything, but because I told it the shape of what it cannot touch. It is perhaps the most useful thing I know how to do. Precision is not the opposite of force. It is force, directed.


The sphere lands where I placed it.


Ilvara is quick. She half-saves, which is professionally irritating but not unexpected — she did not survive this long in Menzoberranzan's social ecosystem by being slow. She takes twelve. The icon takes twelve. The fire roars around Zalthir and Thorin and doesn't touch them.


Both targets absorb the blast.


I have found purpose. I am the feared mage of the Ember Vanguard.


The irony is almost invisible, even to me.


Grygum's voice cuts through the settling heat: *"That worked better than I had any right to expect."*


It's an honest assessment, and I appreciate honesty in tactical partners. Two spells, two priority targets hit, the fight still live but the math changed in our favour. Not a perfect outcome. The icon is still standing. Ilvara is still standing. But twelve points is twelve points and the board looks different than it did thirty seconds ago, and that is the only metric that has ever mattered.


I note that Zalthir is unsinged. I note that Thorin is unsinged.


I don't say anything about it. It was the plan. Plans working is not remarkable.


But I notice.
