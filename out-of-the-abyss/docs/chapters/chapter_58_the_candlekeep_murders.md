# Chapter 54 The Candlekeep Murders


## Daz — Reporting to Bookworm

Fembris Lancer practically vibrated. The adjutant had attached himself to us the way an acolyte attaches to anyone who might be doing something more interesting than copying manuscripts, and he was not going to let us forget the assignment. "I think it's important that we need to go," he said, "we need to go back to the great — the chapter house, and talk to a Bookwyrm, to give her a summary of what happens. After all, don't forget, that is exactly what she asked us to do, to provide a summary of the events."

A summary. Behind me, Grygum was running the word back and forth. Checking it against what we'd actually been told. "True? I thought we were asked to find out what — well, I guess summarizing the events is pretty much the same as finding out who did it from one."

"I thought, yeah, we had to find out who," I offered, which contributed nothing and was meant to.

"I would make a bad detective," Grygum decided, "because I don't have a perfect memory. All the fictional detectives have perfect memories."

"I don't know if they do," Thorin said. "I think real detectives just write everything down."

"Poirot never does."

"No, he doesn't. It's like, at the end of the episode, he's like, okay, everybody sit down, I'm gonna tell you what's what." There was a warmth in it I rarely heard from him. "My wife and I have watched the entire series, and we're down to the last episode, and we're saving it. I'm not sure for why, but —"

I let them have it. I was running a different question, the one Thorin eventually asked aloud as we walked. "Who are we working for, exactly?"

"Justice," said Grygum.

The first rule of any House: know who holds the debt before you decide whether to collect it. Thorin, in his blunt giant-raised way, got to the same place from the other direction. "I guess, who has the power to fire us and tell us to go away?"

Bookwyrm. Of course Bookwyrm. We were working for Bookwyrm, and Thorin — who hates the whole rotten apparatus of who-knows-what — put the obvious crack in it before I had to. "I'm just saying, like, if Bookwyrm ended up doing it, which I don't think she did, but… then." A beat. "This could get awkward."

It could. I sorted her into the column marked *not above suspicion* and kept walking.

The Council of the Great Readers had dispersed. We came into the chapter house to find her already in conference with someone new — a slender half-elf, perhaps fifty, bald scalp framed by black side whiskers chiseled down into a precise goatee. I would have liked to know what they were saying before the door opened. So would everyone. We tried; the trouble is that I had the right tools for reading a page — through a closed door, those tools were the wrong shape entirely. Whatever passed between them, it stayed between them.

The moon elf scribes moved to halt us at the threshold. Bookwyrm waved them off and ushered us in herself, all warmth, and introduced the man as Kei Tigersteel — Lorekeeper of Candlekeep, priest of the Temple of Oghma, "the library's spiritual leader." I noted the seating and the easy way she dismissed him a moment later: *yes, Kei, you can take the body to the Shrine of Oghma for purification.* The body went to be purified. Now she was alone with us, which I also noted.

"Have you concluded your investigation of the crime scene?" Very serious now.

We had. Grygum confirmed it plainly. Thorin pressed her to keep the room sealed, and she agreed three times over — *of course, of course, of course* — which is two more agreements than a thing requires.

Then she took us to a nearby room, Deneir's Sanctum, a little shrine and library given over to the art of writing, and offered it up as our office. Fembris would fetch any witness we named. Vooshadi Moonriver would stand the door — *we wouldn't want anyone the investigators don't want to come in, to come in.* And one more thing, lightly placed: we shouldn't move through Candlekeep without one of them. The library is very easy to get lost in.

I have an alternative reading of generosity, which is that it is rarely free.

"Do we think the room is, like, bugged," I asked the others once we had a moment, "or has, like, secret — like, the stuff we do in there is actually going to be private to us, or do we need —" I kept going, because the offer was too clean. "It's nice and all to offer up that we can use the office, and that they'll go fetch people, and all of those sorts of things, but it's also a little suspect. Like, maybe they're involved, and somehow…"

"It doesn't seem just friendly," Grygum said.

I asked him to say it again.

"It doesn't seem just friendly."

"It could be just friendly." I meant it as a hypothesis to be tested, not a comfort.

"I mean, it could be that they genuinely want to get to the bottom of this."

"Yeah," I said. "It could be." Both columns stayed open.

She wanted to know what we'd told her. So did I — it pays to know exactly how much of an account you've already handed over. "Generalities," I said. "That we went and talked to all the people that we could talk to. And we're forming an opinion on what may have happened, and who — like, we have a couple suspects that we would like to pursue, due to perceiving that they may have a motivation, or that other people have pointed them out. We're still —"

"Is there any — any suspects in particular?" The question came a half-step too fast.

"We refuse to comment on ongoing," Thorin said.

"Yeah," I added, "it's an ongoing investigation, and it's not, not proper for us to share that at this time."

"Very well," she said. And then she sat there, and she did the thing I'd been waiting for. "Was there nobody that you wished to start your investigation with?"

That is not an idle question. That is a question that wants a name back so it can measure where we're pointed. Thorin deflected — *not quite sure who we're gonna talk to next, but you shouldn't read into that* — and I gave her one harmless witness to chew on, Ms. Hollypocket, whom we'd already seen anyway. A name that costs nothing tells you what the asker does with a name.

What she did was want Sylvira Savikas. She never said it. She didn't have to; the want came off her like heat off the smelters. Someone had impersonated Sylvira — Grygum thought it made perfect sense, and tactically it did, the impersonated woman might know who would wear her face. It also meant the most powerful person in this building had a strong, unstated preference about where we aimed first.

I sorted it: *Bookwyrm steers toward Sylvira.* I had no idea yet whether that was the helpful instinct of someone who wants the thing solved, or a thumb on the scale of an investigation she'd rather not have tilted toward herself. I left that account open too.

Then the practical question — together or apart. Thorin liked us as a wall. "It's more imposing to have all, like, you know, four, five, seven, eight, ten of us show up in a room." Grygum wanted to start together and branch later. The reasoning was simple enough: split, and we cover the poison and Sylvira at once, but each of us walks into his own conversation alone, no second pair of hands to steady the work.

"Since we're separated, whatever each of us draws, that's what we're stuck with," Thorin said.

That was the trade. More ground, less margin. I could live with that.

## Thorin — Investigation Strategy and Suspect Review

I have spent enough time in the Underdark to know what a bugged room feels like, and Deneir's Sanctum felt like a gift, which is the same thing. Bookwyrm waved us into it like she was doing us a favor. "Well, you can use this room as your office to interview anyone you wish as part of your investigation, and you can rely on Fembris Lancer to catch… fetch any suspects and witnesses you wish to speak to."

Catch. She said catch and corrected herself to fetch. I kept that.

Daz had the same itch I did. "Do… do we think the room is, like, bugged, or has, like, secret…" He couldn't even finish it. "It's nice and all to offer up that, like, we can use the office, and that they'll go fetch people, and all of those sorts of things, but it's also a little suspect. Like, maybe they're involved, and somehow…"

"It doesn't seem just friendly," Grygum said. "I mean, it could be that they genuinely want to get to the bottom of this." That's Grygum. He leaves the door open for people to be decent even while he's bolting it.

This is the thing about murder among scholars. Back home — and I mean the mountain, the giants — if you wanted to know who broke a thing, you asked, and someone told you, because lying about it was beneath them. Down here, everybody's got a door they won't open. I hate it. I've always hated it.

So when Bookwyrm fished — "Oh, d-do… is there any… Any suspects in particular?" — I gave her the only honest thing I had, which was nothing.

"We refuse to comment on ongoing."

Daz cleaned it up the way he does. "Yeah, it's an ongoing investigation, and it's not, not proper for us to share that at this time."

She tried again. Asked if there was anybody we wished to start with. I shrugged at her. "We're gonna talk to anybody who might be involved, so, you know — not quite sure who we're gonna talk to next, but you shouldn't read into that."

What I wanted to read into was her. "I also want to understand where Bookwyrm was before nine in the morning," I said, once we were our own again. "Timeline of where she was. So, available to be a suspect." Everyone's available until they're not. That's not cynicism. That's just keeping the list honest.

Grygum was paging through our notes and frowning. "You know, there is somebody in our list of characters who seems to have the same priorities that we do. Maybe that would be someone to interview to sort of get tips on what to do next, or their suspicions." He squinted. "It isn't… for some reason, there's no name on that entry. It just says, Fheminor Scrivenbark." Our own notes had started lying to us. That felt about right for this place.

Then the constraint landed — that we'd only get to chase a handful of threads before the thing moved on without us. "Sounds like bullshit," I said, because it was. "I know, but, like, why do we get it, like, what's the hurry? Investigations take as long as they take." Apparently they don't. Apparently things keep happening while you're standing around thinking. Grygum put it plainest: "There'll be more murders."

So we wouldn't split, not yet. "I like us sticking together," I said. "I just think it's more imposing to have all, like, you know, five, seven, ten of us show up in a room." Grygum agreed we'd start together and peel off when two doors opened at once.

We walked the council back through. The whole pack of them, shouting over a body. A'lai Aivenmore wanting a vote and new leadership. The dowdy halfling, Fheminor Scrivenbark, asking whether Janussi had named a successor. Kazryn Nyantani — slender, gray, those wrinkles around the eyes — cutting through with first you must find the murderer. The bronze dragonborn, Alkrist, formidable and furious, the gate warden needs to be dismissed, this happened on his watch. Bookwyrm trying to settle the room. And Daral not even in it — he'd been at the Hearth, asking why isn't Sylvira Savikas here.

Which was the better question. Sylvira wasn't at the council. Sylvira — or somebody wearing her face — had been walking the halls in the dead of night with a lantern.

"Someone impersonating her," Zalthir said. "Yes. Yes."

Here's where I planted my boots. "I feel like physical evidence is more reliable than trying to parse who's lying and who's not, you know?" Down here everyone lies as a reflex. You can't out-talk a culture that talks for a living. But a smear of poison on a dead man's fingers doesn't have a motive. "But, you know, maybe that's from watching too much CSI, what do I know?"

We had a sample of the stuff — off his fingers, and his lip too, Zalthir made sure of that. Grygum volunteered. "I'm happy to do the library research. Poison section, please." And Daz raised the other thread, the one nobody wanted to touch: "There's a missing part of, like, a book that should not be missing… that could have happened a hundred years ago or something, but… seems suspicious, and maybe related."

Then we worked the list of people like a vein of ore, deciding where to dig.

Daz took Fheminor. "I'm looking at the ones that have the least amount of time, or probably have the least amount to talk about, because sometimes those are the ones that have the most insight." Reasonable. I started down the names. "So, Bookwyrm — do we really think Bookwyrm might have done it? Do we want to give Bookwyrm the third degree?" The warning came back sharp: if we accuse her, we'd better have facts, real ones. "Right," I said, "well, we want to know, we're pretty sure, before we accuse anybody." That's the rule. You don't say the word. Not until you're sure.

Daral, we crossed off — not by evidence, by gut. "It's too on the nose for it to be Daral. I don't think it's Daral." Zalthir nodded. "Reading the whole board, I don't think it's Daral." Sylvira, same logic, different reason. "Again, it's someone wore her face, is what we're talking about. I think the whole lantern aspect of it means that it probably wasn't them. She might know something, but it probably wasn't her."

Which left the key. "So, I think, Kalan Strongbranch, we gotta interview." Zalthir was already there. "I'm more curious if he still has his other tower key." Two keys to the high towers. Janussi held one. "Yeah, the key is definitely a thread to pull on."

Zalthir handed Daz his marching orders for Fheminor — find out when the man got beaten up, because beat-up-at-eight tells a different story than beat-up-when-he-died. Daz: "He wasn't dead yet, but was he beat up?" Zalthir: "He was beat up before he was dead." And whether the man noticed anything missing. And, Zalthir added flat as a tombstone, "if he poisoned the guy when he visited, perchance."

One thing kept gnawing me, though, so I backed us up. "Kalan said they'd put the question to the corpse, and that it didn't work. And that didn't work because of Y." The imposter — the false devil-lady — supposedly did it first and locked the corpse's mouth shut for days. Maybe. "Do we know that for sure? I mean, if Kalan's just saying, hey, I did it, but didn't do it? None of us can wring words out of a dead man, so it's not like we can verify, but — whether the corpse was ever truly made to answer is hearsay." I let it sit a beat. "It's also heresy, but…"

This is what I mean. The whole place runs on people telling you what was true so you don't have to find out. The dead man's heart was gone. Two people had stood over him — one who knew exactly where the locket-key hid, and one who didn't and tore through the safe instead. Grygum asked whether the stolen sapphires were worth killing for — expensive, not rare, was the answer — and tucked it into his library list alongside the poison. And the other tower key, the one that wasn't Janussi's, sat in Kalan Strongbranch's pocket.

"Right, but at the end of the day, I'm pretty sure that the person that killed him is the one that took the heart," I said. Everything else is people talking. The heart is a fact.

We split it four ways. Daz to Fheminor. Grygum to the library, poison and sapphires both. "I'll do Sylvira," I said, "and somebody else can do Teles and Kalan." Zalthir would carry his end — and Glabbagool, who is apparently coming along permanently now, convinced down to whatever he uses for bones that he's going to learn to fight from the dark.

Once we were apart, we'd get what we got. No leaning on each other, no second voice in the room to make the words land softer. Just each of us and whatever the other person decided to show.

I'd rather that, honestly. One conversation, one truth, no committee. It's the closest thing to clean I'd found in a long time.

## Zalthir — A Secret Meeting with Kalan

The key went to Grygum. I marked that before anything else — not into Daz's hand, not into Thorin's, not into the open air for whoever was closest. Kalan stepped out of a doorway we hadn't clocked, swept the corridor left, then right, and pressed a small brass key specifically into the cleric's palm. He chose the orc. I don't think it was chance.

"Whatever Bookwyrm is telling you, listen for what she isn't," he said, low. "The High Tower has two keys. Janussi wore one, I held the other. I have given it now to someone who is not me. If I and I do not see you tomorrow, do not look for me at my post."

"You're worried that you'll be killed for the key?" Grygum asked.

Kalan looked left. Looked right. "It is best that if I am killed, the key not be found."

Thorin pushed at what the key actually guarded. The High Tower, Kalan told us, was the entrance to all of the super magical artifacts that every evil entity in the entire world would love. Thorin wanted to know if it had been compromised — whether Kalan himself might have used the key, walked in, walked out.

You don't need the key for that, came the answer. The tower is watched. Always. People stand at it whether the key is in someone's pocket or not. Entry would be seen.

"So that seems to clear him," Thorin said. And the gatekeeper turned and walked away.

I went after him. The rest of them had their questions, but most of those would dissolve into noise. I had a few that wouldn't.

"You need both," I said, when Grygum circled back to why a second key mattered. "Power with two." Thorin called it a war-game swapper, two officers turning keys to launch the missile. The metaphor was loud and it was also correct.

"Before you go," I said. "Did you find anything? Any idea who's behind this?"

He staged it — reminded us, twice, that he had been wanted off the case. Then he turned it on me. Our motives. Why we were here. Why he should trust us at all.

"Purely accidental," I told him. "We have no particular involvement here. Someone put us up to this. We are happy to not get involved." True enough. Filed under things that are also true.

He decided the nature of fate had brought disinterested outsiders to exactly this place, and that this meant something. I offered the simple version: we had the job, he could simply come with us. No. Bookwyrm had made it official that he was to be nowhere near it. "Officially," I said. "See, there you go." Grygum asked whether we could rely on him unofficially, and the ego did the rest of the work for us.

He had picked up a few things. He liked to juxtapose mundane observation with lofty rumination on order, chaos, fate, and futility — and in that rumination he had noticed the poison in the left hand, the saliva. "The black stuff, yes, we saw that." He noticed the heart hacked out — after death, to spoil resurrection. "Yeah, we got that." He noticed the murderer was a magic user who could circumvent arcane and mundane locks. Magic that could talk any lock open, arcane or iron, the very highest order of that art.

That one I hadn't built. "How do you know that?"

Then he tangled himself. He assumed the murderer was the one who opened the safe. I stopped him. The key was in the locket. The locket was around the keeper's neck. The locket was gone. So why would the killer go through the safe at all? Two hands had searched that apartment — one that knew exactly where to look, one that didn't.

Thorin asked the right small question: how did we know the key had ever been in the locket? Kalan pulled out his own locket to demonstrate the method of carry.

"Which is now empty," I said, "and therefore doesn't prove anything." So we asked him to open it and show us. He did. Because the thing he was holding up as proof could just as easily be Janussi's key, handed off by a man building a clean story. The locket was missing. The key was in the missing locket. None of it forced his conclusion.

"So why do you think the murderer is the same person who rifled the safe? There were, like, four different people in and out of that apartment."

He paused. He looked at me. "I don't know. What I thought — but now that you bring it up, perhaps I was a little bit too hasty. Perhaps it was two different people."

I gave him the *okay* and let it sit.

What he suspected, he said, was that one of the Great Readers was the murderer, and that either Bookwyrm was protecting them or someone had convinced her to fire him to protect themselves. Then the cold part: "I believe you're only being employed so you're likely to bungle the case, or else be silent if you do happen upon the right scent. You don't matter. You're outsiders."

Thorin, to his credit, didn't argue the insult. "I don't doubt that maybe they hired us because they thought we were gonna screw it up."

I told him the one thing he didn't have. "Just to point out the obvious — we've already learned more than you knew and corrected one of your assumptions. Would it not be in our interest to collaborate?"

He thought. Then he proposed it in the most extraordinary terms — offline chat, private work streams, a meeting to examine everything one by one, the four of us and him, five streams, merge it all in a few hours.

"I hope you actually used the word sync up," I said. "That makes me happy."

Before he went, I closed the gaps that mattered. He could question the dead. Did he have anything that pried truth loose, or pulled the hidden into view, that whole family of workings? Yes — if such a thing should become necessary. So if we suspected someone was lying, he could sort it out? "Of course I could." Wonderful. Something I'd want when the room was full of people I didn't know.

He gave us his timeline — the eight o'clock meeting where he'd pressed Janussi for more watchers and been refused, the quarter-past-ten visit to Sylvira Savikas over some cosmological question, the return to the Chamber of Lost Lore for private research. Then the morning of the murder: the inspection just after eight, the cordon, the posted watcher, the state of emergency, the doubled guard at the High Tower Library. The account of a man who wanted it on record where he had been.

Then I gave him the thing he didn't have. "Is he aware that someone was pretending to be Sylvira Savikas the night of the murder?"

He went still. "No. That is new information." A pause. "You are very good at this."

"Well, we're trying."

Whoever set this up, he said — naming him to the wrong people, perhaps — would be very disappointed in him. As close to a compliment as the man had in him.

"That's the hope, I guess."

"Given that you're at risk of being killed over a key you don't have anymore, watch your back," I told him. "Keep in mind there are people who can change their form. Shapeshift. Whatever you want to call it."

He looked at me like I'd said something self-evident.

"I'm just saying," I said, and let him go to his research.

## Grygum — Research in the Flora Theca

The librarian who took me down was named Fembris, and he walked like someone who believed noise had consequences. I asked if there was a poison section. There wasn't, exactly. What there was, Fembris told me, was the Flora Theca, a subsection of the Pavilion Naturalis, inside the Great Library, dedicated to plants, herbs, and other flora. Close enough. Poison is just botany with intent.

"Yes," I said. "And do I… do I have access to, whatever sort of laboratory I might need?"

"If you need it later, yes, of course."

Good. I noted that for later and went to work.

It is a strange thing, after months underground, to research in a room with shelves taller than the ceilings I'd learned to stoop beneath. No Faerzress humming behind my eyes. No tunnel that might put an enemy in front of me by the time I turned a page. Just stacks, and quiet, and the work. I started pulling tomes.

And then I found it faster than I had any right to. The right book practically leaned into my hand — a volume titled *Fey-mous Flowers & Fungi*, by one Archibald Roots, Jr., son of the legendary gnome botanist Archibald Roots Sr. I almost laughed. I had been bracing for a week of cross-referencing and instead the answer was sitting there waiting. Stroud couldn't play chess either, and he still rolled the winning move.

I opened it.

The first chapters were the son writing about the father — the explorations, the collections, the long gnomish life spent cataloging things that grow where no one wants to go. His life story, mostly. But buried in it was the thing I came for. *Midnight tears.* A highly toxic pale ochre dust, made from the pollen of a plant usually found only in the Feywild. The book noted, in the careful tone of a man whose father saved lives, that when midnight tears are mixed with the sap of a certain Feywild tree, they lose their toxicity entirely and become a powerful pain-relief solution that all good physicians should stock. Otherwise: lethal. Food or liquid, either delivery worked.

"So," I said, "that means that it's, it's unusual, and only a few would have access to it."

"Yes. Right."

I filed that. A poison from another plane narrows your suspect list considerably.

Then came the part that made the whole thing click. The creature that ingests this poison suffers no effect until the stroke of midnight.

"That is a good match for, for the… what actually happened," I said. "He died at roughly midnight."

You consume it, you're fine — apparently fine — and then you're dead at midnight.

Thorin, naturally, wanted to litigate the metaphysics. "How do they account for daylight savings time? Did you ever… an astronomical midnight? I mean…"

"Did you ever see," I said, "the Schwarzenegger movie End of Days that came out in 1999? Someone tells Schwarzenegger that the world is going to end at midnight on December 31st, blah blah blah, and his response is, *Eastern Standard Time?*"

Nobody had an answer for the time zones.

"It's magic," I said. That covers most of it.

The harder question was how. Food or liquid — which meant Janussi could have taken it any hour of that day, which meant whatever we found in the room wasn't necessarily the source. "So he could have been given this at any point during that day. So whatever we found there isn't necessarily—"

But there was the detail. Some liquid had gotten onto his finger, and the finger had gone black. Thorin asked the right thing — was the black the poison going dark as it took hold? No. The dust stays pale ochre. What darkens it is saliva. The ochre reacts on contact with spit, and a man turning pages licks his fingers to do it.

"Okay," Thorin said, "so somebody doped up the book."

"Yeah, the assumption is pretty…" I didn't finish it. His fingers were black, his lips were black, and he died at midnight. The book was the murder weapon, and he'd read himself dead. *The purpose of a maxim is not to tell you what to do — it is to tell you what you are doing.* Bahamut may or may not have been watching me work it out. I took notes either way.

I had one thing left I wanted out of the library. "So there was one more thing I was going to try to find out. I wanted to find out what spells, Sapphire is specific to. And I don't know whether that's 100 spells or 3. You know, or one."

That, it turned out, wasn't a botany question — it was the kind of thing any wizard knows on sight. It was an old summoning working — the kind that calls a single hidden thing back into your hand. Zalthir had been expecting a working that freezes a thing in stopped time, and seemed almost disappointed.

"Oh, it's a summoning spell," I said, reading it over. "Oh, it needs a very expensive sapphire."

I heard Daz say it in my head  - "Oh, wait a second, this is like a way to teleport it into my hand later kind of thing."

"This would be great for the High Tower Keys," I think.

I wanted that to be clean, and it wasn't. "But that wouldn't work, because they seem to be always carried on a lanyard, and it doesn't work if— although you'd find out who was holding it." Whether a thing worn counts as a thing carried. "I would think so. I'd think that you're carrying anything that you're wearing."

It doesn't, as it turned out. Which reframed the whole use.

"Oh, I see," I said, "so you can put it someplace, you can throw it into a river or whatever. Nobody will find it, and then when you need it back, boom."

It's in your hands. That's the trick. You don't hide the key on a body where someone can search it. You hide it nowhere at all, and you summon it when the door is in front of you.

I closed Archibald's book and sat with that for a moment. A poison from one plane and a spell from no plane in particular, and somewhere between them, a murder. I had pages of it now. It's what I do.

## Daz — Interrogating Daral Yashenti at the Hearth

Fembris went off with the particular efficiency of someone who'd been waiting for a task to belong to, and came back with two words. Daral was not coming to us. We were to go to the Hearth. I noted the inversion before we'd even left the room — the suspect setting the venue, picking the ground, making the famous detectives walk to him. In Menzoberranzan that would have been a move. Up here it was just a drunk who wanted to keep drinking where the drinks were.

We found him exactly that. Boisterous, flushed, conspicuously delighted with his own existence.

"Oh, look, it's Faerûn's most famous detectives! Bookwyrm's brownnosers, I'm going to call you, because you like to—"

"That's not our," Grygum said, "that's not our chosen name."

Daral was already laughing at his own joke. "Light it up, guys, we seem to have gotten off on the wrong foot. Barman, a brown of spice brandies for our beloved brownnosers. My treat."

"Didn't take any effort to read this guy as an ass," I said.

It was not a complicated read. But the more interesting thing was the *mood*. A man who had punched his superior in the face that morning should not be buying rounds. I ran it through the only accounting I have for these situations. In the city, when you go for the big boss and miss, you die — and so does your bloodline, every cousin and aunt and infant, because the whole point of slaughtering them is to make the lesson legible. It isn't anger. It's bookkeeping. You failed, and the account closes.

But Daral had spent his whole life somewhere the punishment for assaulting the keeper of the tomes was, apparently, that you might lose your position. He wasn't celebrating a murder. He was a man who had braced for annihilation and learned he was merely going to be *fired*, and the relief had gone straight to his head. I sorted his good cheer into that drawer.

"Or maybe he's just happy because he finally did it," Thorin said. "He finally killed the person that was holding him back."

A cleaner theory. Possibly the correct one. I kept both.

Daral sat. "What would you like to know?"

Thorin opened with the easy question — when did you last see him, nothing threatening. It's a pattern. It works every time. The eleven o'clock meeting came out without resistance — Janussi the *philistine* who'd wanted to sell off priceless poetry to fund some worthless tome about demonic planes no sane person would visit. "Just to please his horned tart," Daral said. "That's — Sylvira." He said it like the horns were the relevant fact.

I have heard that register before. It's the same one drow use about everything that isn't drow. I didn't say anything. I just marked it.

"He is not fit to be keeper of the tomes. An uneducated bookseller is what he is." A pause, a laugh. "May the gods bless whoever bumped him off."

Then the evening visit. He'd gone back to set Janussi straight and found the man *embraced in one of the very books he'd have me sell* — the gift Daral himself had given him. Thorin walked him in a slow circle around the contradiction: you gave him a book, you were furious to find him reading it. Daral's defense was a small theology lecture about whether books are for enjoyment or knowledge, which Thorin patiently dismantled, and at the end of it Daral had taken his book back, Janussi had *sat upon him like a common thug*, and in self-defense Daral *may have caught him lightly on the nose*.

He produced the book to prove it. And then, telling us how shaken the assault had left him, he wet his finger and began flicking through the pages, reading himself a funny passage until he was laughing again.

I watched the finger go to the mouth and back to the paper. I was fairly sure I knew what I was watching.

The suspects spilled out between page-turns — Teles, the deluded old git; A'lai Aivenmore, the preying cock; Fheminor Scrivenbark, *don't be fooled by her stature, she has a steely nature*. And Bookwyrm. "There's no telling what's going on in her lizard brain." Cold-blooded, he went on, they eat their young, murdering crowbait like Janussi.

Thorin stood up. "Some of my good friends are lizards. What are you trying to say here?"

Daral heard himself a beat too late. "This is just more of — barroom chatter, nothing to be—"

The book had been in his collection for years, he said. One of his favorites. He'd given it away to make a point — that his books should never be sold — by having Janussi read it and understand their worth. And then he'd been enraged to find Janussi reading it.

"It was a long day," Daral said, when Thorin laid the whole thing flat in front of him. "I was drugged. I was upset. I don't know what I was thinking."

"Perhaps things got away from you," Thorin offered, "and one thing led to another, and you ripped out his heart? We've all been there, let's be honest."

"I mean, who hasn't had that," I said.

He deflected to the others — they wanted the top job, every one of them, Bookwyrm and Teles and A'lai and Fheminor, round and round.

Thorin let him finish, then told him plainly: Janussi had been poisoned, the poison carried on a book, transferred by a man licking his finger and turning the pages.

The book hit the table. "Well — you've got to do something!"

"We did want to take it into evidence," Thorin said.

We took it. We'd keep Daral close until midnight — a cleric who could draw poison out, and a sword that could do the same, both within arm's reach. And if he died anyway, when the hour turned, I knew which way that pointed. A man does not lace the pages he's been licking all night. The dead poisoner is the innocent one.

## Zalthir — Glabbagool's Trial at the Whispering Dome

The Whispering Dome was the kind of place I would have liked under different circumstances. Quiet. Low light pooling under the curve of the ceiling. No one talking unless they had a reason. I have spent most of my life looking for rooms like this, and most of my life finding them already occupied by people who would not stop making noise.

Glabbagool was summoned for what they called his little quest. They told me I keep him — that he was, the word they used, permanently attached. I had no objection to log. He moseyed across the floor toward the Dome, an oozing thing moving at his own pace, and I followed. Of course I followed. That was never a question I was going to be asked twice.

He entered and flattened himself against the stone. The Dome is where supplicants go to learn something about themselves. I had not known that when I walked in. I learned it the way I learn most things — by watching what happened next.

A monk posed the question. *What is the art of movement.*

Glabbagool went still.

Not paused. Still. The difference matters. Paused costs something; still costs nothing. The better part of a minute passed. I have done that drill. I have held that exact silence in a shuttered room while some brother I cannot name walked the line correcting our breathing. I held it badly. Glabbagool held it like it was the easiest thing he had ever been asked to do.

Then he answered. *"The body that mimics nothing is already shaped by what it watches."*

The monk smiled. Nodded. Accepted it as wisdom, because it was.

I did not understand it. I want to be precise about that. I heard the words, I registered that they landed, I watched the monk take them as truth — and I did not understand them. There was a class for this. I think it was Brother Heslin, the one who always smelled of incense, or possibly the quiet one with the ink-stained hands. Silent discussion. Communication without sound. I remember the light through the shutters. I do not remember the lesson.

Glabbagool came out of his stillness and said, *"I've been waiting to do this for a very long time."*

And he flowed up onto my arm. *Glabbagool and Zalthir, together now,* he said, settling along my forearm like he had measured it in advance.

"Yes? Can I, like, punch something, or—"

I could. I would be given the particulars. What I felt, watching him arrange himself over my scales, was something I did not have a name for. "This is exciting. We formed Voltron or something."

The particulars arrived. He could teleport in darkness — my native ground, the one curriculum I actually attended. He could throw a flurry of strikes. He could force his acidic shadow key into a wound. And a corrosive embrace, which costs him something, and only works on a target I have already bound. "Hmm. The last one will need some explanation, but this sounds exciting."

"Interesting. Okay, so it will actually ride on my arm, and—"

*We ride,* Glabbagool said.

"I liked it." Then he added that it must be quiet, and I liked that more. The corrosive embrace, when he showed me what it would do to something held still — it was not a wound. It was dissolution. A target restrained, nothing left to hold by the end of it. "This is exciting."

Then the eyes. "Does this also mean I have, like, two eyes on my arm? Like, just kind of looking around?" Four, as it turned out, blinking up from my own scales. "Well, good! So I just admire him now." I suggested he might not need them — that he could blend into me rather than read as a separate creature riding along. "Right, she doesn't need the eyes, right?" He thought people expected him to have them, large and obvious. "Okay, so maybe he can just look more like my normal — scales, whatever I have." He paused over that. Considered it.

Before he settled in, he reminded me the drow in the bag of holding still needed watching. Of course he did. "Well, either way, as long as you check in on the drow, we're all good." He had survived Blingdenstone and the ooze crisis and however long in the dark, and his instinct, at the moment of his own transformation, was housekeeping. Good instinct.

The former shadow monk had been watching the whole exchange. He looked at me and said that if I had paid more attention to the classes on silent communication, I would know exactly what Glabbagool's answer meant. But that, apparently, had been the day I was sleeping.

"Too much gardening research."

There are, by now, a great many days I was sleeping. The student on my arm had mastered the one lesson I most thoroughly missed, and he had done it in under a minute, by going still.

"Right. There's a lot of them at this point. Was I ever there?"
