# Chapter 50 Candlekeep Library and the Littlest Drow in Faerûn


---

## Thorin — Farewell to Eldeth

The sun.

I keep coming back to that first moment of it — the way it hit the stone around the tunnel mouth and turned everything amber and gold, and I just stood there like an idiot with my mouth open. Months underground. Months of bioluminescence and faerzress and Dawnbringer's steady glow being the warmest light any of us had seen. And then this.

Dawnbringer, to her credit, said nothing. She'd been waiting longer than any of us.

The others were finding their own ways into it. Grygum turned his face up like he was receiving a blessing, which I suppose he was. Daz squinted and pushed his spectacles up and looked like a man calculating the precise angle of the light source. Zalthir went still in the particular way he goes still when something has moved him and he doesn't want anyone to notice.

And Glabbagool — Glabbagool just looked up.

I don't know how an ooze looks at something. Technically. But those googly eyes caught the sun and he was absolutely, undeniably looking at it, and there was something in the stillness of him that wasn't his usual stillness. He wasn't being a floor. He was witnessing. Zalthir stood nearby and I watched Zalthir watch him, and I thought: that's the face of someone who did a thing worth doing.

"He's got the stillness working for him," someone said — noting Glabbagool's chief qualification for the monk training Zalthir had apparently, in some sense, offered.

Zalthir's expression made clear that "offered" was doing significant work in that sentence.

"I mean, offered is a strong word."

I considered this. Glabbagool was, by most measurements, extremely stationary. He moved the way continental shelves move. Whatever curriculum Zalthir had in mind — the run, run, slap, slap, slap that Glabbagool apparently had very strong feelings about — it would require some foundational work first.

"Yeah, like, literally taking a step might be a good start."

That seemed fair. I thought about it a moment longer, turning it over the way you do when you're looking for the angle that makes something worth saying.

"But he's unpredictable," I said, "because there are no fixed appendages."

This was true. A fighter with no fixed appendages was genuinely threatening from a tactical standpoint. The form diagrams fell apart entirely.

"And it's easy to do, like, twelve hits in a round when you have twelve arms."

Zalthir looked at Glabbagool with the expression of a man reassessing a problem he thought he understood. I filed the idea away. Twelve simultaneous strikes from an opponent with no readable stance, no telegraphed motion, and a body that was essentially a liquid — that was actually a serious problem for anyone trying to defend against it.

Then Zalthir asked whether he could become an ooze himself.

The answer was no. The answer involved acid damage and what it would do to the grapple rules and I agreed with all of it, though I was curious for about three seconds. Then he pivoted to whether Glabbagool could be equipped as a weapon.

Also no.

"It's not a weapon," I said. "Weapons don't have agency. Except mine."

Dawnbringer made a small sound that I interpreted as acknowledgment. She'd been called worse things than agency. Earlier, in fact, someone had described her as a really cool sword who was kind of annoying sometimes, which she had received approximately the way a master sculptor receives being called "pretty good with rocks."

"I'm annoying also, Dawnbringer," I said. "Look, you know, we got each other."

The hilt warmed slightly. We understood each other on that point.

---

Eldeth found me a few minutes later, while the others were still adjusting to the light. She had that particular set to her shoulders she gets when she's made a decision and is walking toward it rather than away from it.

"Thorin," she said. "Giant friend."

She uses that. Not as mockery — she stopped doing that somewhere in the deep dark, somewhere around the time we stopped having the energy to argue about it and started just surviving together. She says it like it's part of my name now.

"I have to say that here our journey ends. I must go to Mithral Hall. I must inform the dwarves of what is going on in the Underdark. I hope to see you at some point again. If not, it was well met, and thank you for getting me out of the Underdark."

I stood with that for a moment. Not processing, exactly. I'd known it was coming. Eldeth had obligations that didn't include us, that were older than us, that had been pulling at her since Velkynvelve. A king to brief. A clan to return to. The surface world's ignorance to correct before demon lords became something a surface king heard about secondhand from a merchant who'd heard it from a deep gnome who'd heard it wrong.

"I mean," I said, "I'm a dwarf. I'm not invited along."

She explained. Clan business. Which I understood. Which I'd known even when I asked.

The thing is, I had spent most of my life not being the right kind of dwarf for any given room. Too giant-raised for dwarven spaces, too dwarven for the hills. There's a saying the old ones used — I'm translating badly — something like: a rock in the wrong place is just a rock. It doesn't stop being stone. It just doesn't fit the wall yet.

"Is there any way that I can petition to become part of your clan? A pledge, perhaps? Is there a hazing ritual involved?"

She looked at me. Something shifted in her expression — not softening exactly, more like the thing that had been braced against something could stop bracing.

"She goes, giant friend." And then she said it — all of it — the fighting together, the bleeding together, the facing of abyssal creatures together, the whole catalog of what we'd been through in the dark, laid out plainly, the way dwarves will do when they mean something past the point of argument.

I waited for the "but."

"You're not gonna say but there."

"No," she said. "No, that's where it ends."

And then she made the promise. The whole of it — Mithral Hall, Gauntlgrym, the lords who needed to be told about what was underneath them, the briefing that needed to happen without a giant-raised hill dwarf in the room making everyone curious about the wrong things. She said it and she meant it.

"After I have spoken and explained to the lords of Gauntlgrym and Mithral Hall the dangers of the Underdark, I am certain they will want to speak to you, and I will make it a statement of purpose that you shall be embraced by our clan."

And then: "I swear."

Something settled in me at that. The kind of settling that happens when a thing you've been carrying turns out to have been lighter than you thought, or heavier, or just — right. I'm not sure I have the word for it. The giants would have let it sit in the air for three days and known exactly what it was by the end of it. I had maybe four seconds before I had to say something.

"Alright," I said. "There we go."

Not the grandest response to a sworn oath. But it was what I had, and it was honest.

"Just know," I said, "I do want to learn about my dwarven roots. I want to become an integrated person — both my giant upbringing, but also my cultural roots, so, you know."

She nodded. I could see her doing the calculation — she'd already done it, probably, was the reason she'd looked toward Zalthir a moment earlier with that particular expression. A drow in a hood and a dwarf who'd been raised by hill giants, walking into Mithral Hall to brief the king about demon lords in the deep. I knew what she was thinking. She was right. The story needed to be told before the characters told it.

I was taking this seriously, and I needed her to know that.

"I'm taking this seriously," I said. "I don't think you're just being socially nice here. I think you really do want me to."

She extended her hand and I took it, and she held it for a moment the way that means something more than a handshake. Then she let go.

---

Spiderbait was having a complicated moment with the concept of goblins on the surface.

"I haven't been out here in my whole life," he announced to anyone who would listen, vibrating with the particular energy of someone who is absolutely about to make a terrible decision. "I'm just gonna go explore. It's totally safe for goblins, right? Nobody's gonna attack a goblin for being a goblin. We're totally integrated into the society."

"I don't think so," I said.

Daz agreed, in his careful way. Grygum was more specific.

"The state of goblin equality and inclusion is rough at the moment."

Spiderbait absorbed this. Recalibrated. Pivoted.

"Well, you know, I'm just a small goblin, and you're four big, powerful people. Do you mind if I just sort of hang out with you guys? Maybe find a nice place? This Candlekeep place seems like the kind of place someone like me could find a job doing something innocuous."

Grygum endorsed the plan. He endorses most plans that keep small things alive.

I looked back at the tunnel mouth — the last dark edge of where we'd been, the rock framing the light we'd come out into. Eldeth was already gathering herself, the set of her shoulders that meant moving, that meant west to Mithral Hall and the obligations that waited there.

Glabbagool was still looking at the sun.

West, she said. Head west and you'll hit Candlekeep. Simple as that, the way she says most things that aren't simple at all.

I watched her go.

## Zalthir — Shadows at Dusk

The third day of open sky and I still hadn't stopped scanning.

That's the thing about months underground: the habits don't switch off just because the ceiling lifts. I'd spent every waking hour in the Underdark reading distance, reading approach vectors, reading the quality of dark in every tunnel junction. Up here, with the wind and the light changing by the hour, I had too much information and not enough of the right kind. It was disorienting in a way I didn't have language for. Not dangerous. Just — loud. Everything up here was loud, even when it was silent.

Dawnbringer, at least, had no such ambivalence.

I'd been aware of the sword's mood for days. It radiated warmth the way a hearth does — not directed at anything in particular, just present. Thorin carried it, but the feeling belonged to all of us. I didn't blame her. She'd endured months of deliberate darkness and come out the other side into this: open sky, a sun that actually moved, stars that weren't being competed with by bioluminescent fungus. On the second evening I'd watched the light die on the horizon and found myself calculating threat vectors by reflex before remembering there were no walls to press against.

By the third day I'd almost relaxed.

Almost.

Dusk came in from the west, that particular quality of fading light that smears the edges of things. I noticed it because the edges of things are exactly what I watch. We were on the road, moving, the conversation turning easy around me the way it does when people have been walking together long enough that the silence between words stops feeling like something that needs filling. Somewhere behind me Thorin was saying something to Dawnbringer about making a decent team.

Then something in my chest went quiet.

Not quiet like peace. Quiet like the moment before.

There was a class for this. I'm reasonably certain it was Brother Orveth — or possibly Brother Quellin, the one who kept gesturing at the tree line when he talked — something about reading environmental stillness, the specific shape that a forest makes when something inside it is trying not to be. I remember the light in that room. I remember thinking I could be training instead. I don't remember what he said.

What I know now, without having been taught it properly, is that the trees north of us were wrong. Not visibly wrong. The wrongness was in the negative space — the way no birds had moved through that section for some time, the way the undergrowth hadn't shifted with the wind in quite the pattern it should. Something was in there, and whatever it was had been in there long enough to develop the habit of staying still.

Close. Uncomfortably close.

Grygum rolled his dice with the confidence of a man who already knows the answer. I watched him from the corner of my eye. He came up satisfied. "You conclude," came the word, "that what is following you is a deer."

I didn't say anything. Grygum nodded with serene certainty and indicated he could use a nap. Reasonable enough. If you're certain it's a deer, sleep makes sense.

Thorin's number was clean. Mine was solid. Daz, a step to my left, had come to the same place I had. The three of us looked at each other in the way that the Ember Vanguard has developed as a private language: no words, just a shared acknowledgment that the thing in the trees is not a deer.

"The three of you quickly realize," — and this landed with the weight of verification, not revelation — "after many months of being out in the dark, that somebody's following you."

I looked toward the northern treeline.

"The deer," I said.

A beat.

"Oh, no. It's not a deer. It's not a deer."

"Oh, dear," said Grygum, somewhere behind me, apparently now awake.

Twenty to forty feet, Thorin's read suggested. I adjusted my estimate of the gap. Closer to twenty. Whatever was in those trees had been moving with us, shifting position laterally to stay in the blind spots, using the terrain the way someone uses it when they know what they're doing. Not panicked. Not rushing. Patient. The kind of following that isn't trailing — it's pacing.

"Is it night, or what's the time of day?" I asked.

Dusk, we were told. Which meant we'd reached the worst possible window: too dark for fine detail, too bright for shadow work. Whoever was out there had timed this, or had simply been following us long enough to arrive at dusk by coincidence.

Neither option was reassuring.

Thorin raised the idea of Tremor Sense with the optimism of someone who'd forgotten which world we were currently standing on. He ran through the mechanics of it with genuine belief, right up until the point where it became clear that the forest floor was dirt and the forest road was dirt and none of it was stone. Then he looked at the trees, looked at the road, and came to the conclusion that the universe had personally wronged him.

"I picked it because we were gonna be fucking underground all the time," he said, with the conviction of a man delivering a formal complaint to the cosmos. "And I figured there would always be some stone. And then the one time I need it. We're not underground, and we're not surrounded by fucking stone."

"You've got anger issues," said Dawnbringer.

Thorin said something about driving issues. I said nothing. He wasn't wrong about the stone. And his frustration was legitimate — he'd made a reasonable preparation for a reasonable environment, and we'd somehow ended up standing in the one terrain where it didn't apply. Months underground, and the threat finds us on a dirt road.

The tracker had been moving sides. That was the detail that settled it from observation into certainty. They weren't following a fixed line — they were adjusting, reading our formation, keeping themselves occluded. That requires knowing what they're tracking. That requires intent.

Thorin's suggestion was to fireball the trees.

I considered this briefly, which is about as much time as it deserves.

Daz was already thinking past it. I could see it in the way he'd gone still — not my kind of still, but a wizard's still, the kind where the mind is moving very fast and the body has just stopped participating. "I want to get some intel out of this," he said, the way a man says a thing when he's already decided what he's going to do.

Someone speculated it might be Jimjar, coming to drag us back underground.

I did not rule it out.

"I'm going to misty step up into the tree," Daz announced.

The dusk settled around us. North of the road, something that was not a deer held its position and waited.

So did I.

## Grygum — A Shadow in the Woods

The bird call came just as I was settling in for the night.

Not a real bird — that was the point. A cardinal's whistle, clean and bright, floating through tree cover that hadn't seen a cardinal in decades, maybe ever. Daz had been practicing. We all had, in the abstract, the way you talk about a plan more than you drill it. But hearing it land in the quiet of a forest evening, unmistakable against the rustle of leaves, I thought: *good. That actually worked.*

What followed it was Glabbagool, doing his best.

His impression of a bird was everything you'd expect from a creature that has never encountered a bird, never encountered the concept of sound being produced intentionally for communication purposes, and was working from approximately zero reference points. It was enthusiastic. It was completely unrecognizable. It was the sound a bag of wet sand might make if someone dropped it from a significant height while also squeezing it.

He was very proud of it.

"Is it a kind of ooze?" he asked, in that way he has, earnest and completely serious about the question.

"No, no," I said, "they're not oozy at all. They're feathered."

This landed on him like a philosophical problem he hadn't budgeted for. *Feathered.* I watched the concept try to find somewhere to settle and fail to locate purchase. I'd had the same feeling, once, when a teacher of mine described a maxim as something that tells you what you're doing rather than what to do — I'd turned it over for three days before it clicked. Glabbagool didn't have three days. He had the next thirty seconds before we needed to move.

"We'll introduce you," I said. "Yeah, yeah, I wander over there."

I meant it literally. There would be birds. There would be a moment for introductions. The divine plan has us covered, in the broadest possible sense — or at least I choose to believe it does, provisionally, and correct the belief when the evidence demands it.

What I was actually thinking about, moving through the trees toward wherever Daz's signal had originated, was the drow. Whoever they were. Thorin had picked up something in the terrain earlier — movement from the north, not panicked, not aggressive, careful. Close. Twenty, maybe forty feet. Someone who was very good at staying invisible to anyone not actively looking for them, and not quite good enough to stay invisible to a party that had been hunted, chased, contracted against, and ambushed enough times in the last several months to develop opinions about being followed.

Months in the Underdark will calibrate your instincts. You learn to notice the shape of a threat before you can name it.

The note I'd already filed, in my head: *drow, on the surface, daylight hours, following us quietly instead of attacking.* That combination doesn't say assassin to me. It says someone who got handed a bad assignment and is making the best of it. There's a difference between a predator and someone doing a job they'd rather not be doing, and I've learned to read that difference fast. The predator enjoys the approach. The professional just wants it over.

By the time Glabbagool and I cleared the last of the underbrush, Zalthir had already ended the question of whether there would be a fight. There would not. The spy — drow, slight, female, exactly as advertised — was grappled and stunned, held in place with the kind of efficient finality that Zalthir brings to everything he considers a problem worth solving immediately. Thorin was standing over her, asking who number two works for, voice carrying through the trees with all the subtlety of a dwarf who was raised by hill giants and sees no reason to modulate volume as a social strategy. She stared at him with the blank patience of someone waiting for the world to start making sense again.

Daz was already climbing down from the tree.

"It's very rude," he said, which is the most Daz thing I've heard him say, possibly ever, "to follow people without just coming up and talking to them. So we'll talk to you now."

He said it while tying her up. The calm was so complete it was almost medical. I stood back and let him work, watching the spy's face for what it told me — not yet, she was still too stunned, still in that soft-edged blankness that Zalthir's strikes tend to produce — but even so. Even so.

She'd put on a brave face for the whole approach. Whoever sent a drow to the surface in daylight to shadow a party through open forest had, I thought, given that drow exactly the kind of assignment that earns you a very sincere complaint when you get back home. Assuming she got back home. That part wasn't yet settled.

I took notes. It's what I do.

## Daz — Interrogation of the Drow Spy

*(narrated by Daz)*

The stun wore off and she was already calculating. I could see it in the set of her shoulders — that particular Drow recalibration, the moment when violence becomes negotiation and the question shifts from *survive the next five seconds* to *survive the next five minutes.* She was small — Grygum's *Reduce* spell had seen to that — but she held herself with the trained dignity of someone who understood that posture was its own kind of armor.

Zalthir was first, the way he usually is in these moments: unhurried, precise.

"Who are you? Why are you following us? Are you a friend or foe?"

She looked at him. Then at the rest of us. The arithmetic was happening behind her eyes — I recognized it because I had run the same numbers at Velkynvelve, the first morning, when a wrong answer meant a crossbow bolt. She decided she didn't have enough information yet to give anything away.

"And why should I tell you anything? You will kill me on the spot and leave nothing. What do I have to gain from talking to you?"

"I have no particular desire to kill you," Zalthir said, "unless you mean us harm."

Interesting offer. She weighed it. Then, with the careful neutrality of someone who has been trained to give the minimum required to survive: "I am of House T'sarran. I will not give my name. Take my piwafwi if you want a trophy. My matron will know one of hers fell out here, and that is enough."

House T'sarran. The name landed in my chest like a dropped coin — a small, clear note I tried not to react to. I kept my face still and let the others carry the conversation forward.

Thorin tried intimidation. He is, genuinely, a large and battle-tested dwarf who has survived things that would have killed most creatures twice over, so the raw material is there. What he has not spent five years in Menzoberranzan is the instinct for which lever actually moves a Drow. He walked up and told her we could simply let her go — report her failure to her matron, "let them know how pathetic you are." He said this with the confidence of someone who thought he was offering a fate worse than death.

He was not wrong that it was, for someone like her.

She stared at him blankly. The stun was still doing its work; I'm not sure she'd fully processed the comment.

He tried again. She laughed in his face.

I found I didn't feel superior about this. Thorin's instincts have saved us too many times for me to feel superior. What he lacks in Drow politics he makes up for in a kind of irreducible physical honesty that people cannot quite dismiss, even when they're laughing at him. She was laughing, but she was also still talking to us, which is the more important variable.

Grygum, meanwhile, had been watching her the way he watches things — not calculating exactly, more like listening for something the rest of us weren't hearing. There is a quality to his attention I've never fully resolved. He looked at her and I think he saw the whole picture at once: the surface posting, the miserable exposure to the overbright, the desperate bravado of someone who'd drawn a terrible assignment and was trying to make it look like a choice.

"Hey," he said, with the casual tone of someone considering a minor administrative matter, "are you looking for a new job? We could use another helper."

Something shifted in her face. A tiny, involuntary fracture in the composure. *Wait. I might live?*

It is remarkable what that realization does to someone. She had walked into this interrogation with the set expression of a soldier prepared to die with her intelligence intact, and now she looked — for just a moment — like a person who had not yet decided how much she wanted to live. The Menzoberranzan training is very thorough about creating a certain relationship to death: make it seem like a preference, not a threat. But she was young enough, or tired enough, or perhaps just unhappy enough with her assignment, that the offer of survival caught her genuinely off guard.

Then Grygum held out the prayer beads.

He didn't say anything particularly ominous. He just handed them to her and told her to put them on. No threat in his voice. No explanation of what they did.

She looked at a half-orc cleric holding out holy symbols of a deity she knew nothing about, in the overbright that was already making her eyes ache, after having been stunned and grappled and shrunk and laughed at by a dwarf, and something in her assessment of the situation entirely reorganized itself. The beads were the thing that actually broke through the professional armor. A credible threat she understood — extraction, summary execution, reporting her failure up the chain — these were surfaces she had trained to hold against. Unknown religious ritual from an alien faith, administered by a half-orc? That slipped right past every prepared defense.

She looked, in the GM's precise summary, defeated, frustrated, annoyed.

Then Grygum rolled persuasion, and she talked.

"I was just assigned to track you, not to engage. My orders were to report your surface destination, your contact, who approaches you." A pause. Then: "My matron was waiting for you to surface. She sent a couple of us up to the overbright to find where you were. I got lucky, and I spotted you."

"Probably not feeling so lucky right now," Thorin said.

She ignored this with some dignity.

I had been listening. I had been watching. And something had been bothering me since she opened her mouth, a subtle wrongness I couldn't quite name until I let myself actually look at her — properly look, the way you read a room in Menzoberranzan when the words people are saying bear only incidental relationship to what they mean.

She wasn't watching the party.

She was watching me. And working very, very hard to make it look like she wasn't.

Being a drow, you pick some things up. The particular discipline of *not looking at the thing you are most interested in* is one of the first social skills anyone learns in the city. You don't stare at the matron. You don't watch the door where your rival just entered. You keep your gaze distributed, generalized, apparently casual — and the effort of that maintenance, that deliberate control, is itself legible to anyone who knows what they're looking at. I knew what I was looking at.

I let the interrogation continue for one more breath, then:

"So we may be able to figure something out where you live, but first you need to answer some questions. Why am I so important that you're trying hard not to make it look like you're following me, but you're following me?"

A pause. She decided.

"The Matron Mother was interested in you."

"Who's your matron mother?"

She looked at me with an expression that was almost — not quite, but almost — contemptuous. "You have not heard? Are you not drow?"

I said something genuinely stupid at this point, which I will attribute to the fact that I am not, technically speaking, accustomed to conducting interrogations in full sunlight while running on whatever sleep we'd managed in Blingdenstone. "I'm drow, but I've been underground for a while." 

She stared at me.

"You realize how absurd that sounds?"

She was right that it was absurd. I corrected myself: "Let me just put it this way. Let me correct that. I've been away from home for a while."

She told us her matron had ruled House T'sarran for two hundred years. The length of tenure alone told me something — not a house currently in crisis, not a young matron making aggressive plays to consolidate position. Old power. Patient power. The kind that waits.

I thought of something a training instructor had said once, after a particularly messy exercise in leverage: *The first rule of the House: know who holds the debt before you decide whether to collect it.* I hadn't understood it then as well as I understood it now, standing in a field on the surface world, realizing that someone in a house I'd crossed had been watching me since before I knew I was being watched.

The full picture came out under continued pressure — Dawnbringer's light brought close, the beads still around her neck, Thorin floating the suggestion of leaving her out in the full sun in her current reduced state. Not torture. Providing motivation.

"My matron believes that the one who paid — " and here she pointed at me, with the resigned precision of someone who has run out of misdirection — "for him, will reveal themselves once he reaches the surface. We are looking for that person, not for you."

I turned it over once. Twice.

"So basically, you're treating me as bait. You're watching and observing so that when something happens to me, you can go report back what happened to me."

"Yes. Because we are looking for the one who paid to have you protected."

I asked why they cared.

"The matron mother cares, and therefore I care."

Which is the complete answer, in Menzoberranzan. No ambiguity. No further explanation required. The matron's interest is the reason; the matron's interest is sufficient.

I stood there in the sun — which I was, admittedly, still adjusting to — and I understood what had just been said about me. Not Daz. Not even the wizard. The *indicator.* The piece on the board that you move to see who else moves in response. The bait.

I am an eighth-level wizard. I have kept four people alive through the Underdark, through demon lords, through a dragon, through House politics I was running on improvised expertise and nerve. I have apparently been valuable this entire time primarily because someone with resources thinks *watching what happens to me* might reveal a person they are actually interested in.

Milo Goodbarrel would have found a way to feel good about this. Something about being significant. I thought about it for a moment and decided I'd give myself until we reached Candlekeep to develop the appropriate perspective.

She proposed a deal — mutual information sharing, she tells us what she finds, we tell her what we find, everyone wants to know who's protecting me. Thorin was skeptical in the direct way that Thorin is skeptical of things: "I don't buy it. The minute she has the information she needs, she's off, and we get jack shit."

He is not wrong. The deal has no enforcement mechanism. She has no incentive to honor it beyond her own interest, and the moment her interests and ours diverge — which is to say, the moment she has what she came for — the deal dissolves. Drow contracts without consequences attached aren't contracts. They're intentions.

Then Grygum said: "There's only one solution. We need to kill Daz."

Zalthir, entirely deadpan: "It's on the list. The maybe list."

"And then we'll find out who's protecting him," Grygum continued, with the calm of someone describing a routine experimental procedure.

Thorin: "Do it, but make sure we can bring them back."

"Oh, I have a Revivify spell." Grygum, reassuring.

"So that's just a temporary death."

"Just a death light."

Thorin turned to me. "Is Daz uncomfortable with this conversation?"

I am categorically on record as being the only person in this party who treats information as a finite and valuable resource, and yet here I was, the subject of active theoretical murder planning while a restrained enemy watched us debate it. I kept my expression neutral. I have found purpose. I am the feared mage of the Ember Vanguard. This is fine.

She warned us, between our negotiations, that killing her wouldn't stop House T'sarran from sending more agents.

Grygum held up the prayer beads. "I'm not relying on killing you. I'm relying on the prayer beads."

She eventually proposed being escorted to Candlekeep and handed to the guards — and she said it with the confidence of someone who already knew the second half of that plan didn't involve staying in Candlekeep for very long.

"So they know how to escape that prison," I observed. "Is what they're telling us."

Thorin: "She's so confident she can escape, I don't think we want to be taking her into the stronghold."

Accurate. We don't.

Grygum and Thorin exchanged a look. Then, very loudly, making no apparent attempt to lower their voices:

"Those prayer beads are at full power, even though they're shrunken, right?"

Grygum, with great ceremony: "Yes — in fact, I believe they may be more deadly."

"More potent. Good to know."

She looked at the beads. She looked at Grygum. She began, visibly, to reconsider the available options.

## Zalthir — The Pocket Spy and the Road to Candlekeep



The mushroom went down her throat fast. One moment she was a full-sized spy — annoyed, calculating, already looking for angles — and the next she was *very squeaky and indignant*, which is a phrase that does not lose anything in translation. She shrank to about the size of a child's toy, her voice pitched up into something that was still clearly fury but now delivered from below knee-height. Thorin watched this with the expression of a man receiving exactly what he ordered.

"Nickname her Polly Pocket," he said. Then, with the flat authority of someone who knows what he's doing: "It's very demeaning."

It was. I filed that away.

The practical questions followed in order, which is how Thorin approaches everything — you solve the immediate problem, then you solve the next one. Do we have a mushroom to grow her back? Apparently yes. Can she slip through a net? She can now. Daz, with the particular tone he uses when the obvious solution is also the inelegant one, offered: "We can put her in the bag of holding with Glabbagool, and he can just hold onto her."

There was a brief detour into the geography of a bag of holding's interior, which I did not contribute to, and then the ventilation question arose. Poke holes. Let her breathe. *Like a hamster*, Thorin said, and added that he hoped the ridicule was getting to her.

It was. She was starting to wonder whether there were other fates.

I understood that. Weeks in the Underdark with us will do something to a person's sense of available options. She was small enough now that Glabbagool could watch her with a single eye without breaking his stride. That was what I noticed — not the spy's indignation, not Thorin's satisfaction, but Glabbagool, walking with one eye locked permanently on the prisoner. He had been assigned a job. He was doing the job. The care in it — the steadiness of that unblinking attention — was the most earnest thing I'd seen in weeks.

He'd been in the dark of that bag for days. No sun, no space, no way to say whether any of it was strange or expected because he had no reference for what was normal above ground. I'd told him I'd protect him from corrupting forces. I hadn't thought about what it would be like to be carried in a bag with a furious, newly miniaturized assassin as your only company. I should have thought about that.

Thorin, not without a certain gentleness, offered: "At least the bag of holding is shade, you know, so she's not gonna be exposed to full sun."

"There you go," I said.

"This is mercy," Thorin confirmed.

It was the most honest description of our mercy I could have asked for. She was small, surveilled by an ooze, breathing through holes we'd poked in the bag, and being shielded from sunlight as a kindness. The drow woman inside had probably survived worse. But probably had never survived anything quite like this.

---

On the road, Thorin made sure she overheard him: "Those prayer beads are at full power, even though they're shrunken, right?"

Grygum picked it up without missing a beat: "Yes, yes, in fact — I believe they may be more deadly."

"They're more potent. Okay. Good to know."

I watched him say it with the mild interest I reserve for things that are technically his problem and not mine. The prisoner was in the bag. The bag was moving. The misinformation was circulating. All of it was fine.

---

Candlekeep required a book. This was not a problem for us. The problem, as Daz identified it, was which book — because we had, after months in the Underdark acquiring things from people who no longer needed them, accumulated more books than most surface libraries see in a decade. The list, when assembled: Princess Ebonmire's spellbook. Ilvara's prayer book with its Zuggtmoy marginalia, those careful notes documenting the corruption timeline she'd watched unfolding and apparently found worth recording. Asha Vandree's theological argument about Lolth and Zuggtmoy as equivalents — the document I'd watched her press into Grygum's hands. The Velkynvelve prisoner journal. Pliinki's journal from the Whorlstone Caverns, the one where a man had written carefully about helping birth a demonic monster and apparently thought this was worth documenting.

I thought about Asha Vandree, briefly. The way she had looked through me the entire time we were in that room. I had been furniture; I had noticed the quality of her erasure without taking it personally, because it wasn't personal — it was structural, the way the Underdark is cold. You don't take the cold personally. The theological document she'd written would now unlock questions in one of the greatest libraries in the world. She would have hated that.

More books, more questions, more access — the monks of Candlekeep would find all of it interesting, which was the point. One book gets you in the door. Five books gets you *facetime*, which I gathered was the currency that actually mattered there.

Then Thorin said: "Does Glabbagool have anything that he wants to ask also?"

The ooze, apparently, was deeply confused by the concept of a library with finite questions attached to it. He wanted to know why we weren't simply donating every book we'd ever seen and asking questions until the end of time. This was the most logical position I'd heard all week. Glabbagool's confusion was not stupidity; it was a creature without the particular social conditioning that makes you accept "there are rules about how much knowledge you're allowed to receive at once" as a normal thing.

Thorin pressed it anyway: "We give all five books, and we say — Glabbagool, you get one question. Make it count. And I'm really interested to see what he asks."

Grygum said he was curious too.

Five books. Four of us and one ooze who had been in the dark of a bag for most of his conscious existence, watching a shrunken spy with one eye and trusting that the world above ground was worth seeing.

I was also curious what he'd ask. I didn't say so, but I thought about it for the better part of the road to Candlekeep — what a creature with no past and no allegiances and a singular earnest interest in understanding the world would choose to know, given a single question and access to everything.

I didn't have an answer. But I kept watching the bag, the way it moved, the slight weight of it. Somewhere in there, one eye was open.

## Grygum — Entry into Candlekeep

The road to Candlekeep had given me a lot of time to think. Maybe too much.

I'd spent most of it turning the same questions over in my head like stones I kept expecting to find something under. What does Bahamut actually want from me? Not the general want — the "be just, protect the innocent, oppose Tiamat" want that I could recite in my sleep — but the specific want. The personal assignment. Because somewhere in the theological wreckage of the last few months, between the demon lords and the dead gods and Jimjar-who-was-apparently-a-celestial-the-whole-time, I had accumulated a very significant stack of things I did not understand, and I was hoping Candlekeep might help me start working through it.

The place was exactly what you'd expect from the world's most famous library: enormous, self-important, and surrounded by the kind of architecture that said *we have been here longer than your entire civilization and we have the receipts.* I felt like I should have brought a better book.

As it turned out, we'd brought five.

Daz handled the initial approach — he usually does, being constitutionally suited to the kind of careful, measured diplomacy required when dealing with institutions that have rules posted in multiple languages. The gatekeeper explained the terms with the particular energy of someone who has explained the same terms several thousand times and has made peace with that.

"The rules say that you must provide one book per group. But if you have come to ask more than one question and wish to intersect with many of the avowed — who are sort of the monks of Candlekeep, the sages, the professors — providing more books will get you more FaceTime."

I did a quick count. One question per book. Five books. Four of us, plus the Glabbagool consideration, plus the accumulated mysteries of the last several months.

"Okay, well, we have a few books," I said, trying to organize the triage out loud. "And really the question we want to get answered is, who's — who's following Daz, right?"

The gatekeeper, helpful soul, ran through the rest of the list. Zalthir wanted to know about Jimjar. Thorin wanted help with Dawnbringer. Which prompted Thorin himself to note, with the absolute conviction of someone who has thought about this:

"Okay, but I mean, like, I'm looking for a therapist. This is not a one-question type of thing. This is — there are no shortcuts when it comes to therapy."

"It's a process," I said, "not a product."

He nodded gravely. We understood each other.

Then he proposed giving Glabbagool his own question, which I thought was extremely fair. Glabbagool, for his part, was reportedly very confused about the concept of a library with effectively infinite capacity, and didn't understand why we weren't donating every book we'd ever encountered so we could ask questions until the end of time. This is what I mean when I say Glabbagool's perspective on the world is bracing. He's not wrong. He's just operating on a scale we haven't caught up to yet.

We gave all five books.

The First Reader received them with the barely-contained delight of someone who has been waiting for exactly this delivery. She lingered on the Velkynvelve Prisoner Journal with particular reverence. "This journal of the prisoners is a masterpiece of interesting value. I can't wait to study it carefully. It will go great with my other editions in drow prison journals."

I couldn't help myself. "Oh, there's a whole shelf of those, isn't there?"

There apparently was.

Thorin, ever practical, pointed out that we were the primary sources — she could just interview us and save herself the reading. The First Reader seemed genuinely charmed by this. And then Thorin mentioned, with the casual energy of someone producing a rabbit from a hat, that we also had a drow. Specifically, we had a drow who was currently very small, very angry, and in our possession.

The spy's voice came out approximately at the volume of an extremely annoyed insect. "This is highly irregular, and House T'sarran will have its vengeance!"

Daz didn't even look up. "Tiny, tiny little vengeance."

Thorin said: "Right."

I assigned Glabbagool to keep her under control, which felt like the appropriate use of available resources. "We'll have a goal," I said. "Keep her under control."

What followed was one of those moments that reminds you Glabbagool contains multitudes. He told the spy he could poke her eyes out, then immediately clarified — with the earnestness of someone who genuinely wants to be understood — that the googly eyes were just affectations. "I can actually see different ways. This is how ooze work. But I discovered that if you have these cute eyes, people take you more friendly." And then, to demonstrate his actual authority, he started producing eyes. Many eyes. Proliferating eyes.

It got very creepy very quickly.

The spy regrouped and demanded to know about the prayer beads. Which: bold choice, given the circumstances.

Thorin's response was immediate and merciless. "You don't get a question. You did not donate a book."

I mentally filed that under "correct."

The First Reader assigned scholars. Zalthir got the Jimjar thread — which apparently dovetailed neatly with an avowed named Khell-Vire who wanted to learn about something specific. The First Reader clarified: not about the party generally, but about *me* in particular.

Daz observed that there were apparently two famous people in the party.

I tried to look modest about this and mostly failed.

Thorin and Dawnbringer got directed to someone named Philemon — described as "an avowed monk specializing in psychological preservation and historical cataloging of ancient sentient artifacts, with a tremendous track record of success in working through the interpersonal conflict issues of the sentient artifact." Thorin received this information like a man who has been hoping someone would say exactly these words.

"I'm all up for couples counseling with Dawnbringer."

Good. They needed it. I said nothing.

What I did say, looking at the assembled scholars being pointed at us like a hospitality service we hadn't paid for: "I almost feel like they should be giving us books."

And then the room changed.

A half-elf — elderly, frantic, moving at a speed that his body seemed to object to — came through the door at something between a brisk walk and a controlled fall, directed his outrage squarely at the First Reader, and said: "I cannot believe you did not tell me about the arrival of a cleric of Bahamut."

I looked at him. He looked at me. His eyes had the expression of a man who has found a very rare specimen in the wild.

"I'm feeling," I said, to no one in particular, "in demand here."

His name was Vareth. He had, he explained breathlessly, a decades-long specialty in half-orc Bahamut converts. Which had, until recently, been a quiet, small area of study. A niche. Something he'd pursued with quiet dedication while the rest of the academic world found more fashionable problems.

And then Stroud beat Protanther at chess.

I know this story. I know it the way you know a story that someone told you when you were young and uncertain, when you needed proof that the impossible was occasionally available as an option. A dwarf who couldn't actually play chess, sitting across from an ancient gold dragon who had been analyzing the same position for four hundred years. Protanther had mapped every possible move. He was certain. And then Stroud rolled — and it landed perfectly. Definitively. The kind of thing that doesn't just work, it *announces* itself. Protanther was so stunned he joined the alliance. The world shifted because someone who didn't know what they were doing did the exact right thing at the exact right moment.

I have always found this deeply comforting. Four hundred years of preparation still has a blind spot.

Vareth was still talking. Stroud's victory had turned his quiet scholarly backwater into a flood. The controversy had erupted throughout the realms — what did the sudden half-orc interest in Bahamut mean for the orc pantheon? Was it part of Bahamut's plan? And now people wanted to talk to Vareth. They were inviting him to conferences. Funding was arriving. A living half-orc cleric of Bahamut had walked through his door.

He grabbed my hand and practically dragged me.

I let him. I was curious, and also I had questions of my own.

First, though, I wanted to take the measure of him. "You have to understand," I said, with the measured cadence I use when I'm testing someone, "that it is traditional for clerics of Bahamut to want to try to take over the world. But I haven't moved into that phase yet."

Vareth barely paused. "This is all fascinating! I have so many theories I wish to test out." He looked up. "And I do have something I can share with you?"

All right. We were going to get along.

He circled back to Stroud, to the chess game, to the conversion phenomenon that had restructured his entire career. He was trying to find the shape of it, the causality. I felt it coming together and finished the thought for him:

"It truly was legendary."

And then, because I couldn't help it, and because the phrase had been sitting in the back of my mind since we started this journey and I'd never had the right audience for it:

"Stroud's Gambit."

Vareth stopped. His expression went through several stages very quickly — surprise, recognition, something that looked almost like delight at receiving a gift he hadn't known he wanted.

"*Oh.* Oh! Well, that's not how — this is all novel news! I can't wait to take advantage of it! The field of Bahamut half-orc conversions will benefit tremendously from your interview."

"Oh," I said, as warmly as I could manage, "it's all part of the plan."

"This is all fascinating!"

Somewhere in the background, the rest of the party had begun debating Daz's habit of citing Malfire at every available opportunity. I heard Grygum's name pulled into the discussion — *Grygum*, not me, which is how I know the sentence was reconstructed after the fact — but the content was accurate. We had been through enough together that the citations had become a kind of ambient weather condition, something you adapted to rather than fought.

"Yeah," I said, when someone turned to me for confirmation, "it's like we're kind of sick of each other, but somehow still together, yeah."

And then, because the opportunity was simply too good: "As Malfire said in Volume 3."

Nobody laughed. This was the correct response.

Vareth was still watching me with the expression of someone who has found the answer to a question they've been asking for decades and is now immediately generating seventeen follow-up questions. His research proposals were finally getting funded. He had a living data point. He had "Stroud's Gambit" — a term he was clearly already planning to deploy in the first conference he attended.

I let him hold onto that for a moment.

Somewhere in the library, there were answers waiting. About Bahamut's intentions, about what my conversion meant in the broader theological landscape, about why Vareth's previously obscure field had become a flood. I didn't know what I was going to find. I had notes — the prayer book, the theological documents, the accumulated evidence of months in the Underdark — and I had questions, and I had the particular comfort of knowing that sometimes the person who doesn't know what they're doing walks into the right room and everything clicks.

I took notes. It's what I do.
