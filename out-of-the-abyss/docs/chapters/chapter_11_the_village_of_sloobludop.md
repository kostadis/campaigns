# Chapter 11 The Village of Sloobludop 

### 1st day of the 3rd Tenday of Taraskh 1493

Ploopploopeen, the archpriest of the Sea Mother, stares unblinkingly at the escapees. 

And they are confused. Yes, there is a reward, but why should they sacrifice themselves for these fish people? 

Ploopploopeen realizes that the Tongues spell didn’t translate his intent as well as he would like. The escapees think they will be required to fight. 

“No, this is wrong. You will arrive in Sloobludop and stay in my quarters. I will send message to the followers of Leemoogoogon that I have an offering for him. In the morning, my daughter, Bloppblippodd, the high priestess of the Deep Father, will demand to see the offering. I will present you to her, and I will insist that I come with you. She will accept. And then all of my followers will attack. Your role in this will be over.” 

Grygum finally understands, “This is like a flag of parley. Have you considered a white flag?” 

“I tried that, but she didn’t bite. She needs sacrifices for her false god.”

Grygum tries to probe if the sacrifice must be willing. 

“I am not certain how your rituals work, but in the rituals of Leemoogoogon, the sacrifice is essential, not the provenance.” 

Grygum nods, “Yes, our rituals are different.” 

Thorin probes, “But willing sacrifices are sweeter?” 

“No. An unwilling sacrifice is better. This is an evil god.” 

Grygum looks at Plooploopeen and wonders what he has heard. He and his daughter have problems with their relationship, and it’s also unclear if a god named Leemoogoogon even exists. 

Topsy and Turvy, on the other hand, realize that a free-for-all is an excellent opportunity to steal. 

Zalthir wonders why they should bother. They have their problems, and the issues of these two warring tribes are not their concern. 

But Daz sighs, “Man, we’ve been running for days. Having a purpose in life is a good thing. We busted out of prison, and then what? Ran. We’re being chased, and we keep running. One of our group got killed by another of our group. Having something to focus on would be much less depressing than just doing what we are doing.” 

Zalthir shrugs and the group accepts Ploop’s offer. 

The remaining journey to Sloobludop is uneventful. The tunnels eventually end up on the village's western side of about 500 kuo-toans. There is a wall made of nEttin g guarded by Kuo-toans. They snap to attention when Ploop arrives and lets everyone through. 

Thorin notes that the nEttin g isn’t an effective wall against a determined invader. 

Past the walls are the huts of the kuo-toans, and at the other end of the town, almost 500 feet away, is another wall of nets. 

Between the two walls to the south is the cave wall. To the north, between the two nEttin g walls, is the Darklake. Between the walls of nEttin g, the cave wall, and the Darklake is the village. There are over a hundred houses of various sizes. Each home has mud walls, and the ceilings are made from an unidentified dried vegetable matter. 

The Darklake side has docks. Some of the docks have boats. Some are empty. Walking along the docks are groups of Kuo-Toans. These leader types that Shuushar explained are responsible for ensuring that the madness of the Kuo-Toans doesn’t rip the society apart. 

In the middle of the town is a large statue 

Ploop stops in front of the sizeable nine-foot-tall statue. The statue is roughly carved from wood in the shape of a humanoid female, its head and forearms formed from the severed head and claws of a giant albino crayfish. These parts are lashed on with strands of gut and emit an overpowering stench of rotting shellfish. Shells, brightly colored stones, mushrooms, and rotting fish are piled at the statue’s feet and strung in garlands around its neck. 

Four stern kuo-toa slowly circle the statue, alert and on guard, while a few others move about, gazing up at the statue and bowing repeatedly while chanting.

Grygum recalls from somewhere that the Deep Sea Mother is a scavenger goddess and that regurgitating and spitting on her altar is perhaps the perfect form of offering. 

Ploop is delighted when Grygum and Thorin both spit on the altar. 

Meanwhile, Daz, overcome by the rank smell, has his hand over his mouth and tries to rub the smell out of his nose with his thumb. Ploop looks at him, and he smiles weakly as he pretends his nose is itchy. 

“We must eat\!” he shouts. 

Thorin is worried that this will be some raw rotting food, but Ploop, perhaps sensing that, assures him that Kuo Toans eat cooked fish. 

At this, even Zalthir is convinced that perhaps helping the Kuo-Toans isn’t a bad idea. 

After eating, Ploop falls asleep. As is the case with Kuo Toans, he stands up and walks outside. 

At this point, the escapees notice that many kuo-toans just walk around with their eyes open, sleeping. And that some other kuo-toans are moving about as schools of fish. 

At this point, they decide to go check out the altar of Leemoogoogon. They sneak out just close enough to see the idol of Leemooggoogoon the Deep Father. It consists of a large hide cut roughly in the shape of a manta ray and stretched out on cords between two support poles. A dead, splayed-out manta ray is pinned to the center of the hide. Two dead octopuses are draped across the top, their tentacles pinned and artfully arrayed, their heads tied together and painted with red and blue pigments. The idol reeks of decay and the broad stone altar below the idol is stained dark with blood. 

Unfortunately, their attempt to be quiet is anything but. And the kuo-toans circling the Deep Father notice. 

“I say we beat a hasty retreat,” whispers Thorin. 

“These guys are spooky. Let’s get out of here,” mutters Zalthir. 

Safe inside Ploop’s house, another Kuo-Toan stares at them. Uncertain if he is awake or asleep, they ignore him. 

He says, “My name is Glooglugogg. Gloog is what my friends call me. I do not approve of Father’s choice to have outsiders meddle in our affairs, but you showed respect to the Sea Mother, and thus, I see that you respect and understand our ways. Our society is torn to pieces by my sister Bloppblippodd. Father has shown her too much deference, offered her too much, and let her get away with too much. She has no respect for our ways. And she has fallen prey to this madness.” 

Gloog’s dislike for his sister becomes increasingly apparent as he enumerates her ills and flaws as a sister, daughter, and former cleric of the Sea Mother.  

Zalthir asks, “We saw a dozen near the altar. Is that all her support?” 

Gloog burps, “Her hard-core supporters are few, but how many in the village are on our side? Her side is unknown. This is why it must be dealt with now. While we still have the support of the majority.”

Zalthir, again, clarifies his role, “So when we go there, our job is just to sit around and do nothing.” 

Gloog looks at him, “Of course not. You will help us as you, too, see the righteousness of the Deep Mother.” 

“But we won’t throw the first punch?” 

“No, that would insult the honor of my father and our people. This is our problem, and we must resolve it.” 

Zalthir is satisfied. 

Gloog turns to him, “Now get rest. In a while, we will have to fight.” 

As Gloog is about to sleep, he mutters, “You didn’t choose just to give us the derro whom you tied up? That is both honorable and odd.” 

### 2nd day of the 3rd   Tenday of Taraskh 1493

In the morning, twelve kuo-toa led by a kuo-toa monitor arrive at Ploopploopeen’s quarters. The archpriest of the Sea Mother admits the monitor and informs him that he, his whip, and the prisoners will accompany them to see the archpriest of the Deep Father.
