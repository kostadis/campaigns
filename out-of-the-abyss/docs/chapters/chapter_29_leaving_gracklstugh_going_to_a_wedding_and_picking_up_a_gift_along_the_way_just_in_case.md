# Chapter 25 Leaving Gracklstugh, going to a wedding, and picking up a gift along the way, just in case. 
### Grygum


Here I am, Gyrgum, half-orc cleric of Bahamut, and Gracklstugh is what makes an orc camp look pretty. 


Any escape will require some "clever and insane scheme" to get through the barred gates of Laduguer's Furrow. 


And when you think about it, I seem to keep having to escape from some prison, whether it’s the prisons on the overbrite, or the prisons of the Drow, or the prisons of the Duergar. I have a gift for this. I suppose. 


The dead grey ghosts litter the street, and we start wondering how we will find a way into the Darklake District. We have passes, but Eldeth will demand to see what we found. And if we share with her what we found, that will trigger violence. 


Suddenly, a massive shadow falls over us. My eyes, accustomed to the gloom of the Underdark, pick out a dragon, a young red dragon, flying above. By Bahamut, he looks on the chubby side. It's Themberchaud, the Wyrmsmith of Gracklstugh, and he's dive-bombing right for us! My first, most practical thought is that "we're not a match for a dragon". I quickly suggest we try to "talk to him" while positioned "Near something I can duck into". 


A parley is always better than a fight against impossible odds.


Themberchaud lands with a shuddering impact. The landing lacks any of the grace of a dragon. It looks like a bag of potatoes that landed and the bag barely broke. As he stands up, he resembles a farmer trying to steady the bag, stuffing the potatoes into position so it can stand properly. 


I look closer.


The typical taught and defined muscles of the dragon are nowhere to be seen. I wonder why, and it’s obvious, he is a predator who doesn’t hunt. And then I look at him more closely, and his scales are shiny. Someone has been carefully keeping them polished. And his claws are painted. They have none of the telltale signs of rending and destruction that they should have. He looks like a pampered toy, a pet, not the fearsome predator that he is. And he doesn’t realize how ridiculous he looks, fat and pampered and dolled up. 


The dragon’s voice booms and declares to us that we are his “agents, bound by my protection."


He raises his head, and I can’t help but notice the second chin.


 He then declares to all, "The keepers in the flame and their short-sightedness would have you waste precious time navigating checkpoints and patrols." And then he surprises me when he offers his "express service to where you must go next". 


I look at him, confused. And he shouts, playfully and authoritatively, "Climb aboard, unless you wish to question the will of your wormsmith". And I start to wonder. Themberchaud’s maturity has been adversely affected by the peculiar role he serves in Gracklstugh as a glorified lighter. I wonder why the Duergar chose a Red Dragon? Surely magic would be cheaper and less dangerous. 


But enough about that. 


The prospect of riding a dragon is too tempting. It feels "dangerous and stupid," but a dragon ride? So… I'll do\]. I even attempt to flatter him, addressing him as "Great Themberchaud".


We all clamber onto his voluminous back, easily finding space. Themberchaud takes off with a powerful beat of his wings, proudly declaring, "I need to show you my realm". He flies us through the cavern, proclaiming it "my town". It's clear that he "thinks he's in charge". Yet, I know the truth: he's merely a "glorified lighter" for the Duergar forges. My Bahamut-trained sensibilities are appalled at how they treat Themberchaud. To see such a powerful creature so manipulated is a travesty against dragonkind.


But this is not the time to reveal anything. 


### Daz


A young dragon would not have enough space for our gang, but this is a very large dragon. Not a very old dragon, a very large dragon. 


The creature groans as it flaps its wings, and then we are airborne. To fly on a dragon in the overbrite is one thing; to fly on a dragon in a cave in the underdark that is covered in thick clouds of the forges of the Duergar is quite another. 


The dragon shouts, “Where to?” 


Grygum looks at me. Thorin mutters, “We should just tell him everything. Nah.” 


I am relieved. I do not want to have a dragon destroy a city while I am on its back. 


After conferring, we agree that the right place to go is the docks. 


Themberchaud is unimpressed - and roars -  “My agents have more pressing matters than to loiter among the common rabble and petty traitors of the Dark Lake.”


Grygum tries to persuade him but fails. I begin to worry. 


Grygum asks the dragon, “Where do you think we should go, oh Great Themberchaud?” 


As I guessed, he has no idea what to do. He pouts, “Well, I have many agents, and they have many places to look. You're my most recent agents, and you've been out and about for a few days. Where do you think we should be looking for information?


I again insist that we should go to the Dark Lake District, and again, he seems unconvinced. It’s his ego. 


“Are you suggesting I am missing something important?” 


Grygum shouts, “You’re not one of the peons who understands the little things. But we are as little as you get, adn we can find the little things that are big for us.” 


Zalthir, having seen abbots like this dragon, who just want a face-saving gesture, shouts, “There was an attack on the docks, so there might be new intelligence. Your agents didn’t miss it; they just haven’t been there recently.” 


The dragon agrees and tries to make a turn. But he’s fast, and his belly rolls instead of gliding, and then I am falling. 


Not for long, that’s what Misty Step is for. 


And as I look down, I notice Grygum and Thorin falling. Zalthir has sprouted wings. He catches Thorin. 


The ghoul Jimjar shouts, “Anyone want to make a bet that Grygum is going to survive?” 


Grygum seems to shout, “I’ll take the bet.” 


And it would have been a good one for him to take. Jimjar throws a rope and catches the falling orc. And then the group pulls him onto the dragon, which is frantically trying to catch his agent. 


### Thorin 


The flight on the back of a dragon is fun. But I agree with Zalthir that we really should have tied ourselves up rather than just sit on Themberchaud’s back. 


Just then, the Dragon shouts, “Oh, wait, there’s a forge that needs some fire!” 


I'm not sure if we should do this, but Themberchaud looks excited. And he approaches the forge and shouts, “I am Themberchaud the Wyrmsmith, bringer of flame!” 


And he breathes fire. 


Daz turns to me, “The Duergar are rolling their eyes. They don’t take him as seriously as he takes himself.”


I nod. 


And then Zalthir sounds a little bit more worried, “They recognized us. Look at their panic. They weren’t afraid of Themberchaud but when they saw Daz, they suddenly freaked out. They think we are the Ember Vanguard.” 


“So what do we do?” 


“I don’t know. But I can see many of the Duergar rushing around as if they are trying to send warnings.” 


I look down. It is as Zalthir said. The Duergar were unafraid of Themberchaud, but now they are pointing in our direction and moving frantically. 


“What do we do?”


Zalthir looks at me, “They think we are this all-powerful group; we take advantage of it.” 


Themberchaud has not heard a word we said and shouts, “Alright, one more flyby and to the docks!” 


### Zalthir


As we land on the docks, everyone flees except one lonely Duergar Stone Guard who stands in front of us. 


Gartokkar has warned us that everyone is on edge. The Stone Guard is looking for the Ember Vanguard, the powerful mercenary group, and the Grey Ghosts are looking for the Ember Vanguard, and the Deep King is worried. And now that we have come off of Themberchaud, this poor guard must think we are the Ember Vanguard. 


He clears his throat and tries to hide the quaver, “Halt in the name of the Deep King!” 


That’s a surprise. The guard didn’t think we are the Ember Vanguard. Maybe we can get out of here without bloodshed.  


Before I can react, Grygum says that they come in Thembersham’s name. 


The guard still refuses to let us pass. 


“Shall I recall him?” asks the cleric. 


I stand next to Grygum to make myself look as scary as possible. And we fail. The guard is unimpressed, but then he looks at a poster of the Ember Vanguard, and whispers, “I know you, you are the Ember Vanguard! I must warn Captain Errde at once!” 


We have to kill him. As I explain, “In the interest of avoiding mass bloodshed, I think we must.” 


Thorin mutters an old giant saying, “To save the village, you must smash the village.” 


Grygum walks up to the guard and whispers that we are impersonators trying to flush out the Ember Vanguard. “We are not them, but we are on a vital mission.” 


Thorin turns to Grygum, “Deputize them!” 


Grygum nods and pulls out a caltrop, “This is a symbol of our mission. Guard it and keep it secret.” 


The Duergar takes the caltrop and then runs off. 


The cleric has bought us some time. 


We jump into a large boat and head out. As we head out we ponder what we have accomplished. We came to find equipment, and we did that. And as Daz said, we betrayed many people. 


But the other truth is that we didn’t betray the city of Gracklstugh. The secrets we carried - of the Deep King’s plots, the corrupted dragon egg, and the lurking demonic plots - could have provoked mass death and destruction. And we took those secrets with us. The people of Gracklstugh owe us, even if they are unaware of it. 


I find it comforting to know that. 


As we leave Gracklstugh, we debate where to go next. 


Rump-a-dump and Stool want to go to the Neverlight Grove. I explain that there is a very dangerous and mighty demon there, and that we might not want to go there at all. 


Rump-a-dump looks at me, or at least I think he does, “Zalthir, the circle cannot be corrupted. “Our sovereign would never let that happen.” 


I want to explain to him how wrong he is, but then I remember he is a child. 


Sarith insists that we must go there as well. The strange compulsion that drew him to the dancing Myconids in the Whorlstone Caverns draws him to the grove. 


Thorin, having avoided one gift from a demon, seems to want to go again. I shake my head, “You had your chance, don’t you think we shouldn’t try again?”


But then it struck me that Thorin will roll the dice and make strange decisions. And I grit my fangs and mutter, “Perhaps we could first go to the Lost Tomb of Khaem, secure whatever weapon it has, and then go?” 


They all nod. 


Just as we are about to set sail, Jimjar says something bizarre - "I'll bet you an empty shell gem that this circle of joy will reveal itself to be a circle of unwilling vows before you leave it.”


Great, a wedding involving demons and mushrooms. 


I hope no one will take him up on his weird bet, but Grygum does. 


## 4th-6th day of the 2nd tenday of Myrtul 1493


### Thorin 


The first two days are uneventful. There is the usual tedium of the rowing and the darkness and the strange sounds and cries. 


The third day brings excitement when some bloodsucking giant mosquitoes fall from the roof. 


They are killed very quickly. 


I like giants, but not giant mosquitoes.


## 7th day of the 2nd tenday of Myrtul 1493


On the fourth day, Sarith announces that we must get out on a beach and head deeper into the Underdark on foot. 


He explains, “The path to the Neverlight Grove goes past the Lost Tomb of Khaem. But we must leave the boat here.” 


Errde will have probably told the Drow that we came through her city, and they will once again be pursuing us. 


Leaving the boat is like leaving a sign. But we might need it later. 


Gyrgum shouts, “We could scuttle it.” 


I stare at him, perplexed. 


“I can cast mending.” 


Eldeth looks at him, “Really?” 


I look at her, confused. 


“Well, spell casters who could cast mending were in high demand by the navies. And so spellcasters tended to avoid learning that spell. See, in naval combat, the first person you kill is the mender. Because until you kill him, you can’t sink the ship. So finding a spell caster who can cast it is rare.”
