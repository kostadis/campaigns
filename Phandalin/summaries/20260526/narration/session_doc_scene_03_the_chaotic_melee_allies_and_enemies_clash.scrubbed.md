The elk form is not quiet. My hooves were loud on the packed earth, and Gorthok was louder still — that smell of ozone and wet fur and something older than either of those things, the boar's breath like a storm system in miniature. I held the Moonbeam where it needed to be, tracked it across the churned ground to where the great beast stood and where Anchorite Number 2 had planted himself, and I felt the light do its work. Both of them flinched. Neither stayed flinching long enough. The radiance found them and they shook it off — a glancing sting for each — and Gorthok shed it the way a stone wall absorbs a thrown cup.

Then Prutha ran.

He came out of the press of bodies like something that had been waiting for permission, and the orc he found in the cultist line was, from the sound of it, someone he recognized. The Orcish came out in a torrent — guttural, specific, clearly anatomical in its imagery. Vukradin turned to Valphine the way he always turns to Valphine when the world becomes briefly incomprehensible.

"Valphine, you'll have to translate for us."

Valphine considered this for the length of a breath.

"They're greeting each other in traditional orchest form."

Vukradin watched them swing swords at one another with what appeared to be genuine enthusiasm. "Pleasant for swinging swords at each other, but okay, okay, makes sense, I guess."

I did not disagree with Valphine's translation. It was tactful, and in a melee of this size, tact was the only resource that didn't bleed you dry.

Vukradin tried a single word on Gorthok — a command, the kind that should have folded the beast's knees and pressed its snout into the mud. I respected the attempt: a creature of divine storm fury, knelt in the dirt by a syllable, would have been something worth seeing. Gorthok did not kneel. Vukradin's composure, brief as a summer cloud, gave way to a single syllable of his own: *"Asshole."*

I nudged him. "Can't you, the barbed hex that makes luck turn or something, or—"

He was already checking. "Let me see, actually, yeah, there's— do we have the luck to spare? Who's holding that?" A pause. "Nobody is."

The battlefield had that quality of things accelerating beyond anyone's ability to choreograph them. The orc lines had dissolved into individual hatreds, Prutha's voice rising above the rest whenever he found something new to gut. I dragged the Moonbeam — *my* Moonbeam, tethered to my wakefulness, which required me to remain conscious, which was becoming an increasingly optimistic assumption — back over Gorthok, repositioned Boney through the worst of it to keep the hush settled over the Anchorites. Inside that bubble, the cultists' spells simply stopped at the edge of their mouths. Boney moved through it all without fear. I have always appreciated that about him.

The follow-up hoof took Gorthok square. A real hit, felt through the bristled hide, and I let myself feel that for exactly one moment — *"Get hooved, baby"* — before calculating that I had torn something serious out of the beast and he was still standing and still furious and still looking at me.

Valphine took Anchorite Number 1's sight from him. One moment the cultist was oriented, tracking threats, computing angles. Then Valphine's hand moved and something closed behind his eyes and he stood there in the middle of a melee with no useful darkness to retreat into, just the sound of everything coming from everywhere at once. She said, with the particular serenity she reserves for moments of someone else's suffering: "I feel the power of Lathander."

I watched the Anchorite do nothing. Not flinch, not flee — *nothing*, which in a battle is its own category of panic.

"Did he just take your sacrament?" I asked her.

"He did."

He recovered his sight at the end of it — the blindness shook loose — but he'd lost his moment, and in a fight this tangled, moments were the only currency.

Gorthok hit me twice.

The first blow landed and I thought: *I'm not long for this world.* The lightning that followed was just confirming the conclusion. I registered where I stood — worn down, accumulated hits, a margin that felt nearly nothing against something like Gorthok — and then the second strike came in and my elk form simply stopped.

I came back as myself. Tortle shell, leather armor, Meril's staff somewhere on the ground near me, five leaves, none of which could reach Gorthok from the dirt. The Moonbeam went out. That was what I heard Vukradin process in real time: *"No, no more Moonbeam."*

"Yep," I said. "Moonbeam goes away."

Gorthok ended his advance just outside where the beam had been. A margin so thin it was almost absurd — the beast had knocked out my wakefulness with enough precision, accidental or otherwise, that he would not immediately burn.

"Oh, that's so close," Valphine said.

She sounded almost impressed. I was flat on the ground. I would take her impressed.

Around me the orc melee continued its ugly arithmetic. The allied line and the cultist line traded blows in the mass way that only works when there are enough bodies to sustain losses — two allied orcs went down to a concussive burst thrown from outside the hush, a third knocked back hard. The Anchorite outside the quiet zone could still cast. That was the gap in the plan — always had been. Boney had covered the ones inside. The one outside had stepped clear and used the space.

I lay there and listened to the sounds of the fight — Prutha's voice, the concussive push of that burst, Vukradin noting the gap in the coverage with characteristic precision — and I did a quiet accounting of what I had left.

Not much. But I was still inside it. A hollow shell of my former, yes. Still here.
