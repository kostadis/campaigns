The last orc went down before I'd finished the swing. The halberd still had momentum, so I let it carry — cleaved into the next one standing, felt that connect too, kept moving. Enlarged, I covered the ground between targets in two strides. The courtyard of Falcon's Lodge had gone from impossible to manageable somewhere in the last few minutes and I was too deep in the work to notice exactly when.

There was one Anchorite left.

He had almost nothing left in him. I could see it — the way he was still on his feet at all was more stubbornness than capability. But before I could close the distance, Vukradin did something I hadn't expected.

"Yeah, let's do, Phantasmal Force on the remaining anchorite."

I don't know the mechanics of what Vukradin does. I know what I see: the man raises his hand, says words, and the world changes shape. What formed in front of the Anchorite was something vast and blinding. Not a warrior. Not a monster. A god. The god, the sun one, Valphine's and Vukradin's — rendered in whatever light lives inside a bard's head when he reaches for it.

The Anchorite broke.

Not like a soldier breaks, deciding the fight's lost. He broke the other way, the worse way — the inside-out kind where everything you thought you believed turns on you all at once. He started screaming. The words came in pieces, half prayer, half confession.

"She's real! The drows, she's real! Talos, my god! Talos!"

And then, worse:

"I burned the shrine! I broke the handles! I did what you asked! Why is he here? Why is he here?"

I didn't know what shrine. I didn't know what handles. I didn't need to. The man had done terrible things for a god who wasn't answering, and now he was watching a different god arrive in his place, and whatever bargain he thought he'd struck had come apart completely. I've known that feeling. Not the god part. The other part — the moment you understand that everything you sacrificed was for something that was never going to hold.

Valphine said something quiet that I didn't fully catch. Something about needling. She sounded satisfied.

Falcon hit the other anchorite three times in a row. I registered this only peripherally — a shout, the ring of a longsword connecting, connecting again, and then again. He'd been missing all night. Some fights work that way. The moment arrives and suddenly the man who couldn't hit a stationary target finds everything at once. "This is like, you know, the defining moment of the Falcon, right?" Eighteen points of damage between three swings, and I believed it. I'd watched him touch his brass rings before he charged, mutter something to someone who wasn't there. I didn't ask. You don't ask about brass rings.

Prutha put two spear strikes into a prone Anchorite. Both landed. The orc chief hit hard and kept her feet steady. Valphine said "pretty effective" with that tone she has, the one that means she is filing something away.

And then it was my turn.

The last Anchorite — the one Vukradin had broken open — had almost nothing left. Two hits of anything and he was finished, and I had a halberd that was still live with acid and arms that were twice their normal size and the kind of anger that builds slow during a long siege and doesn't care that the fight is almost over. I crossed to him.

Thirteen points of damage. That's what I rolled before I knew the number wouldn't matter.

"He only had 2 hit points, he's dead."

I rolled the final die anyway. Out of principle. The number came up and I noted it and it meant nothing because the Anchorite was already falling, but it felt wrong not to finish the accounting. A kill deserves the full count. That's the only ceremony I have.

The courtyard went quiet. Not silent — there was still the sound of breathing and rain and Falcon somewhere behind me, probably touching his rings again — but the noise of the fight was gone. The thunder Gorthok had carried with him had followed him into death, and what was left was just ordinary night air. Cold. Slightly smoky from whatever Valphine had done earlier.

I stood there with the halberd and waited for the feeling to arrange itself into something I could put a name to. It didn't. It just sat in my chest, the way it always does after — too large for the body, looking for somewhere to go.
