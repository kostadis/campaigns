The silence hit first. Not quiet — silence. The difference matters. Quiet is birdsong gone, fire crackling low, the lodge settling in the dark. Silence is nothing. A wall of nothing, centered on Boney's saddle, moving with the dead horse as Valphine's pale fingers pointed the way.

I'd fought in noise my whole life. Tribal drums, war-cries, the crack of axes on shields. The silence was wrong in my body before I understood it in my head.

But the Anchorites understood it faster. They knew what the quiet meant.

Soma had her moonbeam running — the white cylinder of it dropping through the roof of the world like a hole punched in the sky, forcing the nearest shapeshifters back into their own skin. You can't hold a stolen form through that much radiance, apparently. I watched one of them stagger out of the beam as himself, whatever that meant for a man who'd sold his shape to a storm-god. He looked smaller. Confused. Easier to hit.

I filed that away.

The orcs we'd charmed — Vukradin's work, the quiet diplomatic machinery I appreciated without understanding — were still standing in a loose cluster near the fence, looking at nothing in particular. Vukradin's voice cut through the yard, not loud, just present: "As a free action, I will remind all of our allies that to not attack the hypnotized characters. As long as they are hypnotized, they will remain hypnotized."

I heard him. Smart. Don't break the spell by startling them.

Then the un-charmed orcs woke their friends up.

Shaking. That's all it took. A hand on a shoulder, and ten of Vukradin's carefully persuaded men shook their heads and came back to themselves and to the wrong side of the fight. Three of them, gone just like that. Vukradin made a sound I didn't have a word for.

"F," he said eventually. "Fine. I didn't need 3rd level spells anyway."

I understood the feeling. I'd lost things to bad luck too. Usually more blood than magic.

The lightning came from the side I wasn't watching.

An Anchorite outside Boney's silence-bubble did something with her hands and the sky cracked open sideways, a bolt traveling low and vicious across the yard. I heard it before I felt it — that particular snap that isn't sound so much as the air deciding to be somewhere else. It caught me and Falcon both. I took it in the left shoulder, through the scale mail, and the heat spread in a way that made my teeth ache. Not enough to drop me. I'd felt worse from Cryovain's breath and walked. But it stole a step from me, and I needed every step I had.

Falcon fired twice at the nearest Anchorite while I got my footing back. One arrow hit, clean and hard. The second skipped wide. He didn't comment on it.

Then Gorthok stopped trying to breathe lightning and just used its tusks.

I watched it hit Soma from forty feet away and couldn't do a damn thing. The first strike folded her. The second made sure. The tusks were the color of the inside of a cloud during a real storm — that pale blue-white that means the ground is next — and every hit came with a sound like a bell struck by something too heavy for it. Deep. Wrong. The kind of thunder that doesn't roll; it lands.

Soma went down. She reverted as she fell. Just a tortle again, shell and all, crumpling onto the churned mud of Falcon's yard.

I didn't think about it. There wasn't room to think about it. You grieve later.

I raged.

It comes from somewhere I don't have good words for. Below the ribs. Below thought. My exile tribe had a word for it — a thing I'm not supposed to say now — and the Uthgardt understanding was that you borrowed something from a bigger version of yourself, from the self you'd be if the world were sized right for you. Whatever the truth of it, I felt myself get taller. The reach on the halberd doubled. Everything within twenty feet of me was suddenly within arm's reach.

The Elemental Cleaver. I'd taken it from a dead man, which Vukradin would not like if he thought about it, but he'd never asked and I'd never offered. What mattered now was that the blade bit acid when it wanted to, and tonight it wanted to.

First swing. The halberd came down and across and the Anchorite took it across the collarbone — the acid hit a moment after the steel, a hiss, a smell like burning copper. He staggered. I could see the margins on him. Not much left.

Second swing. Eleven. Miss.

I heard Vukradin: "Only if it fails. But yeah, roll the D4 first."

The Bardic Inspiration was there waiting. I used it.

The halberd hit on the second try, lower, harder, and the Anchorite folded the way things fold when they're done — not falling so much as releasing. Nine points of damage in a single blow was enough to finish what the acid had started.

"9 puts it down? Okay," I heard behind me.

Yeah. Okay.

The silence pressed around Boney's moving shape at the far end of the yard, a bubble of nothing in the middle of a battle that couldn't stop being loud. Lightning above. Thunder in the earth from Gorthok's hooves. Soma's staff on the ground where she'd dropped it, the fifth leaf still breathing in the storm.

I moved to the next target. That's all there is to it, when you're in it. You move to the next one.
