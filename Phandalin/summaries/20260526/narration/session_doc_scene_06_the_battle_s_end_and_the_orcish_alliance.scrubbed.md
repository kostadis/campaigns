The boar was dead. The orcs were not.

Two of them broke from the yard at a run — heading for the treeline, heading away — and Falcon was already reaching for his bow before the dust had settled. I watched him nock and draw and loose with the kind of efficiency that has nothing generous in it.

"Such senseless violence," I said. Nobody heard me, or nobody cared to.

Vukradin looked at me, something in his expression that wasn't quite a challenge. "How's that douchey?"

"It's unnecessary."

"If you don't kill them, they're just gonna come back," Valphine said, and I recognized the voice she uses when she isn't really arguing — when she's simply offering the logic of a world she grew up in, underground, where nothing that fled came back a friend.

I let it sit for a moment. "There's a well of orcs," I said. "There are always more orcs beyond the horizon."

Nobody agreed with me, but nobody shot any more of them either, so I called it a draw.

Then Prutha moved.

I didn't see all of it. I saw enough. The axe came down with the kind of finality that has a sound, and I said "Guys, calm down" to no one in particular, and I said it again — "such senseless violence" — because saying it twice is the tortle equivalent of a strongly worded letter, and I had nothing else left.

Vorga was shouting. I couldn't parse all of it — my Orcish has never been good, and she was at full war-chief volume, which rattled the timbers. But the shape of it was clear enough. She was pleased. She was very pleased. She was gesturing at all of us with the kind of arm-sweep that means *mine*, and her warriors were echoing it back with a sound like a storm deciding to be happy instead.

We were members of the tribe. Honorary, apparently. Bloodlusty cheers rose and fell and rose again.

Valphine started singing. It took me a beat to recognize what — *The Hand Obould Offered*, the old orc-reconciliation legend, the one about the horde that didn't burn the feast-hall down, that sat at the table instead. I knew the song the way you know a story you were told to illustrate a point: distantly, with moderate skepticism.

Then Vukradin's voice joined in.

He'd said something first, half to himself — something about always thinking it was a cheap manipulative song, and here we were singing it and meaning every last word. I watched his face as he sang. He looked surprised at himself. I found that I appreciated that about him, in that moment. He knows when he's been caught being sincere.

I did not sing. But I stood in the yard with blood on my shell and a staff in my hand and listened to an aasimar bard and a drow cleric harmonize with an orc war party at dusk in the ruins of a siege, and I thought: well. Meril sent me out here. Maybe he knew something.

Falcon shouted "Huzzah!" with an enthusiasm that would have embarrassed him a week ago. He was beaming. He was the man who had, by his own recent account, survived by refusing to act — and now here he was, arm around an orc's shoulders, vibrating with the particular joy of someone who has discovered that intervention is actually quite fun when it works.

"He's gone fully the other way," Vukradin observed.

"That's what happens when you find a stranger in the Alps," I offered. I'm not sure anyone understood it, but it felt accurate.

"He has no real core convictions," Vukradin said, not unkindly.

Valphine was already talking to three of the orcs — a quiet word here, a gesture toward the lodge there. The proposal was clean: stay, work, be useful. Three of them agreed. Falcon immediately pivoted from "honorary tribesman" to "lord of the estate" with a speed that I found admirable in its completeness.

"The first thing we'll have to do is teach you how to dress properly," he told them. "The three of you will become the finest stewards in all the North."

"Now it's not my problem," Valphine said, with the particular satisfaction of someone who has just handed off a parcel.

Vukradin had already stopped listening. I watched him drift toward the guest house with the slow, purposeful movement of a man who has decided that the world can manage without him for a few hours.

"I've had kind of a long day," he said, generally, to the air. *"I threw everything I had at them, and most of it bounced. I'm kind of wandering back to the out. Like, finally we're done."*

He paused at the guest house door. "Don't steal any of that treasure. I'm gonna be napping."

"To steal their life, not their treasure," I said after him, which was advice for a different situation entirely, but he was already inside.

Brewbarry came up from the cellar with the expression of a man who has stepped on something he loved. The wine casks — or what remained of them. Staved. Emptied into the earth. The Anchorites' work, apparently, from before any of this began.

"Oh my god," he said. "I am depressed."

He stood there looking at the wreckage with a grief that I recognized as genuine. Brewbarry's sorrows are not small. He has the kind of heart that feels things at full size, and the loss of wine he had been anticipating — wine he had maybe, in some corner of himself, been counting on — sat on him visibly.

"I know Talos is the god of chaos," he said, after a moment. "But come on. Gotta be some limits."

I agreed with that more than anything else said in the last hour.

We talked about the Woodland Manse — empty now, presumably, its residents having just attempted to unmake us. The Boots of Elvenkind were somewhere in there. "Our fair, organic, fair-trade, conflict-free Boots of Elvenkind," Vukradin called them, through the guest house wall, apparently still conscious enough to register the conversation even while committed to his nap.

Valphine raised the question of Don Jon Raskin — the working that recalls a soul from the far side of death entire, she said, musing aloud about some creative ritual, some way to power it that didn't require a cooperative cleric. Her eyes had that particular quality they get when she's entertaining an idea she knows is irregular.

"Oh, boy," I said.

"Red Wizards of Lathander," Vukradin offered, from inside, half-joking or possibly entirely joking. With him you can't always tell.

And then something shifted — the kind of shift you feel before you can name it, a deepening, a new weight settling into the bones alongside the old ones. I sat down on a section of ruined fence and felt the staff settle against my knee, and I thought about what I had left in me and what I might now have access to that I didn't before.

"Surprising after that battle," I said, which wasn't entirely serious, but wasn't entirely not.

New depth. New reach. The staff hadn't grown another leaf — not yet — but I felt it, the way you feel weather coming in: a readiness, a potential, something that would need to be earned before it was given.

Brewbarry read something off a page and said *"going first — actually going first, for once, nice"* in the voice of a man who has been given exactly the gift he wanted and is trying not to look too pleased about it.

Vukradin listed his new workings with the quiet deliberation of a man shopping at a market: *the art of becoming someone else's shadow, the art of rewriting a body entire, the door that opens onto elsewhere.*

"Elsewhere is always nice," I said, about the last one.

It was full dusk now. The lodge behind us was lit from within, the new stewards presumably being lectured about collar starching or something of similar urgency. Somewhere across the yard, Boney stood patient and skeletal and unbothered by any of it, as was his custom.

I stayed where I was for a while. The staff was warm in my hand. The night was coming in.
