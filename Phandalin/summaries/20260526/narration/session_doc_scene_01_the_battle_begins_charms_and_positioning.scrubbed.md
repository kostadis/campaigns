The enchantment broke over the battlefield like a held breath finally released.

I watched Vukradin's working roll out across the enemy lines — not with my eyes, exactly, but with that deeper Drow sense that has never fully left me, the one that reads power in a room the way other people read weather. The hypnosis spread through the massed orcs like oil across stone, and I counted the ones who went still, the ones whose eyes softened into that particular vacancy. Ten. Then the others — six who shook the working loose, Gorthok among them, the giant boar standing at the heart of the courtyard with its flanks sheened in static electricity and absolutely no interest in becoming anyone's favorite.

Vukradin issued his warning as though it were the most obvious courtesy in the world.

"I'll take a moment to remind all of our allies not to attack the hypnotized ones."

He said it pleasantly. He always says things pleasantly. "As long as they are hypnotized, they will remain hypnotized." A pause for the slower comprehensions in the room. "It's not like sleep, they can't wake up. The only thing is that somebody else could wake them." Another pause. "If they took the time to do so."

I have heard him deliver this kind of tactical footnote a dozen times in a dozen engagements — the bard's instinct to make sure everyone understands the rules before the game begins. It is, I think, genuinely generous. It is also, if I am honest, a little deliciously maddening to wait through.

Then someone placed the heart markers on the charmed tokens, and Vukradin read them with immediate warmth.

"They're ours! They love us!"

Soma, without looking up: "They're hypnotized."

There is a theology in that exchange, I thought — not that either of them would recognize it. The difference between compelled affection and genuine devotion. Lathander is interested in both, in different ways.

Vukradin counted the tokens. He got to nine, then ten, then squinted at the map.

"Okay, what about, so you said 6? 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9… I count 10."

The clarification came. Six uncharmed, ten charmed. He confirmed it twice, counting again with the deliberate care of a man who wants to be absolutely certain before he permits himself disappointment.

"I was hoping for more, but alright. Alright."

Soma — patient, dry, tortle-patient — offered the obvious comfort: "I mean, you got 6 and 10, that's pretty decent. Alright, well, you know what's coming."

She meant Gorthok. We all meant Gorthok.

"But the giant boar is not charmed?"

No, it was not. It had thrown off the working with what I imagined was the boar equivalent of contempt, and now it stood in the uncharmed six like a problem that had volunteered itself. There was a brief discussion about whether silence would suppress its lightning — and here the news was genuinely interesting. Its attack was not a spell. It was something older, more reflexive, a thing the creature simply did the way dragons breathe. Silence wouldn't touch it.

Which meant silence needed a different target.

I had already been thinking about Boney.

"My plan is to work a hush onto Boney." I considered this. "Or onto Boney's saddle, or something like that."

Vukradin, quick as always: "Oh, you're gonna ride the ward into their casters?"

"No — I mean, they both ask the same thing of me at once, so no, I can't."

A beat. He reassembled the actual plan.

"No, I mean, you're gonna… you're gonna lay the working on Boney and then move him around to carry that hush around the… the area."

"Yes."

"Strategy, I like that."

I do appreciate when he recognizes a thing for what it is. In Menzoberranzan, a moving pocket of silence was a weapon as much as an architecture — you used it to shut down the spellcasters before they understood they had been shut down. The anchorites here were not Lolth-priestesses, but the principle was the same. Stillness on a moving mount, looped around the back wall to avoid drawing opportunity strikes, positioned over the anchorites like a jar over a candle. No workings out. No workings in.

"I was thinking he would cover the anchorites anyway."

The route was clean once I traced it. Boney had ground to spare. "And I was measuring that he could, you know, loop around the back here and not incur any attacks, because he has — he could push hard and cover the full length of it."

The silence token went down on the map, and immediately everyone disagreed about what they were seeing. Soma saw the yellow circle. No one else did. Vukradin saw a yellow dot. Soma saw a purple circle. There was a brief, very earnest conference about map visibility that I found both charming and slightly absurd — here we were, on the edge of a Talosian siege, and the main tactical discussion concerned what colour circle indicated holy silence on a skeletal horse.

I know Boney well enough by now. The horse has no particular feelings about being enchanted. It simply goes where directed, which is all any vessel is asked to do.

Soma tracked the movement carefully, muttering the geometry aloud — through the door, across the threshold, then the long arc around the back wall to position the bubble over the anchorites. "Alright, so that's a full push."

And Vukradin, perhaps not fully parsing the distinction between tactical movement and other kinds of finality, said: "Yeah, he definitely has to die."

Soma corrected him without inflection. "To get back there without drawing a strike, yeah, that's a full push."

The boar stood in the yard with its static flanks and its intact will, and I watched an anchorite read the approaching silence, understand what it meant, and break toward the edge of the zone — not brave, just calculating. Boney was there with reach and the right angle.

The attack of opportunity opened like a gift.

Soma watched the result. "I think he whiffed."

The bone swung wide. The anchorite made it out of the zone.

I noted his face. I have a good memory for faces.
