# Chapter 42 The Aasimar has Landed. 


---

## Valphine — The Battle Begins — Charms and Positioning

The enchantment broke across the enemy line, and the field sorted itself into two columns: the ones it took, and the ones it didn't.

I watched Vukradin's working roll out across the enemy lines — not with my eyes, exactly, but with that deeper Drow sense that has never fully left me, the one that reads power in a room the way other people read weather. The hypnosis spread through the massed orcs like oil across stone, and I counted the ones who went still, the ones whose eyes softened into that particular vacancy. Ten. Then the others — six who shook the working loose, Gorthok among them, the giant boar standing at the heart of the courtyard with its flanks sheened in static electricity and absolutely no interest in becoming anyone's favorite.

Vukradin issued his warning as though it were the most obvious courtesy in the world.

"I'll take a moment to remind all of our allies not to attack the hypnotized ones."

He said it pleasantly. He always says things pleasantly. "As long as they are hypnotized, they will remain hypnotized." A pause for the slower comprehensions in the room. "It's not like sleep, they can't wake up. The only thing is that somebody else could wake them." Another pause. "If they took the time to do so."

I have heard him deliver this kind of tactical footnote a dozen times in a dozen engagements — the bard's instinct to make sure everyone understands the rules before the game begins. It is, I think, genuinely generous. It is also, if I am honest, a thing I have learned to wait through without comment.

Then someone placed the heart markers on the charmed tokens, and Vukradin read them with immediate warmth.

"They're ours! They love us!"

Soma, without looking up: "They're hypnotized."

There is a theology in that exchange, I thought — not that either of them would recognize it. The difference between compelled affection and genuine devotion. Lathander is interested in both, in different ways.

Vukradin counted the tokens. He got to nine, then ten, then squinted at the map.

"Okay, what about, so you said 6? 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9… I count 10."

The clarification came. Six uncharmed, ten charmed. He confirmed it twice, counting again with the deliberate care of a man who wants to be absolutely certain before he permits himself disappointment.

"I was hoping for more, but alright. Alright."

Soma — patient, dry, tortle-patient — offered the obvious comfort: "I mean, you got 6 and 10, that's pretty decent. Alright, well, you know what's coming."

She meant Gorthok. We all meant Gorthok.

"But the giant boar is not charmed?"

No, it was not. It had thrown off the working with what I imagined was the boar equivalent of contempt, and now it stood in the uncharmed six like a problem that had volunteered itself. There was a brief discussion about whether silence would suppress its lightning — and here the news was genuinely interesting. Its attack was not a spell. It was something older, more reflexive, a thing the creature simply did the way dragons breathe. Silence wouldn't touch it.

Which meant silence needed a different target.

I had already been thinking about Boney.

"My plan is to work a hush onto Boney." I considered this. "Or onto Boney's saddle, or something like that."

Vukradin, quick as always: "Oh, you're gonna ride the ward into their casters?"

"No — I mean, they both ask the same thing of me at once, so no, I can't."

A beat. He reassembled the actual plan.

"No, I mean, you're gonna… you're gonna lay the working on Boney and then move him around to carry that hush around the… the area."

"Yes."

"Strategy, I like that."

I do appreciate when he recognizes a thing for what it is. In Menzoberranzan, a moving pocket of silence was a weapon as much as an architecture — you used it to shut down the spellcasters before they understood they had been shut down. The anchorites here were not Lolth-priestesses, but the principle was the same. Stillness on a moving mount, looped around the back wall to avoid drawing opportunity strikes, positioned over the anchorites like a jar over a candle. No workings out. No workings in.

"I was thinking he would cover the anchorites anyway."

The route was clean once I traced it. Boney had ground to spare. "And I was measuring that he could, you know, loop around the back here and not incur any attacks, because he has — he could push hard and cover the full length of it."

The silence token went down on the map, and immediately everyone disagreed about what they were seeing. Soma saw the yellow circle. No one else did. Vukradin saw a yellow dot. Soma saw a purple circle. There was a brief, very earnest conference about map visibility — here we were, on the edge of a Talosian siege, and the entire tactical discussion turned on what colour circle indicated holy silence on a skeletal horse. In Menzoberranzan, councils died over less.

I know Boney well enough by now. The horse has no particular feelings about being enchanted. It simply goes where directed, which is all any vessel is asked to do.

Soma tracked the movement carefully, muttering the geometry aloud — through the door, across the threshold, then the long arc around the back wall to position the bubble over the anchorites. "Alright, so that's a full push."

And Vukradin, perhaps not fully parsing the distinction between tactical movement and other kinds of finality, said: "Yeah, he definitely has to die."

Soma corrected him without inflection. "To get back there without drawing a strike, yeah, that's a full push."

The boar stood in the yard with its static flanks and its intact will, and I watched an anchorite read the approaching silence, understand what it meant, and break toward the edge of the zone — not brave, just calculating. Boney was there with reach and the right angle.

The attack of opportunity opened like a gift.

Soma watched the result. "I think he whiffed."

The bone swung wide. The anchorite made it out of the zone.

I noted his face. I have a good memory for faces.

## Brewbarry — The Battle at the Lodge — Silence and Lightning

The silence hit first. Not quiet — silence. The difference matters. Quiet is birdsong gone, fire crackling low, the lodge settling in the dark. Silence is nothing. A wall of nothing, centered on Boney's saddle, moving with the dead horse as Valphine's pale fingers pointed the way.

I'd fought in noise my whole life. Tribal drums, war-cries, the crack of axes on shields. The silence was wrong in my body before I understood it in my head.

But the Anchorites understood it faster. They knew what the quiet meant.

Soma had her pillar of light running — the white column of it dropping through the roof of the world like a hole punched in the sky, forcing the nearest shapeshifters back into their own skin. You can't hold a stolen form through that much radiance, apparently. I watched one of them stagger out of the beam as himself, whatever that meant for a man who'd sold his shape to a storm-god. He looked smaller. Confused. Easier to hit.

I filed that away.

The orcs we'd charmed — Vukradin's work, the quiet diplomatic machinery I appreciated without understanding — were still standing in a loose cluster near the fence, looking at nothing in particular. Vukradin's voice cut through the yard, not loud, just present: *"I will say it plainly to every ally in earshot — leave the persuaded ones alone. Touch them and we lose them."*

I heard him. Smart. Don't break the spell by startling them.

Then the un-charmed orcs woke their friends up.

Shaking. That's all it took. A hand on a shoulder, and ten of Vukradin's carefully persuaded men shook their heads and came back to themselves and to the wrong side of the fight. Three of them, gone just like that. Vukradin made a sound I didn't have a word for.

*"F,"* he said eventually. *"Fine. I didn't need those workings anyway."*

I understood the feeling. I'd lost things to bad luck too. Usually more blood than magic.

The lightning came from the side I wasn't watching.

An Anchorite outside Boney's silence-bubble did something with her hands and the sky cracked open sideways, a bolt traveling low and vicious across the yard. I heard it before I felt it — that particular snap that lands more like a blow than a sound. It caught me and Falcon both. I took it in the left shoulder, through the scale mail, and the heat spread in a way that made my teeth ache. Not enough to drop me. I'd felt worse from Cryovain's breath and walked. But it stole a step from me, and I needed every step I had.

Falcon fired twice at the nearest Anchorite while I got my footing back. One arrow hit, clean and hard. The second skipped wide. He didn't comment on it.

Then Gorthok stopped trying to breathe lightning and just used its tusks.

I watched it hit Soma from across the yard and couldn't do a damn thing. The first strike folded her. The second made sure. The tusks were the color of the inside of a cloud during a real storm — that pale blue-white that means the ground is next — and every hit came with a sound too deep for the thing that made it. Deep. Wrong. The kind of thunder that doesn't roll; it lands.

Soma went down. She reverted as she fell. Just a tortle again, shell and all, crumpling onto the churned mud of Falcon's yard.

I didn't think about it. There wasn't room to think about it. You grieve later.

I raged.

It comes from somewhere I don't have good words for. Below the ribs. Below thought. My exile tribe had a word for it — a thing I'm not supposed to say now — and the Uthgardt understanding was that you borrowed something from a bigger version of yourself, from the self you'd be if the world were sized right for you. Whatever the truth of it, I felt myself get taller. The reach on the halberd doubled. Everything that had been a stride away was suddenly within arm's reach.

The Elemental Cleaver. I'd taken it from a dead man, which Vukradin would not like if he thought about it, but he'd never asked and I'd never offered. What mattered now was that the blade bit acid when it wanted to, and tonight it wanted to.

First swing. The halberd came down and across and the Anchorite took it across the collarbone — the acid hit a moment after the steel, a hiss, a smell like burning copper. He staggered. I could see the margins on him. Not much left.

Second swing. Short. The blade went wide.

I heard Vukradin: *"Only if it fails. But go on — call on it."*

The confidence he'd lent me was there waiting, a word spoken at the right moment doing what words sometimes do. I used it.

The halberd hit on the second try, lower, harder, and the Anchorite folded the way things fold when they're done — not falling so much as releasing. A blow that would have shaken a lesser man was enough to finish what the acid had started.

*"That puts it down? Okay,"* I heard behind me.

Yeah. Okay.

The silence pressed around Boney's moving shape at the far end of the yard, a bubble of nothing in the middle of a battle that couldn't stop being loud. Lightning above. Thunder in the earth from Gorthok's hooves. Soma's staff on the ground where she'd dropped it, the fifth leaf still breathing in the storm.

I moved to the next target. That's all there is to it, when you're in it. You move to the next one.

## Soma — The Chaotic Melee — Allies and Enemies Clash

The elk form is not quiet. My hooves were loud on the packed earth, and Gorthok was louder still — that smell of ozone and wet fur and something older than either of those things, the boar's breath like a storm system in miniature. I held the Moonbeam where it needed to be, tracked it across the churned ground to where the great beast stood and where Anchorite Number 2 had planted himself, and I felt the light do its work. Both of them flinched. Neither stayed flinching long enough. The radiance found them and they shook it off — a glancing sting for each — and Gorthok shed it the way a stone wall absorbs a thrown cup.

Then Prutha ran.

He came out of the press of bodies like something that had been waiting for permission, and the orc he found in the cultist line was, from the sound of it, someone he recognized. The Orcish came out in a torrent — guttural, specific, clearly anatomical in its imagery. Vukradin turned to Valphine the way he always turns to Valphine when the world becomes briefly incomprehensible.

"Valphine, you'll have to translate for us."

Valphine considered this for the length of a breath.

"They're greeting each other in traditional orchest form."

Vukradin watched them swing swords at one another with what appeared to be genuine enthusiasm. "Pleasant for swinging swords at each other, but okay, okay, makes sense, I guess."

I did not disagree with Valphine's translation. It was tactful, and in a melee of this size, tact was the only resource that didn't bleed you dry.

Vukradin tried a single word on Gorthok — a command, the kind that should have folded the beast's knees and pressed its snout into the mud. I respected the attempt: a creature of divine storm fury, knelt in the dirt by a syllable, would have been something worth seeing. Gorthok did not kneel. Vukradin's composure, brief as a summer cloud, gave way to a single syllable of his own: *"Asshole."*

I nudged him. "Can't you, the barbed hex that makes luck turn or something, or—"

He was already checking. "Let me see, actually, yeah, there's— do we have the luck to spare? Who's holding that?" A pause. "Nobody is."

The battlefield had reached the point a storm reaches when no one is steering it anymore. The orc lines had dissolved into individual hatreds, Prutha's voice rising above the rest whenever he found something new to gut. I dragged the Moonbeam — *my* Moonbeam, tethered to my wakefulness, which required me to remain conscious, which was becoming an increasingly optimistic assumption — back over Gorthok, repositioned Boney through the worst of it to keep the hush settled over the Anchorites. Inside that bubble, the cultists' spells simply stopped at the edge of their mouths. Boney moved through it all without fear. I have always appreciated that about him.

The follow-up hoof took Gorthok square. A real hit, felt through the bristled hide, and I let myself feel that for exactly one moment — *"Get hooved, baby"* — before calculating that I had torn something serious out of the beast and he was still standing and still furious and still looking at me.

Valphine took Anchorite Number 1's sight from him. One moment the cultist was oriented, tracking threats, computing angles. Then Valphine's hand moved and something closed behind his eyes and he stood there in the middle of a melee with no useful darkness to retreat into, just the sound of everything coming from everywhere at once. She said it the way she does when the one hurting is someone else — quiet, exact, unbothered: "I feel the power of Lathander."

I watched the Anchorite do nothing. Not flinch, not flee — *nothing*, which in a battle is its own category of panic.

"Did he just take your sacrament?" I asked her.

"He did."

He recovered his sight at the end of it — the blindness shook loose — but he'd lost his moment, and in a fight this tangled, moments were the only currency.

Gorthok hit me twice.

The first blow landed and I thought: *I'm not long for this world.* The lightning that followed was just confirming the conclusion. I registered where I stood — worn down, accumulated hits, a margin that felt nearly nothing against something like Gorthok — and then the second strike came in and my elk form simply stopped.

I came back as myself. Tortle shell, leather armor, Meril's staff somewhere on the ground near me, five leaves, none of which could reach Gorthok from the dirt. The Moonbeam went out. That was what I heard Vukradin process in real time: *"No, no more Moonbeam."*

"Yep," I said. "Moonbeam goes away."

Gorthok ended his advance just outside where the beam had been. A margin so thin it was almost absurd — the beast had knocked out my wakefulness with enough precision, accidental or otherwise, that he would not immediately burn.

"Oh, that's so close," Valphine said.

She sounded almost impressed. I was flat on the ground. I would take her impressed.

Around me the orc melee continued its ugly arithmetic. The allied line and the cultist line traded blows in the mass way that only works when there are enough bodies to sustain losses — two allied orcs went down to a concussive burst thrown from outside the hush, a third knocked back hard. The Anchorite outside the quiet zone could still cast. That was the gap in the plan — always had been. Boney had covered the ones inside. The one outside had stepped clear and used the space.

I lay there and listened to the sounds of the fight — Prutha's voice, the concussive push of that burst, Vukradin noting the gap in the coverage with characteristic precision — and I did a quiet accounting of what I had left.

Not much. But I was still inside it. A hollow shell of my former, yes. Still here.

## Vukradin — Vorga's War Cry and the Tide Turns

The Command left my mouth clean — one word, stretched to cover two minds at once. *Grovel.* I'd aimed it at the Anchorite closest to Prutha and at Gorthok simultaneously, the word spreading across both of them like a net, and when it landed, they both went down. The boar first — all that thunder and mass folding sideways into the earth — and the Anchorite a half-second after, knees hitting the ground like a rebuke.

*"On your face,"* I said, *"and everything you had is gone."*

I'd had every edge on the Anchorite already, from Valphine's earlier work. Every compounding factor now, which was a phrase that didn't technically mean anything and which I said anyway, because watching Gorthok's snout press into the dirt produced a specific satisfaction that wanted acknowledging. Even through the chaos of the lodge I felt it — the moment when the field reorganized itself. Combat has a grammar, and sometimes a single word rewrites the sentence.

The Anchorite near Prutha. That had been the choice. The other one was already inside the silence bubble, already cut off from his spellcasting — but this one was engaged, not retreating, and Prutha was there with him. *We gotta save Prutha.* I'd said it plainly because it was true and because naming the thing in my head out loud sometimes kept me honest. He was my first named convert. I'd let Corbin walk into danger I'd helped create; I wasn't going to watch Prutha go down to an Anchorite because I'd chosen the more strategic target.

Valphine moved next, and the bolt she sent in Lathander's name toward the most wounded cultist was unhurried, almost elegant. She named it in his radiance, which she always did — and I always wondered, briefly, what exactly she meant by that. The bolt hit. The Anchorite dropped to nearly nothing, two shallow breaths from ending.

Then Boney took the lightning.

The bolt came from the Anchorite with clear sightlines — no friendlies between him and the skeletal horse, so that's where he aimed. Boney flinched, the whole chassis of those old bones stuttering, and Soma made a sound that wasn't a word. *"Boney, no!"* And then she was recalculating, moving the Moonbeam, repositioning Boney to carry the silence with him, and as Anchorite Number Two tried to flee, Boney's opportunity caught him mid-step. A blow that nearly finished him, then gentled back — and then the Moonbeam washing over the same body, leaving the cultist with almost nothing at all, wearing the look of a man who understood exactly how little margin that was.

The Moonbeam repositioned over Gorthok. Soma announced the working at its fullest reach, dice already in her hand, and I watched her roll.

Two tens. A nine.

*"Nice,"* I said.

She clarified the dice like she felt she owed me a receipt. Twenty-nine, cut in half when Gorthok managed to resist the worst of it — still enough to leave him barely a breath of life. A monster that had cracked the lodge gate like kindling, that had come out of the Woodland Manse ritual ahead of schedule, that had put real fear in the pit of my chest when it first cleared the threshold — barely a breath of life, groveling in the dirt with my word still in its mouth.

Soma's elk hooves came next. She said it with a particular confidence — *"there's no way he survives this unless I roll double ones"* — and then paused, in the way of someone who has immediately cursed themselves.

She didn't roll double ones. She knocked Gorthok dead on the first blow.

And then Gorthok rose.

Not stood. Not rallied. Rose, the way things rise that aren't supposed to — a convulsion of something beneath the flesh, a stored defiance, a single thread of life reconstituted out of Talosian will or bound-god stubbornness. I'd seen it named once in a text I shouldn't have had access to: *Revivify.* A creature that refuses its own ending the first time.

*"That busted,"* Soma said. *"That's why you wanted me to do the Moonbeam first."*

*"Word,"* I said.

The Moonbeam was already positioned. Already waiting. When Gorthok's turn ended, it was still standing over him, and the Moonbeam didn't care about stored defiance. It cared about its touch at the end of every turn, and Gorthok had nowhere to be.

*"He died to the moonbeam,"* Soma confirmed.

*"Moonbeam,"* I echoed.

There was something satisfying about the brevity of that exchange. Not every death in this work requires a speech.

Then Vorga entered the fight.

I'd known she was there — the Warchief of the allied orc contingent, Long-Memory, the one whose name Prutha had spoken with a particular weight. When she let out the War Cry, I felt it physically before I understood what it was. A sound that doesn't just carry volume but *instruction* — the body knows to straighten when it hears it, the shoulders settle back, the lungs fill on their own. She named Falcon, Soma, and Brewbarry — all close enough to feel it — and the advantage moved into them like weather. I wasn't in the radius. I didn't need it. I'd done my work.

*"I'll definitely need that,"* Brewbarry said, in the way that meant he'd been watching his dice all fight and they hadn't been kind.

Around us, the allied orcs were finishing what we'd started. I tracked it in fragments — blows landing, bodies going down, the rapid accounting of a field tipping past its balance point. Prutha's voice was the one I caught clearly, because I was listening for it. He screamed something first in orcish, then in the common tongue: *Die, you scum of scums!* And then he was charging an Anchorite, still screaming, and what he screamed was *for uncle, for my uncle, and the sun god, and Lathander,* and then something about Lathander burning all of them under his fiery bright light.

Valphine said, dryly: *"Alright."*

I didn't say anything. I watched Prutha drive into the Anchorite, and I thought about the night I'd acknowledged him as my first — not abstractly, as a convert, but as a person with a name who had chosen something and was now fighting for it with both hands. I'd connected it then to Corbin. I didn't let myself make that connection again here, because the fight wasn't over, but I noted it. A name you give someone and the weight they choose to carry it at are not the same thing — and Prutha was carrying his at a run, screaming theology at a cultist while the allied orcs closed in on all sides.

One orc killed an Anchorite and shouted something in orcish about the gods and the sun.

The tide had already turned. This was just the turning becoming undeniable.

## Brewbarry — The Fall of Gorthok and the Last Anchorite

The last orc went down before I'd finished the swing. The halberd still had momentum, so I let it carry — cleaved into the next one standing, felt that connect too, kept moving. Grown to twice my size, I covered the ground between targets in two strides. The courtyard of Falcon's Lodge had gone from impossible to manageable somewhere in the last few minutes and I was too deep in the work to notice exactly when.

There was one Anchorite left.

He had almost nothing left in him. I could see it — the way he was still on his feet at all was more stubbornness than capability. But before I could close the distance, Vukradin did something I hadn't expected.

*"Yeah, let's do — put the vision on the remaining anchorite."*

I don't know the workings of what Vukradin does. I know what I see: the man raises his hand, says words, and the world changes. What formed in front of the Anchorite was something vast and blinding. Not a warrior. Not a monster. A god. The god, the sun one, Valphine's and Vukradin's — rendered in whatever light lives inside a bard's head when he reaches for it.

The Anchorite broke.

Not like a soldier breaks, deciding the fight's lost. He broke the other way, the bad way — where the thing inside you turns on you all at once. He started screaming. The words came in pieces, half prayer, half confession.

*"She's real! The drows, she's real! Talos, my god! Talos!"*

And then, worse:

*"I burned the shrine! I broke the handles! I did what you asked! Why is he here? Why is he here?"*

I didn't know what shrine. I didn't know what handles. I didn't need to. The man had done terrible things for a god who wasn't answering, and now he was watching a different god arrive in his place, and whatever bargain he thought he'd struck had come apart completely. I've known that feeling. Not the god part. The other part — the moment you understand that everything you sacrificed was for something that was never going to hold.

Valphine said something quiet that I didn't fully catch. Something about needling. She sounded satisfied.

Falcon hit the other anchorite three times in a row. I registered this only peripherally — a shout, the ring of a longsword connecting, connecting again, and then again. He'd been missing all night. Some fights work that way. The moment arrives and suddenly the man who couldn't hit a stationary target finds everything at once. *"This is like, you know, the defining moment of the Falcon, right?"* Real damage across three swings, and I believed it. I'd watched him touch his brass rings before he charged, mutter something to someone who wasn't there. I didn't ask. You don't ask about brass rings.

Prutha put two spear strikes into a prone Anchorite. Both landed. The orc chief hit hard and kept her feet steady. Valphine said *"pretty effective"* with that tone she has, the one that means she is filing something away.

And then it was my turn.

The last Anchorite — the one Vukradin had broken open — had almost nothing left. Two hits of anything and he was finished, and I had a halberd still live with acid and arms twice their normal size and the kind of anger that builds slow during a long siege and doesn't care that the fight is almost over. I crossed to him.

A solid hit. I knew before the blow landed that the number wouldn't matter.

*"He had almost nothing left. He's dead."*

I finished the swing anyway. Out of principle. The blow landed and I felt it and it meant nothing because the Anchorite was already falling, but it felt wrong not to finish the count. A kill deserves the full count. That's the only ceremony I have.

The courtyard went quiet. Not silent — there was still the sound of breathing and rain and Falcon somewhere behind me, probably touching his rings again — but the noise of the fight was gone. The thunder Gorthok had carried with him had followed him into death, and what was left was just ordinary night air. Cold. Slightly smoky from whatever Valphine had done earlier.

I stood there with the halberd and waited for the feeling to arrange itself into something I could put a name to. It didn't. It just sat in my chest, the way it always does after — too large for the body, looking for somewhere to go.

## Soma — The Battle's End and the Orcish Alliance

The boar was dead. The orcs were not.

Two of them broke from the yard at a run — heading for the treeline, heading away — and Falcon was already reaching for his bow before the dust had settled. I watched him nock and draw and loose with the kind of efficiency that has nothing generous in it.

"Such senseless violence," I said. Nobody heard me, or nobody cared to.

Vukradin looked at me, something in his expression that wasn't quite a challenge. "How's that douchey?"

"It's unnecessary."

"If you don't kill them, they're just gonna come back," Valphine said, and I recognized the voice she uses when she isn't really arguing — when she's simply offering the logic of a world she grew up in, underground, where nothing that fled came back a friend.

I let it sit for a moment. "There's a well of orcs," I said. "There are always more orcs beyond the horizon."

Nobody agreed with me, but nobody shot any more of them either, so I called it a draw.

Then Prutha moved.

I didn't see all of it. I saw enough. The axe came down with the kind of finality that has a sound, and I said "Guys, calm down" to no one in particular, and I said it again — "such senseless violence" — because saying it twice is the tortle equivalent of a strongly worded letter, and I had nothing else left.

Vorga was shouting. I couldn't parse all of it — my Orcish has never been good, and she was at full war-chief volume, which rattled the timbers. But the shape of it was clear enough. She was pleased. She was very pleased. She was gesturing at all of us with the kind of arm-sweep that means *mine*, and her warriors were echoing it back with a sound like a storm deciding to be happy instead.

We were members of the tribe. Honorary, apparently. Bloodlusty cheers rose and fell and rose again.

Valphine started singing. It took me a beat to recognize what — *The Hand Obould Offered*, the old orc-reconciliation legend, the one about the horde that didn't burn the feast-hall down, that sat at the table instead. I knew the song the way you know a story you were told to illustrate a point: distantly, with moderate skepticism.

Then Vukradin's voice joined in.

He'd said something first, half to himself — something about always thinking it was a cheap manipulative song, and here we were singing it and meaning every last word. I watched his face as he sang. He looked surprised at himself. I found that I appreciated that about him, in that moment. He knows when he's been caught being sincere.

I did not sing. But I stood in the yard with blood on my shell and a staff in my hand and listened to an aasimar bard and a drow cleric harmonize with an orc war party at dusk in the ruins of a siege, and I thought: well. Meril sent me out here. Maybe he knew something.

Falcon shouted "Huzzah!" with an enthusiasm that would have embarrassed him a week ago. He was beaming. He was the man who had, by his own recent account, survived by refusing to act — and now here he was, arm around an orc's shoulders, vibrating with the joy of a man who just found out that stepping in is fun when it works.

"He's gone fully the other way," Vukradin observed.

"That's what happens when you find a stranger in the Alps," I offered. I'm not sure anyone understood it, but it felt accurate.

"He has no real core convictions," Vukradin said, not unkindly.

Valphine was already talking to three of the orcs — a quiet word here, a gesture toward the lodge there. The proposal was clean: stay, work, be useful. Three of them agreed. Falcon immediately pivoted from "honorary tribesman" to "lord of the estate" with a speed that I found admirable in its completeness.

"The first thing we'll have to do is teach you how to dress properly," he told them. "The three of you will become the finest stewards in all the North."

"Now it's not my problem," Valphine said, like a woman setting down a parcel she'd carried too far.

Vukradin had already stopped listening. I watched him drift toward the guest house with the slow, purposeful movement of a man who has decided that the world can manage without him for a few hours.

"I've had kind of a long day," he said, generally, to the air. *"I threw everything I had at them, and most of it bounced. I'm kind of wandering back to the out. Like, finally we're done."*

He paused at the guest house door. "Don't steal any of that treasure. I'm gonna be napping."

"To steal their life, not their treasure," I said after him, which was advice for a different situation entirely, but he was already inside.

Brewbarry came up from the cellar with the expression of a man who has stepped on something he loved. The wine casks — or what remained of them. Staved. Emptied into the earth. The Anchorites' work, apparently, from before any of this began.

"Oh my god," he said. "I am depressed."

He stood there looking at the wreckage with a grief that I recognized as genuine. Brewbarry's sorrows are not small. He has the kind of heart that feels things at full size, and the loss of wine he had been anticipating — wine he had maybe, in some corner of himself, been counting on — sat on him visibly.

"I know Talos is the god of chaos," he said, after a moment. "But come on. Gotta be some limits."

I agreed with that more than anything else said in the last hour.

We talked about the Woodland Manse — empty now, presumably, its residents having just attempted to unmake us. The Boots of Elvenkind were somewhere in there. "Our fair, organic, fair-trade, conflict-free Boots of Elvenkind," Vukradin called them, through the guest house wall, apparently still conscious enough to register the conversation even while committed to his nap.

Valphine raised the question of Don Jon Raskin — the working that recalls a soul from the far side of death entire, she said, musing aloud about some creative ritual, some way to power it that didn't require a cooperative cleric. Her eyes had that particular quality they get when she's entertaining an idea she knows is irregular.

"Oh, boy," I said.

"Red Wizards of Lathander," Vukradin offered, from inside, half-joking or possibly entirely joking. With him you can't always tell.

And then something shifted — the way the air shifts before weather you can't see yet. A new weight, settling into the bones beside the old ones. I sat down on a section of ruined fence and felt the staff settle against my knee, and I thought about what I had left in me and what I might now have access to that I didn't before.

"Surprising after that battle," I said, which wasn't entirely serious, but wasn't entirely not.

New depth. New reach. The staff hadn't grown another leaf — not yet — but I felt it, the way you feel weather coming in: a readiness, a potential, something that would need to be earned before it was given.

Brewbarry read something off a page and said *"going first — actually going first, for once, nice"* in the voice of a man who has been given exactly the gift he wanted and is trying not to look too pleased about it.

Vukradin listed his new workings with the quiet deliberation of a man shopping at a market: *the art of becoming someone else's shadow, the art of rewriting a body entire, the door that opens onto elsewhere.*

"Elsewhere is always nice," I said, about the last one.

It was full dusk now. The lodge behind us was lit from within, the new stewards presumably being lectured about collar starching or something of similar urgency. Somewhere across the yard, Boney stood patient and skeletal and unbothered by any of it, as was his custom.

I stayed where I was for a while. The staff was warm in my hand. The night was coming in.
