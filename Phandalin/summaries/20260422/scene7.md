## Valphine — Counseling the Fallen Ranger

The others drifted away — Soma to her own counsel with Xanthopoulos, Brewbarry to wherever Brewbarry goes when there's nothing to break — and I found myself watching Falcon the way I used to watch nobles in Menzoberranzan who'd lost a political game they didn't know they were playing. The signs were the same. The stillness. The eyes that had stopped calculating because they'd concluded there was nothing worth calculating for.

Nihilism in a ranger is a particular kind of waste. Nihilism in a ranger who blames himself for the consequences of being used is something else entirely — it's a wound that keeps reopening every time he tries to think his way back to the moment before everything broke.

I decided I wanted him.

Not cruelly. I want to be precise about that. But Lathander collects precisely these cases — the ones who've been burned so thoroughly they've confused the burning with the truth of things — and I have a professional appreciation for how the conversion works. Also, frankly, a ranger in genuine spiritual crisis, pointed in the right direction, becomes something formidable. The math was obvious.

So I approached him. No ceremony. I simply stood where he could see me and waited for him to acknowledge that I was there, which he eventually did with the particular reluctance of someone who's learned that acknowledgment invites more of whatever's coming.

I looked at him — *really* looked, the way I was trained to look before I ever heard of Lathander, the way that had nothing to do with gods and everything to do with survival. There was no performance in his grief. Whatever Falcon was suffering, he believed it down to the bone. He genuinely thought that the pattern of his life was interference followed by catastrophe. He'd built a theology of his own failure, and he was living inside it.

Which meant the first tool I'd reach for — gentle skepticism, the suggestion that he was misreading the evidence — would slide right off him. He'd heard it from himself a hundred times already and rejected it each time.

I needed a different door.

He looked at me the way surface dwellers look at drow when they think they're being polite about it — that slight hesitation, the recalibration, the effort not to stare at the white hair and the grey skin and whatever he'd heard about Menzoberranzan in whatever wayside inn had educated him about the world.

"How did you become a cleric of Lathander?" he asked. And then, because he was being honest enough to name the obvious: "You're… you're a drow."

I've been asked this question in many registers. Suspicious. Accusatory. Philosophical. His was genuinely curious, which is rarer than people think.

"I was a denizen of Menzoberranzan," I told him. "And after the demons invaded and rampaged the town — came up to the Overworld, and wow, just the vividness of the sun beating down on us. It was a glorious moment."

I let that sit.

What I didn't explain — what I never explain to anyone, because it collapses into inadequacy the moment you try — was what it actually cost to have that moment. The demons came first. The city that had made me, that had taught me how power moves and how to move inside it, came apart in ways that even Menzoberranzan's cruelties hadn't prepared me for. And then the surface. The light.

I'd heard descriptions. I'd thought I understood. I did not understand.

The sun, the first time — it's not warmth. That's what surface dwellers say, because warmth is what they've always known it as. For someone who has lived their entire life under stone, it is annihilation of a specific and glorious kind. It stripped away everything that had been shaded and cool and careful. It forced me to simply *be there*, in it, with nothing to hide behind, every nerve screaming at once.

The pain was extraordinary. The beauty was inseparable from the pain. And in that moment I understood, with the clarity that only comes when you're being remade whether you consent to it or not, that this was something worth worshipping — not because it was comfortable, but because it was *real* in a way that nothing I'd experienced underground had been. Real the way only suffering can be real, the way only light that makes you want to flinch and doesn't care if you do can be real.

I became a cleric because the sun god doesn't offer comfort. He offers revelation. I found that deeply relatable.

Falcon was quiet for a moment. I could see it landing — not the specifics, but the shape of it. The idea that conversion could be violent. That you could come to something sacred through the thing that broke you rather than despite it.

"I don't even know why I ever supported Adabra," he said, and his voice had changed. The blankness was cracking into something more honest and, in its way, more painful. "We need to go deal with those orcs. I probably owe the bard an apology."

Progress.

He looked at me with the particular helplessness of someone who knows what needs to happen but has lost the vocabulary for it. "You have a way with words. Maybe you could convince him that I didn't mean harm?"

I considered him. A man who had aimed himself at the right targets all his life, and bent himself into the wrong shapes serving the wrong authority, and was now trying to figure out how to reassemble without a pattern.

"You know," I said, "get a little bit of thrill and sensation. The pain is a good way to kind of, like, really get a kick in your life."

It sounds absurd, written plainly. But I meant it, and more importantly — he heard it. Because what I was actually saying was: *the fact that you feel this is the evidence that you're not finished. The pain of seeing clearly what you did wrong is not punishment. It is the beginning of something.* Lathander isn't about atonement that soothes. He's about the dawn that forces you to see what the dark was hiding. Falcon had been living in the dark of his own guilt, and I was suggesting — gently, with the practiced patience of someone who builds congregations one broken person at a time — that the searing light of accountability wasn't something to flee from.

As for the apology to Vukradin — I suggested a gesture of genuine value. A musical instrument, something worthy of the bard's artistry. Or perhaps a room in his retreat, a studio where Vukradin could work — space and silence being among the more practical forms of respect.

Falcon received this with the nod of a man who'd been given a task he could actually complete. Something concrete. Something that didn't require him to find the right words but let the object carry them.

He asked me to help mediate the conversation itself, when it came. He lacked the language, he said.

I found I didn't mind.

Across the room, Xanthopoulos was taking his leave of Soma, and I caught the tail end of it — something murmured in Soma's direction, quiet and weighted with the particular precision of a man choosing his final words carefully. That perhaps Meril had made the right choice in selecting her.

It was, I noted, a remarkable thing to say. And it was said to exactly the right person.

I turned back to Falcon. One conversion at a time.
