## Soma — The Aftermath and Next Steps

The fight was done. The hall was quieter than it had any right to be.

Lyra straightened herself — the kind of straightening a person does when they're trying to remember what it felt like to have dignity — and offered to buy us all drinks at the Stonehill. I noticed she said it to all of us, but she was looking mostly at Vukradin when she said it. Gratitude has its own grammar.

I didn't argue. My feet hurt and there was still dried something on my forearm that I chose not to examine.

---

The Stonehill is always the same. Worn wood, familiar smell, the low hum of people trying to forget the day. I sat and let the conversation happen around me the way you let a stream move around a rock. The shell-sprouts were talking about orcs.

They had opinions. They had several opinions each, and they were willing to share all of them simultaneously. Privy Council obligations. Phandalin's perimeter. Duties. Responsibilities. The word *responsibility* was used with the particular enthusiasm of people who had only recently acquired any.

I drank my drink. The orcs were a real problem. The council was a real problem. The question of which real problem to address first was, apparently, a problem that required considerable volume.

---

Neverwinter won. Or rather, Neverwinter won *and* the orcs might get handled along the way, which is the kind of plan that sounds like a plan until you're standing in front of an orcish raiding party on a road to somewhere else. I have seen this exact shape of optimism before. I said nothing. Some lessons only stick when you learn them yourself.

---

It was somewhere in the middle of the third round of drinks that it came together for me.

Falcon's hostility. The way he'd looked at Vukradin — not like a man who'd been wronged in some clear and specific way, but like a man who had felt the floor move under him and still couldn't say exactly when. That particular expression. I recognized it.

Vukradin doesn't know he's doing it, I think. That's the most honest version. He speaks and things shift — people's certainty, their resolve, the angle at which they see themselves in a situation. He calls it music. He calls it honesty. And it *is* honest; I've never caught him in an outright lie. But there are people walking around Phandalin who made different choices than they would have made without him in the room, and some of them have started to notice, and some of them have started to wonder whether what happened to them was entirely their own idea.

Resentment is a slow thing. It grows in the gap between what someone chose and what they suspect they were led to choose.

The kid doesn't see it yet. That's the thing about silver tongues — you spend so long meaning well that you don't notice when the people you've moved start doing the arithmetic.

I didn't say this out loud. It wasn't the moment for it, and anyway, what would I say? *Your gift makes some people feel managed even when you're not trying to manage them*? He'd take it seriously and it would cost him something. There would be a time for it. Maybe.

---

Xanthopoulos had said it quietly, almost to himself, toward the end of things: *perhaps Meril made the right choice.*

I had heard it. I don't think he intended me to hear it as much as I did.

The staff sat against my side where I'd rested it, the way it always does — familiar weight, slightly warm, fewer leaves than it had last month. I have been thinking about Adabra and the conclave and every druids' meeting where someone looks at me like I'm an interloper carrying something I found in the road. I have been thinking about it more than I admit to anyone, including myself, on most days.

*Perhaps Meril made the right choice.*

Seven words. I turned them over the way you turn a stone over in your palm — not looking for anything in particular, just feeling the edges.

Meril had trained me late. I came to the craft old by any reasonable measure, and she knew that, and she gave me the staff anyway. I used to think I understood why. I am less certain I do. But Xanthopoulos had watched the whole of it — the trial, the argument, whatever it was we'd just come through — and that was the thing he'd said.

Not *she made a defensible choice*. Not *the evidence supports the decision*. 

*The right choice.*

I am old, and I have seen much, and I have spent a considerable number of years suspecting that Meril saw something in me I cannot see clearly in myself. I am not going to make anything sentimental out of seven words from a man who gives his opinions sparingly. But I filed them away somewhere careful.

The drinks were good. The company was loud. Outside, Phandalin was still standing, and somewhere to the northwest there were orcs and a road toward Neverwinter, and the staff was still warm against my side.

That was enough for one night.
