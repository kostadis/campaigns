## Vukradin — The Road to the Druid Council

The road out of Phandalin smells like pine resin and cold mud, the kind of morning where the mountains are still wrapped in cloud and the light hasn't quite committed to being light yet. I have my flute in my hand — not playing, just holding it, the way some people hold worry beads — and I am thinking about Adabra.

I admire Adabra. This is inconvenient, given everything.

She is principled. She is consistent. She has, in my observation, never once said something she did not mean, which is rarer than people acknowledge. We simply mean very different things. She thinks Soma carrying Meril's Staff is a violation of something sacred and structural. I think Soma is perhaps the most careful person I know, and that sacred structures are occasionally wrong. This is the kind of disagreement that does not resolve through a good conversation. I have been aware of this for some time, and it has not stopped me from hoping I am wrong about it.

Then Thomas found us on the road, and the hoping became somewhat harder.

He materialized the way Lyra's people do — not from nowhere, exactly, but from the specific nowhere that well-trained agents occupy when they want to seem casual about it. He had the look of someone delivering bad news they'd been rehearsing. Lyra's agent, carrying Lyra's anxieties, which are considerable and usually well-founded. He told us Adabra was moving to ostracize Lyra.

Ostracize. Not debate. Not challenge. *Ostracize.*

I turned this word over a few times while Thomas kept talking. Ostracism is not a philosophical disagreement resolved through patient dialogue. Ostracism is a verdict. It means Adabra has already done the arithmetic, already knows the votes, and called this council not to discover an outcome but to announce one. That is a different kind of room than I had been preparing for.

The detail that landed heaviest: Reidoth was away. The nominal head of the local Emerald Enclave chapter — absent. And Adabra had called the council during his absence.

I have sat on the Privy Council. I know exactly what it looks like when someone calls a meeting they intend to win before it starts. You pick your moment. You pick your room. You make sure the people most likely to complicate your majority are occupied elsewhere. It is not dishonest, precisely. It is just the architecture of a predetermined conclusion.

"Even if Reidoth were present," Thomas said, with the particular weariness of someone who has thought about this extensively, "he could not stop Adabra from speaking."

No. Of course not. But presence matters. Presence is a kind of pressure, even when it cannot be translated into procedural obstruction. Reidoth standing in that room, saying nothing, watching — that changes the texture of what Adabra can do. His absence gives her a room with better acoustics for her purposes.

We talked about reaching Reidoth. We kept it as an option, held in reserve, not dismissed — the way you keep an alternate chord progression in mind when you're not sure how the main one is going to land. Maybe. Maybe it becomes relevant. We did not send for him yet.

What I kept turning over, walking that road with the flute still unplayed in my hand, was the timing. Not the ambition — I understand ambition, even ambition I disagree with — but the *precision* of the timing. This was not opportunism. This was a scheduled event. Adabra had looked at the calendar, identified the window, and placed her move there. That is something closer to certainty than confidence. She believes she has the numbers.

Soma was ahead of me on the path, her shell catching the grey morning light, Meril's Staff in hand. The staff that is the whole of this argument, the artifact around which all of Adabra's parliamentary maneuvering has been constructed. The leaves on it have been diminishing. Soma knows this. She carries it anyway, and she carries it because Meril trusted her with it, and because something that powerful needs someone careful rather than someone credentialed.

I believe that. I believe it plainly and without reservation.

The question — the one I was walking with, the one Thomas's warning had sharpened into something with an actual edge — was whether believing it would be sufficient. Whether the room we were walking into could be moved by the truth of it, or whether it had already been arranged to reach a different conclusion.

When we talked through our strategy, Soma made her decision quietly and without much theatrics: she was going to lean into what she actually believed. Not apologize for the interventionism. Not soften it or reframe it as something more palatable to druidic conservatism. She had intervened, actively and deliberately, because the situation had required it. She was going to say so.

I felt something settle in me when she said it. Pride, maybe, or something adjacent to pride — the particular satisfaction of watching someone choose the harder honest thing over the easier approximate thing.

It was also, I noted privately, the higher-risk posture. Walking into a room Adabra has already organized, taking up a position that asks the room to change rather than asking it to forgive — that requires the room to be persuadable. And I did not yet know if this one was.

The mountains were still in cloud. The road wound on ahead through the trees. I turned the flute once between my fingers and thought: *read the room when you get there, Vukradin. You don't know the acoustics yet.*

But I had a feeling. And the feeling was that this was going to require every word I had.
