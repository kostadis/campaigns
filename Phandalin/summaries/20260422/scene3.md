## Soma — Adabra's Accusation

The staff told me what I needed to know, and I didn't like it.

That's the honest account of what happened in that chamber. Not that Adabra was wrong, not that she was right, not that the whole thing resolved cleanly. Just that I pressed Meril's staff toward the woman making her case before the council, held the question steady in my mind — *is she deceived, is she controlled, is she lying to herself, is something wearing her conviction like a coat* — and the staff answered me the way it always answers: plainly, without sympathy for what I was hoping to hear.

No. No. No.

Vukradin muttered it before I could say anything myself. "Yeah, no, she's a true believer."

He said it like that settled something. I suppose it did.

I'd been watching Adabra since she opened her mouth. She hadn't raised her voice once. She wasn't performing outrage or grief or righteousness — all the things you do when you're trying to convince people of something you're not certain of yourself. She stood before that council the way old trees stand: not moving, not needing to move, because they've already decided where they're rooted.

"I will be brief," she said. "I am not here to argue philosophy with you. I'm here to tell you what I found."

I believed her. That was the problem.

She walked to the Spring Grove herself. Three weeks ago, on her own feet, with her own eyes. The treant: dead. Roots collapsed. Crown black. Not wounded. Not sleeping. *Dead.* She said each word with the same weight, not more, not less, the way you speak when you've been carrying something heavy long enough that the carrying has become ordinary.

I looked at the staff in my hands. The leaves are fewer than they were. I notice, the way you notice a grey hair — not with alarm, just acknowledgment. Meril would have known what to do with this woman. Meril always knew when to argue and when to simply witness. I'm still learning the difference, forty-five years in.

Adabra wasn't finished.

"The last time I spoke with this party, I believed the ritual had failed. The tree had still lived. When I heard there was a planar creature collecting materials near the grove, I was — I will say this plainly — pleased. I called it a planar magpie. I said it was the world working as it should. I gave the bard five gold pieces for what he told me."

She didn't look at Vukradin when she said it. That was almost worse.

"That day, I believed him."

I have known shell-sprouts who lied to my face and I've known shell-sprouts who lied to everyone including themselves and I've known shell-sprouts who told the truth and watched people not believe them anyway. Vukradin has a gift for making things sound true. It's not a flaw, exactly — it's the thing that makes him useful and frustrating in equal measure. But Adabra had walked to the grove. She had pressed Lyra's people. She had found the gap between what was reported and what existed. And then she had come here.

"I'm not asking you to condemn anyone today," she said. "I'm asking you to look at what I found before you listen to anyone tell you what it means."

Falcon was sitting beside her. I noted that the way I note a change in weather — not judgment, just fact. Falcon, who has been watching us since before we were worth watching. Falcon, who has opinions about how the balance should be kept and who should keep it. Falcon, sitting next to Adabra, in a council chamber, while she laid out a case against us with the calm precision of someone who has had weeks to be certain.

The staff was quiet in my hands.

Vukradin tried. He is nothing if not persistent, my bard shell-sprout, and he has survived worse rooms than this one. He leaned into the cross-examination the way he leans into most things: charm held back, just enough room for logic.

"Between when you heard this account — which you claim was false — and when you saw that the treant was dead. How much time between those two points?"

Adabra: "Two weeks."

"Two weeks. A lot can happen in two weeks. How do you know that what you heard from me wasn't true, and that the treant died within that two-week period before you personally went and saw that it had died? You don't know that."

It was a reasonable point. It was the kind of reasonable point that sounds convincing right up until you look at the woman it's aimed at and realize she's already considered it, walked around it, and decided it doesn't change the shape of what she found.

Adabra didn't answer that moment — she didn't need to, not yet. The council would weigh it. Maybe some of them would find purchase in it.

Then Thomas stood up. And Lyra. And they said the quiet part in full sentences, out loud, in front of everyone:

"Lyra asked us to go deal with the metastasized treant. We tried, we failed, the party showed up, they killed it."

No shame in Thomas's voice. Not defiance either. Just: *this is what happened*. The same register Adabra had been using the whole time. The same weight.

I held the staff a little tighter and said nothing.

The thing about a true believer is this: you cannot out-argue them with facts, because the facts they have are real too. Adabra went to the grove. The treant is dead. That is true. That she was given a false account first — also true. That we killed the treant to stop something worse from spreading — also true. And there is no version of this conversation where all those truths sit comfortably in the same sentence, and everyone agrees what they mean.

I have seen much, in forty-five years. I have watched creatures die who shouldn't have died, and watched things survive that should have ended. I have made choices that the natural order would call interference. I have made them anyway.

Adabra would call that the problem. She isn't wrong that there's a problem. She's wrong about what it is.

But I can't prove that to her. The staff already told me so.
