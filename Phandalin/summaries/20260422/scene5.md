## Soma — The Druid Council Debate

The moment Vukradin walked out, I felt every eye in that circle shift to me.

Right. So it's just me, then.

I'm not naturally a speaker. I'm a watcher. I sit with things until I understand them, and then I act — quietly, usually alone, without needing anyone to observe. Meril never made speeches. He made decisions. I learned from him that words are mostly noise and that what you do with your hands is what the forest actually remembers.

But Aldric Stone Path was standing there, arms folded, jaw set, with that specific look of a man who has been ignored for a long time and is now clinging to the one person who finally nodded at him. I recognized it. I'd worn something like it myself, once, at the edge of another circle where the older druids talked over my head about things I'd been watching from the ground for years.

Adabra had listened to him. That was real. I wasn't going to pretend it wasn't.

But listening isn't the same as solving.

"I don't know how you don't see those as a problem as well, sir." I kept my voice level. Not soft, not hard — the voice I use when I'm telling someone a plain fact they've been avoiding. "She's bringing in yet another problem — these planar anomalies, these infestations, these blights on the land. Adabra says let nature take its course and get obliterated, basically." I let that sit for a breath. "What does Adabra say the solution is?"

Aldric opened his mouth. Closed it.

I wasn't trying to humiliate him. I genuinely wanted to know if I'd missed something. If there was an answer I'd failed to think of in all the hours I'd spent watching the staff's leaves fall.

Adabra herself stepped into the silence. Her voice was careful, measured — the voice of someone who has given this argument many times and believes it completely.

"The displacement is downstream of the intervention. The answer is to stop the cascade by withdrawing. The ecosystem finds equilibrium without help."

I've heard that word a lot lately. Equilibrium. As if the world is a pond and all you need to do is stop throwing stones.

I thought about the planar rift at the Falcon's Cabin. The way the blight moved through soil that had been healthy two seasons before. The Talosians with their hands pressed into the earth and the earth going grey and wrong under their touch. That's not a pond returning to stillness. That's a wound that festers.

"Equilibrium comes in many forms." I heard my own voice come out quieter than I expected — not because I was uncertain, but because I wanted them to lean in. "One of these equilibriums could be the end of everything, of all life." I met Adabra's eyes. "That's not the equilibrium I'm here to protect."

Aldric sat down.

I noticed Adabra notice him sitting down. Something in her expression went still and recalibrated. She wasn't finished — she's never finished — but she'd lost something and she knew it.

Then the centaur arrived.

I had not expected a centaur. I should have, perhaps — Xanthopoulos has ties to the high valleys, and his people see more of what moves through the mountain passes than most — but the arrival of a centaur at a druid council debate is still, in my experience, the kind of event that requires a moment.

He was deliberate in the way that old creatures are deliberate. Not slow — considered. He looked at the circle. He looked at Adabra. He looked at me.

Then he looked at the staff.

The way he looked at it made something tighten in my chest that I didn't have a name for.

"I know whose staff that is," he said. "I know what it means that Meril gave it to her. He did not give it for sentiment. He gave it because he… picked her."

I have been carrying this staff long enough that my arm has learned its weight. I know which knot of wood catches on my belt if I'm not careful. I know the particular resistance of it when I walk over uneven ground. I had stopped thinking about what it meant — what it said about me, or about Meril's judgment — because that kind of thinking doesn't help you walk.

But Xanthopoulos said it out loud, in front of everyone, in a tone that expected it to be believed.

"It's just a staff," I said. "Isn't it?"

The words came out more like a question than I intended.

No one answered. That was its own kind of answer.

Then Xanthopoulos told us about Corbin.

"I have news. Corbin is dead. Sister Kayla walked out. She's gone to the wind."

The circle went quiet in a different way.

I stood there and let it land. There wasn't a version of this where I didn't feel it. Corbin had been a farmer before he was a bandit — a shell-sprout who'd made bad choices and was, maybe, starting to understand the cost of them. Sister Kaella was a killer in priestly clothes, and we had left her in the same room as him because we were moving fast and we had somewhere to be and we told ourselves it would be fine.

They left a farmer turned bandit with a deadly assassin because they were in a rush to intervene.

"And Boney stood by and did nothing?" I heard myself say.

It wasn't the right thing to say. I knew it wasn't the right thing to say. But Boney is a skeletal horse of uncertain philosophical orientation and I was looking for somewhere to put the feeling, and that was what came out.

Adabra, of course, was already moving.

"See, this is the price of interventionism. Dead bodies everywhere, by careless, careless intervention."

Xanthopoulos cut her off with the same flat calm he'd used on everything else. "I am not on your side. I'm not on theirs. I'm telling you what my people saw and what happened."

The circle was watching me.

I could have walked around it. Found softer ground, more forgiving language, let the silence do the work. I've done that before — let things settle into ambiguity because ambiguity is survivable.

"This was — we were planning on our —" I stopped. Started again. "I can't sugarcoat it. The ask was, do I sugarcoat it, or do I fess up?" I looked at Adabra. "I'm not sugarcoating it."

She'd want me to reach for some grand justification. She'd want me to make excuses, so she could dismantle them. I wasn't going to give her that.

But I also wasn't going to give her Corbin's death as a trophy.

"The interplanar treant?" I said. "Yeah, that's — I'm not going to cry for that. That treant was going to bring a lot more manifestation and horror with it."

And then I heard myself making the argument I'd been building toward since before I even knew I was making it.

"We've seen things — if left unchecked, the Talosians are starting to blight the land itself." I thought of the grey soil, the way the grass had stopped growing in concentric rings around their corrupted sites. "Given that the Falcon's Cabin was overtaken by Talosians — and it's weird, you know, the Falconer asked us to intervene, but now he's an anti-interventionist. He's mad that we failed to intervene, and yet…"

I let it trail off. The contradiction was self-evident. I didn't need to finish it.

Corbin was dead because we moved too fast. That's true.

The blight would have spread to three valleys if we hadn't moved at all. That's also true.

These truths don't cancel each other out. They just both live in the same body — in my hands, in the weight of this staff — and I carry them both, and I keep walking.

That was the only argument I had. I was fairly certain it was the right one.
