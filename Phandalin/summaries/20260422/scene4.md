## Vukradin — The Emerald Enclave Schism

The moment Soma stood up, I found something to admire in her.

She did not hedge. She did not qualify. She planted her feet — all four of them, metaphorically speaking, tortles being what they are — and she told the council exactly what she was. *The land is hurting, and intervention is the only way out. We are here to protect the land at all costs, and that makes us interventionists. Then so be it. We're loud, we're proud.* I could have composed something from that. A manifesto set to music, maybe. Soma as the opening movement: confident, declarative, no wasted notes.

I was still thinking about the melody when Falcon stood up.

I knew Falcon. Or I thought I did — the comfortable retired adventurer at his comfortable hunting lodge, the man who had once asked us to protect that lodge and whose fire we had watched from a distance when it burned. There was a version of Falcon I had assembled from available evidence: melancholy, yes, and perhaps a little embittered, but fundamentally on the right side of things. People who build hunting lodges and invite heroes in for meals are not, in my experience, the kind who turn on you in public forums.

I had not updated this model recently.

"I came because someone finally sat long enough to listen," he said. His voice had the particular weight of a man who has rehearsed his grievances until they feel like philosophy. "You can keep cheering your victories. I've seen what comes after them."

This, I thought, was a general statement. A mood. The kind of thing a retired adventurer says when the joints ache and the trophies have gathered dust. I was still being charitable.

Then he pointed at me.

"She's right! She's right about what needed to happen. You all wanted it simple. Well, this is what simple looks like. Trees are dead because of men like *him.*"

The room. The entire room, looking at me.

I want to be precise about what I felt in that moment, because I think precision matters here and I am, above all things, a precise person. It was not embarrassment. I have been on stages, I have been reviewed badly, I have played to rooms that did not want to be played to. Embarrassment is something I have developed a tolerance for. What I felt was closer to — bewilderment. A specific bewilderment, the kind that comes when someone changes the key signature mid-phrase and doesn't tell you, and suddenly you are hearing a song you thought you knew as something entirely different and you cannot find where you went wrong.

Trees are dead because of men like him.

Men like me.

I pulled Falcon aside. I needed to understand. This is, I think, my fundamental nature — I do not accept that things are broken when it is possible they are only mistuned.

"What's going on here?" I asked him. "Like, I thought we were… Why are you pointing me out in front of this group? What did I do?"

He said: "You intervened, and in your intervention, people died. Adabra sees it clearly."

And there it was. Adabra. Of course. I admire Adabra — genuinely, I have said so, I mean it — but I am also aware that Adabra has a gift for finding people at their most disillusioned and handing them a framework. Falcon had been given a framework. He was wearing it very comfortably.

"Didn't you used to intervene?" I asked. "Like, what… why the change of heart?"

"And look what it got me."

I looked at Falcon. I thought about the lodge. The comfortable life. The years of reputation and the stories people told at festivals about the great Falcon of the hunting lodge. I thought about all of it, and I thought about the fact that he was standing in this council pointing his finger at me while wearing boots that adventuring had paid for.

"As far as I can tell," I said, keeping my voice even, keeping it musical, keeping it in the register where things land cleanly, "it got you a beautiful hunting lodge, and lots of fame, and glory… and a whole life built on a lie. Got you a lot, that intervention. But now, now… you're gonna screw me over."

He started to speak. I did not let him finish.

"I could tell everybody about what you did. About how you intervened."

This is not a threat I am proud of. I want to note that. I am not a man who makes threats. But there is a particular kind of injustice — the injustice of the reformed sinner who has outsourced his guilt onto the people who haven't yet stopped sinning — that gets past my usual restraint. I had done nothing Falcon had not done. I had done it for the same reasons he once had. And he was standing there in a council room using me as his confession booth, pointing at me as his evidence of the world's wrongness, and I found that I was angry in a way that was clean and simple and not particularly musical.

"Ever since I spoke to Adabra," he said, dismissively, "I have learned the error of my ways."

"Well then," I said, "you should have no trouble repenting in front of the group and explaining the errors *he* made."

I meant it as a calling of the bluff. What I had not accounted for was that Falcon would call it back.

He stood. He addressed the council. And what followed was — I will give him this much — a genuine confession. He told them about his adventuring days. About the dragons he had failed to fight. About the lodge that burned while heroes stood by. About the baubles and the money and the way nature had eventually torn it all down regardless. He wept, a little, I think, or something close to it. "Once again, nature tore it down," he said. "Once again, the Falcon Lodge was burnt. Once again, I asked heroes to intervene, to protect my lodge, and what did they do? They did nothing, and my lodge burned down."

It was, objectively, a very good speech.

It was also, objectively, still entirely about him.

I had wanted him to confess that his attack on me was hypocritical. What he had done instead was confess that his entire life was tragic, which is not the same thing, and which — somehow — made him more sympathetic to the room rather than less. I had handed him a spotlight and he had stepped into it beautifully and used it to make himself the protagonist of a story about his own failure, and the room was watching him with something close to compassion, and I was still standing there being the thing trees had died because of.

I am a musician. I know when a room has turned.

"I'm going back to Phandalin," I said. "You all can stay. I do not like the personal insults. I'm not interested in helping Lyra anymore."

Nobody tried to stop me. That was its own information.

Behind me, as I reached the door, I heard Brewbarry — loyal, enormous, dear Brewbarry — ask the remaining party the only question that had ever truly been in his register: "Do we kill all these people, or do we leave?"

And Soma, patient as tide and twice as inevitable, pulled him back from it. "Without the druids, the land is even more screwed than with half of them."

I was already outside. The air was cooler than it had been in that room, which was a relief.

I had one thing left I could do from here. I reached for the particular resonance that connected me to Reidoth — the old ranger, the man who actually understood what these woods were before they became anyone's ideology — and I sent my voice toward him across the distance between us, the way a melody travels through walls. I told him what was happening. The schism. The council. Adabra's faction and Falcon's confession and the room that had turned before anyone had voted on anything.

Reidoth needed to know. Someone sensible needed to know.

Then I walked back toward Phandalin, with the sound of the council fading behind me, and I composed nothing. Some things don't want to be songs yet. Some things need to sit and get cold first before you can find the key they're actually in.
