## Flags

### [1] Em-dash (narration prose — LLM-default rhythm)

> From now on, every time we find treasure — whether it belongs to us or not — there should be a find-us fee.

**Why:** Brewbarry's voice in the examples is staccato declarative ("Order of Gauntlet. I know them. They fought against my tribe."); paired parenthetical em-dashes are a writerly aside he does not use.
**Suggested rewrite:** "From now on, every time we find treasure, whether it belongs to us or not, there should be a find-us fee."

### [2] Em-dash (narration prose — LLM-default rhythm)

> Some legal word, *sheetment*, that Valphine dug up — a way to force a real election so Harbin Wester stops being a fig leaf with a chain around his neck.

**Why:** The dash hangs an explanatory clause off the sentence; Brewbarry would set it down flat as its own short sentence.
**Suggested rewrite:** "Some legal word, *sheetment*, that Valphine dug up. A way to force a real election so Harbin Wester stops being a fig leaf with a chain around his neck."

### [3] Em-dash (narration prose — LLM-default rhythm)

> We dropped Vorga and her tribe at Icespire — escort done, debt paid — and Prutha took his five bright-eyed orcs off to convert Vorga's lot to Valphine's morning god.

**Why:** The bracketed "escort done, debt paid" is exactly the kind of clipped tally Brewbarry likes, but the em-dashes wrap it as an aside instead of letting it stand; commas keep the staccato.
**Suggested rewrite:** "We dropped Vorga and her tribe at Icespire. Escort done, debt paid. Prutha took his five bright-eyed orcs off to convert Vorga's lot to Valphine's morning god."

### [4] Em-dash (narration prose — LLM-default rhythm)

> Destroy the heathens, sure — unless they want to buy bathrobes, in which case they're our customers.

**Why:** Same parenthetical-dash habit; a comma carries the plain reversal in his rhythm.
**Suggested rewrite:** "Destroy the heathens, sure, unless they want to buy bathrobes, in which case they're our customers."

### [5] Em-dash (borderline — rendered spoken line)

> Yeah — but blood can be turned into something soft and wonderful, like a Brewbarry bathrobe.

**Why:** This is Brewbarry's own spoken beat rendered as un-quoted narration; the em-dash performs a hesitation he wouldn't take. Borderline because it sits on the speech/narration line, but the dash is still an LLM default here.
**Suggested rewrite:** "Yeah, but blood can be turned into something soft and wonderful, like a Brewbarry bathrobe."

### [6] Aphorism / tell-not-show (mild)

> But unfair is just a fight you haven't named yet.

**Why:** The fight-framing fits him (party.md: he measures everything in contests), but stated as a free-floating maxim it tips toward authored wisdom rather than Brewbarry reacting to a specific moment; the spec warns against abstract argument over concrete response.
**Suggested rewrite:** "But unfair is a fight. You just have to name it." (keeps the contest frame, lands it as two flat beats instead of an epigram)

## Verdict

Strong voice fidelity — the diction, the bathrobe-business monomania, the "he sets the rule and I carry it" deference, and the bodily contest imagery are all squarely Brewbarry, and the register-wrong vocabulary scan (shape/geometry/structure/axis/etc.) came back clean. The one recurring issue is the paired em-dash aside (five instances) that smooths his staccato into a more writerly rhythm; spot-edit those to commas/periods rather than re-narrate.
