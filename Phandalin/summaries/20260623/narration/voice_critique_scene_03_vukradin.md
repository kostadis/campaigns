## Flags

### [1] em-dash (convert)

> So I leaned in and reached for menace instead, and that landed flatter still — I felt it leave my mouth and die in the air between us.

**Why:** Narration em-dash setting off a consequence clause; reads as a clean sentence stop, not performer rhythm.
**Suggested rewrite:** "...and that landed flatter still. I felt it leave my mouth and die in the air between us."

### [2] em-dash (convert)

> That was the trick of it — you don't summon a crowd with a bell, you summon them with joy.

**Why:** Em-dash here is functioning as a colon introducing the thesis; a colon better fits the "here is the principle" cadence of his moral-case register.
**Suggested rewrite:** "That was the trick of it: you don't summon a crowd with a bell, you summon them with joy."

### [3] em-dash (convert)

> I felt the words leave me and I knew, the way you know a chord is going to ring before your hand is off the strings, that they had landed exactly — every syllable the right weight, dropped in the right place, the crowd already leaning toward me before the last word cleared my teeth.

**Why:** Em-dash introduces an expansion list; a colon does the same work without the dash tic, and the musical-metaphor sentence is strong enough not to need the visual interruption.
**Suggested rewrite:** "...that they had landed exactly: every syllable the right weight, dropped in the right place, the crowd already leaning toward me before the last word cleared my teeth."

### [4] em-dash (convert)

> There was cheering — general, rising, real.

**Why:** Em-dash before a three-beat appositive; a colon lands the verdict-like list cleaner.
**Suggested rewrite:** "There was cheering: general, rising, real."

### [5] em-dash (convert)

> And here, I confess, is where the thing began to curdle even as it triumphed — and I did not see it then, because I was too busy being right.

**Why:** Joins two independent clauses; this is a full stop or semicolon, not a performer aside.
**Suggested rewrite:** "...is where the thing began to curdle even as it triumphed. And I did not see it then, because I was too busy being right."

### [6] em-dash (convert)

> And there it went — the beautiful clean gift, dragged onto the operating table to have its eligibility carved up.

**Why:** Em-dash functions as a colon naming what "went"; convert for consistency. (See also flag [9] on the metaphor.)
**Suggested rewrite:** "And there it went: the beautiful clean gift, dragged into a key it was never written in to have its eligibility carved up."

### [7] em-dash (convert)

> Because the clever solution to the eligibility problem was that we — the adventurers, the donors — were now also citizens, and citizens receive UBT, and so a portion of what we gave away came flowing right back to us.

**Why:** The paired dashes bracket a simple appositive ("the adventurers, the donors"); commas carry it without the tic, and this sentence is doing ledger-arithmetic reasoning where commas read more procedural.
**Suggested rewrite:** "...was that we, the adventurers, the donors, were now also citizens, and citizens receive UBT..."

### [8] em-dash (borderline — likely keep)

> I stood in his office and told him to convene the Privy Council — now, today — and he balked at the speed of it... So soon? So quickly? Right now? ... Not as good as inside — never as good as inside — but they were drunker now... I should say — I believe this.

**Why:** These four narration em-dashes ("— now, today —", "— never as good as inside —", "I should say —") read as genuine Vukradin performer rhythm: insistent asides and the repeated-phrase emphasis the spec describes ("clause-stacking, self-propelled sentences"). The bracketing dashes around the italic child-chant *— do we get UBT? do we? —* (line 208) are also doing legitimate quotation framing.
**Suggested rewrite:** Keep as-is, or downgrade only the calmest ("I should say, I believe this.") to commas if the pass wants uniform conversion. Flagging for the record per the em-dash scan; recommend retaining as voice.

### [9] cliché / register-adjacent metaphor (borderline)

> And there it went — the beautiful clean gift, dragged onto the operating table to have its eligibility carved up.

**Why:** "Operating table... carved up" is a surgical metaphor, off Vukradin's native musician/idealist register; the spec names music as his organizing metaphor and the examples reach for harmony/dissonance, not clinical imagery, when a good thing gets ruined.
**Suggested rewrite:** "...the beautiful clean gift, dragged into a committee to have its eligibility tuned out of it" — or a music image: "...handed to a room that would argue it out of key one clause at a time."

### [10] cliché simile (borderline)

> I could feel the hubris rising in me like heat off a forge.

**Why:** "Like heat off a forge" is a stock fantasy simile and forge/smithing isn't Vukradin's native register (it recurs with "while the iron was molten," line 156). The examples render his internal heat through music and conviction, not blacksmithing.
**Suggested rewrite:** "I could feel the hubris rising in me, bright and certain, the way a held note swells right before it breaks." (Or simply cut the simile: "I could feel the hubris rising in me, hot and certain.")

## Verdict

Strong, on-voice block: the celestial-gift interiority, the coin/ledger reflexes ("bitten a coin and found it tin," "five percent, tax-free"), and the chord-before-your-hand-leaves-the-strings metaphor are all authentic Vukradin and should stay. The only systematic issue is em-dash density in narration prose — seven convertible instances plus borderline performer ones; resolve those in the same mechanical pass, then spot-fix the two non-musical metaphors (forge heat, operating table).
