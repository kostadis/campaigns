My fellow citizens,

Today I stand before you to address a longstanding injustice that has hidden in plain sight.

For generations, adventurers have descended into dungeons, stormed monster lairs, looted forgotten ruins, and returned laden with treasure. We celebrate their courage. We sing songs of their victories. We admire the glittering rewards they bring home.

But let us ask a simple question:
Whose treasure is it?

When a dragon sits atop a mountain of gold, that wealth was not earned. It was stolen. When bandits, goblins, or other creatures hoard valuables, those valuables were often taken from merchants, farmers, pilgrims, and travelers. When treasure is found in ancient ruins, it may have belonged to civilizations long vanished or to families whose names have been lost to history.

In some cases, the rightful owner can be identified. In those cases, justice demands restitution.

But what of the rest?

What of the coins whose owners are forgotten? What of the jewels whose heirs cannot be found? What of the magical relics that have passed through so many hands that their origins are forever obscured?

For too long, we have accepted a flawed answer. We have allowed the first adventurer to reach the treasure to become its owner. We have transformed recovered stolen property into private wealth through the simple act of finding it.

I propose a different vision.

I call it Universal Basic Treasure.

The principle is simple: treasure with no identifiable rightful owner belongs not to the adventurer, not to the crown, and not to a wealthy merchant guild. It belongs to all the people.

After reasonable efforts to locate the rightful owners have been exhausted, recovered treasure shall be placed into a public trust. The value of that trust shall then be distributed equally among every citizen of the realm.

Every farmer.

Every blacksmith.

Every innkeeper.

Every laborer.

Every widow.

Every child.

Every citizen.

Some will object.

They will say, “Without treasure, adventurers will have no reason to face danger.”

I disagree. Adventurers may still receive fair compensation for their efforts. We already pay city guards, soldiers, and caravan escorts. Courage deserves reward. But there is a difference between earning a wage and claiming ownership of an entire dragon hoard.

Others will say, “The treasure belongs to whoever risked their life to recover it.”

But if a guard recovers a stolen purse, we do not award him the purse. If a fisherman finds a chest of stolen goods washed ashore, we do not declare him its owner. Recovery is not ownership.

Universal Basic Treasure recognizes a simple truth: wealth of unknown ownership is a common inheritance. It should benefit the entire community rather than a fortunate few.

Imagine what this means for our town.

Every recovered hoard would help every family.

Every forgotten cache would strengthen every household.

Every dungeon cleared would create prosperity shared by all rather than luxury enjoyed by a handful of adventurers.

This is not merely an economic proposal. It is a moral one. The purpose of treasure recovery should be justice, not enrichment. The purpose of heroism should be service, not accumulation. The purpose of wealth should be the flourishing of the community, not the glorification of greed.

Let us build a realm where treasure is no longer a prize for the lucky, but a blessing for everyone.

Let us establish Universal Basic Treasure.

Let us return stolen wealth to the people.

Thank you, and may fortune be shared by all.

Vukradin, the Bard, gave a speech in Phandalin in 1496 that started a revolution
