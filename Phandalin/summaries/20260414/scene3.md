## Valphine — Inspection and Strike at Mountain Toes Gold Mine

The mine sat quiet at the mouth of the mountain, which was itself the problem.

I noticed it before we were through the gate — that particular absence of sound that means something has gone wrong with money. No picks. No cart-wheels grinding on stone. No vibration in the earth under my feet, the deep percussion of work. Just wind, and the nervous shuffling of the Whiskered Gang, who were arranged outside the entrance in a way that suggested they had been standing there for some time doing precisely nothing.

Zeleen Varnaster saw us coming and rearranged his face into something optimistic.

"Hey, boss! What's up?"

I looked at him for a moment. I let the silence sit.

"Are you working hard today?"

It was a simple question. I delivered it the way you might read an inventory list — flat, procedural, without particular feeling. Zeleen's expression crumpled into something resembling a man watching his house burn down.

"They've been working really hard. I promise you, boss, they've been working really, really hard."

He gestured vaguely toward the interior of the mine, where no sounds whatsoever were occurring. I filed that away and said nothing. Surface dwellers have a touching faith in assertion. They believe that if you say a thing with sufficient conviction, it becomes more true. Drow society had disabused me of that notion approximately eleven minutes into childhood.

We went inside.

Brewbarry's confusion at least had the virtue of being genuine. He looked at the equipment — idle picks, empty carts, tools that hadn't been touched — and something in him simply couldn't reconcile the image.

"Disconnect," he said. "What is wrong with those tools? I mean, what happened to Rock and Stone?"

The answer, when it came from the foreman Dwolin the Grout, was delivered with the particular gravity of a man who has spent his entire adult life being right about something no one else understands.

"You have to understand, gold. A gold vein is shy and must be treated with absolute respect. If you don't treat it with the respect, it doesn't come out."

I processed that. A shy gold vein. The earth itself, apparently, had feelings about the scheduling.

Horia Stonetallow stepped in beside him with the weary affection of someone who has explained a difficult colleague at great length on many previous occasions.

"Dwolin is the best seeker of gold I have ever known. He's got his little rituals, and his little gimmicks, and his little things…"

She trailed off in the way people do when they love someone who is also exhausting. I had some sympathy for the position. I found myself thinking of the early days of my temple, when I'd discovered that spiritual conviction and organizational coherence did not always arrive together.

But that was a problem for later. The present problem was a mine that was not mining, and a gang of were-rats who appeared to have concluded that standing near the mine counted as doing their job.

I called the light.

Daylight erupted from a point above us — not the soft glow of torches or the polite illumination of a lantern, but the full, searing brilliance of midday sun compressed into an enclosed space, pouring into every corner. The were-rat guards flinched as one, a ripple of physical discomfort running through them. Good. Lathander does not do gentle reminders.

I surveyed the scene in its sudden clarity.

"This is…" I paused, letting the light do its work. "Lathander won't be pleased."

Then I turned back to Zeleen.

"Are these dwarves mining?" I asked. "Did you tell me that these dwarves are mining? Are these dwarves mining?"

He opened his mouth.

I gestured to the empty minecart beside us.

"Is there gold in this minecart?"

"No, but Darwin says that we need the Earthstone Gem before we can get the gold."

"And have you gotten this earth stone gem for the dwarves?"

Stunned silence, which is a particular type of answer. Then:

"Boss, we're paid to protect the mine, not to go on fetch quests."

Brewbarry had reached the end of his patience, which, to his credit, he had extended further than I might have expected.

"The only reason you're protecting the mine is because the mine is useful to us to acquire gold. If the mine is not producing gold, then you're not doing your job."

Admirably direct. I followed through.

"It means… pull your fingers out of your rat's ass, and do whatever is necessary for the dwarves to be happy miners. We want to hear picks."

Zeleen looked between the two of us with the expression of a man searching for a loophole in a contract that contains no loopholes.

"Okay, so what does that mean? Like, so we can stay here then, right? Because you don't need the mine, right?"

I stared at him. I truly did not know where to begin with that.

He tried again.

"Well, boss, I mean, I think you make an excellent point. I think you're making an excellent point, but, like, I mean to be rude, but to get this gem, we have to go into the forest where the orcs are, and this is more of a…"

I let him get that far. It seemed fair. Then:

"Is that any of my problems? Am I getting paid with fees to, you know, make sure that the mine is productive? What am I paying you for? If all you do is eat the cheese, and the mine is not producing gold, then I'm wasting my cheese, aren't I?"

He swallowed.

"A waste of cheese," I said. "That's what you are. Unless you go into the forest, find the gem, get the bloody dwarves to mine. Is that how you want to be remembered?"

There is a particular moment in any negotiation when the other person's will simply gives way. You can feel it — the shoulders drop a fraction, the eyes stop calculating. Zeleen reached that moment. He reached it loudly.

"I think we could… we could make that happen, Mr. Boss. Mr. Right."

Behind him, I heard him screaming at his were-rats that they hadn't done it yet, that this was completely taken care of, that Mr. Boss had every confidence in them. Or words to that effect.

Brewbarry, for his part, was tapping his halberd against his palm with the methodical patience of someone who has learned that visual communication saves time. It did seem to accelerate Zeleen's enthusiasm.

I waited until the noise subsided.

"Alright," I said. "So you keep the gold rolling, we'll keep the cheese rolling, and we'll both be friends forever."

It is not the most stirring speech I have ever given. But it was accurate.

What followed was the more interesting conversation — the one Vukradin was not part of.

Horia, once Zeleen's delegation had departed into the forest with the specific energy of people who have been told exactly what consequences await them, turned to us with something approaching relief.

"I was gonna propose you give me some money so I can go buy a fake gem, but you know what? Your solution is way better, man."

I did not say anything. There is wisdom in letting a person finish arriving at your conclusion before you confirm it.

Brewbarry was already ahead of it.

"Let's call the fake gem Plan B," he said, "and in case the were-rats, you know, fail — which I have high expectation of them failing — "

Sensible. I co-signed that assessment with a small nod. The were-rats were motivated, which was something, but motivation and competence are not the same resource, and I had not formed a high opinion of their reserves of the latter.

The funding question was where it got interesting. Horia wanted fifty gold pieces sourced from the mine's own accounts. Brewbarry's reaction was immediate and genuine.

"You want gold. You… you're working a gold mine. You see the irony of what you're just saying, right?"

Horia, to her credit, had a document for this.

"According to… look, I'm a big believer in contractual obligations and procedures and rules. And if you'll notice, we have a no-skimming clause that Mr. Bucherton insisted we put in."

Brewbarry stared at her.

"How about the Barbarian in your face clause? Did you read that in your contract?"

Horia elected to propose a solution before that negotiating point was developed further.

"I'm perfectly happy to have an addendum signed by Mr. Brewberry. Thumbprints."

"Alright, I'm out of ink. How about I draw a little blood from you, so we can sign that addendum?"

There was a brief pause. Horia appeared to perform some rapid recalculation.

"I think, I think we could assume that the addendum is signed."

She tried to lean in conspiratorially and encountered the geometry of a three-foot dwarf attempting to whisper to a seven-foot Goliath. She was aiming approximately at his kneecap. To her credit, she adjusted, looked up, and delivered the intelligence that mattered:

"I do suggest we keep this little addendum between ourselves. I don't think Mr. Rukradin would be open to these kinds of negotiations."

Brewbarry looked momentarily puzzled.

"What do you mean? It's honest work. You're, you know, using gold from the gold mine to buy a fake gem so that we can mine more gold."

I kept my expression entirely neutral. The logic was not wrong, exactly. It was just the kind of logic that Vukradin would spend forty minutes unpacking from the wrong direction — and we didn't have forty minutes, and the mine needed to function, and a man whose theology includes the phrase *conflict-free gold* was perhaps not the ideal audience for a scheme involving a fake mystical gem and a superstitious foreman who believed veins of earth metal had feelings.

Some things are better handled quietly.

I said nothing. That was my signature on the addendum.
