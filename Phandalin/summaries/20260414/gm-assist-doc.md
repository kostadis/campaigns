## Soma — Leveling Up and Strategic Planning

The cold was still in my bones from the rooftop when it happened — that particular stillness that settles after a fight, when the body finally stops insisting it's about to die. The others were taking stock of themselves. I was doing the same thing I always do: noticing what changed.

Something had shifted in me. I could feel it the way you feel a tide turning before you can see it — a quiet deepening, like another ring added to the heartwood. Vukradin, characteristically, had not reached the same place. He'd given everything he had up there. I noted this without comment.

Valphine was already exploring what she'd become, turning the new thing over in her mind the way you turn a stone in your hand to understand its weight. There was something about bonds — a way of threading her presence between allies so that courage could travel along the connection. She described it with that particular intensity she brings to all divine matters, and I thought: *that's a cleric thing*. Real, useful, but built on the assumption that proximity to other people is safe.

Then came the discussion I've learned to recognize: the hypothetical combat mathematics that the others love and that I endure. Someone raised the possibility of a sixth-level ability that would let a protector throw themselves into harm's path — teleport to a wounded ally and absorb what was meant for them.

I listened.

I thought about what I could now do with abjuration — a proper warding zone, the kind that would seal off extra-planar filth and give us clean ground to stand on. I said so: the casting time makes it useless mid-fight. One minute. Might as well ask the aboleth to wait. It's the sort of power that matters *before* things go wrong, not while they're going wrong, and we are almost never anywhere before things go wrong.

That left the question I'd actually been turning over since the fight ended.

Spirit Guardians. I know their value. Spectral forms orbiting like satellites, punishing anything that comes close. Meril never used them — she didn't need to, she had the forest itself — but I've seen what they do, and I understand the appeal. Hit things before they reach you. Sensible. Tortles understand proximity.

But I kept thinking about Brewbarry on that rooftop. Vukradin bleeding through songs. The way this group tends to accumulate damage like a boat taking on water — steadily, from every direction, until someone asks why the floor is wet. The aura I could now carry would breathe life back into everyone near me, every few seconds, without me having to choose who gets the next casting. Continuous. Ambient. Like standing in good air.

I chose the aura.

It's not the flashiest choice. Meril would have approved, which is either a point in its favor or evidence that I'm still doing what I was trained to do rather than what I've figured out myself. I'm not sure yet. Probably both.

The discussion moved to tactics, the way it always does after a fight — everyone suddenly very wise about what should have happened differently. I half-listened and held the staff, and felt the subtle presence it always carries now, and thought: *one more thing I don't fully understand that Meril left me responsible for.*

I have so little time left. I would like to spend less of it explaining why I didn't pick the more offensive option.

## Brewbarry — Harvesting the Dragon

The dragon was still warm.

That's the thing nobody tells you. You think cold, you think ice — it's a white dragon, it breathes frost, it had been sleeping inside a mountain since before most of Phandalin's oldest residents were born. But the body was warm. Not warm like a fire. Warm like something that had been alive very recently and was only now beginning to understand it wasn't anymore.

I stood over it and didn't say anything for a while.

I have killed things. My tribe taught me to kill things before they taught me much else. Deer. Wolves. Men, sometimes, though that is not something I think about when I want to sleep well. But I had never stood over something like this. Never felt the particular silence that settles when a creature that large stops breathing. The battlements had been so full of noise — the cold and the wind and the thing's own roaring — and then suddenly they weren't. Just us. And it.

Cryovain. That was its name, apparently. It had a name. I find that harder to deal with than I expected.

Soma was already moving. She always moves like she knows exactly what she is doing, which I respect, even when I'm not completely sure she does. She knelt beside the carcass with a focus that reminded me of the best hunters I'd known — that total quiet you get when a person is really paying attention, when their hands know the work and their mind is somewhere deeper than thought. I have seen it in old men at the end of long hunts. I had not expected to see it from a tortle on a rooftop.

I helped where I could. Held things. Moved the body when it needed moving. A dragon, even a young one, is enormously heavy in the particular way of something that was made to be impossible to move, and the cold had started settling into the joints. My hands found purchase. I am good at this part — the physical work, the bracing and hauling. Vukradin is better at the talking. Soma is better at the knowing. I am good at holding things still.

She went for the throat first.

I will not pretend I was not squeamish. I grew up in the mountains. I have cleaned game. But there is something different about watching someone reach into a dragon's throat with that much precision and intention, knowing exactly what they are looking for, and finding it. The breath pouch came free carefully — a strange, swollen thing, heavier than it looked, and when it moved, you could feel the cold coming off it. Not temperature cold. Something older than temperature. The kind of cold that means something.

We held it, and I thought: there is a sound in there. Contained. Waiting.

I thought about what it would cost to become something that carried a sound like that.

The scales were easier to think about, or at least easier to look at. Soma worked them free from the hide with the same methodical attention, and they were beautiful in the particular way of things that were not made for beauty but ended up that way anyway — supple and pale and catching the light like polished stone. I held one up and turned it. My reflection came back distorted, cold-colored. I looked strange in it. I always look strange in things.

We talked about what to do with them.

I understood parts of this conversation better than others. Dragon scale mail — that I understood. Armor made from the thing that tried to kill you. There is an Uthgardt logic to that, even if my tribe would have done it differently, would have made it louder, would have worn the skull. Vukradin mentioned costs. He always mentions costs, which I appreciate even when the numbers make me go somewhere quiet in my head. The math of the surface world still sometimes feels like a language I am reading phonetically — I can say the words, I am not always certain I understand them.

But the breath pouch. That I understood differently.

Something made from the cold that was supposed to kill us, turned around, made into a tool. That is not just resource management. I have heard Vukradin use that phrase and I think it means counting things. This was something more. This was taking what was done to you and deciding it was yours now.

I don't have much that is mine. A sword that glows faintly wrong. A helm that makes my eyes look like something you should be afraid of in the dark. The exile took most of what I had, and I became something after that — something I am not proud of, something I drank my way into and am still, slowly, drinking my way back out of. The mine shares are something. The studio is something, or will be. But those are futures. Partial stakes in things not yet real.

These scales were real. This pouch was real. We had killed something enormous and terrible and now it was material.

I held a scale in my palm for a moment longer than was probably necessary.

Phandalin was next. The Mountain's Toe Mine on the way. I knew both of those things already. I let myself have one more moment with the weight of the scale in my hand — the texture of it, the way it was just warm enough to be unsettling — and then I set it down with the others and helped pack what we had taken.

We were going home. Or at least, the closest thing I currently have to one.

That felt like enough.

## Valphine — Inspection and Strike at Mountain Toes Gold Mine

The mine sat quiet at the mouth of the mountain, which was itself the problem.

I noticed it before we were through the gate — that particular absence of sound that means something has gone wrong with money. No picks. No cart-wheels grinding on stone. No vibration in the earth under my feet, the deep percussion of work. Just wind, and the nervous shuffling of the Whiskered Gang, who were arranged outside the entrance in a way that suggested they had been standing there for some time doing precisely nothing.

Zeleen Varnaster saw us coming and rearranged his face into something optimistic.

"Hey, boss! What's up?"

I looked at him for a moment. I let the silence sit.

"Are you working hard today?"

It was a simple question. I delivered it the way you might read an inventory list — flat, procedural, without particular feeling. Zeleen's expression crumpled into something resembling a man watching his house burn down.

"They've been working really hard. I promise you, boss, they've been working really, really hard."

He gestured vaguely toward the interior of the mine, where no sounds whatsoever were occurring. I filed that away and said nothing. Surface dwellers have a touching faith in assertion. They believe that if you say a thing with sufficient conviction, it becomes more true. Drow society had disabused me of that notion approximately eleven minutes into childhood.

We went inside.

Brewbarry's confusion at least had the virtue of being genuine. He looked at the equipment — idle picks, empty carts, tools that hadn't been touched — and something in him simply couldn't reconcile the image.

"Disconnect," he said. "What is wrong with those tools? I mean, what happened to Rock and Stone?"

The answer, when it came from the foreman Dwolin the Grout, was delivered with the particular gravity of a man who has spent his entire adult life being right about something no one else understands.

"You have to understand, gold. A gold vein is shy and must be treated with absolute respect. If you don't treat it with the respect, it doesn't come out."

I processed that. A shy gold vein. The earth itself, apparently, had feelings about the scheduling.

Horia Stonetallow stepped in beside him with the weary affection of someone who has explained a difficult colleague at great length on many previous occasions.

"Dwolin is the best seeker of gold I have ever known. He's got his little rituals, and his little gimmicks, and his little things…"

She trailed off in the way people do when they love someone who is also exhausting. I had some sympathy for the position. I found myself thinking of the early days of my temple, when I'd discovered that spiritual conviction and organizational coherence did not always arrive together.

But that was a problem for later. The present problem was a mine that was not mining, and a gang of were-rats who appeared to have concluded that standing near the mine counted as doing their job.

I called the light.

Daylight erupted from a point above us — not the soft glow of torches or the polite illumination of a lantern, but the full, searing brilliance of midday sun compressed into an enclosed space, pouring into every corner. The were-rat guards flinched as one, a ripple of physical discomfort running through them. Good. Lathander does not do gentle reminders.

I surveyed the scene in its sudden clarity.

"This is…" I paused, letting the light do its work. "Lathander won't be pleased."

Then I turned back to Zeleen.

"Are these dwarves mining?" I asked. "Did you tell me that these dwarves are mining? Are these dwarves mining?"

He opened his mouth.

I gestured to the empty minecart beside us.

"Is there gold in this minecart?"

"No, but Darwin says that we need the Earthstone Gem before we can get the gold."

"And have you gotten this earth stone gem for the dwarves?"

Stunned silence, which is a particular type of answer. Then:

"Boss, we're paid to protect the mine, not to go on fetch quests."

Brewbarry had reached the end of his patience, which, to his credit, he had extended further than I might have expected.

"The only reason you're protecting the mine is because the mine is useful to us to acquire gold. If the mine is not producing gold, then you're not doing your job."

Admirably direct. I followed through.

"It means… pull your fingers out of your rat's ass, and do whatever is necessary for the dwarves to be happy miners. We want to hear picks."

Zeleen looked between the two of us with the expression of a man searching for a loophole in a contract that contains no loopholes.

"Okay, so what does that mean? Like, so we can stay here then, right? Because you don't need the mine, right?"

I stared at him. I truly did not know where to begin with that.

He tried again.

"Well, boss, I mean, I think you make an excellent point. I think you're making an excellent point, but, like, I mean to be rude, but to get this gem, we have to go into the forest where the orcs are, and this is more of a…"

I let him get that far. It seemed fair. Then:

"Is that any of my problems? Am I getting paid with fees to, you know, make sure that the mine is productive? What am I paying you for? If all you do is eat the cheese, and the mine is not producing gold, then I'm wasting my cheese, aren't I?"

He swallowed.

"A waste of cheese," I said. "That's what you are. Unless you go into the forest, find the gem, get the bloody dwarves to mine. Is that how you want to be remembered?"

There is a particular moment in any negotiation when the other person's will simply gives way. You can feel it — the shoulders drop a fraction, the eyes stop calculating. Zeleen reached that moment. He reached it loudly.

"I think we could… we could make that happen, Mr. Boss. Mr. Right."

Behind him, I heard him screaming at his were-rats that they hadn't done it yet, that this was completely taken care of, that Mr. Boss had every confidence in them. Or words to that effect.

Brewbarry, for his part, was tapping his halberd against his palm with the methodical patience of someone who has learned that visual communication saves time. It did seem to accelerate Zeleen's enthusiasm.

I waited until the noise subsided.

"Alright," I said. "So you keep the gold rolling, we'll keep the cheese rolling, and we'll both be friends forever."

It is not the most stirring speech I have ever given. But it was accurate.

What followed was the more interesting conversation — the one Vukradin was not part of.

Horia, once Zeleen's delegation had departed into the forest with the specific energy of people who have been told exactly what consequences await them, turned to us with something approaching relief.

"I was gonna propose you give me some money so I can go buy a fake gem, but you know what? Your solution is way better, man."

I did not say anything. There is wisdom in letting a person finish arriving at your conclusion before you confirm it.

Brewbarry was already ahead of it.

"Let's call the fake gem Plan B," he said, "and in case the were-rats, you know, fail — which I have high expectation of them failing — "

Sensible. I co-signed that assessment with a small nod. The were-rats were motivated, which was something, but motivation and competence are not the same resource, and I had not formed a high opinion of their reserves of the latter.

The funding question was where it got interesting. Horia wanted fifty gold pieces sourced from the mine's own accounts. Brewbarry's reaction was immediate and genuine.

"You want gold. You… you're working a gold mine. You see the irony of what you're just saying, right?"

Horia, to her credit, had a document for this.

"According to… look, I'm a big believer in contractual obligations and procedures and rules. And if you'll notice, we have a no-skimming clause that Mr. Bucherton insisted we put in."

Brewbarry stared at her.

"How about the Barbarian in your face clause? Did you read that in your contract?"

Horia elected to propose a solution before that negotiating point was developed further.

"I'm perfectly happy to have an addendum signed by Mr. Brewberry. Thumbprints."

"Alright, I'm out of ink. How about I draw a little blood from you, so we can sign that addendum?"

There was a brief pause. Horia appeared to perform some rapid recalculation.

"I think, I think we could assume that the addendum is signed."

She tried to lean in conspiratorially and encountered the geometry of a three-foot dwarf attempting to whisper to a seven-foot Goliath. She was aiming approximately at his kneecap. To her credit, she adjusted, looked up, and delivered the intelligence that mattered:

"I do suggest we keep this little addendum between ourselves. I don't think Mr. Rukradin would be open to these kinds of negotiations."

Brewbarry looked momentarily puzzled.

"What do you mean? It's honest work. You're, you know, using gold from the gold mine to buy a fake gem so that we can mine more gold."

I kept my expression entirely neutral. The logic was not wrong, exactly. It was just the kind of logic that Vukradin would spend forty minutes unpacking from the wrong direction — and we didn't have forty minutes, and the mine needed to function, and a man whose theology includes the phrase *conflict-free gold* was perhaps not the ideal audience for a scheme involving a fake mystical gem and a superstitious foreman who believed veins of earth metal had feelings.

Some things are better handled quietly.

I said nothing. That was my signature on the addendum.

## Brewbarry — A Hero's Welcome in Phandalin

The road into Phandalin felt different when people were glad you were on it.

I don't know how to explain that. My tribe never had a road like this. We had paths through rock and wind, and when you came back from somewhere, people checked whether you'd brought the right things home. They didn't run to meet you. They didn't put their small hands up for you to hit with your large ones. But these children did, streaming out from between buildings, bare feet on the dirt, and I had to slow down so I didn't knock any of them over.

I hit back. Gently. Every hand.

They were cheering for something. For us, I suppose, though it took me a moment to understand that. The dragon parts helped. We'd brought pieces of what had come down on this town — scales, a claw, evidence that the thing was real and that something had ended — and the crowd read that faster than any announcement. A murmur went through, then a shout, then something that was properly loud in a way that pressed against my chest from the outside rather than from within it.

I stood at the edge of that sound and let it happen.

Harbin Wester found his way to the front of it, naturally. The man has a gift for finding fronts of things. He was already talking by the time I registered he'd started, his voice arranged into the particular shape of someone taking a careful share of something they did not earn.

"This is exactly why we needed a privy council with the extraordinary talents of Mr. Vucrodin. And without… I just cannot stress enough how with his undefatigable support and his management to lead this group of heroic adventures, the threat of dragons has abated."

I looked at Vukradin. Vukradin had the face he always has when Harbin speaks — attentive, gracious, containing something that is neither of those things. The crowd didn't notice. They cheered again. I let it wash over me and decided it was fine. Harbin had been afraid. People who are afraid do strange things with credit. I understand that.

The festival came together the way Phandalin does things — quickly, practically, with someone's cousin producing a fiddle from somewhere and someone else arriving with bread that was still warm. The town green filled in around us. I found that I kept being handed things: a small cake, a ribbon from a child who had apparently decided I needed one, the corner position at the longest table, which seemed to be the seat of honor and which I occupied because it fit.

And then the innkeeper came.

I know the innkeeper. I have spent meaningful time at his bar. He is a man who pours drinks with a considered expression, like each one is a small judgment he is making about you. He had a bottle under his arm that he was treating differently from usual bottles — keeping it against his body rather than carrying it by the neck — and his expression was the specific one of someone about to give you something they've been saving.

"I always gave you the good stuff," he said, and set a cup in front of me. "This is the very good stuff. And Brewbarry," — he said my name correctly, which matters more to me than it probably should — "I only have two barrels. So you're just gonna have to, like, remember that."

I picked up the cup. I looked at it. I looked at him.

"I'll be the judge of that," I said.

I drank.

It was better. It was genuinely, measurably better — the difference between a fire that heats a room and a fire that *is* the room. I held it in my mouth for a moment before I swallowed, because I have learned, since the tribe, that some things are worth the extra second. Then I set the cup down and looked up at the festival happening around me, and the children still moving through it, and Vukradin laughing at something across the green, and I thought: I signed a thing. I pressed my thumb into wax for this town. I brought my halberd to a mountain for this town. And this town gave me its best barrel.

That is an exchange that makes sense to me.

I was still sitting with that when Ser Kaelen appeared at the edge of the celebration with the particular posture of a man who needs to ruin something pleasant. He moved through the crowd without taking any of it in, which told me the urgency was real. He found Soma first.

"Soma," he said. "We must speak urgently."

I watched them. The fiddle kept playing. Somewhere a child knocked over a cup and someone laughed. I put my hand around the special reserve and waited to find out what the pleasant thing was that was about to end.

## Soma — Urgent Council at the Temple of Lathander

We came down from the mountain smelling of dragon and cold stone, and somehow ended up in a temple discussing politics. That's the job, apparently.

Valphine's acolytes met us at the door with the particular enthusiasm of people who have found religion and want everyone to know it. They showed her their new blisters. Sun damage, mostly — too much time in the courtyard, not enough shade. I looked at the angry red patches on their forearms and thought: there are easier ways to demonstrate devotion. I did not say this. I've learned to keep certain observations to myself.

Ser Kaelen was already waiting inside, which told me the news was urgent enough that he hadn't stopped to consider whether we wanted it yet.

He told us about Adabra.

She'd found out about the Whispering Grove. What really happened — the treant, the concealment, all of it. And she had decided, in the way that Adabra always decides things, that the correct response was to convene a gathering and be loudly right about it in front of as many people as possible.

"Adabra has found out about what really happened at the Whispering Grove," Kaelen said. "She has decided, she has gone with her allies and is going to attempt to convince the druids that your reckless interventionism has brought harm to the forests."

I'd been waiting for this. Not hoping for it — waiting for it. There's a difference.

I thought about the last time I'd spoken to Adabra, and how she'd looked at me with that particular expression that means *you understand*, and how I'd let her think that. It sat in my chest like something undigested.

"She's confused," Kaelen continued, with the careful manner of a man delivering a medical diagnosis. "She sees the harm that was caused by those who defended against the dragons, and thinks that the problem was that the defenders against the dragons were the problem, not the dragons. She sees the planar incursions as… not the symptoms, because she never experienced those symptoms. She experienced the consequences of war."

"Yeah," I said. "She's bringing her own war out here."

There wasn't much else to say about it. Adabra had drawn a conclusion from her evidence and her evidence was real — she just hadn't seen the part that came before it. That's not stupidity. It's a gap. Gaps don't argue themselves closed.

I asked whether the Lords Alliance or the Order could do anything about it.

Kaelen shook his head. "The Lord's Alliance doesn't want to intervene, and the Order of the Gauntlet, although we would like to intervene, cannot, because, well, the War of the Dragons, and then the War of the Giants, and then the war… Let's just say there's fewer of us to go around these days."

"It's all on us, huh?"

I didn't say it with despair. I said it as a fact, the way you'd note that it's raining. He confirmed it was.

Then he mentioned the staff.

He said it carefully, the way men of sword and action say things they don't entirely understand. He said it held symbolism among the druids — standing, he called it — and that it might tip the balance at the conclave if I were willing to bring it.

"Standing with the druids — I'm barely a druid myself!"

It came out more blunt than I intended, but it was true. Meril sent me out after a year. One year of learning something that takes decades, with a staff I still don't fully understand, from a teacher who thought sending me off with it was the right thing to do and never quite explained why. I know what the staff does in my hands. I don't always know what it means.

"Yes, but apparently this staff is… I don't… look, look, druids have rituals and beliefs that I, as a… as a man of sword and action, don't always understand," Kaelen said. He had the grace to look slightly embarrassed about it.

I can respect that. Honest uncertainty is underrated.

Then he told me the rest of it.

"The only caution I give you, Soma, is that there are some there who have suggested that they should even evaluate whether you should retain the staff."

The room didn't change. The light was the same. The blistered acolytes were still blistered somewhere behind me. But something went very still inside my shell, which is where I keep the things that are not for public inspection.

*Retain the staff.* As if it were a matter of proper custody. As if Meril's hands weren't in the grain of it, and Xanth's recognition of it hadn't meant what I knew it meant.

The staff has fewer leaves than it once did. I've noticed. I notice things like that.

"They're not taking it from me," I said. "This has at least sentimental value."

Sentimental. I chose that word deliberately, because it is the word that ends conversations. Nobody argues with sentiment. What I did not say is that the staff is the last physical thing that connects me to Meril, and that I am forty-five years old, and that whatever time I have left I intend to spend it with the things that matter close at hand. What I did not say is that I don't fully know what this staff is yet, and that the not-knowing feels important — like something I need to work out for myself, in my own time, without a conclave of druids deciding the answer for me.

Kaelen nodded. Then he put his hand on the hilt of his sword — not drawing it, just touching it, the way you do when something resonates — and said, with a kind of quiet conviction: "I agree with you. A stat — that no order can take my sword."

It was a little mangled, as declarations go. But I understood what he meant. Some things are yours not because you earned them or because you're qualified to hold them, but because they chose you and you chose them back, and that's its own kind of legitimacy.

I held the staff a little tighter and said nothing more about it.

Kaelen's attention had already shifted to Brewbarry, who had his halberd out in that way large people sometimes carry weapons — not threateningly, just present.

"Is that a halberd?" Kaelen said, with the expression of a man who has opinions about polearms. "Because that is a very fine halberd, Brewberry. We should parry."

Brewbarry looked at him with the patience of someone who has had this offer before. "You don't want to have a conversation with it, trust me."

I looked at the two of them — a knight admiring a weapon that had very recently helped fight a dragon, and a Goliath who was done being interesting about it — and felt something loosen slightly in my chest. Not everything, but enough.

The conclave was coming. The druids might try to take the staff. Adabra had already decided we were the problem.

I am old. I have seen much. I am not going to give anyone my staff.
