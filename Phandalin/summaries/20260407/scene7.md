## Vukradin — A Message from the Gauntlet

The Carver was dead. The dragon was dead. The possessed man who'd carried the obsidian sword onto this rooftop was dead, and the thieves who'd accompanied him were dead, and I was standing on the battlements of Icespire Hold with the cold of the peaks pressing in from every direction, and the silence that follows a thing being over.

I reached for the sending.

It is, fundamentally, a twenty-five-word telegram fired across the ether by means of a copper wire you hold in your fingers — which I had only recently realized makes it, in a meaningful metaphysical sense, a telephone. Brewbarry had found this funnier than I'd expected. I found it clarifying. You pick up the receiver, you say your piece, the other end picks up. Simple. Civilized. The sort of thing that should have been invented sooner.

I composed it in my head while the smoke was still settling. *The dragon is dead. So is the Carver. We're heading to Neverwinter.* Twenty-five words. I am not, generally, a man of twenty-five words, but the spell imposes discipline and I have learned to be grateful for it.

I sent it.

Ser Kaelen Thorn, Knight of the Order of the Gauntlet, Candlekeep correspondent, man who irons his letters before sending them — Ser Kaelen came back with what I can only describe as a prepared statement.

"Praise Torm! Your righteous victory brings light to the north. The gauntlet awaits your arrival in Phandalin. We shall celebrate your conflict-free triumph together."

There was a pause on the rooftop. A small, shared pause, the kind that happens when something has landed in a register no one quite expected.

Then Brewbarry said, with the flat authority of a man delivering a verdict: "Fighting a fucking dragon — that was conflict."

He was not wrong.

I turned the phrase over in my mind. *Conflict-free triumph.* He had used my language. My exact language, the shorthand I use for a very particular kind of ethical accounting — gold that came without a body attached, work that doesn't require you to look away from anything afterward. He had taken my vocabulary and deployed it in a sentence about a dragon fight in which at least three people had died and I had paralyzed a man's body against his will, and he had done it without apparent irony.

Which meant he had studied me. Or he had listened carefully enough to notice what I cared about. Or he had been coached. None of those possibilities were entirely comfortable.

And tucked inside the polished phrasing, like a directive someone hoped you'd miss: *come to Phandalin quick. Issues with the Emerald Enclave.*

That was what the second sending was, the one underneath the ceremonial one, the real twenty-five words stripped of the flourishes. Adabra's faction. The Enclave cooling on us. Something had moved while we were busy at the top of a mountain.

He's about as close as someone we can actually trust as anybody.

I heard myself say it and felt the sentence curdle slightly even as it left my mouth.

"No," I said. "I don't trust him. He's as close as anybody."

Which is true. Which is the honest version. Which is not the same thing as trustworthy — it only means the competition is weak. In a field of confirmed liars, a man who deploys your own principles back at you in a Sending response is, statistically, your best option. That is not a ringing endorsement. That is a description of the field.

Brewbarry had already moved past the analysis. He had heard the word *celebrate* — that was the operative word, that was the anchor — and everything else was logistical.

"I heard 'celebrate,'" he said. "Say no more. Let's go."

And honestly. Honestly. I could not argue with the logic. The dragon was dead. The Carver was gone. We were alive and standing on the highest point in the region in the cold clean air of the peaks, and somewhere at the bottom of this mountain there was a town that had been frightened for a long time, and we were going to go tell them they didn't need to be frightened anymore, and then apparently we were going to go deal with whatever had gone wrong in Phandalin while we were away.

I just wanted my goddamn music studio. I ended up having to save the world.

That's the story, more or less. I composed twenty-five words into a copper wire and sent them into the sky, and what came back was someone else's vocabulary worn over mine like a borrowed coat, and a polite instruction to hurry up and go deal with the next thing.

The sending spell. Magical telecommunications. Someone should write a song about it.
