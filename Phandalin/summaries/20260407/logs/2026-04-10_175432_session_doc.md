## Section — Brewbarry — The Dragon's Mountain Fortress

The mountain did not care who climbed it. It sat there, indifferent, carved stone and cold air and a dragon circling forty feet above the floor, and it waited to see who would still be standing when the whole thing was over.

I was still standing.

Barely. But that's a question of degrees, and I don't do degrees. You're standing or you're not. I was.

The summoning circle had been glowing when we found Cryovain — a low, sick light that pulsed like a second heartbeat, the Carver's possession spreading through the dragon the way cold spreads through meat. The dragon felt it. We could see it in the way it moved, something enormous and old being hollowed out from the inside. I've been accused of being drunk when I was sober, been told I was something I wasn't until I had no choice but to believe it. What they were doing to Cryovain was the same thing done to a creature that could raze a city. I didn't think it then. I just moved.

When I came up to my full height — the giant form settling over me like armor made of self — the dragon was already thirty feet above the stone floor, beyond reach of anything smaller, and I swung the Dragon Slayer upward with everything I had. I felt it connect. Felt it twice more. The blade was made for this. I was made for this, or something close enough to it that the distinction didn't matter on a rooftop in the middle of a mountain.

Then the dragon breathed.

The first blast hit me like being buried in the coldest thing I've ever touched. Not a burning cold, just cold — the kind that reaches inside your armor, inside your skin, into whatever's underneath skin. I heard the ice forming on the stone around me. I heard nothing else.

And I was still standing.

The second breath came before I'd finished deciding what to do about the first. It found the same body, the same armor, the same Goliath who was apparently not done yet. The cold went deeper. The margins I'd been fighting with narrowed to something I could almost measure. Almost. What was left in me was not strength exactly — it was the body's last argument against stopping, and it was running mostly on stubbornness and years of being hit by things that thought they'd finished me.

I did not go down.

I don't know what that cost me. I know what it felt like, which is that it felt like nothing for a few seconds, which is how you know it was bad.

Valphine found the dragon with sacred flame at the end — that clean, burning light she carries cutting through the cold and the possession-glow and whatever the Carver thought it was building here. Cryovain dropped. And with it, the Carver unraveled — not dramatically, not with a sound, just the slow bleeding away of something that had been hungry for a very long time, until the hunger was gone and the air was just air again. The summoning circle went dark.

Two deaths at once. One of flesh, one of something older. The fortress belonged to silence for a moment, and I let it.

There was a veteran on the floor. Paralyzed, still breathing. Soma was already talking.

"Don't kill, don't kill! He was just a victim!"

She wasn't wrong. She's rarely wrong. I looked at the man on the stone and I thought of someone afraid, of a tribe lost, of the particular expression fear makes on a face that doesn't want you to see it — and then I looked at everything the dragon had been made to be and what this man had been part of building, and I pointed at the body and said into the stone walls:

"I win, you lose — tradition upheld."

It was not an argument. It was a verdict. I know the difference. The celebration and the mercy were pulling in opposite directions, and I had a voice loud enough to fill the room. Soma had her record. I had mine.

The fortress was cold and quiet and ours.

I turned toward Vukradin — the man who plays music that doesn't hit like a drum, that slides into your head and stays there — and I said what felt true.

"I hope this will inspire a song in your new music studio."

I meant it. That's the whole thing, for me — the part that deserves to last, the part that someone else needs to keep because I'm not the keeping type. Vukradin makes things last. I just make things happen. Between the two of us, something might actually survive long enough to matter.

There was no booze. Of course there was no booze. There had been a barrel, earlier, that had been mine and was now gone, and when someone asked about it I gave them the only answer that covered both the question and the explanation:

"I was thirsty."

And then, because it also needed saying:

"I just want to celebrate our victory."

After two blasts of freezing breath on what was barely enough left to call a body, I had earned that barrel. Every drop of it.

Root Smasher stands.

That's the story.
