## Section — Vukradin — The Spoils of Victory

The treasure was a block. That is perhaps the most honest way to describe it — not a chest, not a pile, not the scattered artful coinage you see in illustrations. A block of fused ice and residium and gold and gems and trade bars, all melted and refrozen together into something that had lost its individual components entirely. Three thousand gold, more or less, by estimation. None of it spendable. None of it separable, apparently, without the services of a planar theorist, because Soma identified — with the flat certainty she reserves for things that are absurd and true simultaneously — that the ice had been fused through some kind of planar trickery.

So: a fortune I could not spend, welded into a frozen mass by magic I did not understand, atop a tower I had just helped clear of a dragon and a body-snatching alien intelligence.

I stood there looking at it and thought: *yes, this is approximately how things go for me.*

The obsidian sword was there too — the Carver's weapon, the instrument of the domination ritual, the thing we had spent the better part of the last several hours trying to stop from completing its work on a white dragon. I picked it up. I examined it. And then I noticed the crystal in the pommel, and my heart did something complicated and involuntary.

It was a mental amplifier. The gem could project music — *directly into the minds of an audience.* No room acoustics to fight. No back-row listeners who couldn't quite hear. No bad nights where the lute is slightly out of tune and nobody tells you until afterward. Just thought, translated directly into experience, received by every mind in range with perfect clarity.

I stood there holding it and understood, in my bones, what this instrument could do. What *I* could do with it.

I said: "It seems like this was sent by the devil to tempt me, yes. Yes, save me."

The GM said something about how a lot of care and attention had gone into the selection of that phrase — *mental amplifier* — and I was already three steps ahead, because I had already named it.

"It is absolutely the Bluetooth sword. Done."

There was protest. There is always protest when I name something correctly on the first attempt. I accepted the clarification — "Magical Bluetooth swap, got it, sorry, thanks for clarifying" — because I am a reasonable person and I am capable of updating my terminology when presented with additional product information. The underlying concept remained sound.

But here is the thing. Here is the thing about the gem.

It had to go back to its owner.

I know how this sounds. I know exactly how this sounds, standing on a frozen battlement holding the single most useful instrument for my particular professional ambitions that I have ever encountered, freshly looted from the corpse of an alien entity that had been trying to use it to enslave a dragon. I know. And I said it anyway, because it is true: the gem belongs to someone, and that someone is not me, and until I can establish through proper documentation that the item has no rightful owner or that the rightful owner consents to transfer, I cannot keep it. That is the principle. That is what I said I would do. "Handcrafted for my personal quest," I told it, because I am aware of the irony even when I cannot act against it.

"Save me," I had said.

I meant it.

---

The main block of treasure was the next problem. Three thousand gold, mostly unspendable, and already I could feel the shape of the argument forming: *who does it belong to?* The gold a dragon hoards over a long life does not start out belonging to the dragon. It starts out belonging to somebody. Merchants, probably. Villagers, probably. People the dragon killed or displaced or simply robbed by existing in a radius that made trade impossible. The fact that it had then been fused into a residium-ice block by planar magic did not change the underlying ownership question. It just made it harder to divide.

I said this. I framed it as a criminal analogy because I think those land better than philosophical abstractions: scratching the serial number off a stolen item does not make it yours. That is what the ice was. Scratched-off serial numbers. The moral situation was unchanged.

Soma looked at the sky and said: "Look, the universe is safe, right? Like, or, you know, the plane of existence is safe, that's what matters most, nothing else."

She is not wrong. She is almost never wrong about the large shape of things. But the large shape of things and the specific ethical obligation are not the same question, and I was operating on the specific ethical obligation, which required me to declare the gold stolen goods and begin the documentation process.

"I am enraged," I said, "at all the corruption and evil that is stinying me from my music studio."

*Stynying.* I meant *stymying.* It came out wrong because I was genuinely upset and because Soma's cosmological calm has a way of making my particular frustrations sound small, which they are not, they are enormous, they are everything I have been working toward for years.

Harbin Wester would try to claim credit. The Lord's Alliance would try to claim the gold. Some functionary in Neverwinter would produce documentation explaining why the frozen block of dragon treasure was, actually, technically, the property of the Crown, and I would be left having saved the world and earned nothing for it except a sending stone message from Ser Kaelen telling me to come celebrate in Phandalin. "Conflict-free triumph," he had said. He had studied my vocabulary. He had absorbed it and fed it back to me in twenty-five words. I did not know whether to be moved or suspicious, and I was not sure those were different reactions.

"He's about as close as someone we can actually trust as anybody," I said.

Beat.

"No, I don't trust him. He's as close as anybody."

That sentence contains everything I currently believe about the people around me.

---

And then the statue.

The GM explained it simply, which is the kindest and most damaging way to deliver bad news: there was a choice we had to make, we made a choice, and the particular individual we wanted to return the blue quartz mermaid to was no longer available for us to return it to.

We chose the dragon. We chose to come here, to stop the Carver, to save Icespire and by extension Phandalin and by extension whatever cosmological safety Soma was gesturing at with her plane-of-existence framing. We made the right call. I believe that.

"Well, then that, you know, fuck them," I said. "We gotta go. Her next of kin? And they're in Neverwinter."

But while I was saying it, there was a voice underneath the words — not a voice from outside, just the particular internal noise that shows up when you are doing the right thing and paying a price for it simultaneously. *You could have your music studio. All you have to do is compromise a few principles here and there.*

"You don't get it," I said.

Which is true. The voice doesn't get it. That is the whole point of having principles — they are not negotiable tools you deploy when convenient and set aside when costly. They are the thing you are. The moment you start treating them as items in a cost-benefit analysis, you have already lost them. I know this. I have always known this.

"I just wanted my goddamn music studio," I said, "and I ended up having to go save the world."

Brewbarry said he heard the word "celebrate" and that was enough. Ser Kaelen's sending had mentioned it, and Brewbarry's relationship to celebration is like his relationship to drink: he orients toward it by instinct, without requiring further information. We didn't have any drink. He was thirsty. He had killed two veterans and helped drop a dragon, and he was standing on a frozen battlement being thirsty, and somehow this was funnier than anything else that had happened all day.

The block of gold sat there, unfissured, inseparable, belonging to someone we had not yet identified.

The gem sat in the pommel of the sword, perfectly designed for a purpose I was not permitted to use it for yet.

The Carver was gone, and the world was, apparently, safe, and the plane of existence was intact, and the right owner of the blue quartz mermaid was unavailable, and Ser Kaelen was waiting in Phandalin to celebrate our conflict-free triumph.

I just wanted my goddamn music studio.
