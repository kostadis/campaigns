## Section — Brewbarry — The Dragon's Mountain Fortress

The mountain fortress of Icespire Hold didn't look like somewhere you'd bring a dragon to heel. It looked like somewhere the mountain itself had given up trying to keep the cold out.

We found Cryovain inside a summoning circle on the rooftop battlements — a great ring of burning light, geometric and wrong, the kind of geometry that doesn't belong to any stonecutter or architect I've ever seen. The dragon stood at the center of it. Or was held at the center of it. The distinction mattered, and I wasn't sure which was true.

The Carver was doing something to him. That's the only way I know to say it. The obsidian weapon, the mental amplifier, whatever Vukradin wanted to call it — it was working. Cryovain's eyes had a film over them, a glaze I recognized from men I'd seen after too many days without sleep or water. Presence, but not quite home. He was fighting it. Or he had been. Hard to say how much of the dragon was still left in there.

Cryovain was enraged. Of course he was.

He launched off the stone and climbed the air in one beat of those enormous wings — thirty feet up in an instant, and suddenly my halberd was swinging at nothing. I stood there for a moment with the weapon raised and the wind from his ascent hitting me in the face like an open hand.

Valphine sent a bolt of divine light up after him. It carved a bright line through the cold air and missed. The mountain swallowed the light.

Then Soma did something quiet with her staff, and a column of silver moonlight descended out of nowhere, out of sky that didn't have a moon positioned for it — the kind of thing I have learned not to question about Soma. The moonbeam found the dragon and the dragon made a sound that I felt in my back teeth.

That opened something in me. I won't dress it up. I am Goliath. I was built for this.

I grew.

There is no cleaner way to describe what happens when the rage comes properly. I am already large. And then I am more. My reach extended, the battlements shrank, the thirty feet of air between me and the dragon became manageable. I brought the halberd up and then I brought it down, and then again, and then again — each strike finding the place where scales meet scale, where the neck joins the shoulder, where size means you can put weight behind every swing that would stagger a lesser creature. Cryovain was not a lesser creature.

But I hit him hard enough to matter. Several times.

I felt each impact as much as I delivered it. That's the honest account. The cold bites through everything when you're that exposed. Dragon breath is not like standing in bad weather. It reorganizes you. The first time it hit, I understood in a bodily way — not a thinking way, a bodily way — that I was at the edge of what I could absorb. The second time it hit, I was somewhere past that edge, running on a kind of forward momentum that isn't quite decision-making.

My body was doing the math. The math was not comfortable.

Soma, somewhere behind me, was saying don't kill, don't kill — he was just a victim. I heard her. I was not sure what to do with the information in that moment. Cryovain had just breathed ice through me twice and the Carver's influence was still inside him, still pressing, still trying to make the ritual stick. The Carver needed three more rounds. I had fewer rounds left than that if I took another hit like the last one.

This is what the tribe never understood about me. They thought the rage was the absence of thought. They were wrong. The rage is very clear. The rage is math.

The dragon came down. The halberd went in. I don't remember the exact sequence — that's also the honest account. What I remember is the sound the circle made when it broke. The burning geometry guttered and went out, and the Carver's essence — whatever it was, whatever had been reaching through that obsidian instrument into the mind of a white dragon — dissipated. Like breath in cold air. Like it had never been more than that.

Cryovain was dead before I consciously decided he was finished.

I stood there on the battlements in the aftermath, twice my normal size and bleeding from places I'd stopped cataloguing. The wind came off the mountain and I let it. My feet were still under me. That counted for something.

"I hope this will inspire a song in your new music studio."

I meant it. Vukradin needed to hear something like that. He carries weight that isn't his to carry all the time — the studio, the politics, the gold being clean or not clean, all of it pressing on a man who said, once, that he just wanted the music. This was the kind of thing songs were made of. A dragon. A mountain. A circle of burning light that failed. We had been here. We had done this. That deserved a song.

"I win, you lose — tradition upheld."

Also meant it. The tradition is real. I didn't invent it this session; I just keep upholding it, which is how traditions work.

"I just want to celebrate our victory."

This too.

There was no booze. Apparently we had climbed a mountain into a dragon's fortress and not brought any. I stood on the battlements, the cold coming in now that the adrenaline was making room for it, and I reported the only fact that seemed relevant.

"I was thirsty."
