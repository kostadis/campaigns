## Section — Vukradin — A Message from the Gauntlet

The spell has a material component that nobody thinks about until someone mentions it, and then the whole thing becomes suddenly, absurdly literal. Copper wire. That is what Sending runs on. A thread of copper stretched thin across however many hundreds of miles lie between a dragon-blasted rooftop and wherever Ser Kaelen Thorn is standing when the words arrive. Someone in the party said it aloud — *copper wire* — and I said *yes, per phone*, because that is exactly what it is. A magical telephone. We have been carrying magical telephones this entire time and just calling them spells.

I composed it mid-combat. This is worth noting. The dragon was still breathing, the Carver's vessel still frozen mid-ritual under Hold Person, my companions still bleeding and swinging and holding a rooftop together with tactical improvisation and stubbornness, and I was drafting a message in my head with the precise economy of a man who knows he has twenty-five words and a reputation to manage. *The dragon is dead, so is the Carver. We're heading to Neverwinter.* That is what I sent. A statement of fact, a declaration of intent, a very short performance review submitted to my nominal superior in the Order of the Gauntlet — an organization I remain skeptical of my membership in, a membership I wear pinned to my chest like a question I haven't decided to answer yet.

The reply came back fast. Too fast, almost, for a man who is supposed to be managing crises across the North.

"Praise Torm! Your righteous victory brings light to the north. The gauntlet awaits your arrival in Phandalin. We shall celebrate your conflict-free triumph together."

I read it twice.

*Conflict-free triumph.*

He learned that phrase from me. I use it the way other people use punctuation — it marks where my values go, the conceptual territory I have staked out and intend to defend. And here was Ser Kaelen Thorn, across however many hundreds of copper-wire miles, deploying it back at me like a man who had looked up what I care about and dressed his reply accordingly. Whether that is loyalty or calculation, I genuinely cannot tell you. Both would produce the same message. Both would sound exactly like that.

David said something about em dashes. About hearing them. About the whole reply having the texture of something that went through at least one revision before it was sent, which is technically impossible in the spell's mechanics but was not, aesthetically, wrong. Someone else — Brewbarry, I think, already orienting toward the exit, already thinking about the next thing — cut through the analysis with the only part of the message that mattered to him: *"I heard 'celebrate,' say no more. Let's go."*

I love Brewbarry. I want that on the record. He does not complicate things that do not need complicating.

But I stood there for a moment with the fading residue of a Sending in my chest — the copper-taste of magic spent — and I took the measure of the man who had replied.

"He's about as close as someone we can actually trust as anybody."

The sentence came out. I listened to it. I corrected it before anyone else could.

"No, I don't trust him. He's as close as anybody."

The correction comes before the sentence is cold. That is the honest version. The first version is the one I want to believe because it is simpler and because trust, even provisional trust, makes the next hundred miles easier. The second version is arithmetic. Ser Kaelen Thorn is at the top of a very short list because everyone else on that list is shorter still — because this land, as I have noted repeatedly and apparently cannot stop noting, is full of liars and cheats and people who will call you a saint in one breath and a thief in the next without registering any contradiction between the two.

*Well done. Come to Phandalin quick. Issues with the Emerald Enclave.*

Not twenty-five words — he had twenty-five, that was the cap, and he used less. He had room. He chose not to fill it. *Issues with the Emerald Enclave* is not a sentence that tells you anything. It is a sentence shaped to summon without informing, to create urgency without surrendering control of the briefing. I know what that sounds like because I have written songs that work the same way — you leave the gap, the listener fills it with their own fear, and then their fear does your work for you.

Phandalin, then. Not Neverwinter, not yet. Back to the town where I am apparently a public hero, which I find genuinely burdensome in ways I have not fully articulated to anyone, back to whatever Adabra has decided about us, back to whatever the Emerald Enclave problem looks like in daylight.

Brewbarry wanted a drink. We had none. He was thirsty. This is the condition in which we depart.

I just wanted my goddamn music studio, and I ended up having to go save the world.
