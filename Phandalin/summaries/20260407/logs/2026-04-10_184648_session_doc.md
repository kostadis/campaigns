## Section — Vukradin — A Message from the Gauntlet

The rooftop stank of blood and cold air and something else — ozone, or scorched stone, or the particular smell a dragon leaves behind when it's been reminded it can die.

I had maybe twenty-five words. That is the constraint of Sending: twenty-five words and then silence, the copper wire going taut, the spell collapsing in on itself like a poorly structured verse. You do not get to revise. You do not get to add an aside, or clarify what you meant by *dead*, or specify that when you say *the Carver* you mean the vessel, the entity, the whole enterprise of it. You get twenty-five words, and then the other person has twenty-five words, and then you're done.

*The dragon is dead,* I sent. *So is the Carver. We're heading to Neverwinter.*

I held the words in my head for a moment before releasing them, the way you might check tuning before a performance. Not because I thought they were wrong. Because I wanted to know what I was committing to by saying them out loud to Ser Kaelen Thorn, in a message that would arrive in his head as my voice, with my breath behind it, with no way to take it back.

The reply came almost instantly. That should have told me something.

*"Praise Torm! Your righteous victory brings light to the north. The gauntlet awaits your arrival in Phandalin. We shall celebrate your conflict-free triumph together."*

I stood there for a moment on the blood-wet stone.

*Conflict-free triumph.*

He had studied me. That was the only explanation. Somewhere in a very well-organized report — and there was definitely a report, Ser Kaelen is the kind of man who generates paperwork the way a campfire generates smoke, automatically and in all directions — someone had written down *Vukradin, conflict-free gold*, and he had filed it, and he had retrieved it, and he had deployed it back at me in my own voice. Whether that was loyalty or just very good operational intelligence, I genuinely could not tell. I still cannot tell.

"He's about as close as someone we can actually trust as anybody," I said.

A beat. The wind off the peaks. Brewbarry's breathing, still slowing from the fight.

"No, I don't trust him. He's as close as anybody."

That is the truest thing I said all day. It is not a high bar. It is the bar I have, and I am measuring against it honestly, which is more than I can say for most of the people I have met in this entire region, who have been, without exception, liars and schemers and claimants and opportunists. Ser Kaelen is a man with an institutional agenda and a very capable communications team and a *Praise Torm* that sounded like it was written by committee. He is also the most trustworthy person available to me. This is where I live now.

David — Valphine — said something about the Sending, about the material component. Copper wire. And I turned it over in my head: the spell literally runs on copper wire. We have been sending magical text messages. Every time I have pushed my voice through the weave to someone twenty miles away, I have been making a telephone call. The world invented telecommunications before the wheel, and nobody noticed.

"Yes," I said. "Per phone."

There was something relieving about that — the small absurdity of it. The way the universe keeps reassuring you that the systems underneath everything are essentially ridiculous.

What the message actually told me — past the *Praise Torm* and the *righteous victory* and the recycled vocabulary — was buried in the second draft, the one that must have slightly overrun the word count and come back corrected: *Come to Phandalin quick. Issues with the Emerald Enclave.*

That is twenty-five words compressed down to nine. That is not a man celebrating. That is a man managing. Adabra is in that sentence somewhere, and Lyra, and whatever the Enclave has decided about the Treant and the grove and all the things we said and didn't say. Phandalin first. Neverwinter second. The Emerald Enclave, apparently, has opinions about the order of operations.

Brewbarry, bless him, heard exactly one word.

"I heard 'celebrate,'" he said. "Say no more. Let's go."

And I laughed — actually laughed, short and real, the first thing all morning that felt like breathing — because he was right. We had killed a dragon. We had stopped whatever the Carver was about to become. The obsidian sword was not going to amplify anything into anyone's skull, not today, and the sky above Icespire Hold was blue and empty and ours.

The rest of it — Ser Kaelen's polished reply, the Enclave, the word *quick* that he slid in there like a blade between ribs — that was tomorrow's problem. Or Phandalin's problem. Or whoever-writes-the-reports problem.

I had wanted a music studio.

I had gotten considerably more than that.
