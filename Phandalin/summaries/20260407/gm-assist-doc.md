## Brewbarry — The Dragon's Mountain Fortress

The circle was wrong. That was the first thing I understood — not the Carver, not the sword, not the word "possession." The circle on the floor was wrong because nothing should glow like that. It should not pull at the air the way it did. It was hungry and patient and very old, and Cryovain was standing in the middle of it like something that had already been eaten and didn't know yet.

I have fought animals. I have fought men. I have fought things that were neither. But I had not seen a creature be unmade slowly, still breathing, while the thing doing the unmaking wore its eyes and moved its wings. The dragon's eyes were wrong too. Like two fires with someone else's hands on the bellows.

Then the eyes found us.

The roar cracked something loose in my chest — not fear, exactly. The word I would use is *recognition*. My body knew what that sound meant before my mind finished receiving it. This is a dying thing, it said. It is dying from the inside out and it is furious about it and it will take the world with it if it can.

Then Cryovain was thirty feet above us. Just — gone up. The rope of it, the ease of it, the way those wings swallowed the space between the dragon and the stone floor. Gone. And the melee reach I had — my arms, the halberd, everything I am — meant nothing. The fortress ceiling was the only thing keeping us together.

Valphine sent light after it. Bright, aimed, certain. It cut wide. I heard the shape of the miss in the sound — a crackle of intention and then silence where the hit should have been. She did not flinch. She never does. But I noticed.

Soma put a column of moonlight into the air. I did not know she could do that. Pale and cold and reaching up like a hand, and Cryovain couldn't move fast enough — it caught the wing, the flank, something. The sound the dragon made when the radiant light hit was not a roar. It was smaller. More personal. Like finding out something you trusted is not what you thought.

I know that sound. I have made that sound.

I did not think about what happened next. I do not think that is inaccurate — thinking was not the part of me I was using. Something in me opened up and the world got larger. The ceiling got lower. My hand on the halberd felt like it was my whole arm, and my arm felt like it was the length of a tree. I have done this before. It is always strange, the way the first step in the new size sounds different, heavier, like the floor is surprised.

The dragon was thirty feet up. The distance was less than it had been.

I hit it. And then I hit it again. And then again.

Bone and blade and scale. I could feel every strike like a conversation — *here*, *and here*, *and here*. The halberd knows what it is for. I do too.

## Valphine — The Fall of Cryovain

The breath came without warning — or rather, it came with exactly the warning a white dragon always gives, which is no warning at all. One moment Brewbarry was standing. The next, the air between us crystallized into something that had no business existing at this altitude, and the cold hit him like a wall being dropped from a great height. I watched him take it. I watched him *absorb* it, the way stone absorbs a hammer blow — not without damage, but without yielding. A handful of hit points. That was all that stood between the largest member of our party and the floor of Icespire Hold's battlements. I found myself cataloging this with the cool precision I reserve for things that genuinely impress me. He should not still be standing. The arithmetic didn't support it. And yet.

Vukradin moved. He sang something — the specific words are less important than the shape of what he was doing, which was making certainty where there had been probability. Bardic inspiration, that particular Eloquence-College trick of his, landing like a thumb pressed down on the scales. The critical strike found the dragon through some combination of his intervention and whatever the universe had already decided. I noted this. I note everything. It would have been useful to have that precision earlier in the fight, but I had not been asked, and I don't offer things that haven't been asked for.

Soma had been doing what Soma does, which is to work with the quiet determination of someone who understands that patience is a form of power. The moonbeam had been burning through Cryovain's scales for what felt like longer than the fight warranted — a searing column of silver-white that she had held in place through sheer druidic will, the kind of sustained effort that surface dwellers tend to overlook because it doesn't announce itself. I do not overlook it. I recognize discipline when I see it, whatever form it takes. The dragon was already dying. We simply hadn't told it yet.

I should note, for the sake of accuracy, that my contribution to this fight up to this point had been — architecturally speaking — a sequence of near-misses so consistent they had begun to function as a pattern. Sacred Flame is not a complicated spell. Lathander reaches through me, channels his radiance into a burning column of divine light, and the target either passes or fails. Simple. Elegant. Difficult to miss, by design. I had been missing. Not every attempt, but enough. Enough that someone — and I will not name them because the name is irrelevant and the observation is what matters — had seen fit to comment on this. To suggest, in whatever phrasing they chose, that perhaps I might do something *useful*.

I looked at them. I let the silence sit long enough to be uncomfortable.

"I don't think," I said, with the careful precision of someone selecting exactly the right instrument for a delicate task, "that is the way you want to talk to me right now."

It was not a threat. I don't make threats. Threats require the other party to understand what consequence is being implied, and I find that explaining consequences is rarely worth the time. It was an observation. A piece of tactical advice, delivered in the register of a casual remark, intended to land exactly as hard as it did.

I turned back to the dragon.

I want to be honest about what happened next, because I think honesty is more interesting than mythology, and I have never had much patience for the kind of clerical narrative where every divine act is perfectly choreographed and arrives wrapped in golden light. What happened next was this: I rolled a three.

A three. By the mathematics of spellcasting, a three is nearly the worst outcome available. It is the kind of number that, in other circumstances, might have produced another miss, another near-pass, another moment for someone to remark that perhaps Lathander had opinions about my usefulness. The kind of number that makes people doubt.

Cryovain failed its saving throw.

The Sacred Flame landed. Not because of my roll — the roll tells me nothing about what Lathander intended, it tells me only what I contributed, and I contributed a three, which is nearly nothing. But Lathander reached through that nearly-nothing and did what he does, which is to burn. The radiance struck the dragon at the base of the skull, where the scales thin and the flesh beneath is older and less armored than it appears, and the Carver's essence — that alien bureaucratic presence that had been riding this creature like something settling into a borrowed chair — dissipated. Not dramatically. Not with a speech. It simply stopped existing in this particular location, and took its mild inconvenience with it.

Cryovain collapsed.

I want to be precise about what I felt in that moment, because feeling is data and data is worth preserving. There was satisfaction, yes. But satisfaction was not the primary register. The primary register was something closer to aesthetic appreciation — the pleasure of a pattern completing, a theorem finding its final term. I had missed, and missed, and missed, and Lathander had watched this with what I can only interpret as patience, because my god is not a god who acts before the moment is right. He waited until the dragon was dying and the Carver was faltering and the precise remaining hit points were exactly such that a three, channeled through his divine will, would be sufficient. He did not need my roll to be better. He needed my roll to arrive on schedule.

I find this delightful, in the way that I find most things delightful that other people would find humbling. Being the instrument of a god's precision while contributing almost nothing personally to the delivery — there is something very Lathanderian about that. The light does the work. The vessel holds still.

The dragon did not move again. The obsidian sword clattered somewhere below us, no longer relevant. The cold at the top of Icespire Hold was still cold — that doesn't change when the creature producing it dies — but it was ordinary cold now, mountain cold, the kind that simply exists rather than the kind that intends you harm.

I looked at the others. Brewbarry was still standing, as he had been standing since the breath hit him. Soma was releasing the moonbeam with the quiet efficiency of someone who knows when a tool is no longer needed. Vukradin was — Vukradin was whatever Vukradin is after a fight, which is usually several things at once, not all of them compatible.

I looked at the person who had suggested I do something useful.

I said nothing. I didn't need to.

## Soma — The Fate of the Veteran

The veteran was already on the ground when the argument started. Paralyzed, helpless, someone's son or nephew or camp cook gone wrong — I didn't know which, and I didn't need to. I know what a man looks like when he can't move and can't run, and I know what the party looks like when it's already decided.

I said it anyway.

"Don't kill, don't kill! He was just a victim!"

Shell sprouts, all of them, but I was looking at Brewbarry when I said it, because Brewbarry was the one with the halberd and the look in his eyes that meant the battle hadn't finished for him yet, whatever the rest of us were experiencing. You can't argue with that look. I have seen it on bears. I have seen it on stags. The fight doesn't leave the body just because the enemy has stopped moving.

I'm not sure why I expected different. I am forty-five years old. I should know better.

But I did try. I want that on the record: I tried.

I hit him myself — bear claws, my hands still half-shifted from earlier — intending it to count for something. Intending it to be the damage that ended this without ending him. Then I walked the moonbeam across to where he lay, the pale column of light settling over him like it was considering what he was worth. I was doing the math in my head. Down, weakened, accounted for. Not a threat. Not a problem that required a final solution.

I do not know what Brewbarry heard in the silence that followed. Maybe nothing. Maybe everything the battle had needed to say to him.

The halberd came down.

Afterwards, Brewbarry looked at Vukradin with something I can only describe as satisfaction. Satisfied is the wrong word — it was more than that, more personal, like a man completing a sentence he had started in another language.

"I hope this will inspire a song in your new music studio."

Then, pointing at what was left of the veteran on the ground —

"I win, you lose — tradition upheld."

I stood there. I am old, and I have seen much, and I am not going to die today, but I will admit the moonbeam felt a little stupid still hanging in the air over a dead man.

What I wanted to say was: *he wasn't fighting you anymore.* What I said was nothing, because the thing about recording an objection is you only have to say it once. Either the world adjusts, or it doesn't, and this world very clearly doesn't.

Vukradin wanted to celebrate. Brewbarry wanted to celebrate. The dragon was dead, the Carver was finished, the rooftop was ours — I understood the impulse, I did. It just transpires we didn't have any wine on hand, which Brewbarry addressed by producing the barrel he'd apparently been carrying and finishing it entirely before anyone else had raised a cup.

"I was thirsty."

We stood on the battlements while the wind came off the mountain and someone — I don't remember who — said something about how I'd become an interventionist instead of a naturalist. That I'd crossed over. That the thing I was describing, arguing for the life of a paralyzed man at a dragon's roost, was not naturalist philosophy.

I thought about that. Meril never told me what to call it. I just stood there.

"I… apparently I am."

I'm on the record as not being pro-killing, but that's okay. The plane of existence is intact. The veteran is not, but the plane is, and I have learned, traveling with these shell sprouts, to measure my victories at the appropriate scale.

I moved the moonbeam. It had nothing left to illuminate.

## Vukradin — The Spoils of Victory

The hoard was recognizable only by what it had been. Coins, gems, trade bars — all of it welded together by the dragon's cold into something that looked less like wealth and more like geology. A single mass of residium-laced ice and compressed fortune, fused at a temperature I couldn't begin to calculate and shaped by something that Soma immediately identified as planar meddling.

"You'll want a planar theorist," she said, with the flat authority of someone who has seen this kind of wrongness before and has opinions about it.

I made a note of that and moved on to the body.

The Carver's vessel was crumpled where it had fallen, still smoking faintly at the collar. The paralysis had held until the end — my contribution, small and precise, the one thing I could do cleanly. I was not looking at it for long. What I was looking at was the sword.

The obsidian blade was a prop. I'd known that from the moment I'd seen it in use — a vessel for the real instrument, which was the crystal set into the pommel. Clear and deep and faintly luminous even in the grey morning light of the battlements. I picked it up with the care of a man handling something he both wants and fears.

I held it for a moment. Then I understood what it did.

A mental amplifier. Not Bluetooth — I said Bluetooth, and there was immediate and passionate correction from behind me, and I accepted the correction in the spirit in which it was offered, which is to say I accepted the clarification and ignored the spirit entirely.

"Magical Bluetooth swap. Got it, sorry. Thanks for clarifying."

"It is absolutely the Bluetooth sword. Done."

The correction that followed had genuine feeling in it. I noted that feeling. I continued.

What it *actually* did — project music directly into the minds of an audience, bypassing ears entirely, bypassing the acoustic failures of bad rooms and worse weather and the particular cruelty of a crowd that won't stop talking — was so perfectly suited to my specific needs that for a moment I simply stood there on a frozen rooftop in the mountains and held it and understood that the universe had either a very good sense of humor or no sense of humor at all.

"It seems like this was sent by the devil to tempt me," I said. "Yes. Yes, save me."

Handcrafted, essentially. Purpose-built for my exact problem. The studio I have been trying to build since before any of this started — before the dragon, before the Carver, before Sister Kaella and the privy council and the lighthouse and all the accumulating weight of a world that keeps generating crises precisely when I have something I'd rather be doing.

And it was stolen goods.

Of course it was.

The gem had to go back to its owner. That was not a question. The question was *who* the owner was, because the crystal hadn't always been in that pommel, and the pommel hadn't always been on that blade, and the Carver was not a man who had purchased things through legitimate channels. I knew this with the same certainty that I know any criminal transaction: you can melt the coins together and plate them in ice until they're unrecognizable, but that doesn't make them yours.

I looked at the mass of fused treasure. Same principle.

"What, so that means it's not stolen? Like, because you scratched the serial number off? Like a criminal would? Exactly."

I was not talking to anyone in particular. I was working something out.

The gold — all of it, the full geological weight of a dragon's collected plunder — was going to require documentation. Binders' fees. A legend-lore process I had already committed to out loud, which meant I was committed. Not because anyone was holding me to it. Because I'd said it, and I say what I mean, and the moment I stop being the person who says what he means I am something else entirely and I do not know what that is.

Soma, watching the horizon with the patience of someone who has accepted that the universe operates on its own schedule, observed that at least the world was safe.

"Look, the universe is safe, right? Like, or, you know, the plane of existence is safe. That's what matters most, nothing else."

She was right. She is almost always right. It does not make the other things less real.

What was less right — what arrived like a second blow after the first had already landed — was the news about Neverwinter. The statue. The art collector we'd been carrying that obligation toward since the beginning. The window we'd missed because we'd made a choice, and choices have shapes you can only see after you've made them.

The particular individual. No longer available.

House Margaster.

"Well, then that, you know, fuck them." I could hear how thin that sounded. "We gotta go. Her next of kin? And they're in Neverwinter."

It was the right thing to say. It was also the sound of a man reordering his obligations in real time and pretending this is not exactly the kind of thing that has been happening to him continuously for months.

Then someone said — and I think it was the world itself, or the part of the world that is unkind — that I could have the studio. Right now. All I had to do was compromise a few principles here and there.

I am paraphrasing. The sentiment was accurate.

"You don't get it," I said.

And then, because honesty is the thing I have when nothing else is left:

"I just wanted my goddamn music studio, and I ended up having to go save the world."

I was not performing that line. I want that noted. I was not performing anything in that moment. I was a man standing on a frozen rooftop holding a crystal that would have changed everything about what I do, knowing I was going to give it back to someone who had never asked me to carry it this far, in a land full of people who are going to let Harbin Wester take credit for the dragon and call it done.

The Bluetooth sword was not mine to keep.

Neither, apparently, was anything else.

I put it down carefully, because it deserved that much, and turned to face whatever came next.

## Soma — Unwelcome Claimants

The dragon was dead. The smoke hadn't even finished rising.

And here she came.

Red-haired, composed, moving through the ruined gate of Icespire Hold like she'd been waiting just around the corner for the fire to stop — which, I realized about four seconds after she opened her mouth, was probably exactly what she'd been doing. She had a sister at her elbow, wide-eyed and nodding along. I catalogued them both in the time it took her to draw breath: the posture of someone who has run this particular play before, the sister who exists to provide the chorus, the exact placement of the hands — just visible enough to seem harmless.

"Are you Vukridin the Bard?" she said. "The one who returns the treasure to those that it has been stolen from?"

*Vukradin.* She'd gotten his name slightly wrong, which is the sort of thing that happens when you've heard it third-hand and didn't care enough to verify. I filed that away.

I could see the whole thing, the way you can see a fruit already splitting on the vine: the flattery first, then the claim, then the outrage when the claim fails. It would take perhaps two minutes. I didn't say anything. I am old enough to know when a thing is going to happen regardless of whether I point at it.

Vukradin, to his credit, looked uncomfortable. "No," he said, with the particular exhaustion of someone who has been handed an identity they didn't ask for. "I haven't done very well with the return."

She didn't hear him. Or she heard him and chose not to incorporate it, which amounts to the same thing. She turned to her sister — Runa, that was the name — with the look of someone who has just been proven correct by the universe.

"Runa, didn't I tell you that that saint, Vukradin, would come and get our treasure for us? I knew it, I knew that if all we had to do was pray to the gods, a kind soul like Vukradin would come here and deal with that dragon, and give our treasure back."

Runa was already nodding. "Blessed be! Blessed be!"

Brewbarry, standing just to my left, said, with the flat precision of a man stating a geological fact: "Well, that's the quickest we've ever found treasure's owners before."

I almost laughed. Almost.

I watched Vukradin's face. He is not difficult to read if you have been watching him as long as I have — the way his jaw sets when the exhaustion tips over into contempt. He'd been called a saint and a divine instrument in the space of thirty seconds by a woman who'd learned his name from gossip and his principles from rumor, and he could hear it for exactly what it was.

"Lady," he said, "I don't know who you are, I don't know how you think that all three thousand gold that this dragon hoarded over its long life is somehow yours, but I don't really believe you. You're probably gonna try to steal this, like everybody else steals. You're one of them. Why don't you just get out of here?"

Here it was. The pivot.

"I am hurt," Sylvine said. "You are a thief!"

She made it sound like a discovery. Like the accusation had surfaced from genuine pain and not from the back pocket where she'd been keeping it since she walked through the gate. I have lived forty-five years. I have watched creatures of every kind — predators and prey, honest things and dishonest things — and the tell was the same in all of them: the ones who plan their exits before they've finished the entrance.

"I am tired of all these liars and cheats in this land," Vukradin said. "Get out of here. There's nobody left worthy."

"Phonies," Brewbarry said, with deep personal feeling. "These goddamn phonies."

I stood there, staff in hand, and I thought about how one of the first things I was taught — really taught, not just told — was to watch what a creature does when it doesn't get what it wants. That is the moment when its actual nature becomes available.

"Second plan," Sylvine said. "We kill them all."

There it was.

She had veterans with her. I will give her that: she had thought through the contingency. What she had not thought through was Brewbarry, who entered the fight with the focused happiness of a man who has been waiting for someone to give him a good reason. "Try not to die too quickly," he told the nearest of them, with what I can only describe as genuine hospitality. "I want to enjoy this."

I am on the record as not being pro-killing, but that was fairly inevitable from the moment she walked through the gate. I knew it before Vukradin started talking. I knew it when she called him a saint, when the sister chorused on cue, when she angled her body just slightly toward the door she'd come through.

It ended quickly. It usually does, with Brewbarry in it. One of the veterans had the good sense to reassess his career in real time — turned, looked at what remained of his colleagues, and made a decision. I respect that. That is an animal's wisdom: know when the fight is over before the fight is over.

"Screw this shit," he said. "There's more money to be found elsewhere."

Brewbarry planted his feet over the last one to fall and said, with the satisfied finality of a man completing a sentence he started at birth: "Root Smasher stands, you don't. That's the story."

I noted it. I will probably still be noting it when everything else about this afternoon has faded.

The smoke kept rising. The fortress was quiet again — the particular quiet of a place that has been very loud and is now catching its breath. I looked at where Sylvine and Runa had been standing.

They hadn't even waited long enough to see the dragon actually die. They had smelled the smoke and come running. That detail, more than the flattery, more than the invented claim, more than the "second plan" delivered without a beat of hesitation — that detail was the one that stayed with me.

Opportunists. The world is full of them. The world has always been full of them. You drive off the dragon, and before the snow even settles, someone is already measuring the hoard.

I put my hand on the staff and thought about Meril, who had a particular phrase for people like that — practical, unflattering, and accurate. She would have said it out loud. I kept it to myself.

The universe, at least, was still intact. The plane of existence was safe. That was what mattered most.

The rest of it was just tidying up.

## Brewbarry — Skirmish in the Fortress

The moment Sylvine said it — *Second plan! We kill them all!* — something in my chest unlocked.

I don't know how else to describe it. There's a door inside me, and most of the time I keep it latched, because Vukradin says words first and I respect that. But Sylvine had already used up her words. She'd used up flattery and piety and outrage and theft, all in about thirty seconds, and now she was at *kill them all*, and the door came open.

The red came in. It always comes in from the edges — the corners of the eyes, the jaw, the hands. By the time it reaches the center of me, I'm already moving.

Valphine was first on her feet. She does something with light — she makes it heavy, makes it *hurt* — and I could feel the warmth of it spreading out from her as the bandits pushed in. They came through the door and immediately walked into something that didn't like them. Good.

It didn't hold. One of them got to Valphine — got through the light, got a hit in — and I saw her flinch, saw her focus break, and the warmth went out of the room like a candle snuffed. She kept standing. I noted that. Valphine doesn't go down easy.

Soma went bear. She does that sometimes and it still takes me a small moment to re-sort the world — one second there's a Tortle, next second there is a bear — but then the bear hit one of the veterans and I re-sorted fast. Good bear. Good.

The bandits made a move for the treasure slag. Vukradin's treasure — the slag from the hoard, the thing we'd earned, the thing I understood we were keeping until someone proved it was theirs, which Sylvine had very much not done. They grabbed for it. Vukradin held on. He's small but he holds on. I filed that under things I already knew about him.

Then Vukradin raised his hand and two of the veterans just stopped. Not fell — stopped. Locked in place, standing up, eyes open, not moving. Frozen like they'd been carved from the same stone as the fortress walls. Paralyzed. I know what that word means now. I've seen him do it before.

Paralyzed.

My halberd knows that word too.

I don't remember the exact sequence very well — the red does that, takes the details — but I remember the weight of the halberd connecting. I remember the first veteran going down and the sound it made. I remember the second one — the same. Paralyzed means they can't move. Can't dodge. Can't put up a hand. My tribe called this efficient. I call it finished.

*I win, you lose. Tradition upheld.*

I think I said that out loud. I usually do.

The last one ran. One of the bandits, when the veterans were on the ground, looked at the room — Soma-bear on one side, Valphine upright and unmoved despite the hit she'd taken, me with the halberd still warm — and apparently decided this was not the employment she had signed up for. She ran out of the fortress, and I let her go. The rage was already cooling by then, and there was nothing about her that needed the halberd. She'd made a reasonable assessment of her career and acted on it. I respected that more than I would have if she'd stayed.

Soma said something, after. She said she was on the record as not being pro-killing. She said it the way she says a lot of things — flat, clear, matter-of-fact, like she was noting the weather. Clouds today. Also, Brewbarry killed the paralyzed men.

I heard her. I didn't argue. The veterans had been trying to kill us, or steal from us, or both, and they'd made that choice when Sylvine said her second plan. I wasn't cruel about it. The rage isn't cruel. It's just — complete.

Vukradin was already talking to Ser Kaelen. Magic sending — I still don't fully understand it, except that it works like a very fast message that costs something — and he had the words already ready in his head. I could tell by how fast he spoke.

"The dragon is dead, so is the Carver. We're heading to Neverwinter. Spread the word."

Just like that. Clean. Seventeen words and the world knew.

The fortress was quiet. Soma was a Tortle again. Valphine was still standing. The veterans were not. The hoard slag was still in Vukradin's hands.

I was thirsty.

## Vukradin — A Message from the Gauntlet

The Carver was dead. The dragon was dead. The possessed man who'd carried the obsidian sword onto this rooftop was dead, and the thieves who'd accompanied him were dead, and I was standing on the battlements of Icespire Hold with the cold of the peaks pressing in from every direction, and the silence that follows a thing being over.

I reached for the sending.

It is, fundamentally, a twenty-five-word telegram fired across the ether by means of a copper wire you hold in your fingers — which I had only recently realized makes it, in a meaningful metaphysical sense, a telephone. Brewbarry had found this funnier than I'd expected. I found it clarifying. You pick up the receiver, you say your piece, the other end picks up. Simple. Civilized. The sort of thing that should have been invented sooner.

I composed it in my head while the smoke was still settling. *The dragon is dead. So is the Carver. We're heading to Neverwinter.* Twenty-five words. I am not, generally, a man of twenty-five words, but the spell imposes discipline and I have learned to be grateful for it.

I sent it.

Ser Kaelen Thorn, Knight of the Order of the Gauntlet, Candlekeep correspondent, man who irons his letters before sending them — Ser Kaelen came back with what I can only describe as a prepared statement.

"Praise Torm! Your righteous victory brings light to the north. The gauntlet awaits your arrival in Phandalin. We shall celebrate your conflict-free triumph together."

There was a pause on the rooftop. A small, shared pause, the kind that happens when something has landed in a register no one quite expected.

Then Brewbarry said, with the flat authority of a man delivering a verdict: "Fighting a fucking dragon — that was conflict."

He was not wrong.

I turned the phrase over in my mind. *Conflict-free triumph.* He had used my language. My exact language, the shorthand I use for a very particular kind of ethical accounting — gold that came without a body attached, work that doesn't require you to look away from anything afterward. He had taken my vocabulary and deployed it in a sentence about a dragon fight in which at least three people had died and I had paralyzed a man's body against his will, and he had done it without apparent irony.

Which meant he had studied me. Or he had listened carefully enough to notice what I cared about. Or he had been coached. None of those possibilities were entirely comfortable.

And tucked inside the polished phrasing, like a directive someone hoped you'd miss: *come to Phandalin quick. Issues with the Emerald Enclave.*

That was what the second sending was, the one underneath the ceremonial one, the real twenty-five words stripped of the flourishes. Adabra's faction. The Enclave cooling on us. Something had moved while we were busy at the top of a mountain.

He's about as close as someone we can actually trust as anybody.

I heard myself say it and felt the sentence curdle slightly even as it left my mouth.

"No," I said. "I don't trust him. He's as close as anybody."

Which is true. Which is the honest version. Which is not the same thing as trustworthy — it only means the competition is weak. In a field of confirmed liars, a man who deploys your own principles back at you in a Sending response is, statistically, your best option. That is not a ringing endorsement. That is a description of the field.

Brewbarry had already moved past the analysis. He had heard the word *celebrate* — that was the operative word, that was the anchor — and everything else was logistical.

"I heard 'celebrate,'" he said. "Say no more. Let's go."

And honestly. Honestly. I could not argue with the logic. The dragon was dead. The Carver was gone. We were alive and standing on the highest point in the region in the cold clean air of the peaks, and somewhere at the bottom of this mountain there was a town that had been frightened for a long time, and we were going to go tell them they didn't need to be frightened anymore, and then apparently we were going to go deal with whatever had gone wrong in Phandalin while we were away.

I just wanted my goddamn music studio. I ended up having to save the world.
