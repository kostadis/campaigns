The henge was behind us — stone and ash and the smell of something that had been wrong for a long time finally being allowed to stop. I didn't linger on it. That was done. The question was what came next.

Two choices. That was how it was framed. Woodland Manse, where another ritual was building toward something loud and terrible, or Falcon's Hunting Lodge, where whatever the Talosians had left behind was still festering. Infinite choices, technically. But the shape of the moment made it two.

I didn't deliberate.

"I decided what I was going to do," I said. "I refuse to do anything more for this guy unless I get an apology from him."

Soma's response was immediate and characteristically unvarnished. "The Falcon? Yeah, fuck that guy."

It wasn't the diplomatic register I usually aimed for, but I wasn't inclined to correct her. What Falcon had done at the Druid Council — standing up in front of people I needed to persuade and taking shots at me, making himself useful to Adabra at my expense — that had cost us something. It had cost *me* something. I had been building something careful there, and he had leaned into it like a man who didn't understand the weight of what he was handling.

I had let it sit. I had not contacted him. Valphine had been working something with him in the background — I knew that much, though not the specifics — and I'd decided the next move was his to make. But now the lodge needed clearing, and apparently that required me to act first.

"You want me to call him? I mean, I can call him, but he better apologize."

Soma offered a fallback with the dry patience of someone who had seen too many apologies fail to materialize. "If you don't get the apology, you can just, you know, we can take care of the infestation and not exactly protect the hunting lodge. Just… just throwing that out there as, you know, possible ways out of this mess."

I appreciated the option. I also didn't want to need it.

The tone was the thing I was thinking about. There's a way to demand an apology that makes you look small — the aggrieved party rattling its grievances into a silence that has already moved on. I didn't want that. I wanted something with a certain quality of *audacity* to it. A tone that communicated I was doing *him* a favor by asking at all.

"What's, like, the famous… So Thomas… Justice Thomas. At some point, his wife called Anita Hill, right? Do you remember this? He said, like, are you finally ready to apologize to my husband for what you did to him during his confirmation hearing? So I got that sort of… and like, that was just ridiculous that she did that. But she actually did. So that is the indignant tone that I take, just as Thomas's wife calling Anita Hill."

I felt the spell open between my hands — the reach and cost of it, the attention it required, the way it compressed everything I meant to say into a few precise words and sent them across whatever distance separated us.

"Are you finally ready to apologize for the way you treated me at the Druid Council?"

Valphine added, from beside me: "Clarify the context. We're considering whether or not to go clear out all the, you know, the unwanted guests at your hunting lodge."

"Yeah, no, I'll add that." I felt the sending still holding. "The party — my friends here tell me we need to go take care of your problem at the hunting lodge. But are you ready to apologize for what you did at the Druid Council?"

You learn to edit. The working only carries so much.

The response came back the way it always does — not sound exactly, more like thought arriving from outside your own head, carrying another voice's weight. Falcon's voice. He said: *I apologize.* Categorical. Not hedged. No qualifications attached, no *I'm sorry if you felt* construction that isn't an apology at all. Just the thing itself, and then: *he's on the road home. Hopes to see us there.*

I stood with that for a moment.

"Sounds like he did apologize, though." I heard myself say it. "Okay. We can go."

It was a relief, if I'm being honest — not the apology itself, but that he'd given it cleanly. Valphine had done something with him beforehand; the readiness of it told me that. But the call had been mine, and the answer was yes, and now we could move without me carrying the weight of having let it go.

We spread the documents out and went through them again — Flenz's sealed letters, Narux's vellum sketches, the half-burned scrap from Yargath. The shape of it was clear enough: a summoning ritual confirmed underway at the manse, a backup ritual waiting if the first failed, and if both failed, whoever was in charge would attempt the summoning personally. The redundancy of it was deliberate. These people were not being casual about this.

The Talosian attack on the lodge puzzled me. It had been their lodge — we'd learned that the hard way — so why turn on it now?

"Scenario 1," Valphine said, with the cadence of someone sorting options for their own amusement, "they're Talosian, and then we destroyed them. Scenario 2 is that they're not Talosian, and we get some alloys."

I had no clean answer for either. It was the kind of question that would only resolve once we were there.

Prutha knew something, or thought he did. He'd heard songs about the lodge — heard the orcs had taken it, heard about the dogs. He didn't know who was staying there now. But he confirmed the three factions: former tribe, Talosian-aligned, independent. And the Anchorites had moved their own people through. That last detail felt important in a way I couldn't fully articulate yet.

I inventoried what I had left. Two minor workings spent, two serious ones gone — one of them just now on Falcon's apology. The count wasn't encouraging.

"You know, I've got… I've burned through the small workings and two of the heavy ones."

"I just need something to resist lightning," I said. "Do we have anything for resisting lightning?"

Soma checked herself and came up empty. Valphine suggested obscuring mist, or darkness — something to complicate targeting. I turned it over.

"Well, Darkness prevents targeting, but lightning doesn't require targeting."

A fire spell doesn't care whether it can see you. Neither does a storm. That was the problem.

"Well, we definitely need to camp at some point, right? It's more than a day's journey to the lodge, right?"

Closer than that. Hours away — not a day's march.

I looked at the others. Valphine wanted the rest — wanted to be full going in, which was reasonable. But if the ritual was already moving, time spent recovering was time we might not have. And we didn't know that it was active today. Only that it would be, eventually.

"Valphine, you can decide."

She looked at me with the particular quality of attention she uses when she's determining whether I'm being genuine or performative. I was being genuine.

"We'll approach the hunting lodge from the south, though," she said finally.

Good enough.

"Let's head to the lodge," I said. "We can scope it out, we'll be there before long, and we can see what we see. That's fine."

I meant it. Whatever we were walking into, we were walking in with what we had. I had made do before with less.
