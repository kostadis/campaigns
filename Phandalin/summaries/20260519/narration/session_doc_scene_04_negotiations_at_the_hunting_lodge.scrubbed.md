Vorga looked at Valphine funny. Then she looked at Prutha funny.

I caught that — the slight tilt of Vorga's head, the flicker of something calculating in her eyes as they moved from the drow to the orc at our backs. I filed it and said nothing. That's the kind of look you wait on.

Valphine had opened in orcish, which left the rest of us standing in the lodge courtyard with nothing to do but watch two people have a conversation we couldn't hear. I am old enough to be comfortable with that. Brewbarry was not. He shifted his weight from foot to foot like a man who had misplaced his axe and only just noticed. Vukradin tilted his head as if proximity to the sounds might eventually yield meaning.

What I could read was posture, and Vorga's posture said: not hostile. Occupied, maybe. Tired, certainly. The twenty or so people arranged around the courtyard — elders, children, a man who moved like his hip had been broken and reset badly — were not the profile of a raiding force. They were people who had found an empty building and moved into it the way animals find caves. Not conquest. Necessity.

I'd seen that before. After Cryovain came down from the mountain, a lot of creatures relocated to where the cold hadn't reached. The dragon had rearranged an entire valley's worth of lives without ever intending to. I wondered if it had known, or cared, or if dragons simply don't think in those terms at all.

Valphine switched to Common.

"I say they come from the southern mountains, and I told them about the Talosian nearby."

Vukradin turned immediately. "Oh, were they displaced by the dragon?"

"Sounds like it," Valphine said. Then, with the particular timing she has that I still haven't fully mapped: "dragon that you killed."

Vukradin looked at the assembled orcs. Then, louder than necessary: "We killed the dragon."

They stared. Twenty people, some armed, some not, all of them going absolutely still. It had the quality of a sentence in a language they understood but hadn't expected to hear spoken aloud in their presence.

"You killed Dragon?" Vorga said it the way you say something when you're testing whether you've heard correctly.

"Yep," Vukradin said. He had, I noticed, put his chin up slightly. He does that.

Vorga processed this. "Dragon is dead. That home is now free to go back?"

"Yeah, you can go back," he said.

Valphine added, mild as water: "But something worse than the dragon."

It was accurate. I found myself nodding. The current occupancy of Icespire Hold was, as far as any of us knew, unconfirmed. Vukradin pivoted to an offer of escort, which was kind of him and potentially over-optimistic, which is also very much him.

Vorga chose to stay. She walked him through it — and I watched her do it, the way she moved through her own reasoning with the efficiency of someone who has made this calculation before, in other places, with other stakes. The open woods against a pursuing force. Twenty people who could not run fast enough. She pointed to an elder near the wall, a man whose age showed in the careful way he held himself, and said: these people don't move fast enough. The creature would catch up.

Vukradin tried. "Brewbarry can carry everybody." He glanced at Boney, at me. "Put everybody on Brewbarry's back, and we got Boney, too."

I said, "And you can ride the elk, I can carry a couple."

It was worth offering. The instinct to find another way is a good instinct, and I've learned to let the shell-sprouts exhaust the alternatives before I agree that the harder path is the right one. Vorga walked through it anyway. The elk, the horse, the Goliath — the old man near the wall would still have needed carrying, and carrying the old man was not the problem. The problem was what came after: a summoned creature moving through forest while you were moving through forest, slower, encumbered.

"Creature will catch up," she said.

She said it a third time, or some version of it, and I think it was the third time that Vukradin actually heard it.

Her plan was simple. Kill the creature here, where they knew where it was coming from. Then walk to Icespire in safety. She had done the geometry of survival already, before we arrived. We had just joined a plan in progress.

The note surfaced briefly — something about a boy from the East, recovered at the Circle of Thunder, someone had called him a good recruit. I looked at Prutha. He was standing quietly, as he often does, with that particular stillness of someone who has learned that stillness is safer than motion. I thought about the way Vorga's eyes had moved to him when Valphine first spoke. I didn't say anything.

Then Brewbarry screamed.

Not the battle-cry kind. The other kind — the kind that comes out of a man who has sustained a genuine personal loss.

He appeared from the interior of the lodge holding a broken cask. The wood was dark with old moisture. Whatever had been inside was gone, soaked into the floor planks, wasted. Not drunk. Wasted.

"I will kill them all!" he announced to the courtyard.

Vukradin moved toward him immediately, hands up, voice calibrated. Every scrap of warmth and persuasion he had went into promising more alcohol would be found, that calm was achievable, that this was a recoverable situation. Brewbarry accepted this in the way a man accepts a rain delay — reluctant, not persuaded, still certain someone was going to pay.

I watched him and thought: the Talosians have not yet met Brewbarry properly. They are going to.

Valphine gathered herself during the lull — I heard the quiet particular way she has of doing it, like a held breath released on purpose — and then the working went out, a warm brace moving through us, the kind that doesn't close wounds but steadies the body against the ones coming, deepens the reserve you draw on before you finally go down. I noted it, filed the difference.

"It can be healed," I said. "Semi-temporary."

"It's better than temporary," Vukradin said.

"No, it is," I said. "Because it can be healed."

Vorga suggested someone stand on the wall so the Falcon would see a friendly face and not come in shooting. Vukradin volunteered. "We're friends now," he said, and there was something genuine in it — the particular warmth of a reconciliation he'd been waiting on. "He apologized."

He stood up on the palisade and the Falcon came out of the trees with war paint on his face and a bow already in hand.

Vukradin's voice dropped an octave very quickly. "Jay! You made it! Oh, oh, oh, I'll come down, I'll come down, I'll come down."

The Falcon's bow came down when Vukradin reached him. They spoke. The Falcon listened to the whole of it — the orcs, the alliance, the incoming Talosians, the plan — and when Vukradin finished, he said: "You have my sword."

I thought about the last time I'd seen Falcon, at the Enclave council. The way grief wears a man differently than anger does. This version of him — paint on his face, weapon ready, walking toward a fight with something like purpose — was different from that one. Not healed. But aimed.

Valphine pointed at the map and said something about the gatehouse having fantastically defensible positions, and we put three orc archers at the arrow slits. They settled in with the quiet competence of people who know how to hold a position.

The lodge went still. Waiting.
