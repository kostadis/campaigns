The lodge looked the way I expected it to look, which is to say: occupied by people who had every reason to be afraid, and were doing something about it.

Two stories, timber and stone, a guest house off to one side, stable and smithy joined at the hip. The palisade walls rose high in a rough perimeter, and even from the treeline I could see the repair work — newer planks lashed in with cord where the older wood had been damaged or rotted out, mismatched grain and color giving the whole structure the patchwork look of something rebuilt under pressure rather than planned. Not vandalized. Improved. Whoever held the lodge had decided to stay.

Three figures on the walkway, watching the treeline. They'd seen us already. Two had bows up, not raised to draw but not lowered either — the posture of people who had made the calculation and not yet decided. The third was calling something down into the courtyard. A warning, or an order; I couldn't yet hear the words.

I had been here before this moment, in a way. Not to this compound — but to the conversation I knew was coming. Falcon had trusted me enough to ask me to mediate his apology to Vukradin, which meant I knew something about the cost of what had happened here, and something about the shape of whatever came next. The dead partner. The attack the party had failed to prevent. The cloak that was probably still on its hook inside, unworn, because no one who mourns reaches for their lover's clothes.

I was wrong about the cloak.

Prutha moved before any of us spoke. He was watching the banners, and then he said it clearly, loud enough for the walkway to hear: *"Eastern Heart! Three banners on the wall! I see one! Sun and Stone! My uncle's clan march! They are family!"*

He was already walking. Deliberate pace, hands open — not a supplicant's gesture but a recognition, an announcement of his own. I watched him cover the distance to the gate. Close enough to be heard clearly, he stopped, brought his right fist to his left shoulder, and lowered his head. Then he reached inside his armor and drew out the bone whistle — the same one whose tradition had moved me more than I'd expected when I first saw it — and blew the dawn prayer into the cold air.

I held very still.

The sentry on the walkway lowered her bow. Returned the gesture. Shouted something down into the courtyard, and Prutha answered, and then she shouted back toward the gate.

*"Bring your drow, bring your Aasimar, bring all of them. We are listening."*

*Your drow.* Not "the drow." Possessive. Prutha's. I filed that.

Soma settled Boney near the stables once we were through the gate — *"Here you go, Boney. Chill out here"* — and the skeletal horse did not protest, which was more than could be said for some members of this party in new situations. Then we moved into the courtyard proper and I took in the full picture.

Women with infants. Old men around a cookfire, tending something in an iron pot. Children playing a stick game against the far wall, engrossed, not frightened by us yet. A dozen warriors spread through perhaps thirty orcs total. They watched us the way I have seen people watch strangers in places they did not choose to be but cannot leave. Recognition without welcome. Evaluation that was also calculation.

Refugees, then. Not raiders. The distinction mattered.

She came down from the walkway. Middle-aged, gray at the temples, broken nose healed badly, a scar along her jaw that had been made by something old and serious. She was wearing Falcon's hunter cloak over her own leather — not his colors or insignia reworked, not a trophy displayed with pride, but practical: the warmest thing in the closet, worn by someone who was cold. Someone who had been inside the lodge and taken what she needed.

Vukradin said something quiet to me. *"Not worthy."* I agreed, out loud, to confirm that I was paying attention to the same thing and had arrived at the same conclusion: this was not the moment to press on a dead man's cloak. *"Not worthy."* He said it once more, satisfied, and I let the matter close.

The older orc was already looking at me. He stood beside the woman — Vorga, I would learn — leaning on his spear with the ease of someone who had done that for decades and found it a more reliable support than younger men. His eyes had gone to my holy symbol the moment I walked into the courtyard, and they stayed there with a quality of attention I recognized. Not hostility. Hunger, maybe. Or the specific alertness of someone who has prayed a long time and is not certain those prayers have been going anywhere.

He said one word. Orcish. Direct.

*Dawn.*

I keep my face composed by long practice — Menzoberranzan does not reward readable expressions, and the habit survives in me even now, even in the woods of the surface world with the sky open overhead. But something in my chest answered that word before I could assess it.

I looked back at him. I answered in Orcish, because he deserved that, and because it was the right instrument for the conversation.

"Yes," I said. "I'm the bringer of the dawn."

I let him see the symbol clearly.

"A painful dawn."
