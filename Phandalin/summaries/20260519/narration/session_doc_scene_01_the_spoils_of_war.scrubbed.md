The shield was sitting on the ground between us when I got a proper look at it.

Dark oak, black-oiled, banded in iron. Someone had taken care of it once. You could tell by the carving — concentric rings of standing stones cut into the face, old work, the kind that takes patience. Names above the rings, worn but legible if you got close. Druid craft, Soma said, or something like it. And then, jammed over the center like a boot heel on a grave, a hammered iron disc with Talos's bolt stamped into it.

Vukradin clocked it immediately. "Oh… oh, right. That'll stand out like a sword." He said it the way he says most things — like the information was obvious but he wanted everyone to know he knew. "The Cleric of Lathander is wielding a shield that has the image—"

He didn't finish the sentence. He didn't need to.

Valphine was already looking at it. She tilted her head, the way she does when something interests her more than she wants to let on. Then she reached out and wiggled the bolt. It popped free in her hand. Just like that. The whole Talosian centerpiece, loose as a bad tooth.

It had been stuck on crookedly from the start. The original carving underneath was never sanded smooth — you could still feel the druidic geometry through the iron disc, the older pattern asserting itself. The bolt sat on top of it the way a brand sits on skin: something added, not something belonging. Valphine turned the shield over. The leather straps inside were newer than the wood by a generation, at least. They smelled of pine tar and wet dog.

And there, on the inside of the rim: a half-orc tally. Four notches and a slash. Five somethings. Kills, maybe. Storms weathered. Hard to say.

Vukradin didn't hesitate. "Clearly, clearly not yours. We need to return it. Please mark it as blood."

Valphine said: "Done."

That was that. Or it should have been.

Prutha had been watching the whole exchange with the expression he gets when adults are doing something confusing. He is enthusiastic in ways that don't always map onto the situation. He pointed at Valphine and said what he thought was a helpful suggestion: "Valphine, use good shield! Valphine, cleric of sun!"

Valphine started to answer him — "You can use my shield" — but Vukradin cut across it.

"You can give them your shield, you can do whatever you want with your shield. But that shield?" A pause. "Not yours."

Prutha protested. Vukradin looked at him directly.

"Not yours either."

Prutha went quiet. Valphine confirmed the point without warmth: "It's not mine. Be sure." She tucked the shield under her arm and said she'd hold it until the owner turned up. Sensible. I thought about the half-orc with five somethings marked in wood. Probably dead. Probably not going to come asking for it back. But Vukradin's right about the principle, even when the principle costs nothing — especially then, maybe, because the habit has to go somewhere.

Vukradin added, almost as an afterthought: "I recommend you guys do a better job hiding your crown merchandise." He meant the Talosians. He meant the bolt. It was good advice delivered with the tone of a man who has given up expecting it to be followed.

Then someone brought up my halberd.

I had the halberd from the shipwreck. It's mine. It fights well. I didn't see the need for extensive discussion.

Vukradin did the thing where he looks surprised and isn't. "You got a new halberd? Yo, what is going on here, guys?"

"I got a divorce," I said.

That got a pause. Then Vukradin, bless him, provided the cover story without being asked: "This is the halberd you've had all along. You just sharpened it on a sharpening stone. It just looks more lethal."

Valphine added: "And it looks a little waterlogged."

"You're mistaken," Vukradin said, to no one in particular. "This is the halberd you've had all along. You just sharpened it on a sharpening stone. And it just looks more lethal. That's totally fine. You can sharpen your halberd as much as you want. No problem with that."

I said nothing. The halberd sat against my leg, waterlogged and lethal. Some things don't need more words than that.

Valphine started talking about Lathander then — the real Lathander, her version. The argument was that people have it wrong about what light is. It isn't tender. It burns twelve hours a day, it hurts everything it touches at full strength, and when it's gone people beg for it to return. They're afraid of the dark. That's not worship of goodness. That's worship of power. Power that's in plain sight, unashamed, causing harm constantly, and called *good* because the alternative is worse.

Vukradin listened. Then he said: "I like that the cleric is changing the god instead of the god changing the cleric."

Nobody disagreed with that.

I thought about what a god looks like to someone who was told, for years, that the wrong thing was holy. The Uthgardt have their own version of Talos — storms, power, the thing you can't argue with. I was sober when I was found guilty. I was drunk by the time I understood what that meant. The shape of belief doesn't always match the thing it's built around.

The halberd is mine. The shield belongs to whoever has five somethings marked in oak. That's the accounting, and it's simple enough.
