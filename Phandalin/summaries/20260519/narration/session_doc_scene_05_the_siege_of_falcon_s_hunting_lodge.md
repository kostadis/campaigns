The storm came wrong.

I knew it before I understood it — a pressure behind my eyes, sudden and complete, like a fist closing around the inside of my skull. Then the sky went. Afternoon to dark in a single breath, and the wind came up hard enough to rattle the palisade logs in their lashings.

The animals felt it first. The boars in Falcon's pens started throwing themselves against the walls of their enclosure, squealing in a register that made the hairs on my arms stand up. I've heard boars charge. This wasn't that. This was panic — pure, animal, knowing.

Drubbak had gone very still. The old orc stood beside the gatehouse without moving, his head tilted like he was listening for something underneath the wind. Then he said it, quiet enough that I almost missed it under the noise:

"It come wrong. There is no chant. Where is the chant?"

I didn't know what that meant. Drubbak knew storms the way I know a weapon's balance — by feel, by something that lived below words. Whatever he was feeling, I could see it in how he held himself. That particular stillness is worse than shouting.

Lightning split the tree line a hundred yards out. The light was white and absolute, and the crack came a half-second later, loud enough to feel in the chest. Then the river. The water jumped up in a white column and the sound rolled across the palisade like a boulder. Then the third bolt came down inside the compound and blew an empty barrel into kindling, and the smell of it — ozone and charred wood — hit me like a slap.

Soma held up her staff. A new leaf had pushed out from one of the buds, fresh and green, curled at the edge like it was still deciding.

"Guys, it's sprouting!"

"It means an angel got its wings," she added, "don't worry."

It was the driest thing she'd said in days, and I found myself grinning despite everything.

Valphine moved through us without hurrying — she does everything without hurrying, which I have always found either admirable or unsettling depending on the situation. Whatever she did, I felt it land. Something settled in my chest, a fraction more solid than before. An extra layer between me and whatever was coming through that gate. I'm not philosophical about magic the way Vukradin is. I just know when something useful has been done.

We placed the orc archers in the gatehouse tower, behind the arrow slits. Valphine had called them "aerosluts," which I had no immediate response to, and the orcs didn't seem offended. Boney went inside the palisade, near the stable house, which Vukradin described with great seriousness as "cannon fodder." Falcon took position with his bow. We spread through the courtyard.

This was the part I was good at. Not the positioning, not the tactics — those belong to Vukradin and Valphine. The part where everything was decided and you just had to hold the line. I checked my grip on the Dragon Slayer. I flexed my hands once inside the gauntlets. The Dread Helm sat warm on my skull, and I knew from experience my eyes were doing the red-glow thing, which tends to get a reaction.

Then Vukradin said Gorthok was there.

Not almost summoned. Not coming. There. Among the attacking forces. Already.

Soma said she hadn't expected him until the summoning was complete, and the tone she used was the tone she uses when she is trying very hard not to say *you idiots.* The answer was that we had come to the lodge instead of dealing with it. We had negotiated, and rested, and the world had not waited.

Vukradin said we'd made the right call.

Maybe. I thought about the Uthgardt stories of the Great Boar — a thing conjured out of storm and lightning, older than the gods that claimed to own it. I thought about how you stop something like that. I thought: *probably you don't stop it. Probably you just get in front of it.*

That part didn't bother me.

Vukradin rolled a twenty-two for himself and a seven on Falcon's behalf, announcing the latter with magnificent condescension. He was looking at the enemy positions with the expression he gets when he sees an opportunity — eyes slightly narrowed, already building the moment in his head.

"Oh, they are nicely grouped, too."

"Convenience," Soma said.

"How convenient, indeed." He tapped his fingers. "30 foot cube, man. Something that uses a 30-foot cube. And it's not Fireball, because I don't have that spell."

At this point someone — from the direction it came, I think it was meant to be my voice, my thinking, some approximation of what I would say — offered the core strategic wisdom: *they are savages, we kill them all.*

Vukradin agreed with great enthusiasm. "You're right, we will kill them! Huzzah! We are agreeing on everything!"

This is what I mean when I say I like Vukradin's music. Most people hear *music* and think: gentle thing, careful thing, the thing that stops the fight. When Vukradin hears it, he hears *kill them all* as an opening position and agrees wholeheartedly. We have always understood each other on the important points.

The lightning came from a hundred and twenty feet out.

A bolt, not from the sky but from the darkness beyond the palisade — aimed, deliberate, the kind that doesn't happen by accident. It hit the gate.

The doors came apart. Not damaged. Gone. The logs and iron fittings that had made up the lodge's main entrance ceased to be a gate and became debris, and through the gap and the smoke and the afterimage burned on my eyes, I could see shapes moving.

Gorthok first. You feel something like that before you see it — a mass, a wrongness, the particular way large violent things displace the air in front of them. The Anchorites clustering behind, the Talosian forces converging on the hole.

Vukradin was muttering something about Hypnotic Pattern. Soma was watching her staff. Drubbak had not moved from his place, still listening for the chant that wasn't there.

My sword was already in my hand.
