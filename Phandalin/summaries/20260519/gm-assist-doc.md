# Chpater 42 A Storm is Coming

## Brewbarry — The Spoils of War

The shield was on the ground between us when I got a proper look at it.

Dark oak. Iron bands. Carving on the face — stones in rings, old work. Names worn but legible up close. Druid craft, Soma said, or something like it. Then a hammered iron disc nailed over the center, Talos's bolt on the front. Different hands.

Vukradin clocked it immediately. "Oh… oh, right. That'll stand out like a sword." He said it the way he says most things — like the information was obvious but he wanted everyone to know he knew. "The Cleric of Lathander is wielding a shield that has the image—"

He didn't finish the sentence. He didn't need to.

Valphine was already looking at it. She tilted her head, the way she does when something interests her more than she wants to let on. Then she reached out and wiggled the bolt. It popped free in her hand. Just like that. The whole Talosian centerpiece, loose as a bad tooth.

It had been stuck on crookedly from the start. The original carving underneath was never sanded smooth — you could still feel the druidic geometry through the iron disc, the older pattern asserting itself. The bolt sat on top of it the way a brand sits on skin: something added, not something belonging. Valphine turned the shield over. The leather straps inside were newer than the wood by a generation, at least. They smelled of pine tar and wet dog.

And there, on the inside of the rim: a half-orc tally. Four notches and a slash. Five somethings. Kills, maybe. Storms weathered. Hard to say.

Vukradin didn't hesitate. "Clearly, clearly not yours. We need to return it. Please mark it as blood."

Valphine said: "Done."

That was that. Or it should have been.

Prutha had been watching the whole exchange with the expression he gets when adults are doing something confusing. He is enthusiastic in ways that don't always map onto the situation. He pointed at Valphine and said what he thought was a helpful suggestion: "Valphine, use good shield! Valphine, cleric of sun!"

Valphine started to answer him — "You can use my shield" — but Vukradin cut across it.

"You can give them your shield, you can do whatever you want with your shield. But that shield?" A pause. "Not yours."

Prutha protested. Vukradin looked at him directly.

"Not yours either."

Prutha went quiet. Valphine confirmed the point without warmth: "It's not mine. Be sure." She tucked the shield under her arm and said she'd hold it until the owner turned up. Sensible. I thought about the half-orc with five somethings marked in wood. Probably dead. Probably won't come asking. Vukradin's right anyway.

Vukradin added, almost as an afterthought: "I recommend you guys do a better job hiding your crown merchandise." He meant the Talosians. He meant the bolt. It was good advice delivered with the tone of a man who has given up expecting it to be followed.

Then someone brought up my halberd.

I had the halberd from the shipwreck. It's mine. It fights well. I didn't see the need for extensive discussion.

Vukradin did the thing where he looks surprised and isn't. "You got a new halberd? Yo, what is going on here, guys?"

"I got a divorce," I said.

That got a pause. Then Vukradin, bless him, provided the cover story without being asked: "This is the halberd you've had all along. You just sharpened it on a sharpening stone. It just looks more lethal."

Valphine added: "And it looks a little waterlogged."

"You're mistaken," Vukradin said, to no one in particular. "This is the halberd you've had all along. You just sharpened it on a sharpening stone. And it just looks more lethal. That's totally fine. You can sharpen your halberd as much as you want. No problem with that."

I said nothing. The halberd sat against my leg, waterlogged and lethal.

Valphine started talking about Lathander then — the real Lathander, her version. The argument was that people have it wrong about what light is. It isn't tender. It burns twelve hours a day, it hurts everything it touches at full strength, and when it's gone people beg for it to return. They're afraid of the dark. That's not worship of goodness. That's worship of power. Power that's in plain sight, unashamed, causing harm constantly, and called *good* because the alternative is worse.

Vukradin listened. Then he said: "I like that the cleric is changing the god instead of the god changing the cleric."

Nobody disagreed with that.

Valphine talked about Lathander. I thought about my Talos. The Uthgardt one. Storms. The thing you don't argue with. I was sober when they found me guilty. That was a long time ago.

The halberd is mine. The shield belongs to whoever has five somethings marked in oak. That's the accounting, and it's simple enough.

## Vukradin — Planning the Next Move

The henge was behind us — stone and ash and the smell of something that had been wrong for a long time finally being allowed to stop. I didn't linger on it. That was done. The question was what came next.

Two choices. That was how it was framed. Woodland Manse, where another ritual was building toward something loud and terrible, or Falcon's Hunting Lodge, where whatever the Talosians had left behind was still festering. Infinite choices, technically. Two, really. Manse or lodge.

I didn't deliberate.

"I decided what I was going to do," I said. "I refuse to do anything more for this guy unless I get an apology from him."

Soma's response was immediate. "The Falcon? Yeah, fuck that guy."

It wasn't the diplomatic register I usually aimed for, but I wasn't inclined to correct her. What Falcon had done at the Druid Council — standing up in front of people I needed to persuade and taking shots at me, making himself useful to Adabra at my expense — that had cost us something. It had cost *me* something. I had been building something careful there, and he had leaned into it like a man who didn't understand the weight of what he was handling.

I had let it sit. I had not contacted him. Valphine had been working something with him in the background — I knew that much, though not the specifics — and I'd decided the next move was his to make. But now the lodge needed clearing, and apparently that required me to act first.

"You want me to call him? I mean, I can call him, but he better apologize."

Soma offered a fallback — the kind she offers when she's already decided the apology won't come. "If you don't get the apology, you can just, you know, we can take care of the infestation and not exactly protect the hunting lodge. Just… just throwing that out there as, you know, possible ways out of this mess."

I appreciated the option. I also didn't want to need it.

The tone was the thing I was thinking about. Demanding an apology from a man who has already moved on makes you look small. I needed it to land differently. I wanted *audacity* — a tone that communicated I was doing *him* a favor by asking at all.

"What's, like, the famous one… Lord Halueth Verres. Right? When they were confirming his elevation to Lord Magister of Neverwinter, there was that war-mage from Waterdeep — what was her name — she came up at the hearing and testified against him. The artifact thing, the contract with the apprentices, the whole mess. He got the seat anyway. And then *ten years* later, Lady Verres calls her on a Sending. Out of nowhere. 'Are you finally ready to apologize to my husband for what you said at his confirmation?' Ten years later. So ridiculous. And the war-mage actually replied. So that is the indignant tone I'm taking. Lady Verres on a Sending."

I burned a Sending — twenty-five words, one shot.

"Are you finally ready to apologize for the way you treated me at the Druid Council?"

Valphine added, from beside me: "Clarify the context. We're considering whether or not to go clear out all the, you know, the unwanted guests at your hunting lodge."

"Yeah, no, I'll add that." I felt the sending still holding. "The party — my friends here tell me we need to go take care of your problem at the hunting lodge. But are you ready to apologize for what you did at the Druid Council?"

Twenty-five words. You learn to compress.

The response came back the way it always does — not sound exactly, more like thought arriving from outside your own head, carrying another voice's weight. Falcon's voice. He said: *I apologize.* Categorical. Not hedged. No qualifications attached, no *I'm sorry if you felt* construction that isn't an apology at all. Just the thing itself, and then: *he's on the road home. Hopes to see us there.*

I stood with that for a moment.

"Sounds like he did apologize, though." I heard myself say it. "Okay. We can go."

He apologized clean. Valphine had done the groundwork — I could feel it in how ready the answer was. But the ask had been mine. Done.

We spread the documents out and went through them again — Flenz's sealed letters, Narux's vellum sketches, the half-burned scrap from Yargath. Their plan was clear enough: a summoning ritual confirmed underway at the manse, a backup ritual waiting if the first failed, and if both failed, whoever was in charge would attempt the summoning personally. The redundancy of it was deliberate. These people were not being casual about this.

The Talosian attack on the lodge puzzled me. It had been their lodge — we'd learned that the hard way — so why turn on it now?

"Scenario 1," Valphine said, sorting it for her own amusement, "they're Talosian, and then we destroyed them. Scenario 2 is that they're not Talosian, and we get some alloys."

I had no clean answer for either. It was the kind of question that would only resolve once we were there.

Prutha knew something, or thought he did. He'd heard songs about the lodge — heard the orcs had taken it, heard about the dogs. He didn't know who was staying there now. But he confirmed the three factions: former tribe, Talosian-aligned, independent. And the Anchorites had moved their own people through. That last detail felt important in a way I couldn't fully articulate yet.

I inventoried what I had left. Two minor workings spent, two serious ones gone — one of them just now on Falcon's apology. The count wasn't encouraging.

"You know, I've got… I've burned through the small workings and two of the heavy ones."

"I just need something to resist lightning," I said. "Do we have anything for resisting lightning?"

Soma checked herself and came up empty. Valphine suggested obscuring mist, or darkness — something to complicate targeting. I turned it over.

"Well, Darkness prevents targeting, but lightning doesn't require targeting."

A fire spell doesn't care whether it can see you. Neither does a storm. That was the problem.

"Well, we definitely need to camp at some point, right? It's more than a day's journey to the lodge, right?"

Closer than that. Hours away — not a day's march.

I looked at the others. Valphine wanted the rest — wanted to be full going in, which was reasonable. But if the ritual was already moving, time spent recovering was time we might not have. And we didn't know that it was active today. Only that it would be, eventually.

"Valphine, you can decide."

She looked at me the way she does when she's deciding whether I mean it. I was being genuine.

"We'll approach the hunting lodge from the south, though," she said finally.

Good enough.

"Let's head to the lodge," I said. "We can scope it out, we'll be there before long, and we can see what we see. That's fine."

I meant it. Whatever we were walking into, we were walking in with what we had. I had made do before with less.

## Valphine — Arrival at Falcon's Hunting Lodge

Two stories, timber and stone, a guest house off to one side, stable and smithy joined at the hip. The palisade newer in patches than the wood it had been lashed to — repairs under pressure, mismatched grain, the patchwork look of something rebuilt by people who could not afford to plan. Not vandals. Tenants.

Three figures on the walkway, watching the treeline. They'd seen us already. Two had bows up, not raised to draw but not lowered either — the posture of people who had made the calculation and not yet decided. The third was calling something down into the courtyard. A warning, or an order; I couldn't yet hear the words.

Falcon had asked me to mediate his apology to Vukradin. That gave me the floor here in advance. The dead partner, the attack the party had failed to prevent, the cloak left on its hook — I had inventoried all of that before we walked in.

I was wrong about the cloak.

Prutha moved before any of us spoke. He was watching the banners, and then he said it clearly, loud enough for the walkway to hear: *"Eastern Heart! Three banners on the wall! I see one! Sun and Stone! My uncle's clan march! They are family!"*

He was already walking. Deliberate pace, hands open — not a supplicant's gesture but a recognition, an announcement of his own. I watched him cover the distance to the gate. Close enough to be heard clearly, he stopped, brought his right fist to his left shoulder, and lowered his head. Then he reached inside his armor and drew out the bone whistle — the same one whose tradition had moved me more than I'd expected when I first saw it — and blew the dawn prayer into the cold air.

I held very still.

The sentry on the walkway lowered her bow. Returned the gesture. Shouted something down into the courtyard, and Prutha answered, and then she shouted back toward the gate.

*"Bring your drow, bring your Aasimar, bring all of them. We are listening."*

*Your drow* — possessive, not categorical. Prutha's. I noted what that meant for the alliance she thought she was joining.

Soma settled Boney near the stables once we were through the gate — *"Here you go, Boney. Chill out here"* — and the skeletal horse did not protest, which was more than could be said for some members of this party in new situations. Then we moved into the courtyard proper and I took in the full picture.

Women with infants. Old men around a cookfire, tending something in an iron pot. Children playing a stick game against the far wall, engrossed, not frightened by us yet. A dozen warriors spread through perhaps thirty orcs total. They watched us the way I have seen people watch strangers in places they did not choose to be but cannot leave. Recognition without welcome. Evaluation that was also calculation.

Refugees, then. Not raiders. The distinction mattered.

She came down from the walkway. Middle-aged, gray at the temples, broken nose healed badly, a scar along her jaw that had been made by something old and serious. She was wearing Falcon's hunter cloak over her own leather — not his colors or insignia reworked, not a trophy displayed with pride, but practical: the warmest thing in the closet, worn by someone who was cold. Someone who had been inside the lodge and taken what she needed.

Vukradin said something quiet to me. *"Not worthy."* I agreed, out loud, to confirm that I was paying attention to the same thing and had arrived at the same conclusion: this was not the moment to press on a dead man's cloak. *"Not worthy."* He said it once more, satisfied, and I let the matter close.

Vorga's elder was already looking at me. He leaned on his spear the way men do when the spear has outlasted their sons. His eyes had gone to my holy symbol the moment I walked into the courtyard, and they had not left it. Not hostility. Hunger. He had been praying a long time and had not been answered. I could see it without him saying it.

He said one word. Orcish. Direct.

*Dawn.*

Menzoberranzan does not reward readable expressions; the habit holds even under the open surface sky. But the word *Dawn* in his mouth touched the bodily place my own conversion had started from. The doctrine answered before the analysis did.

I looked back at him. I answered in Orcish, because he deserved that, and because it was the right instrument for the conversation.

"Yes," I said. "I'm the bringer of the dawn."

I let him see the symbol clearly.

"A painful dawn."

## Soma — Negotiations at the Hunting Lodge

Vorga looked at Valphine funny. Then she looked at Prutha funny.

I caught it — the slight tilt of Vorga's head, the flicker of calculation in her eyes as they moved from the drow to the orc at our backs. I said nothing. That's the kind of look you wait on.

Valphine had opened in orcish, which left the rest of us standing in the lodge courtyard with nothing to do but watch two people have a conversation we couldn't hear. I am old enough to be comfortable with that. Brewbarry was not. He shifted his weight from foot to foot like a man who had misplaced his axe and only just noticed. Vukradin tilted his head as if proximity to the sounds might eventually yield meaning.

What I could read was posture, and Vorga's posture said: not hostile. Occupied, maybe. Tired, certainly. The twenty or so people arranged around the courtyard — elders, children, a man who moved like his hip had been broken and reset badly — were not the profile of a raiding force. They were people who had found an empty building and moved into it the way animals find caves. Not conquest. Necessity.

I'd seen that before. After Cryovain came down, every creature that could move had moved. Refugees with claws.

Valphine switched to Common.

"I say they come from the southern mountains, and I told them about the Talosian nearby."

Vukradin turned immediately. "Oh, were they displaced by the dragon?"

"Sounds like it," Valphine said. Then, with the timing she uses when she wants the room to tilt: "dragon that you killed."

Vukradin looked at the assembled orcs. Then, louder than necessary: "We killed the dragon."

They stared. Twenty people, some armed, some not, all of them going absolutely still. Even the children stopped moving.

"You killed Dragon?" Vorga said it the way you say something when you're testing whether you've heard correctly.

"Yep," Vukradin said. He had, I noticed, put his chin up slightly. He does that.

Vorga processed this. "Dragon is dead. That home is now free to go back?"

"Yeah, you can go back," he said.

Valphine added, mild as water: "But something worse than the dragon."

It was accurate. I found myself nodding. The current occupancy of Icespire Hold was, as far as any of us knew, unconfirmed. Vukradin pivoted to an offer of escort, which was kind of him and potentially over-optimistic, which is also very much him.

Vorga chose to stay. She walked him through it — and I watched her do it. She had made this calculation before, in other places, with other stakes; you could see it in how fast she got to the end. The open woods against a pursuing force. Twenty people who could not run fast enough. She pointed to an elder near the wall, a man whose age showed in the careful way he held himself, and said: these people don't move fast enough. The creature would catch up.

Vukradin tried. "Brewbarry can carry everybody." He glanced at Boney, at me. "Put everybody on Brewbarry's back, and we got Boney, too."

I said, "And you can ride the elk, I can carry a couple."

It was worth offering. The instinct to find another way is a good instinct, and I've learned to let the shell-sprouts exhaust the alternatives before I agree that the harder path is the right one. Vorga walked through it anyway. The elk, the horse, the Goliath — the old man near the wall would still have needed carrying, and carrying the old man was not the problem. The problem was what came after: a summoned creature moving through forest while you were moving through forest, slower, encumbered.

"Creature will catch up," she said.

She said it a third time, or some version of it, and I think it was the third time that Vukradin actually heard it.

Her plan was simple. Kill the creature here, where they knew where it was coming from. Then walk to Icespire in safety. She had done the geometry of survival already, before we arrived. We had just joined a plan in progress.

The note surfaced briefly — something about a boy from the East, recovered at the Circle of Thunder, someone had called him a good recruit. I looked at Prutha. He was standing quietly, as he often does. He'd learned that one early — stillness is safer than motion. I thought about the way Vorga's eyes had moved to him when Valphine first spoke. I didn't say anything.

Then Brewbarry screamed.

Not the battle-cry kind. The other kind — the kind that comes out of a man who has sustained a genuine personal loss.

He appeared from the interior of the lodge holding a broken cask. The wood was dark with old moisture. Whatever had been inside was gone, soaked into the floor planks, wasted. Not drunk. Wasted.

"I will kill them all!" he announced to the courtyard.

Vukradin moved toward him immediately, hands up, voice calibrated. Every scrap of warmth and persuasion he had went into promising more alcohol would be found, that calm was achievable, that this was a recoverable situation. Brewbarry accepted this in the way a man accepts a rain delay — reluctant, not persuaded, still certain someone was going to pay.

I watched him and thought: the Talosians had not met Brewbarry yet. That was going to be a short conversation.

Valphine cast something during the lull — not a healing. A bracing. The kind that makes a body harder to drop. I felt it in my shell.

"It can be healed," I said. "Semi-temporary."

"It's better than temporary," Vukradin said.

"No, it is," I said. "Because it can be healed."

Vorga suggested someone stand on the wall so the Falcon would see a friendly face and not come in shooting. Vukradin volunteered. "We're friends now," he said, warm — he'd been waiting on this reconciliation. "He apologized."

He stood up on the palisade and the Falcon came out of the trees with war paint on his face and a bow already in hand.

Vukradin's voice dropped an octave very quickly. "Jay! You made it! Oh, oh, oh, I'll come down, I'll come down, I'll come down."

The Falcon's bow came down when Vukradin reached him. They spoke. The Falcon listened to the whole of it — the orcs, the alliance, the incoming Talosians, the plan — and when Vukradin finished, he said: "You have my sword."

I thought about the last time I'd seen Falcon, at the Enclave council. The way grief wears a man differently than anger does. This version of him — paint on his face, weapon ready, walking toward a fight with something like purpose — was different from that one. Not healed. But aimed.

Valphine pointed at the map and said something about the gatehouse having fantastically defensible positions, and we put three orc archers at the arrow slits. They settled in. They knew the work.

The lodge went still. Waiting.

## Brewbarry — The Siege of Falcon's Hunting Lodge

The storm came wrong.

I knew it before I understood it — a pressure behind my eyes, sudden and complete, like a fist closing around the inside of my skull. Then the sky went. Afternoon to dark in a single breath, and the wind came up hard enough to rattle the palisade logs in their lashings.

The animals felt it first. The boars in Falcon's pens started throwing themselves against the walls of their enclosure, squealing in a register that made the hairs on my arms stand up. I've heard boars charge. This wasn't that. This was panic — pure, animal, knowing.

Drubbak had gone very still. The old orc stood beside the gatehouse without moving, his head tilted like he was listening for something underneath the wind. Then he said it, quiet enough that I almost missed it under the noise:

"It come wrong. There is no chant. Where is the chant?"

I didn't know what that meant. Drubbak knew storms the way I know a weapon's balance — by feel, by something that lived below words. Whatever he was feeling, I could see it in how he held himself. That particular stillness is worse than shouting.

Lightning split the tree line, close enough that the light was white and absolute, and the crack came a half-second later, loud enough to feel in the chest. Then the river. The water jumped up in a white column and the sound rolled across the palisade like a boulder. Then the third bolt came down inside the compound and blew an empty barrel into kindling, and the smell of it — ozone and charred wood — hit me like a slap.

Soma held up her staff. A new leaf had pushed out from one of the buds, fresh and green, curled at the edge like it was still deciding.

"Guys, it's sprouting!"

"It means an angel got its wings," she added, "don't worry."

It was the driest thing she'd said in days, and I found myself grinning despite everything.

Valphine moved through us without hurrying. She never hurries. Whatever she did, I felt it land. Something settled in my chest. An extra layer between me and whatever was coming through that gate. I'm not philosophical about magic the way Vukradin is. I just know when something useful has been done.

We placed the orc archers in the gatehouse tower, behind the arrow slits. Valphine had called them "aerosluts," which I had no immediate response to, and the orcs didn't seem offended. Boney went inside the palisade, near the stable house, which Vukradin described with great seriousness as "cannon fodder." Falcon took position with his bow. We spread through the courtyard.

This was the part I was good at. Not the positioning, not the tactics — those belong to Vukradin and Valphine. The part where everything was decided and you just had to hold the line. I checked my grip on the halberd. I flexed my hands once inside the gauntlets. The Dread Helm sat warm on my skull, and I knew from experience my eyes were doing the red-glow thing, which tends to get a reaction.

Then Vukradin said Gorthok was there.

Not almost summoned. Not coming. There. Among the attacking forces. Already.

Soma said she hadn't expected him until the summoning was complete, and the tone she used was the tone she uses when she is trying very hard not to say *you idiots.* The answer was that we had come to the lodge instead of dealing with it. We had negotiated, and rested, and the world had not waited.

Vukradin said we'd made the right call.

Maybe. I thought about the Uthgardt stories of the Great Boar — a thing conjured out of storm and lightning, older than the gods that claimed to own it. I thought about how you stop something like that. I thought: *probably you don't stop it. Probably you just get in front of it.*

Vukradin had the measure of the field before anyone else did — reading the enemy positions with the expression he gets when he sees an opportunity, eyes slightly narrowed, already building the moment in his head. Falcon, he assessed with magnificent condescension, was slower off the mark.

"Oh, they are nicely grouped, too."

"Convenience," Soma said.

"How convenient, indeed." He tapped his fingers. "I need a working that takes them all at once, man. Something that swallows a crowd whole. And it's not the fire-burst — I spent that particular gift elsewhere."

I said the obvious thing. *They are savages. We kill them all.*

Vukradin agreed with great enthusiasm. "You're right, we will kill them! Huzzah! We are agreeing on everything!"

I like Vukradin's music. Most people don't understand what I mean by that. Today they would.

The lightning came from well beyond the palisade.

A bolt, not from the sky but from the darkness past the wall. Aimed. It hit the gate.

The doors came apart. Not damaged. Gone. The logs and iron fittings that had made up the lodge's main entrance ceased to be a gate and became debris, and through the gap and the smoke and the afterimage burned on my eyes, I could see shapes moving.

Gorthok first. You feel something like that before you see it. A mass. A wrongness. The air going wrong in front of him. The Anchorites clustering behind, the Talosian forces converging on the hole.

Vukradin was muttering something about the working that tangles a mind in its own visions. Soma was watching her staff. Drubbak had not moved from his place, still listening for the chant that wasn't there.

My halberd was already in my hand.
