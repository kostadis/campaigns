Twelve of them.

Vukradin said "Two twigs and an anchorite — the start of a joke," and he wasn't wrong, but twelve is not a joke. Twelve is a wall of dry, clacking wood and too many limbs, and they came fast. I was already moving.

That's the thing about the rage. People think it makes you stupid. It doesn't. It narrows everything down to what matters — where the threat is, where the gap is, where you can put your body to do the most good. The hill was steep and the standing stones were wrong, humming at a frequency that made my back teeth ache, and the two half-orcs at the center of it all were still dancing, still chanting, and I wanted to be the thing that made them stop.

The lightning bolt came before I reached them.

Vukradin had already said it, dry as old bark: "It's nice that we're all lined up in a perfect line there — that's convenient for him." He wasn't wrong about that either. The bolt split the air between us and I got my shoulder turned into it and the current raked through my scale mail and I was still standing because I am Brewbarry Root Smasher Ogalakadu and I have stood inside a dragon's breath, and a half-orc in a boar mask is not a dragon. Valphine took it worse. I saw her flinch — something going tight across her face that she would never call pain but that looked exactly like it. The Bless she was carrying, the invisible weight of Lathander's favor pressing on the die, and it still rolled against her twice. Some days the gods are funny.

Vukradin didn't waste the opening. He spoke and the two anchorites stopped dancing. Just — stopped. Mid-step, mid-chant, arms out, frozen like figures in a story someone stopped telling. Hold Person. Both of them. I've seen him do things with words that make no sense to me and probably never will, but this I understood immediately: they were objects now. Problems with a shape. I moved.

The handaxe is not my preferred statement. I am a big man with a big sword and I know how to use it, but the blights were between me and the anchorites and the blights needed clearing, so I pulled the rage up from wherever it lives in my chest — it feels like a door opening, every time, like something I've been keeping in a small room finally being let out — and I threw.

The first throw was embarrassing. The axe spun wide and I had a half-second of pure goliath shame before Valphine slapped a bardic inspiration into my hand, metaphorically speaking. I rolled the die. The die rolled an eight. The axe was already back in my hand — it comes back, that's the trick, that's what I've been calling the Elemental Cleaver, because naming things makes them more real — and I adjusted and threw again and the blight I hit came apart like kindling. The momentum carried: one blight dead, the kill gave me a heartbeat of something clear and sharp, and I used it to take a second. Two down. One more and I missed, and by then the battlefield was moving around me in all directions.

Boney bit a paralyzed anchorite. The skeletal horse that Soma has somehow convinced is a willing member of this party bit a frozen man in a boar mask and I respect that completely.

Prutha ran through blights like they were grass. He put a javelin into the second anchorite and then charged in with his greataxe and I watched him and thought, briefly, of myself ten years younger, when violence was still simple, before the exile made it complicated. The anchorite he hit was already staggered. Not down, but close.

Soma became an elk. I have seen this before and it still takes a moment. One second: Soma, tortle, shell, old and unhurried. Next second: enormous antlers, enormous mass, sixty feet and accelerating. She hit the anchorite like a wall of forest deciding to move. He didn't have a choice about it.

Then she turned and hit one of the standing stones.

I thought: is that part of it? The henge was wrong, humming, tainted — Talosian hands had been on it, doing whatever ritual left a deer split open at the center with its heart replaced by something black and glassy and cold. Breaking a stone felt right. Felt like undoing something.

Valphine was moving through the twig blights like a woman who finds this situation aesthetically appropriate. The Spirit Guardians she called up swirled around her in radiant arcs and the blights caught in them just — went. Three and four gone. Then six. Then eight. She crossed the field and the light went with her and I kept count because counting kept me sharp.

The anchorites never got free of the Hold. Vukradin said, somewhere to my left, "Hold Person is a very powerful spell," the way a man says something he already knew but is pleased to have confirmed.

At the end, Valphine said her battle cry. She said: *"Die ye then."*

She hit the last anchorite and he went down and the chant that had been threading through the air for however long we'd been on this hill — it stopped. The clouds kept moving for a moment, like they hadn't been told yet. Then they peeled back. Slow. Reluctant. Like something exhaling. The pressure in my ears dropped. The ozone smell faded to wet leaves and old rain. The stones went quiet.

No thunderclap. Just the end of it.

I stood in the sudden silence and looked at the two bodies. Young, both of them. Younger than I expected under the boar masks. One with storm-cloud tattoos spiraling up his neck, each one with an open eye. The other with crossed boar tusks down both forearms. They'd been someone's devoted. Someone had put that ink in their skin and told them this mattered, and they'd believed it, and now they were dead on a hill in the Neverwinter Wood with their masks rolled free of their faces.

I didn't feel bad about it. I felt the way I always feel after a fight: present, tired in the right way, aware of every part of my body. Alive.

Vukradin called it: "Huzzah!"

I exhaled.
