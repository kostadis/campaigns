The hill had been climbing for most of the afternoon, and then it stopped. The treeline thinned and there it was — a ring of standing stones against a sky that hadn't decided whether to storm. The clouds above the henge moved the way clouds don't move: inward, circling, held in place by something that wasn't wind.

Two half-orcs stood among the stones. Twelve twig blights ranged around them like a second ring, bark-dry and motionless. The half-orcs were moving — not marching, not chanting. Dancing. Slow, deliberate steps that traced a pattern I couldn't fully see from the treeline but could feel the shape of: something being called toward. Something being opened.

Corbin, I thought. Then I pushed the thought somewhere useful.

Valphine was the one who named it. She watched for a moment, head tilted, that particular stillness she gets when she's cataloguing rather than reacting. "Summoning ritual," she said. Quiet, certain. The way she identifies things she's seen before.

I looked at the dancers again with that frame on them. "Oh, yeah, you would recognize that in this," I said. "Animate Dead." Not a question. The motion had that quality — purposeful vacancy, movement in service of something absent.

Soma watched. Brewbarry watched. Neither of them saw it the way Valphine did, and I was moving through the same fog — you don't spend your early years in Phandalin studying Talosian summoning rites. What I felt was the thing underneath the recognition: we were already late. Whatever they were calling, they had been at it for a while.

"I wonder who they're summoning," I said.

"I mean, you'll find out," came back at me from somewhere to my left, dry as bark.

"No, we won't."

There are promises you make in a treeline. I meant it as one.

The tactical question resolved itself quickly in my mind into a single clean answer: Silence. You cast it on the chanters, you cut the ritual at its voice. I had used it before, at the lighthouse, at worse angles. "Alright, I'm gonna cast Silence," I said. "And I, once again, have no, like, connection to the board, so — but I am casting silence on whoever it is is chanting." I kept my voice level. "And if that is unknown, then the two spellcasters."

Except they weren't chanting. That was the thing. We waited, someone went back through the logic of it, and the answer came back clear and slightly absurd: the ritual had no vocal component. They were dancing. Twelve twig blights and two half-orcs turning slow deliberate circles in a stone ring, silent as a held breath.

"Is there no vocal component to the ritual?" I asked, already hearing the answer.

There wasn't.

"Awesome," I said. The word landed flatter than I intended.

I filed the Silence away. "I mean, if there's no vocal component, there's whole person — that's also an option." Hold Person. Different spell, same interruption. The ritual, we determined, would end only when the dancers stopped. The standing stones couldn't be disrupted from the outside; the movement had to cease. Which meant the people making it had to cease.

Brewbarry was watching the half-orcs step their slow circles in total silence. "So they're like, what, a pantomime club?" he said.

I looked at him. I looked at the henge. "Okay, guys," I said. "Do you want me to go with Silence or Hold Person?"

Before anyone answered, someone caught the gap: we hadn't rolled initiatives yet. The observation arrived with that particular mild embarrassment of a procedural thing almost skipped over in the press of the moment.

We rolled. I drew the short straw on the tie-breakers — except I didn't. I won them. Several, one after another, my name settling ahead of the others in the order like the situation had opinions. I had no strong feelings about going first. I had feelings about what going first meant I would have to do.

"Alright," I said, once the order was settled and clear. "So I've not cast Silence — because we're following initiative order, alright?"

The hill was still there. The stones were still there. The dancers had not stopped.
