The last orc standing looked at me with the specific expression of a man who has just watched nine of his fellows die and is working out the arithmetic of the situation. He was holding his greataxe. I was watching his eyes.

I said everything I had to say to him. The words came from the same place they always come from — not performance, not calculation, but the genuine conviction that there is a true frame available to any moment and I am reaching for it. The sun above the gully. The Drow cleric wreathed in impossible light. The dead anchorite of Talos cooling behind us. I laid it out for him. The picture was honest. It required no embellishment.

He set down the greataxe.

He looked at Valphine — this radiant, painful, sunlit figure that no orc who grew up praying to the Stormlord could have anticipated encountering in a forest gully — and the working-out in his eyes resolved. He spoke in halting Common, his voice somewhere between wonder and practical assessment.

"Talos offer power. Lathander — more power. Lathander all-powerful."

Then he rushed toward Valphine and announced himself a follower of the Drow Cleric of Lathander.

I said, honestly: *"Wow. We've got a follower!"*

Because that was exactly what we had.

His name was Prutha. The party began debating conversion names before I could object — which I did, loudly, the moment Valphine suggested *Thrall.*

"Harsh, man, harsh. Wow." I let the word sit there. Then: "You know, I object. You know, he doesn't know what thrall means, but I know what thrall means. That is not appropriate for our new friend."

Prutha kept his name. As it should be.

What he told us next required more than just the one moment to take in. His great-uncle had been his clan's shaman. The Talosians killed him deliberately, early, to break the clan's resistance and make recruitment easier. They called the old man weak, abomination, thunder meat. And then they stood in front of what remained of his family and offered the terms Talos always offers: fight, or be what his great-uncle was.

Prutha had fought. Three skirmishes. Never killed by choice.

I thought about Corbin — briefly, painfully, the way I always think about Corbin now, a flash of the face and then the weight settling. I had pushed a man into something similar and called it recruiting. I wasn't going to do that accounting here, in the middle of a gully. But the thought was present.

Then Prutha reached into his clothing and produced a small bone whistle.

He explained that in his clan, the orcs had greeted the sunrise each morning by blowing a bone whistle. Eastern tradition, his great-uncle's tradition. He held it up. He looked at it for a long moment.

And then he blew it.

One note. Simple and thin and very old.

And then he began to cry.

Valphine read him with a sharpness I could not have matched — later she told me her read was simply that he'd realized how long it had been since he last blew it. That was what the tears were for. Not the dead uncle. Not the three skirmishes. Not the men who died in the gully behind him. The whistle. The gap between the whistle then and the whistle now.

I found I had nothing to say for a moment.

Then Prutha gave us the line his great-uncle had apparently lived by, the thing the old shaman had passed down before the Talosians named him thunder meat and put him in the ground:

*"Dawn comes for everyone, even those born in shadow."*

The table went quiet.

"Wow," I said. "It's quite poetic."

I meant it completely. I said to Prutha — honestly, reaching for the right thing to say to a man who had just blown a whistle for the first time in what seemed like years: "You're my first."

And then the thought arrived, the one I voiced aloud because it was obvious and true: "He wants to play the whistle at the dawn, but with Lathander, it's like it's the dawn all the time. So, like, he could play the whistle all the time."

The theology here was not complicated. Lathander is perpetual dawn. The whistle greets the dawn. The math resolves. I was genuinely pleased with how cleanly it fit.

Valphine reached for a Religion assessment of the situation and came back with a result she described only as "oh my god" in a tone that suggested the dice had not cooperated. Despite this, some kind of genuine insight arrived anyway — the notion that the whistle ritual could be woven into her own evolving practice, that Lathander was not moving further from her for it, but closer. I thought about Valphine's church, which the party had described diplomatically as a masochism cult, and then thought about the bone whistle and the weeping orc and the words about dawn and shadow, and I thought: well, this is how religions acquire depth. It is not always tidy.

I turned to Prutha and delivered what I had to give him.

"Lathander blesses you today, my child."

He looked at me. "What does that mean?"

"Lathander blesses you today, my child."

He thought about it. Then: "He thinks that's awesome." He had, apparently, lots to think about.

"Lathander — power. Beyond doubt. Lathander all-powerful." He turned back to Valphine and pledged himself again, firmly, in case anyone had missed it the first time.

Then he kicked the fallen anchorite's body several times to make a point about theological transition.

I disapproved of the corpse-stringing that followed — when Valphine and Brewbarry hauled the anchorite up as a warning, Prutha was visibly enthusiastic in a way that landed somewhere I preferred not to examine — but I didn't intervene. I had a follower. He had just blown a bone whistle and wept and recited a line of genuine beauty in his second language. I was prepared to allow him the kicks.

He laid out his terms for the path forward: orcs no longer following Talos, we spare. Committed Talosians are another matter entirely.

Convert or die. A fluid approach to religion, yes. But it worked for the anchorite in the other direction, didn't it? The Talosians had offered exactly that framing to Prutha's great-uncle, and the only difference between that moment and this one is which door leads somewhere worth walking through.

I asked about the orc who was still unconscious somewhere behind us. Whether he might be revivable, convertible, another Prutha waiting to emerge.

The answer was uncertain. We pressed on.

Someone — I will not say who — made a joke about whether Corbin would have liked Prutha.

The DM's response was that it was cold.

He was right. It was cold. And also, yes. Corbin would have liked Prutha very much. He would have recognized something in the whistle, in the terms offered and refused and survived through compromise, in the great-uncle's line about dawn and shadow. He would have been moved by it.

I was moved by it. I was carrying that, along with everything else.
