We found Xanthopoulos where Vukradin's Sending had promised — at the corner where the Triboar Trail bends toward Phandalin, hooves planted in the road like he'd been there for hours and had enjoyed every minute of waiting. That is a centaur's patience for you. They do not fidget.

He was pleased with us. Conspicuously, thoroughly pleased, in the way that Xanth expresses everything — with his whole chest and a good portion of his mane.

"My good friends, you have done such an amazing job. It was absolutely fantastic. Soma, you were superb."

I accepted this the way I accept most good news: quietly, with internal suspicion that more work is coming. Meril's name followed, as it tends to when Xanth and I speak. *Meril was very interested.* In what, exactly, I did not ask. Meril left me a staff and a year's worth of half-finished lessons and whatever was left of his faith in something he hadn't quite named. Xanth has always known more about what that means than he's said. I've learned not to push. The staff knows things before I do, and it hasn't led me wrong yet. One leaf left on it now. I try not to think about that too directly.

Boney clopped forward as Xanth greeted us, all bones and dignity, and I remembered I was supposed to be managing his decisions in a fight. The arrangement had been sorted earlier with less ceremony than it deserved.

"Yeah, I mean, I'm just gonna pretend he's, you know, stomping and riding and charging and shit."

That is my strategy. That has always been my strategy. Boney is a sensible horse in the way that only skeletal horses seem to manage — he does not startle, he does not complain about the weather, and he has never once tried to eat my pack. Lady Alagondar made good choices, whoever she was. I respect her taste in horses.

Thirty miles. That is what a full day in the woods looks like on a map: a line the length of your ambition. On your legs it is something else. The forest swallowed us gradually, the road becoming a track becoming a suggestion, and all the while I was reading it the way you learn to read anything if you've been looking long enough — the angle of lichen on the north faces, the places where animals had stopped walking, the shapes that told you the canopy hadn't been disturbed in years and the shapes that told you it had, recently, and not by weather.

Xanth moved through the trees with the ease of a creature who belongs to them. I moved behind him and tried to do the same, and almost managed it.

When the light began to go, someone raised the question of pressing on.

"We're not in a rush, are we?"

Nobody argued. Valphine had already solved the camp problem the way she solves most problems — by asking the person who already knows the answer. Xanth had scouted a good site. Of course he had.

The fire was small and sensible. Xanth settled beside it like a landmark settling into its proper place, and Vukradin — because Vukradin cannot help himself, and I mean this with genuine fondness — asked him for stories.

He had stories. Stars above, he had stories. Birds and flowers and ancient heroes and the complicated grievances of things older than Phandalin, older than the road we'd walked, older than most of the trouble we'd spent the last several months wading through. He spoke about the depredations of Neverwinter in the tone of a creature who has watched the same mistakes accumulate over centuries and has developed a very refined kind of sorrow about it.

The others went quiet in the way that is not listening. I have watched shell-sprouts do this my entire life — the eyes settle somewhere past the speaker, the face goes politely neutral, and the mind is somewhere warmer and more comfortable. I did not blame them. I was listening, but I am old and I have developed a fondness for sitting still while someone who knows more than me talks. You pick up things that way. You pick up things you didn't know you needed until you need them.

What I heard underneath the birds and flowers was something colder.

He talked about the forest. The trees, the creatures, the way things had moved before the white dragon's winter came and broke the quiet. That, I already knew. Cryovain's disruption had scattered everything with sense enough to scatter. But then Xanth said something that sat differently.

The new orcs. The Talosian ones. They were not, he said, normal orcs. Something fundamentally more evil than the typical kind. Something fundamentally more dangerous.

I turned that over while the fire burned down. The forest around us felt inhabited in a way I couldn't fully account for — not wrong, not yet, but watching. The way any old living thing watches when something has been done to it that hasn't finished hurting.

The others were mostly asleep or nearly so. Xanth was looking at the canopy with the patience of a creature made to outlast things.

I listened to the trees for a while longer before I let myself rest.
