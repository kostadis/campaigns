The ridge came up fast — one moment we were picking our way across uneven ground, the next we were standing on the crest of it, looking across sixty feet of open air at a gully ten feet deep, and then, cresting the opposite ridge like a wave breaking, the orcs.

Ten of them. I counted. And at their center, a humanoid figure with elongated claws and the particular quality of someone who has consecrated themselves to a very loud god.

The battle cries hit me before I fully processed the numbers. There is a sound that fighting people make — not the sound of people who are angry or scared or even violent in the ordinary way — but the sound of people who have decided, cosmologically, that noise itself is holy. The Talosian clergy understand this. They are not trying to intimidate you. They are performing liturgy.

I found this, in the moment, genuinely interesting.

Initiative. The call went up and I felt that particular clarity settle over me — the moment when a situation stops being a situation and becomes, briefly, something you can actually navigate. I had the first opening. I knew it before the confirmation came.

"Yes, you do."

Good. Sixty feet to the opposite ridge. An acolyte who definitely looked like an acolyte, as these things go — the claws, the posture, the absolute conviction radiating off him like heat. And around him, ten orcs who were, it occurred to me, simply orcs. People with a context I did not yet fully understand. People who had probably been told many things about what was coming for them, and very few of those things would have included a bard who wanted to tell them about the wonders of Lathander.

"I'm going to use Universal Speech."

I chose my targets with care. "Targeting the leader and the three nearest him."

I thought about what I actually wanted to say to them. Not platitudes — Lathander doesn't traffic in platitudes, or at least Lathander as I understand the concept. Something real. Something about light as a principle rather than a weapon. The wonders. The genuine, actual wonders.

I spoke.

The cleric did not receive this well. I was told — as a courtesy, and I appreciated the honesty — that it was not going to work on him. That it enraged him, actually, a little bit more.

"Okay," I said. "Well. Maybe the other three."

And the other three: something shifted. Not conversion, not surrender, but the particular slowing that happens when an idea enters a mind that wasn't expecting one. They would still come forward. Of course they would — they had momentum, they had companions, they had a screaming cleric behind them — but they would not rush. They would move at the pace of people who had heard something unexpected.

I considered this a successful opening verse.

"And once I've cast a spell, I can use that again. Which I will plan on doing for the others as we go."

There was a gentle caution from somewhere to my left: proselytizing about Lathander would be less effective once the combat fully began, if I understood the warning correctly.

"I mean, if we attack," I said. I thought this was obvious, but I said it anyway. "I'm here to proselytize about Lathander. For now."

There is a position — I have held it for years, and I hold it still — that most conflicts are misunderstandings conducted at escalating volume. The solution is almost never to match the volume. The solution is to find the frequency underneath the noise that people actually respond to. Not everyone, all at once. Three orcs who might slow down. Then three more. Then the situation has changed.

Valphine, moving next, was considering her options. The offer of lightning resistance came up briefly and passed. Then she said, with the particular tone she uses when an idea has occurred to her that she finds both practical and doctrinally significant:

"Well, I can cast D-Lite."

I squinted. The sun was already out. It was, as I pointed out, quite sunny.

"It's sunny out."

Brewbarry, to his credit, saw something in it. "Yeah, man, they're not wearing sunglasses. Maybe they could be blinded by the daylight."

I considered this. There is something theologically elegant about the idea of Lathander's light being so intense it overwhelms the uninitiated. Something that works as metaphor and as tactics simultaneously.

"Blinded by Lathander's glory," I said.

Valphine settled on her holy symbol as the vessel, and then the light came.

I was standing behind her when it happened, which I suspect is where one wants to be. Sixty feet in every direction, blazing and absolute, the kind of brightness that doesn't ask permission. The opposite ridge was flooded with it. The orcs were flooded with it. And the Talosian cleric, caught in the full wash of a light his entire theological framework identifies as an affront —

He screamed.

"Kill the false cleric of Lathander! Kill the drow cleric of Lathander, the abomination! Destroy! Destroy the false god!"

Soma, who has more patience for this sort of categorical error than I do, objected on Valphine's behalf: "She's not false, she's the real deal."

This was, I thought, exactly correct, though I doubted the cleric was in a position to receive the clarification.

I looked across the gully. The light. The orcs, some of them slowing, some of them still screaming, the cleric in the center of it all committing entirely to the wrong interpretation of what was happening. He thought we were here to fight him. He thought the light was a weapon. He thought Valphine was an abomination, which — and I say this with genuine respect for Valphine — he had badly miscalculated in the direction of his own destruction.

We were not here to fight. We were here to resolve a misunderstanding.

The cleric, however, was not a misunderstanding. He was the reason the misunderstanding had been allowed to grow into an army.

"We'll have to focus all of our attacks only on the leader," I said, and I meant it, "and try to convert the rest."

A pause from someone nearby, some flicker of disbelief at how this situation was apparently going to be conducted.

"Alright," I said. "Sounds good."
