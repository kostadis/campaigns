The Enclave meeting had the shape of a trial and the texture of a grudge settled in public. I knew Adabra would be hostile — she telegraphed that weeks ago, in every clipped sentence and conspicuous absence of eye contact with me, and in the particular way she looked at Vukradin: the look you give something you once found useful and have since reconsidered. I'd catalogued it. What I hadn't calculated was how far she would reach back.

She mentioned the five gold pieces. In front of everyone. Made them load-bearing.

I watched Vukradin's face when she said it, and I'll admit — even I found that instructive. He'd expected argument, evidence, the procedural weight of an adversarial hearing. He had not expected her to drag out five gold pieces like they were a wound she'd been nursing. The personal nature of it landed on him visibly, and he is usually much harder to read than that. Adabra had decided that this wasn't just about philosophy. It was about him, specifically, and what he owed her, specifically, and she intended everyone present to understand that she had been wronged in a way that went beyond doctrine.

She fumed when she left. Soma noted — quite accurately — that Vukradin had also fumed. Whether he was retreating strategically or had simply reached the end of his considerable patience, I wasn't sure at the time. Knowing him, it was probably both at once, and he would have difficulty explaining which.

What came next, though — that I did not predict.

He sat down somewhere quiet, and he counted out six gold pieces. Five plus one, for interest. He found an envelope. He addressed it. And then he walked to the postal system — the same one he'd used before, the civic infrastructure of a town that still half-believed him a hero — and he dropped it in.

From the organic fair trade gold, naturally.

Brewbarry, to his credit, asked immediately why six. The question had the directness of someone who genuinely needed the arithmetic explained, and I found I was almost sorry when it was.

Vukradin's reasoning was simple, once stated aloud: *Now that she's lost, she can have her money back.*

I turned this over for a moment, the way you turn over a stone to examine what lives beneath it. It was petty, yes — obviously, deliberately, precisely petty. But it was also something else. It was a closing of accounts. Adabra had tried to use an outstanding debt as evidence of character, and Vukradin was sealing that argument in an envelope and sending it back to her through official channels, with interest, so that she could never again say the words *he stiffed me* without being demonstrably, documentably wrong.

Surface dwellers, I have found, generally reach for either forgiveness or resentment when someone wrongs them in public. They rarely reach for the ledger.

Vukradin reached for the ledger.

I don't think he would recognize it as a drow thing to do. I'm not sure I'll tell him.
