## Valphine — A Petty Repayment

The Enclave meeting had the shape of a trial and the texture of a grudge settled in public. I knew Adabra would be hostile — she telegraphed that weeks ago, in every clipped sentence and conspicuous absence of eye contact with me, and in the particular way she looked at Vukradin: the look you give something you once found useful and have since reconsidered. I'd catalogued it. What I hadn't calculated was how far she would reach back.

She mentioned the five gold pieces. In front of everyone. Made them load-bearing.

I watched Vukradin's face when she said it, and I'll admit — even I found that instructive. He'd expected argument, evidence, the procedural weight of an adversarial hearing. He had not expected her to drag out five gold pieces like they were a wound she'd been nursing. The personal nature of it landed on him visibly, and he is usually much harder to read than that. Adabra had decided that this wasn't just about philosophy. It was about him, specifically, and what he owed her, specifically, and she intended everyone present to understand that she had been wronged in a way that went beyond doctrine.

She fumed when she left. Soma noted — quite accurately — that Vukradin had also fumed. Whether he was retreating strategically or had simply reached the end of his considerable patience, I wasn't sure at the time. Knowing him, it was probably both at once, and he would have difficulty explaining which.

What came next, though — that I did not predict.

He sat down somewhere quiet, and he counted out six gold pieces. Five plus one, for interest. He found an envelope. He addressed it. And then he walked to the postal system — the same one he'd used before, the civic infrastructure of a town that still half-believed him a hero — and he dropped it in.

From the organic fair trade gold, naturally.

Brewbarry, to his credit, asked immediately why six. The question had the directness of someone who genuinely needed the arithmetic explained, and I found I was almost sorry when it was.

Vukradin's reasoning was simple, once stated aloud: *Now that she's lost, she can have her money back.*

I turned this over for a moment, the way you turn over a stone to examine what lives beneath it. It was petty, yes — obviously, deliberately, precisely petty. But it was also something else. It was a closing of accounts. Adabra had tried to use an outstanding debt as evidence of character, and Vukradin was sealing that argument in an envelope and sending it back to her through official channels, with interest, so that she could never again say the words *he stiffed me* without being demonstrably, documentably wrong.

Surface dwellers, I have found, generally reach for either forgiveness or resentment when someone wrongs them in public. They rarely reach for the ledger.

Vukradin reached for the ledger.

I don't think he would recognize it as a drow thing to do. I'm not sure I'll tell him.

## Brewbarry — Return to Phandalin

We came back into Phandalin with mud on our boots and a dead man's name on everyone's tongue. Corbin. Nobody said it out loud on the road, but it was there, hanging between us like smoke that won't clear.

The Stonehill was warm and loud and smelled like bread and beer and I will not pretend that wasn't good. Some things are just good.

I noticed Jenna Roscoe at a corner table, nursing something dark and watching the door. She looked like a woman waiting to be noticed back. I did not oblige her yet.

Ser Kaelen found us almost immediately. He has that quality — the kind of man who is already in the room before you notice him. His eyes went straight to Soma's staff, and something in his expression shifted. Careful. Calculating.

"I see you were able to keep your staff," he said.

Soma said nothing that I caught. She just held it, the way she holds everything — like it belongs to her in some deeper sense than ownership.

Then Lyra came through the door behind us, and whatever weight the room had carried for a moment dissolved entirely. She was incandescent. She looked the way I feel after a very good fight — glowing with it, like the energy has nowhere to go.

Ser Kaelen saw her face and said, slow and a little surprised: "Wow, you are in a good mood."

And then Lyra opened her mouth and the whole thing poured out.

"Thanks to the heroism and the nobility, and the dignity of none other than Vukradin."

I watched Vukradin receive this the way he always receives praise — sideways, deflecting, already backing away from it.

"Oh, someone did all the hard work," he said. "I just walked out at the right time."

"It was brilliant!" Lyra had no interest in his modesty. She was going to give him the full account whether he wanted it or not. "You turned everybody — you focused all of Adabra's rage on yourself, and then walked out, rendered her arguments pointless and meaningless. She had prepared this whole vicious attack on you, and then, by leaving the field, you diffused it, enabling Soma to step in and to take over. It was brilliant. I haven't seen such brilliant oratory in my entire life. And then to do it on a matter of —"

"Principle!" someone supplied.

She turned to Spider-Man — that is truly his name, I have asked twice — and said: "Get this man at least two beers."

Vukradin, because he is Vukradin, immediately said: "Okay, I'll take two beers, that sounds good. How many for Brewbarry?"

I felt something land in my chest. Small and warm. He didn't have to do that. He never has to do anything for me. He just does.

Lyra waved her hands. "No, no, no — Brewbarry, two for you."

"Two for you," she said again, to make sure it landed.

It landed.

My tribe never bought me drinks. My tribe said I was negligent. My tribe sent me away with nothing but the shame they'd assigned me, and told the story wrong every single time they told it. And here was this druid I had met perhaps twice, ordering two beers for me by name, in a warm room full of people who had not decided yet what I was worth. Who were still deciding, and seemed, so far, to be deciding something good.

I drank both.

Soma declined a beer in two syllables — "T." Just tea. Vukradin squinted at her like she'd said something philosophically challenging. "Only tea?" And Soma said: "Don't drink!" And Vukradin, with the certainty of a man who has thought very carefully about this comparison, said: "Gandalf. Just tea."

I did not know who Gandalf was. I added it to the list of things to ask Vukradin later. The list is long. I am working through it.

Valphine, who had been standing at the edge of everything with her usual expression of mild detached observation, noted that the druids had been disappointed not to hear great music. This was her way of asking for a performance without asking for anything, because Valphine does not ask.

Vukradin shrugged. "Yeah, we can put on a show. Let's see how it goes."

It went fine. Decent. The kind of performance that makes people happy without making them forget anything they were thinking about. Valphine sang with him, in that hybrid Lathanderian/Lolthian register she has been refining; the room found it more interesting than they had words for. Lyra was pleased. The room was pleased. Vukradin told us afterward that he had not spent his best on it — "I'm not gonna waste my Bardic inspiration on that, sorry" — with the tone of a man who is very clear about where his resources belong and a tavern on a Tuesday night is not it. I respect this. I would not waste my good axe throws on a fruit.

Then it was time to talk about what came next.

Orcs. Quests. The options arranged on the table like a hand of cards nobody was excited to play. The Woodland Manse, burned and occupied. Falcon's Lodge, overrun. The lair of the half-orc Talosian spellcasters, with Xanth offering to guide us. The Shrine of Savras, its dragon now dead, but the shrine itself never cleared.

Soma said, with a weight that wasn't quite grief but was shaped like it: "Revenge Corbin!"

Vukradin said, gently: "Rename it."

Soma said: "I feel more like Avenging Corbin after what we learned at the Druid Enclave."

Nobody argued with that.

Then I looked at the list and there was a name on it that made no sense to me.

"And why we have a Bob Saget the Musical Quest?"

Vukradin said, patient as always: "It's a reference to the previous campaign."

What followed was an education. There had been, apparently, a traveling theatrical troupe. A demon of dad jokes. A zombie apocalypse narrowly averted in Silverymoon. The astral plane involved somehow, and two bags of holding, and children who were seven and eight years old when they invented the whole thing and wanted the demon to pursue a career in stand-up comedy in hell.

"There's no such thing as a bad dad joke," I said, because this seemed to me to be obviously true. Also, I had said a dad joke once and Vukradin had laughed, so I knew they worked.

Apparently Bob Saget the Demon agreed with me. He was not respected for this view.

"The Rodney Dangerfield of Bob Saget," Vukradin said, and whatever that meant, the others found it exactly right.

Then Vukradin did the thing with the money. He had been carrying five gold coins that Adabra had paid him at some point before everything went wrong between them. He put six in an envelope — five plus one for interest, he said, fair-trade, obviously — and addressed it to Adabra, and dropped it with the mail.

"Why 6? Sorry," I asked, because I genuinely did not follow.

He explained. She had made a speech before the Druid Enclave about how she had given him five gold and he had, in her view, done her wrong by it. So now she was getting it back. Plus interest. Now that she'd lost, she could have her money back.

I thought about this for a moment.

I thought about what it means to be the kind of person who doesn't forget what he owes, even to someone who just tried to ruin him. Who returns the money anyway. With interest. After winning.

My tribe would not have done that. My tribe would have called it weakness. My tribe was wrong about most things.

Vukradin went to find Ser Kaelen and tell him about Sister Kaella. About Corbin. That conversation I watched from across the room, and I will leave the weight of it where it fell.

Then Jenna Roscoe appeared at Vukradin's elbow and began her pitch. Resources. Prestige. The Lord's Alliance could find Sister Kaella. Could support a musical studio. Could do so many wonderful things for a person of Vukradin's obvious talents and noble character.

Vukradin listened with the expression of a man who has already decided, and is simply waiting politely for the speaker to arrive at the same conclusion. Then he redirected her, smoothly, toward Valphine's church. "Did you know my friend Valphine has a church, and they're looking for donations?"

Jenna faltered. Just slightly. Just for a moment. The idea of donating money to Valphine's operation had reached her face before she could stop it.

"Torture cult," she said, or close to it.

"Torture cult!" Vukradin objected.

Then: "It is kind of a torture cult."

I said: "It kind of is."

Vukradin corrected this with care. "It's more like a masochism cult."

I considered it. Freedom of worship is a thing Vukradin has explained to me. Valphine has never tried to hurt me without my consent. She has, in fact, kept me standing on several occasions when I should not have been. "Whatever. Freedom of worship, and —"

"Yeah," said Vukradin. "Etc."

Jenna recovered. She turned to me.

She started talking about how an Uthgardian barbarian who joined the Lord's Alliance and accepted the civilizing —

I let a very loud burp out.

It echoed. It was sincere. It said everything I could have said in ten minutes of explanation, and it said it better.

She got the point.

She walked away.

I picked up my second beer. It was still good. Everything, right now, was still good.

## Vukradin — Planning the Next Quest

The morning we chose the Circle of Thunder, I was sitting with our quest list spread across my mind like an unfinished score — three different orc problems, each one demanding resolution, and not enough bars to fit them all in.

"So I want to tell you what… you want to know what Vukradin is doing in Phandalin?"

They looked at me. I spread my hands.

"So I'd like to ask, we've got three different orc… quests here."

I ticked through them aloud, partly for the party and partly because I find that speaking a thing clearly helps it make sense. "You've got the Woodland Manse." The one the orcs had burned — the GM confirmed as much, filling in what we already knew: attacked, occupied, destroyed. "Falcon's Hunting Lodge got overrun." Soma started to suggest we scratch it from the list, that we'd already fumbled that one, but I shook my head. It was still there. The problem was still there. And then: "Xanth offered to take us to the lair of the evil half-orc spellcasters." Three quests, three locations. Not interchangeable. Each one its own piece of the problem.

Soma asked whether the dragon quest was still on the board. It wasn't. He's dead — we killed Cryovain, which I will never entirely stop feeling complicated about. I noted that we could still explore the Shrine of Savras, since we'd never actually cleared the shrine itself, even if the orcs who'd previously occupied it had already come to find us and lost that argument badly at Mountain's Toe Mine. This is the sort of thing that bothers me. A loose thread is a loose thread.

So: three orc problems. Which one first?

The lodge had a sentimental claim on me — Falcon's special wood for the studio, the venue partnership, the promise of building something real. But Falcon has not yet personally apologized to me. He attacked me at council. He stood in front of a room of people and — I understand he was manipulated, or frightened, or both, and I do not blame him for the feeling, but he chose the action, and an apology is not a gift, it is a debt, and I have not received it yet.

"I think the Falcon has not yet personally apologized to me, although he intends to, but he hasn't yet, so I'm currently inclined to go with Xanth."

Valphine was doing something with her hands. Soma nodded in the slow, patient way she has, the way that always makes me feel like I'm being assessed by someone who's seen several of me already.

That settled it. We would go to the Circle of Thunder first.

Which brought us to the question of surviving the trip.

Soma's memory of our last encounter with Talosian spellcasters is more charitable than mine in the sense that she was willing to name it plainly: "We last got our asses whooped by the Talosian spellcasting orcs. It was not pleasant."

"Yeah, this might not be pleasant either, but hey."

This is the honest answer. I will not pretend otherwise. These are half-orc spellcasters who have had their way with us before, and I have no reason to think they've become less dangerous in the intervening time. The difference is that we are also, I think, somewhat less bad than we were. Somewhat. "Bring potions of ground," Soma said, which I took to mean potions of some sort of earthly anchoring, though she may have meant something more literal.

The lightning problem is real. I raised it because someone needed to.

"Do you need, like, lightning resistance or something like that? Because we've been lighteninged many times."

There was a brief technical interlude about whether it was lightning or thunder — two different things, apparently, and worth distinguishing. The confirmation came: lightning. I pressed the point. "Do you want to get, like, some sort of lightning resistance? Like, we could inquire about that."

Brewbarry thought it was a good idea. I noted, without rancor, that we don't actually have money for it at the moment, but the idea was sound. Then Valphine began leafing through the architecture of what she knows and what she can carry into a day's work — she found something, *Protection from Energy*, concentration, up to an hour. One target.

"Nice."

Soma immediately identified the cost: that spell would compete with the Spiritual Guardians, the whirling radiant presence she summons around herself in serious fights. It was the right question. You do not trade one protection for another without understanding what you're giving up.

"The other concentration spells," I echoed, mostly to confirm I was following the logic.

Valphine was still weighing it. I left her to that. It is her area, not mine.

My area, as it happened, was communication.

"We need to make contact with Xanth the Centaur to find out where the lair of the evil half-orc spellcasters is."

Lyra, who was nearby and overheard this, immediately offered to help. I cut her off before she could finish the thought.

"I got it."

The look on her face when she understood what I meant — mild horror is not too strong a phrase.

"Are you just going to be using… she suddenly has this horrified look. Are you just going to be sending messages to everybody all day?"

Soma leapt to my defense. "I mean, that's what the spell is for!"

She's right. That is exactly what it is for. I told Lyra — and I will admit I was a little delighted — "Man, I got a new cell phone, and I really like this texting thing." Brewbarry laughed his big canyon laugh and contributed something about unlimited calls. I made the necessary correction: "Limited range! Any plane of existence!"

The Sending found Xanth, as I knew it would. His reply came in compressed, efficient words — the way a centaur speaks when reduced to a certain number of syllables, I imagine. He suggested the corner of the Triboar Trail where it bends toward Phandalin. We would meet him there.

"Excellent."

Before we left, there was the question of whether we needed to resupply in Phandalin. Money is tight — I said as much, without embarrassment: "No money, but you guys feel free." Soma pointed out we couldn't craft anything useful from the dragon materials here anyway. Someone suggested healing potions. Brewbarry, in the specific way that is entirely Brewbarry, announced that a couple of beers had provided him all the healing he required. I love this man, I genuinely do.

I ran the actual inventory. "Are we out of potions of healing? Consumables… we are out of potions. We have one potion of greater healing, and one potion of superior healing, and one scented potion of healing."

"Scented," Brewbarry confirmed, as if the word were its own complete thought.

Valphine had a question about the superior potion — something about its contents, whether it was what it claimed to be. I caught myself mid-answer and reversed course immediately.

"Oh, oh, sorry, don't use the superior in front of me."

She looked at me. "Oh, I see." A pause. "Okay. We'll use it on you when you are unconscious."

There are things I do not want to know. Soma asked if she had just drunk a tainted potion. I held up a hand. "Don't tell me what happened." I meant it. "Keep me in the dark, man."

Not everything has to be known. Some questions are better left as questions.

We headed for the forest.

## Soma — Journey into the Woods

We found Xanthopoulos where Vukradin's Sending had promised — at the corner where the Triboar Trail bends toward Phandalin, hooves planted in the road like he'd been there for hours and had enjoyed every minute of waiting. That is a centaur's patience for you. They do not fidget.

He was pleased with us. Conspicuously, thoroughly pleased, in the way that Xanth expresses everything — with his whole chest and a good portion of his mane.

"My good friends, you have done such an amazing job. It was absolutely fantastic. Soma, you were superb."

I accepted this the way I accept most good news: quietly, with internal suspicion that more work is coming. Meril's name followed, as it tends to when Xanth and I speak. *Meril was very interested.* In what, exactly, I did not ask. Meril left me a staff and a year's worth of half-finished lessons and whatever was left of his faith in something he hadn't quite named. Xanth has always known more about what that means than he's said. I've learned not to push. The staff knows things before I do, and it hasn't led me wrong yet. One leaf left on it now. I try not to think about that too directly.

Boney clopped forward as Xanth greeted us, all bones and dignity, and I remembered I was supposed to be managing his decisions in a fight. The arrangement had been sorted earlier with less ceremony than it deserved.

"Yeah, I mean, I'm just gonna pretend he's, you know, stomping and riding and charging and shit."

That is my strategy. That has always been my strategy. Boney is a sensible horse in the way that only skeletal horses seem to manage — he does not startle, he does not complain about the weather, and he has never once tried to eat my pack. Lady Alagondar made good choices, whoever she was. I respect her taste in horses.

Thirty miles. That is what a full day in the woods looks like on a map: a line the length of your ambition. On your legs it is something else. The forest swallowed us gradually, the road becoming a track becoming a suggestion, and all the while I was reading it the way you learn to read anything if you've been looking long enough — the angle of lichen on the north faces, the places where animals had stopped walking, the shapes that told you the canopy hadn't been disturbed in years and the shapes that told you it had, recently, and not by weather.

Xanth moved through the trees with the ease of a creature who belongs to them. I moved behind him and tried to do the same, and almost managed it.

When the light began to go, someone raised the question of pressing on.

"We're not in a rush, are we?"

Nobody argued. Valphine had already solved the camp problem the way she solves most problems — by asking the person who already knows the answer. Xanth had scouted a good site. Of course he had.

The fire was small and sensible. Xanth settled beside it like a landmark settling into its proper place, and Vukradin — because Vukradin cannot help himself, and I mean this with genuine fondness — asked him for stories.

He had stories. Stars above, he had stories. Birds and flowers and ancient heroes and the complicated grievances of things older than Phandalin, older than the road we'd walked, older than most of the trouble we'd spent the last several months wading through. He spoke about the depredations of Neverwinter in the tone of a creature who has watched the same mistakes accumulate over centuries and has developed a very refined kind of sorrow about it.

The others went quiet in the way that is not listening. I have watched shell-sprouts do this my entire life — the eyes settle somewhere past the speaker, the face goes politely neutral, and the mind is somewhere warmer and more comfortable. I did not blame them. I was listening, but I am old and I have developed a fondness for sitting still while someone who knows more than me talks. You pick up things that way. You pick up things you didn't know you needed until you need them.

What I heard underneath the birds and flowers was something colder.

He talked about the forest. The trees, the creatures, the way things had moved before the white dragon's winter came and broke the quiet. That, I already knew. Cryovain's disruption had scattered everything with sense enough to scatter. But then Xanth said something that sat differently.

The new orcs. The Talosian ones. They were not, he said, normal orcs. Something fundamentally more evil than the typical kind. Something fundamentally more dangerous.

I turned that over while the fire burned down. The forest around us felt inhabited in a way I couldn't fully account for — not wrong, not yet, but watching. The way any old living thing watches when something has been done to it that hasn't finished hurting.

The others were mostly asleep or nearly so. Xanth was looking at the canopy with the patience of a creature made to outlast things.

I listened to the trees for a while longer before I let myself rest.

## Valphine — Lore of the Storm God

The firelight was modest — just enough to push the dark back a few feet — and Xanth took up a considerable portion of that lit space, the way centaurs do, as though they were built for amphitheatres rather than campsites. I settled into the shadow at the edge of the circle, which is where I prefer to think.

Vukradin opened it, the way he always opens things: with a question that sounds casual and isn't.

"Xanth, how much further to the lair of the half-orc spellcasters?"

"Another day's hike."

A beat. Then Vukradin, with that particular quality of his — the one I've catalogued as *idealism in the guise of curiosity* — leaned toward the fire and said, "Got any good stories to tell us tonight?"

The centaur's expression shifted into something that might have been fondness, or might have been the particular satisfaction of someone who has been waiting to be asked. "Stories, my friend, I have stories to tell you."

He spoke of the forest first. How it had been — not peaceful in the soft pastoral sense, but balanced, which is a different thing entirely. The white dragon had broken that balance. The animals had grown afraid. The orcs had hoarded, crowded, radicalized in the way that frightened creatures do. And these new orcs, Xanth said, were not merely dangerous in the ordinary sense. There was something *fundamentally more evil* about them, something *fundamentally more dangerous than the typical orcs*.

I filed that phrase carefully. Fundamentally more evil. That's theological language, whether Xanth intended it or not. It means something has touched their nature, not just their circumstances.

Vukradin caught the thread. "How could they become more dangerous? What happened?"

Xanth paused — a good pause, weighted — and said: "Why, they have become members of the cult of Talos."

The name landed the way these names always land around surface dwellers: with a kind of theatrical gravity, as though saying it might summon something. I watched the campfire rather than my companions. I already knew what Talos was. I knew it the way I know most things about theological extremism: from close proximity, from having spent decades in a society that ran on divine terror, from recognizing the grammar of fanaticism in every language it's ever spoken.

Vukradin asked where the cult originates from, and Xanth drew himself up to explain.

"The story of Talos. Talos is the deity of storms and destruction. Wherever there are those who pursue destruction — wherever, and who wish to spread destruction, Talos is there. It is not… he is not… he is not in some specific place. He is in the breast of all those who seek the power to destroy, and hear his message of destruction, and revel in that destruction."

There it was. The first doctrine I ever learned in Menzoberranzan, rephrased: *the god is not a place, the god is a condition.* Lolth was the same. You couldn't point to where she lived and be done with her. She lived in the moments when you watched your sister's back and imagined a knife in it. She lived in ambition, in hunger, in every calculation about who could be useful and who could be discarded. The Underdark didn't contain her. She contained the Underdark.

Talos was a cruder instrument, but the mechanics were identical: find people who already want to destroy, give them a name for the wanting, give them a doctrine that makes the wanting holy. I recognized it the way you recognize a knife you've seen in a different hand.

Vukradin pressed forward, which is his nature. "What's changed recently? I mean, I'm sure Talos has been around for a long time, he's been corrupting people for centuries. Like, has something happened that he's caused him to gain lots of power recently?"

Xanth paused again. Then: "That is a very good question. Demonstrating an awareness and wisdom far beyond your age."

I will say this for Vukradin: he asks the right questions, even when he doesn't know why they're the right questions. He has the instincts without the framework. It's like watching someone navigate a political court by feel alone — unsettling and impressive in roughly equal measure.

Xanth explained that the cult was historically small, unstable, structurally incoherent. Outlawed by every civil society worth naming. "The cult of Talos is like a disease. It explodes, it destroys everything, and then it destroys upon themselves. They are not a very — how should I say — stable god force."

He paused, and something shifted in his expression — a sidelong acknowledgment of an argument he had already rehearsed. "Adabra would say that they represent the necessary element of distraction in the cosmic balance."

I looked up at that. Interesting framing. *Distraction.* Not destruction, not evil — distraction, as though the cosmos required chaos as a corrective, a self-regulating fever.

Xanth's voice flattened into something drier. "I would say nonsense. He's a lunatic of a god who wants to destroy things left, right, and center, and finds other stupid people who want to destroy things. And he empowers them, and they have these fantasies of destruction, and he gives them power to implement these fantasies of destruction."

*Fantasies of destruction.* There was the phrase I'd been waiting for. That was the diagnostic. That was the thing that distinguished genuine theological violence from political violence dressed in religious costume — the fantasists, the ones who needed the doctrine to be true not because it gave them power, but because it gave their hunger a shape it couldn't otherwise have. In Menzoberranzan, they were the ones who lasted longest. Not the cynical operators — those burned out or were eliminated. The true believers. The ones whose violence was an act of faith.

These orcs believed.

"So he has a new Chosen," Vukradin said.

"Yes, most likely. There is always a chosen. There's always some cleric who somehow became convinced by Talos that he has some divine message and inspiration."

"Find out," Vukradin said.

I almost smiled at that. *Find out.* As though Xanth had merely forgotten to look. But Xanth took it in stride.

"He's not in this forest, that I do know. If he was in this forest, I would know who he was, and I would be taking you there. You'd probably die, but at least we'd learn something about him." A beat. "That was a joke. I hope you appreciate the talk."

"I appreciate it," Vukradin said.

"Excellent."

The fire settled. I stayed where I was in the shadow, thinking about chosen clerics and the particular shape of conviction that turns a fanatic into a weapon. I had known priests like that. I had worked alongside them, assessed them, occasionally outmaneuvered them. They were not cautious. They were not subtle. They did not negotiate.

That, as a tactical matter, was both their greatest weakness and what made them so exceptionally dangerous.

## Brewbarry — The Wildcat Loggers

We were moving through the forest toward the Circle of Thunder, Xanth keeping pace alongside us, when we heard the shouting.

Not angry shouting. More like the kind of arguing that happens when people who are deeply right about something have no idea how to explain why. High-pitched, urgent, overlapping. I knew the sound before I saw who was making it.

Young druids. First season. Maybe second. The ones who still think the problem is other people not understanding what they understand.

They saw Soma's staff and it was like watching water find a crack. Suddenly all of that frantic energy had somewhere to go. They surged toward her, all of them at once, words tumbling over each other, pointing back toward the treeline where a crew of men with axes had gone very still and were watching us.

I'd seen that stillness before too. Men who work with their hands and don't trust that the people yelling at them have ever worked with theirs.

The druids' complaint came out in pieces, each one interrupting the last. Loggers. No permits. Tibor Wester — and at that name Vukradin made a sound that was less a word than a soft warning — Harbin's brother, running a wildcat operation, hiring new crews who didn't know or didn't care about the agreements that were supposed to govern this forest.

"Permits?" Soma said, and there was genuine puzzlement in her voice, the kind that makes young druids recalibrate.

The permits, it turned out, were part of an arrangement between the city of Neverwinter and the forest's caretakers. Sanctioned cutting. Agreed limits. The young druids weren't opposed to logging, they were quick to clarify — they understood the balance, they understood that dead growth had to go, that there were competing interests at stake.

"I mean, you have to," Soma said, "to protect the forest. Otherwise you get dead growth and other issues. It's just good practice."

Right. Just not this. Not men hired by a man cutting wherever the trees were thickest, with no thought for what they were taking or what would grow back.

Vukradin was trying to find the center of it. "So what's the conflict here?"

"Well, I think they want to log, and the druids don't want them to log, right?"

"Why are we against logging?"

"I mean, I think we're more against Tibor Wester than anything, aren't we?"

I was watching the loggers while this went on. Not to threaten them. Just watching. They had the look of men who'd taken work because work was available, not because they'd thought hard about who was offering it. That was a look I recognized. My people had that look, sometimes, in the years after a bad winter. You don't ask about the permits when you need to eat.

That was a thought I kept to myself.

Then Soma said something that surprised everyone, including, I think, Soma.

"We all start somewhere. You guys look like pretty naive druids as well." She was talking to the young ones, and her voice was gentle without being soft. "Why don't you show them the ropes? Show them how to properly log."

The druids blinked. The loggers blinked. There was a pause that had a different quality than the arguing.

"Can I get Vukradin's help here?"

Vukradin looked at her. "What am I saying?"

"I'm just trying to persuade them that the druids shouldn't fight them, they should use this as a teaching moment — explain to these loggers how it's correct to."

And then Vukradin opened his mouth.

I will not pretend I understood everything he said. I am a man of the stone peaks, not of the lowland forests and their civic arrangements. But I understood the shape of it. The way he talked about the forest wasn't like someone reciting rules — it had movement in it, the way good music has movement, phrases that built on each other and landed somewhere unexpected. He talked about the sanctity of what grew here, about balance, about what sustainable meant not as a regulation but as a kind of honesty between people and the land they needed. He talked about fair trade in a way that connected it to labor, to what honest work meant, to the difference between taking and receiving.

He had quips. He had something in his voice that made the whole speech feel like it was happening specifically to each person who heard it.

The axes came down.

Not dropped. Set down carefully. Which was different. Which meant something.

One of the loggers — a broad-shouldered man with the kind of face that has been weathered into something simpler than it started as — pressed the heel of his hand against his eye. He wasn't the only one. The young druids had gone quiet. The forest had gone quiet. Vukradin finished, and the loggers said they were sorry, and they said they would find honest work, and they said they would report back to the city about what Tibor Wester had been running out here.

"Wow," said Soma.

"Wow," said Vukradin. Then: "Okay, our work here is done, gentlemen."

My chest was doing something I didn't fully expect.

I've pulled trees out of the ground with my bare hands. Not many, but some. When you're large enough to do a thing and the thing needs doing, you do it. I never thought about the tree. I thought about the obstacle. I thought about what needed to move.

Standing in that forest with the loggers weeping and the druids standing quietly with their hands at their sides, I thought about the tree. I thought about all the trees.

It came out of me before I could arrange it better.

"Man, I had the tears."

Vukradin looked at me. "It's not all underhanded intimidation tactics."

"Yeah," I said. "Thinking about all the trees I've pulled out of the ground, you know."

Hands. Guilty now.

I was still thumping my chest a little, the way my body does when it doesn't know what else to do with a feeling. I'm told that contributed to the atmosphere. I believe it. The loggers were probably looking at me and thinking about a great many things they didn't want to do anymore.

But I wasn't thumping my chest to frighten them.

I was just — feeling something. The way a man does when someone puts into words the thing he didn't know he'd been carrying.

## Soma — The Cursed Clearing

The clearing was wrong before I even stepped into it.

The staff told me first — a faint tremor up through the wood, the kind of feeling you get when you touch a beam in an old house and realize the rot has gone all the way through. Then the smell hit: char and something older underneath it, something that had nothing to do with a natural fire. The pine was forty feet of naked black needle, all its limbs burned away long ago, and whatever had lived in that tree was not living there anymore. Nothing had nested in it. Nothing was sheltering under it. Even the insects were giving it a wide berth, which, if you know anything about insects, should terrify you.

My one word for all of that was "Wet."

I meant it as a complete sentence.

Brewbarry wanted to know if his memory helped, and it turned out it did. He came back with something from his Uthgardt days — an old story about what those dolls meant, ten ghastly little things of twigs and black hair bundled at the base of the dead tree. He said it flat and clear: "Black magic, voodoo, no touch. That's what Brewbarry remembers."

That was good enough for me. I have learned to listen when a man that large sounds that certain about something being dangerous.

Vukradin asked Xanth if this was typical of the Cult of Talos. Xanth, to his mild credit, set the record straight: "Mr. Vukradin. I am not a cultist of Talos. I don't know what is popular and what is not popular." He added, after a pause: "Good point, good point, but I know more, but not that much."

I appreciated that. A creature who knows the edges of his own knowledge is someone I can work with.

Then the argument started, which I also could have predicted before we entered the clearing.

Brewbarry said: "Guys, I don't know much, but I do know that ghastly dolls hanging from burnt trees is probably not a good sign."

Vukradin suggested collecting them. Then destroying them. Then burning them. Brewbarry said he wouldn't touch them. Vukradin said we'd come to kill the evil half-orc spellcasters anyway, so what did it matter if we disturbed their dolls? Brewbarry, admirably, pointed out that we didn't actually know the spellcasters made the dolls — they could belong to enemies of the spellcasters.

Vukradin did not appear to process this information.

I was still reading the staff's distress, still feeling the way the wrongness in this place had saturated the soil. The roots of that tree would have had years to grow deep and strange. Whatever ritual logic was buried here, it had been patient. Patient things are always more dangerous than urgent ones.

I was standing right in the middle of the clearing when Vukradin destroyed the first doll.

The pig's heart hit the ground still beating.

He destroyed the heart too.

I will not pretend I had time to think anything particularly wise in the next moment. The roots erupted. I felt them before I saw them — the ground seizing under my feet, something coiling around my legs with the grip of old timber and old spite, and then I was stuck. Trapped solid, roots up to my shins, and the staff's tremor had gone from a murmur to a shout.

I said, very calmly, in my own head: I want to go home.

Boney went down beside me. Good old Boney. Faithful skeletal horse, completely immune to embarrassment, deeply vulnerable to root traps. I watched his bone legs disappear into the dirt and thought, well. At least I have company. Out loud, I said what was true: "Anyway, when he gets knocked down, he just, you know, gets back up again."

The roots weren't loosening. My old legs have seen better decades, and the grip of the earth on them was not something I was going to muscle through without help. I had a choice to make, and I made it without ceremony: burn a wild shape, or stay stuck in the dirt while my shell-sprouts fended for themselves.

"Alright, we're gonna burn a wild shape into an elk."

The giant elk. They have the strength for it. I pulled that form up from whatever place it lives in — the deep well of the world's shapes, the one Meril spent a year teaching me to reach into — and felt the clearing reorganize around my new size. The roots thought they had me. The roots were wrong.

Valphine's blessing was already running through me, a warm current that steadied the final heave, and I was free. The bark came loose. I shook it off. I moved to Boney next, because of course I did, because you don't leave your people in the dirt.

Vukradin, to his credit, kept working. He was burning through those dolls with the focused determination of a man who has decided that a thing must be done and that he personally must do it, and nobody should interrupt him. Brewbarry was beside himself. "Horrible plan. Horrible. We do no touch. That's what Brewbarry said, you're not listening." Valphine agreed with some economy: "No touching. Bad touching."

Vukradin said he was busy and had roughly twenty more spells to go and it would only take a couple of minutes.

I stood in the clearing in elk form, roots crumbling behind me, and watched this unfold with the profound patience of a very large ruminant.

One by one, the hearts fell. One by one, Vukradin destroyed them. The tree stopped being what it had been — I felt it through my hooves, through the new size of me, through whatever it is the staff communicates that I still don't have good words for. The wrongness was leaving. Like pressure draining from a wound.

When the last one was gone, the clearing went quiet in a different way. Still. The tree was just a dead tree.

Vukradin declared that we had saved ten people who had been injured by the dolls. I had my doubts about the causal logic but chose not to raise them.

"Homer," he said — he always calls me Homer when he's accounting for damage — "did you lose a total of 10 hit points?"

"It would have been 10, yeah."

"A wild shape."

"A wild shape."

"Wow," he said, with genuine feeling. "What a great trap."

I agreed that it was a great trap. I also asked, just to be clear, who had sprung it.

"That was me," Vukradin said.

He seemed to feel it was worth it. He seemed to feel we had accomplished something meaningful. He mentioned the trap-maker deserved credit for the effort.

I thought about Meril, who had stood in clearings like this and made choices about when to intervene. I thought about what the staff had felt when I first stepped through the tree line — that immediate recognition, that this is not natural, this is not decay, this is intention. I had been right. The staff had been right. Knowing you're right about a bad thing doesn't make the bad thing not happen to your legs.

Vukradin said, in the voice of a man who has thought this through: "Must fight evil!"

I said, because it was also true about me, because it is the reason I am walking this road at forty-five years old with a borrowed staff and a skeletal horse and three companions I never would have predicted: "Must intervene."

## Vukradin — The Ridge Encounter

The ridge came up fast — one moment we were picking our way across uneven ground, the next we were standing on the crest of it, looking across sixty feet of open air at a gully ten feet deep, and then, cresting the opposite ridge like a wave breaking, the orcs.

Ten of them. I counted. And at their center, a humanoid figure with elongated claws and the particular quality of someone who has consecrated themselves to a very loud god.

The battle cries hit me before I fully processed the numbers. There is a sound that fighting people make — not the sound of people who are angry or scared or even violent in the ordinary way — but the sound of people who have decided, cosmologically, that noise itself is holy. The Talosian clergy understand this. They are not trying to intimidate you. They are performing liturgy.

I found this, in the moment, genuinely interesting.

Initiative. The call went up and I felt that particular clarity settle over me — the moment when a situation stops being a situation and becomes, briefly, something you can actually navigate. I had the first opening. I knew it before the confirmation came.

"Yes, you do."

Good. Sixty feet to the opposite ridge. An acolyte who definitely looked like an acolyte, as these things go — the claws, the posture, the absolute conviction radiating off him like heat. And around him, ten orcs who were, it occurred to me, simply orcs. People with a context I did not yet fully understand. People who had probably been told many things about what was coming for them, and very few of those things would have included a bard who wanted to tell them about the wonders of Lathander.

"I'm going to use Universal Speech."

I chose my targets with care. "Targeting the leader and the three nearest him."

I thought about what I actually wanted to say to them. Not platitudes — Lathander doesn't traffic in platitudes, or at least Lathander as I understand the concept. Something real. Something about light as a principle rather than a weapon. The wonders. The genuine, actual wonders.

I spoke.

The cleric did not receive this well. I was told — as a courtesy, and I appreciated the honesty — that it was not going to work on him. That it enraged him, actually, a little bit more.

"Okay," I said. "Well. Maybe the other three."

And the other three: something shifted. Not conversion, not surrender, but the particular slowing that happens when an idea enters a mind that wasn't expecting one. They would still come forward. Of course they would — they had momentum, they had companions, they had a screaming cleric behind them — but they would not rush. They would move at the pace of people who had heard something unexpected.

I considered this a successful opening verse.

"And once I've cast a spell, I can use that again. Which I will plan on doing for the others as we go."

There was a gentle caution from somewhere to my left: proselytizing about Lathander would be less effective once the combat fully began, if I understood the warning correctly.

"I mean, if we attack," I said. I thought this was obvious, but I said it anyway. "I'm here to proselytize about Lathander. For now."

There is a position — I have held it for years, and I hold it still — that most conflicts are misunderstandings conducted at escalating volume. The solution is almost never to match the volume. The solution is to find the frequency underneath the noise that people actually respond to. Not everyone, all at once. Three orcs who might slow down. Then three more. Then the situation has changed.

Valphine, moving next, was considering her options. The offer of lightning resistance came up briefly and passed. Then she said, with the particular tone she uses when an idea has occurred to her that she finds both practical and doctrinally significant:

"Well, I can cast D-Lite."

I squinted. The sun was already out. It was, as I pointed out, quite sunny.

"It's sunny out."

Brewbarry, to his credit, saw something in it. "Yeah, man, they're not wearing sunglasses. Maybe they could be blinded by the daylight."

I considered this. There is something theologically elegant about the idea of Lathander's light being so intense it overwhelms the uninitiated. Something that works as metaphor and as tactics simultaneously.

"Blinded by Lathander's glory," I said.

Valphine settled on her holy symbol as the vessel, and then the light came.

I was standing behind her when it happened, which I suspect is where one wants to be. Sixty feet in every direction, blazing and absolute, the kind of brightness that doesn't ask permission. The opposite ridge was flooded with it. The orcs were flooded with it. And the Talosian cleric, caught in the full wash of a light his entire theological framework identifies as an affront —

He screamed.

"Kill the false cleric of Lathander! Kill the drow cleric of Lathander, the abomination! Destroy! Destroy the false god!"

Soma, who has more patience for this sort of categorical error than I do, objected on Valphine's behalf: "She's not false, she's the real deal."

This was, I thought, exactly correct, though I doubted the cleric was in a position to receive the clarification.

I looked across the gully. The light. The orcs, some of them slowing, some of them still screaming, the cleric in the center of it all committing entirely to the wrong interpretation of what was happening. He thought we were here to fight him. He thought the light was a weapon. He thought Valphine was an abomination, which — and I say this with genuine respect for Valphine — he had badly miscalculated in the direction of his own destruction.

We were not here to fight. We were here to resolve a misunderstanding.

The cleric, however, was not a misunderstanding. He was the reason the misunderstanding had been allowed to grow into an army.

"We'll have to focus all of our attacks only on the leader," I said, and I meant it, "and try to convert the rest."

A pause from someone nearby, some flicker of disbelief at how this situation was apparently going to be conducted.

"Alright," I said. "Sounds good."
