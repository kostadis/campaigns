## Vukradin — The Ascent to Icespire Hold

The mountain had been watching us for weeks.

From Phandalin, on the clear days when the light came in low and golden from the east, you could see it — a white peak at the edge of everything, patient and permanent, the kind of landmark that makes you feel simultaneously small and oriented. I had looked at it from the market square, from the Privy Council steps, from the window of the inn when I was working on a new piece and needed something to rest my eyes against. I had looked at it so many times that when we finally began the ascent, the recognition that landed in my chest had a quality almost like reunion.

"So we've seen it before," I said, more to myself than anyone. "Oh. Interesting."

Soma looked up from the path ahead of her, steady and unhurried as she always is. "We can see it from Phandalin."

"It's like Mount Fuji," I said.

I meant it as a compliment. I meant: it is one of those mountains that is so perfectly itself that it becomes a symbol — not just a peak but a reference point, a fixed note in the chord of a landscape. A mountain that gets painted. A mountain that earns its name. The others received this with varying degrees of enthusiasm.

The path was where music becomes necessary, as a practical matter. Silence invites attention to the wind, and the wind at that altitude has opinions about you specifically and they are not flattering. So I began composing as we climbed — a marching song, something in three-four because three-four wants to move forward, a little push built into every measure. I was still working out the bridge when we hit the first stretch of real difficulty and I had to put the composition aside to concentrate on not falling.

"Brr," I said, which is not a line from any of the songs I have written, but which felt true in a way that the most carefully constructed lyric could not have matched in that moment.

The cold was a living thing up there. It had texture. It came from below as much as above, up through the soles of boots and into the ankles, and it came sideways on the wind with a persistence that felt almost personal. I looked at Brewbarry, who was moving with the particular unconcerned ease of a man whose relationship with discomfort is a casual and largely unthreatening one.

"That won't be a problem for Brewbarry," I noted. "He'll be fine."

He appeared not to hear me, or to need the observation confirmed. Either way, he kept moving.

We found our footing on the path — that is the best way I can describe it, the way the group settled into a rhythm, each person contributing the thing they were best at: Soma's patient attentiveness to the terrain, Valphine's navigation, Brewbarry's absolute immunity to the conditions that were making the rest of us miserable. The exhaustion that should have accumulated on a climb like that somehow did not grip us the way it might have. I like to think the song helped. I was still humming the verse when the fortress came into view.

Two structures. Joined by a stone bridge. Stone and ice and altitude, exactly as ancient and indifferent as it looked from Phandalin, except now we were standing in front of it rather than half a region away. Icespire Hold. I had a moment of the sort of feeling that does not translate well into words — the gap between a thing seen at a distance and a thing seen close up, the way the imagination fills in details that the reality then replaces. I had imagined something forbidding and dramatic. The reality was more than that. It was simply there, the way mountains are simply there, beyond the human vocabulary of threat or welcome.

The alternative route presented itself as a two-hundred-foot wall of sheer ice rising alongside the main path.

"That appears to be the only safe path," I said, studying the routes available to us. Then, because the question seemed natural: "What are the unsafe paths?"

No one answered in a way that made the ice wall sound appealing, and I took this as useful information. We were not climbing the ice wall. This seemed like a reasonable collective decision.

The rock slide was another matter. A section of the trail — perhaps twenty feet of it — had gone wrong at some point, boulders and rubble collapsed across the path in a way that would have stopped a less determined group. 

"There's a rock slide!" I said, spotting it, which is the kind of announcement that is true and yet somehow insufficient to the situation. The others, to their credit, treated it as a problem to be solved rather than a reason to abandon the enterprise, and in fairly short order we had either climbed over or navigated around the worst of it. Soma, at some point during this process, shifted — there is no other word for what Soma does, and I have watched it many times now and still cannot quite describe it adequately — into the shape of a wolf spider larger than a man. A giant wolf spider, specifically, as she herself would later feel it necessary to clarify, with some dignity.

"I'm a giant wolf spider," she said, navigating the rubble with eight legs and complete competence, "not... it's a medium-sized beast, not a... not a little rinky-dink spider."

The clarification landed in the middle of a rockslide on the slope of a mountain at altitude, and I found that I had no adequate response to it. I filed it away as something that would eventually find its way into a lyric, and kept moving.

We came up over the last rise and the fortress was fully before us. And there, on the roof of the main structure — blending into the snow with a kind of casual mastery, white against white, enormous against enormous — was the dragon.

"Oh," I said. The cold had a new quality suddenly. "You can see the dragon."

I had known, in an abstract sense, that there would be a dragon. This was, after all, Icespire Hold. There is a dragon in the name. But abstract knowledge and the actual sight of the creature were in different categories, and my mind was doing the work of moving the information from one category to the other.

"The dragon has a name?" I asked. "Cryovain?"

"Yeah," said Soma, from somewhere to my left, four of her eight legs braced against a boulder. "It's the first I've heard of it."

Cryovain. It was sleeping. Its scales — white, almost translucent at the edges — were barely distinguishable from the ice beneath it. It was the size of a structure. It was sleeping in the way that large, dangerous things sleep, which is to say with complete apparent unconcern about anything that might be observing it.

I looked at it for a long moment. I thought about the marching song I had been composing on the way up, the three-four rhythm, the verse I hadn't finished.

"The obvious path is a trap," Soma said, in her particular tone of patient certainty.

"I don't know," said Valphine, thoughtful. "The gatehouse just seems so obvious."

We stood there on the edge of the approach, the wind working at us steadily, looking up at the fortress and the sleeping dragon on its roof. From Phandalin, on a clear day, you can see this mountain. I had looked at it dozens of times and thought: *that is far away.* Now I understood that far away and here are the same place, separated only by the willingness to climb.
