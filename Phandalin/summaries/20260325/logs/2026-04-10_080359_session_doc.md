## Section — Soma — Soma Scouts the Roof

The wall was stone, old and rough, and in spider form that was enough. Eight legs found purchase where two never would have, and I pulled myself up the face of the fortress with the patient, methodical efficiency of something that had been climbing things since before the concept of hesitation. The wind off the peaks was cold and sharp. I didn't rush.

I crested the edge and went still.

The roof opened up before me, and what I saw there made the cold feel like a distant, irrelevant thing.

Cryovain. I recognized the shape of a white dragon even like this — even reduced to this. He was chained at the center of a summoning circle, the markings carved or burned into the stone beneath him, and he wasn't moving. Not sleeping. Not still in the way of a creature that has chosen stillness. He was held. His scales had gone the color of old ice left too long in shadow, and his eyes — something had gone wrong with his eyes. The angles of his face were off. The hollows where alertness should live were empty in a way that wasn't unconsciousness.

*Where's his nose,* I thought, and I meant it literally. Something had been taken from him, or something had been done to him that had taken it by degrees.

The Carver stood over him. Performing something. Moving in the way that people move when they are speaking words they believe to be important, and perhaps they are right to believe that. I held myself against the parapet and watched and did not breathe, which is easier when you have as many legs as I did. He didn't look up.

I watched Cryovain long enough to understand what I was seeing. The stillness wasn't resignation. Underneath all of it — underneath the chains and the circle and whatever had been done to hollow his eyes — there was something seething. The kind of rage that has been compressed past the point where it still resembles rage and has become something closer to madness. He wasn't waiting. He wasn't biding time with any intelligence behind it. If those chains came off, he would not think. He would not choose. He would simply destroy everything within reach, and we would be within reach.

I filed that away. Carefully.

The Carver had not seen me. I confirmed this by remaining exactly where I was and continuing to not die, which is my preferred method of confirming these things. Then I climbed back down.

---

When I told the others what I'd seen, Vukradin leaned forward the way he does when he's already building an argument before you've finished speaking.

"Maybe you should get, like, a plus eighteen on your stealth. You're a spider."

"That would have been embarrassing," I said. "I wouldn't have been a spider for long."

He absorbed this, and moved on, which I appreciated. "Oh, he has chained the dragon and put it in a summoning circle."

"He's looking very, very zombified," I said. "Like, where's his nose?"

The question stood on its own. Nobody had a good answer for it.

Then came the part I had been thinking about on the way down. "I mean, he's in chains. Are they real chains? Are they, like, spectral chains? I worry that killing the Carver or something would, like, make the chains evaporate."

"Well, we're gonna have to kill the Carver regardless." Vukradin, certain as stone.

"I would like to understand," I said, carefully, "you know — is he load-bearing on the dragon kind of thing."

There was a pause that told me my meaning had landed.

Valphine considered this with that particular expression she wears when she's found an angle. "The enemy of my enemy is my friend."

I looked at her.

"I don't think the dragon's gonna see it that way."

"When the enemy of my enemy is released," she said, with the patience of someone explaining something she considers obvious, "then my enemy does not have the resources he's looking for."

"You'll just," I said. "You'll talk him out of it."

She seemed to take this as a reasonable plan. I let it go.

"Yeah, he doesn't look like a sane fella," I added, which I meant as a footnote to the entire conversation.

Vukradin, undeterred by all of this — and I say this with the weary affection of someone who has watched him be undeterred before — offered: "Actually, after we save the dragon, the dragon may be very grateful."

I thought about those eyes. The thing behind them that wasn't waiting for anything anymore.

"Oh boy," I said. "So, there's a dragon down there that's just kind of being held down. Alright. I guess I relay that to the team."

There was more, though. Someone — I had missed something, apparently, in my look at the chains. Someone who understood such things better than I do worked out what had actually been done to Cryovain.

"Yeah," I was told. "He is. The sword isn't."

The Carver had not simply chained a dragon. He had taken one.

"Oh," I said.

From beside me, Vukradin: "Oh…"

We sat with that for a moment. A dragon — Cryovain, who had terrorized Phandalin, who had been a threat and a monster and a problem to be solved — was somewhere inside that fortress, hollowed out, held down, wearing his own rage like a second set of chains. Not freed. Not tamed. Dominated.

I thought about what would be left of him if we broke those chains. I thought about what he would do to the first thing he could reach.

I thought: *this is going to be complicated.*
