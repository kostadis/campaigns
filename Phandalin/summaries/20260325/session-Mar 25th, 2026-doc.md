## Valphine — The Ascent to Icespire Hold

The mountain does not reveal itself gradually. One moment the clouds part, and there it is — Icespire Peak, so enormous it could be seen from Phandalin on a clear day, and yet somehow standing in its presence still registers as an assault. Two stone structures joined by a bridge, perched on an icy spur as if the mountain had grown the fortress as an afterthought, and around it: nothing. Wind. Cold. The specific, scouring cold of elevation, the kind that finds every gap in a cloak and treats it as an invitation.

I am not unacquainted with harsh environments. The Underdark has its own brutality — stone that presses from all sides, the absolute dark, the weight of knowing that everything living down there is either predator or prey and frequently both. But the Underdark's hostility is intimate. It whispers. This — *this* — is something else entirely. This cold is not trying to kill you. It simply doesn't care that you're here. There's a distinction. I find it clarifying.

The others noticed it too, in their ways. Vukradin looked up at the peak the way he looks at most things that could kill us — with an expression of determined goodwill, as if the mountain might be persuaded to cooperate given the right framing. Soma was already reading the terrain. I was reading the light.

Surface light. I should stop cataloguing my reactions to it, and yet. At this elevation, with nothing to diffuse it, Lathander's light hit the ice and fractured into something extraordinary — every slope a different quality of brilliance, every shadow a shade of blue that had no equivalent underground. My eyes ached with it in the particular way that I have come to associate with worship. Gloriously painful. The exact phrase. I could not have improved on it if I had chosen the words in advance.

The routes up were neither of them generous. A narrow path wound treacherously around the mountain's face. The alternative was a sheer ice wall, two hundred feet of it, and Vukradin's arithmetic was correct if not particularly elegant: *two hundred is more. That's more. That's tough.* I didn't say anything. There's a particular pleasure in watching surface dwellers arrive independently at conclusions the Underdark would have taught them immediately.

The Carver had taken the path. Soma's question — whether he might have simply carved directly through the mountain — was reasonable, and the answer that he'd used the ordinary route was somehow more unsettling than the alternative. Powerful things that don't bother to display their power. I have known several. They are uniformly more dangerous than the ones who announce themselves.

Cryovain was visible from the approach, if you knew what you were looking at. White scales against white snow, the dragon's massive form slumped on the fortress rooftop with the particular stillness of a thing that has surrendered to something. Not sleep. Restraint. I noticed before anyone said it aloud. I didn't point it out. It wasn't the right moment.

The debate about which entrance to use was brief. I suggested the obvious path — the gatehouse, direct, unambiguous. "Maybe we should just take the obvious path." This is what I said. The logic being: we were already past subtle, we had a time constraint, the Carver knew someone was coming.

Soma pushed back, and she wasn't wrong. "If we come in from the side entrance, we might actually avoid an encounter or two." A valid point, and the kind of valid point that should probably outweigh aesthetics. I noted my own reasoning — "The gatehouse just seems so obvious" — and let it be overruled. Vukradin: "Alright. Sold. Do it. Go for it." He does this. Commits fully to the last reasonable option presented to him. In a different context it would be exhausting. Up here, in the wind, against a deadline, it was actually efficient.

Soma became a spider. A medium-sized giant wolf spider, she wanted us to understand — not a small thing, not something you'd miss in the dark. She skittered up fifty feet of sheer ice and dropped us a rope. The rest of us climbed. The rope swayed. The wind pulled at us with what I can only describe as personal investment. I passed the climb. So did Brewbarry, who expresses himself primarily through successfully executing whatever he is pointed at. So did Vukradin, who provided a climbing song for the occasion.

*Up the ice, fire climbing, we will go.* The bard. I find him — I find him interesting. In the way that organisms which survive conditions designed to eliminate them are interesting.

The fortress interior told the story of several failed tenancies in compressed form. Rubble from the earthquake that had collapsed two corners. Arrow slits looking out over a drop no one would want to take. Rooms where the Carver had smashed through doorways and found nothing worth keeping. And then the orc quarters — the ash-choked fireplace, the burned furniture, the particular quality of filth that accumulates when a displaced population makes do.

Vukradin looked at the room and immediately began constructing a resettlement policy. "We need to secure Icespire so the orcs can return safely." Soma: "Eastern Homeland Reclaiming." The GM — the mountain, Lathander, the universe — confirmed: "Yes, I mean, you're all about the decolonization." I catalogued this without comment. The political instincts are sincere. That doesn't make them less predictable.

I found the secret door.

There is no modest way to present this. My perception caught something in the fortress wall that nothing else about the room suggested — a seam, a shadow, an irregularity that the others had walked past. The DM gave me the joy of rolling, and I gave him a 29. "You found a secret door." Vukradin immediately suggested it had been a DC 30 and I'd missed it. He was joking. Probably.

The discovery collapsed the remaining deliberation instantly. Vukradin: "We're going to the Dragon, it's right there, you just opened the door, it's right there." This required no further analysis. "Let's go to the dragon." Three words. The kind of decision that sounds simple and costs everything. Soma: "We're not fucking around."

Correct.

Then Soma went ahead to scout, still in spider form, clinging to the fortress wall above the summoning circle while the Carver performed his ritual below. She reported back what she'd seen: Cryovain bound in something — spectral chains, or real ones, she couldn't say — and the Carver working over it with the concentrated purpose of someone who believed they were about to have everything they'd always wanted.

The question she raised was tactical and precise. "I mean, he's in chains. Are they real chains? Are they, like, spectral chains? Like, I worry that killing the carver or something would, like, make the chains evaporate."

Vukradin: "Well, we're gonna have to kill the carver regardless."

"I understand we're gonna have to. I would like to understand, like, you know, is he load-bearing on the dragon kind of thing."

There was no answer. Not one anyone could give with certainty. The chains might hold. The chains might not. The dragon, Soma had confirmed after a perception check of 24, was psychotically enraged — not an animal in distress, not a creature that would orient toward its rescuers with gratitude. A second threat. A consequence we would have to manage.

I offered the alternative framing. "The enemy of my enemy is my friend." Which is a drow formulation but also simply accurate, and I don't see why truth should have a cultural patent.

Soma: "I don't think the dragon's gonna see it that way."

"When the enemy of my enemy is released, then my enemy does not have the resources he's looking for."

Vukradin, who finds optimism in everything, offered the most optimistic reading: "Actually, after we save the dragon, the dragon may be very grateful." And then someone referenced Sister Kaella's last report — the Carver grown furious, the ritual accelerating. Soma: "Yeah, he doesn't look like a sane fella."

None of this changed anything. I hadn't expected it to. The logic of alliance wasn't about the dragon's gratitude. It was about denial of resources. If the Carver needed Cryovain, then Cryovain was leverage, and leverage is the only currency that consistently retains its value in the moments before everything goes wrong.

The wind kept howling. The light kept scattering off the ice in its precisely unbearable way. Below us, on the rooftop, the Carver stood over a bound white dragon, and the summoning circle pulsed, and the sword at his side had been waiting — I understood this now, in the back of my mind where the interesting thoughts settle before they become certain — far longer than any of us.

We went in.

## Vukradin — Bypassing the Gatehouse

The mountain does not care about you. That is the first thing you understand, standing at the base of a two-hundred-foot sheer ice wall with the wind trying to peel your coat from your body. Icespire Peak is visible from Phandalin on a clear day — visible from *Phandalin*, where we started all of this, where Harbin Wester was too frightened to leave his office — and now here we are, standing at its feet, and the mountain is looking down at us the way mountains do, which is to say not looking at all, not caring, simply being enormous and cold and entirely indifferent to the ambitions of a bard from the coast.

"That appears to be the only safe path," I said, because someone had to say it. "What are the unsafe paths?"

The narrow winding path corkscrewed up to the smaller gatehouse, manageable, watched. The other option was the sheer wall. Two hundred feet of it. The GM told us so plainly.

"Two hundred is more," I said. "That's more. That's tough."

I am aware this is not my most penetrating analytical contribution. But sometimes the situation calls for honest acknowledgment of scale.

---

The debate about the approach took longer than the approach itself, which is traditional.

Valphine looked at the gatehouse path and said what any tactician would say — "Maybe we should just take the obvious path" — and then almost immediately said what any tactician *also* says — "The gatehouse just seems so obvious."

Soma understood. "If we come in from the side entrance, we might actually avoid an encounter or two." She looked at the ice wall with the particular expression of someone who has options unavailable to the rest of us. "Aren't we trying to catch up with the Carver? Like, isn't there a time schedule here?"

There was a time schedule. There was definitely a time schedule.

"Alright," I said. "Sold. Do it. Go for it."

The decision snapped into place. I am not a man who benefits from extended deliberation at the base of ice walls. Once the logic is sound, you commit. You trust the ensemble. You let the people with the skills do the thing with the skills.

I had one idea of my own, for the record.

"What if," I said, and I could already feel the specific energy of a proposition that will not survive contact with reality, "stick with me for this a minute — I have that spell that can make you see whatever I decide. You think it's real. So we can just have you experience climbing it, and then you will have seen it."

There was a pause.

"No," said the GM. "It doesn't work that way."

I spread my hands. "I can't do anything in this campaign."

---

What I *could* do was hand Soma a piece of inspiration before she shifted. It felt like the right gesture — a conductor giving the downbeat before the soloist steps forward. She was about to become something with eight legs and a stealth problem, and I wanted her to have something in reserve. A little melodic reinforcement. Something that said: *I see you, I believe in you, the music knows your name.*

She became a giant wolf spider. Medium-sized, as it turned out. Not the inconspicuous skittering thing we had imagined, but a creature of genuine presence and unsettling geometry, which scrambled up fifty feet of sheer ice face with the confidence of something that simply does not acknowledge the possibility of falling.

"Why doesn't your stealth stat change when you're a spider?" I asked, genuinely curious.

She dropped the rope without answering. Some questions are rhetorical.

---

I pulled myself up hand over hand, the ice cliff indifferent beneath the rope, the wind working against me in the way wind always does at elevation — not dramatically, not heroically, just persistently, as if it has somewhere better to be and you are in the way. My hands found their holds. My boots found the wall. A DC 7 on Athletics, and I was up.

"We need a good climbing song," I said, because I was already composing one.

*Up the ice, fire climbing, we will go —*

The first line came easy. The second line required structural consideration. The third line I abandoned when someone's boot sent a small rock slide of ice crystals down past my face.

*There's a rock slide.*

That one was less a lyric and more a field report, but in the moment the distinction felt academic.

---

Inside the fortress, the mountain's indifference became something more personal. The place had been broken by an earthquake — two corners collapsed, great piles of rubble where walls had been — and then broken again by something larger than earthquakes. There was a hallway where Cryovain had come searching for prey, had found nothing, had smashed through a doorway in the particular frustration of an apex predator encountering absence. The scale of the destruction put the dragon in a different register. Not a shape on a rooftop. A force that had passed through here and left its signature in shattered stone.

We found the orc room. Every piece of furniture burned in the fireplace. The stench of desperation and displacement, of people who had nowhere to go and chose warmth over everything else.

"Oh, right," I said, and the pieces arranged themselves. "And then the dragon came and scared them all out, and then they had to go attack all the people. Right — we need to secure Icespire so the orcs can return safely."

Soma looked at me. "Eastern Homeland Reclaiming."

"Yes," said the GM, dry as the stone beneath our feet. "I mean, you're all about the decolonization."

I am. I think it's the correct framing. The orcs did not choose displacement; displacement chose them, via apex predator, and the appropriate response is not to congratulate yourself for surviving the orcs' justified desperation but to address the underlying cause. This seems to me so obviously right that I do not understand why it requires saying, but apparently it does, so I say it, and I will keep saying it until Harbin Wester makes a policy.

Valphine, meanwhile, was pressing her hand against a wall in the particular way of someone who suspects a wall is lying. A 29 on Perception. The hidden door opened.

"Sadly," I said, "it was a DC30. You missed it."

The GM looked at me. "Dude, I'm a bad man, but I'm not that bad."

Fair.

---

"We're going to the Dragon," I said, and I heard my own voice do the thing it does when the debate is over. "It's right there, you just opened the door, it's right there."

Valphine said: "Let's go to the dragon."

Soma said: "We're not fucking around."

And that was it. No more side rooms. No more inventory of what the orcs had burned. The Carver was up there. Cryovain was up there. And Soma had already been there, in spider form, clinging to the outer wall with the bardic inspiration I'd given her humming somewhere in her borrowed nervous system — stealth 16, enough to not be noticed, enough to come back with the news.

"Oh boy," she said, when she returned. "So, there's a dragon down there that's just kind of being held down."

I looked at what she was describing in my mind. A white dragon, enormous, pale as the snow it had been sleeping in, now held in place by chains that might be real and might be something else entirely. The Carver above it, conducting whatever ritual it had climbed two hundred feet of ice not to bypass but to perform.

"What I see," I said, carefully, because I wanted to be precise about the tactical situation, "is that the dragon is immobilized until we deal with the Carver."

Then the other thing occurred to me, the thing that is perhaps the truest thing I believe: "Actually — after we save the dragon, the dragon may be very grateful."

Soma looked at me with an expression I have learned to recognize. It is the expression of someone who does not want to be unkind but cannot fully suppress the instinct toward accuracy.

"I mean, he's in chains," she said. "Are they real chains? Are they, like, spectral chains? Like, I worry that killing the Carver or something would, like, make the chains evaporate."

"Well," I said, "we're gonna have to kill the Carver regardless."

"I understand we're gonna have to. I would like to understand, like, you know — is he load-bearing on the dragon kind of thing."

That is the question, isn't it. Is the man load-bearing on the dragon. I have sung at smaller venues than this problem.

Valphine offered the principle: "The enemy of my enemy is my friend."

Soma offered the counterpoint: "I don't think the dragon's gonna see it that way."

Valphine, undeterred: "When the enemy of my enemy is released, then my enemy does not have the resources he's looking for."

I stood in that corridor, with the hidden door open and the dragon somewhere above us and the wind doing what wind does, and I thought about what comes next with the particular clarity that precedes terrible and necessary things. The Carver had to die. The chains were a secondary problem. Gratitude from a freed dragon was, perhaps, an optimistic projection.

But hope is what I have, and I was not prepared to abandon it yet.

## Soma — Soma Scouts the Roof

The climb was not what anyone expected.

I mean, I told them. I said wolf spider, not rinky-dink spider — medium creature, not the little thing you're imagining tucking itself into a crack in the stonework. But they nodded and waved me up anyway, and Vukradin had that look he gets, the one that means *we're doing this* and also *I have complete confidence in outcomes I haven't fully thought through.* I am old, and I have seen much, and I know that look very well by now.

So eight legs on ice-slicked stone, hauling a medium-sized body up the outer wall of Ice Spire Hold in a howling mountain wind. Conspicuous is the right word. Every second on that wall was a second of *please don't look up, please don't look up.*

Vukradin offered the inspiration before I went. He reached out and gave me that little bardic nudge, that extra note of possibility sitting warm in my chest even in this body, this borrowed form. I held onto it and kept climbing.

The natural 3 on the check did not inspire confidence. I felt it happen — felt the slip, the overcorrection, all eight legs scrabbling for purchase — and burned the inspiration immediately, let it pour into the roll. Sixteen. The GM paused just long enough to make my chelicerae tighten.

*He does not notice you. That is actually sufficiently high for him not to notice you.*

I allowed myself one moment of private relief.

"That would have been embarrassing," I said, when I was back among the party, still running on the adrenaline of *that was very close*. "I wouldn't have been a spider for long."

---

But before that — before the relief — there was the rooftop.

I had peered over the edge, all my new eyes taking it in, and the scene had hit me the way bad omens always hit: not like a revelation, but like a confirmation of something I already suspected and hoped was wrong.

Cryovain. The dragon. *Enormous.* Even bound, even held in the summoning circle, the creature's scale was something that had to be felt to be understood. A body that should have been airborne, free, terrible — reduced to this. Chains. Glowing, uncertain chains, magical or spectral or something I didn't have a word for, because when I tried to puzzle out the nature of the ritual, the arcane logic of it, I came up empty. Got nothing. The magic slid away from me like oil from water.

*You got nothing. Other than whatever you think as a player, you got nothing.*

I didn't push back on that. What I think as a player doesn't matter. What mattered was what I could see and what I could feel.

What I could see was the Carver standing at the center of it, the ritual pulsing with sickly purple light around that obsidian sword. What I could feel — what the part of me that knows animals, knows creatures, knows the way something lives in its own skin — what that part understood was that the dragon's eyes were wrong. Not fearful. Not calculating. Not even simply angry. Psychotic. The rage was in there but it had nowhere to go, couldn't route itself into action, and that backed-up pressure of a creature fighting against every instinct it had ever possessed — that was what I was looking at.

Cryovain was not sleeping. Not subdued. Not waiting to be reasoned with.

A bomb. That's the simplest word for it.

The Carver, for his part, did not look like a sane fella. I noted that plainly, because it was plain. Whatever had brought him here, to this rooftop, to this particular intersection of dark ritual and chained apex predator, it had not been a journey that left a person intact.

---

When I rejoined the others and reverted, I tried to be methodical about it.

"Oh boy," I said first, because that's what the situation warranted. "So. There's a dragon down there that's just kind of being held down. Alright."

Then the real question. The one that had been turning over in my head the entire way back down the wall.

"I mean, he's in chains. Are they real chains? Are they, like, spectral chains? Like, I worry that killing the Carver or something would, like, make the chains evaporate."

Vukradin said we'd have to kill the Carver regardless. Which: yes. Obviously. I know that.

"I understand we're gonna have to. I would like to understand, like, you know, is he *load-bearing* on the dragon kind of thing."

The question hung there. Nobody had an answer. Because nobody knew, and I certainly didn't — the arcana had given me nothing. But somebody needed to ask it, and I had been the one on the wall, and so it was mine to carry into the room.

Valphine, to her credit, was already turning it over tactically. *The enemy of my enemy is my friend.* Drow pragmatism, clean and sensible on its surface.

"I don't think the dragon's gonna see it that way," I said.

She reframed it — not an ally, just an absence of one of the Carver's resources. When the enemy of my enemy is released, the enemy loses what he was trying to gain.

Which is fine, as logic goes. The problem is that I had looked into those eyes. And the thing I had seen there wasn't looking to negotiate its freedom into anything useful. When those chains went — if those chains went — that creature was going to take the nearest living thing apart, and then the next one, and then keep going until the fury burned itself out or something stopped it.

Cryovain was nobody's friend. Not right now. Possibly not ever.

I said what I knew and left the strategy to the rest of them. I had done my part. I had climbed the wall, kept my legs under me, brought back the truth of what was up there.

That had to be enough.

## Brewbarry — Confrontation at the Summoning Circle

The rooftop hit me like a wall of cold the moment we came through. Wind sheering off the peak, the whole sky pale and wrong. And there he was — the Carver — standing at the far edge of the summoning circle with Cryovain in chains below him, purple light spilling from that black sword.

I didn't think. There's nothing to think. I raged.

The rage came up through my feet and into my chest and I was already moving before it finished arriving. I wanted to close the distance. I wanted to put the halberd through him. Simple. Clean. The fortress had other ideas.

The walls cut me off. The routing around the battlements, the angles — every path I could see had something in the way, and the Carver was on the other side of all of it, unhurried, conducting his ritual like we weren't even there. I pressed forward as far as I could go, and then I stopped, and I was still too far. My lungs were full of cold air and fury and I had nowhere to put it.

Then he moved.

No ordinary man moves like that. He used something — some power in that blade — and covered thirty feet without a single opportunity for me to touch him. Soma called him a cheating bastard. She wasn't wrong, but I didn't have words for it. I just stood in the space where he'd been, empty-handed, my breath coming hard, the rage with nothing to bite into.

Then Vukradin said *Hold Person.*

The Carver locked up rigid. Frozen. Like someone had stolen the life out of him mid-step.

And I heard Vukradin's voice, triumphant and warm and entirely himself: *"He is paralyzed! Excellent! Well done, team!"*

For one moment I believed it. That the fight was over. That we'd done it.

*"Yes,"* the GM said. *"He casts Dominate Person."*

Vukradin said: *"He's paralyzed."*

*"Yeah, he is. The sword isn't."*

The obsidian blade pulsed. Just once. A slow, cold throb of purple-black light, and then the chains around Cryovain dissolved like smoke. The man was standing there, locked and helpless, utterly irrelevant. The sword didn't need him anymore.

And then it spoke.

Not from the paralyzed man's mouth. Not from the dragon. From inside my skull, cold and contemptuous and completely sure of itself: *"Coward's magic, you mute the ritual because you lack the strength to shatter the vessel. And now, now I shall have everything I always wanted."*

I have heard people speak from places of power before. My old tribe's war-chiefs, when they wanted you afraid. The voice in my head was nothing like that. Those voices wanted something from you — wanted fear, wanted obedience. This voice didn't want anything from me. It had already decided I didn't matter.

Psalmist's light clarified something, somewhere in that moment — I felt it like a pressure behind my eyes. The real Carver was never the man. The man was a vessel. The sword was the Carver. The sword had always been the Carver. Everything we had prepared, everything we had tracked across these miles, had been built around a lie we didn't know we were inside.

Vukradin said it out loud, slowly, his theatrical certainty dissolving into something almost childlike: *"The carver... the sword... the... oh. The carver is the sword."*

Yes. The carver is the sword.

Three rounds. That's what we had. Three rounds before the sword finished dominating Cryovain completely — before it didn't just have the dragon, it *was* the dragon. The beast was already in a homicidal rage, already loose, already wrong, and we had three rounds to kill it before it became something worse.

Vukradin looked at me across the chaos of the rooftop.

*"Crewbury. Kill the dragon."*

That's all he said. That's all I needed.

Valphine's radiant bolt hit first — a lance of light that left Cryovain exposed, advantage laid out for whoever came next. Soma had already given up her own turn for this, positioning silently, no complaint. I didn't fully understand what she'd sacrificed until later. In the moment I just felt the opening and I took it.

I drew the Dragon Slayer Sword and I charged.

The first blow landed with the advantage Valphine had made for me, the blade biting into white scales with a sound like something breaking that was always going to break. The second blow hit just as hard. Forty-three damage between them. The sword knew what it was made for, and so did I.

Then Cryovain breathed.

Thirty feet of cone. Ice and cold and the specific kind of pain that my rage couldn't protect me from. My rage stops blades, stops arrows. Cold is cold. Stone's Endurance took the edge off — I felt it pull from somewhere deep in my blood, my Goliath inheritance shaving ten points off the worst of it — but thirty points still got through, and I won't pretend I didn't feel every one.

And then the dragon beat its wings and climbed. Thirty feet up, hovering above the battlements. Out of reach. Looking down at me with eyes like frozen over lakes, chains gone, sword in control, clock still running.

I stood there. Ice in my beard, damage in my chest, Dragon Slayer Sword in my hand and nothing in range to use it on.

Vukradin said, almost gently: *"I got nothing, guys. Other than Fireball."*

Someone asked if he was actually going to use it.

*"Nope."*

The beads are mine. He'd given them back to me in word and in principle and he wouldn't touch them even now, even with a dragon thirty feet overhead and two rounds left on the clock. That's Vukradin. That's who he is. I don't always understand the surface world, but I understood that.

And then he cracked a thorn whip upward into the sky and landed a critical hit for twenty-six damage on an airborne white dragon, and Soma said *Way to go, face*, and even then — ice-burned, grounded, sword still ready — I felt something close to joy.

## Valphine — Battle Against Cryovain

The first thing I did was point at the dragon and let Lathander's light do what it does.

There is a particular pleasure in Guiding Bolt that I doubt any sun-priest born topside has ever understood. They speak of radiant energy the way they speak of forgiveness — softly, as though the goal is comfort. They have never had to locate their god in the dark. Lathander is not a gentle light when you find him from below. He is a lance. He is the thing that sears the dark so completely that shadows have nowhere left to go. When I left the Underdark, I did not find a kinder divinity. I found a more powerful one, and I thought: *yes, this one will do.*

The bolt struck Cryovain across the chest and the dragon lit up like a confession.

Fifteen radiant damage, visible to anyone on the battlements. More importantly: the beast was *marked* now, outlined in coruscating light, every subsequent strike against it guided by Lathander's eye. A tactician's spell. A sniper's gift. I filed away the pleasure of watching a white dragon shimmer like a coal and turned my attention to what it had purchased.

Brewbarry was already moving.

The goliath raged in that particular way he has — not theatrical, not announced, just a kind of compression that happens in the shoulders and the jaw right before something very large causes a great deal of pain to something else. Valphine's mark does its work; the Dragon Slayer Sword found purchase twice, twenty-one and twenty-two, forty-three total. The numbers themselves are satisfying in the way that precise instruments are satisfying. You build the opening. Someone else walks through it. The exchange is clean.

Then Cryovain inhaled.

I have catalogued many unpleasant things in my life. The feeling of thirty feet of freezing breath against skin that is already cold-adapted and still *alarmed* by the drop in temperature was not among the experiences I had anticipated. The cone swept the battlements and caught Brewbarry full, and I watched thirty points of cold damage express itself as ice crystallizing up his arms, across his shoulders, along his beard. He staggered. He didn't fall. I noted this as relevant to future calculations about goliath durability and filed it.

And then the dragon *rose.*

Thirty feet up, hanging in the air above us with something very close to deliberate malice in its positioning. Close enough to breathe again. Far enough to deny us the satisfaction of hitting it back with anything serious. Cryovain is a white dragon in a psychotic rage under active attempted Domination by a sentient sword, and it is making geometrically sound tactical decisions. I found this more alarming than the cold.

Soma said, dry as gravel: *"A lot of thinking in his enraged state."*

I didn't have a response to that. She wasn't wrong, and I don't perform agreement.

Vukradin pressed himself against the stone wall with the focused expression of a man calculating options and finding them insufficient. I have watched him work his way through these moments before — the theatrical optimism compresses down to something quieter, something that resembles actual thought, when the stakes remove room for performance. He surveyed the airborne dragon. He surveyed his party. He came to the same conclusion I had.

*"I got nothing, guys,"* he said. *"Other than Fireball."*

Soma said: *"You're actually gonna use it?"*

Vukradin said: *"Nope."*

There is a specific texture to Vukradin's stubbornness that I find genuinely interesting, in the way one finds an unusual specimen interesting — it shouldn't survive contact with pragmatism, and yet it persists. The Necklace of Fireballs belongs to Brewbarry. Therefore Vukradin will not use it. A dragon is airborne thirty feet above us under active hostile domination, the clock is bleeding, and the bard with the most explosive resource on the battlefield declines to deploy it on principle. Surface dwellers are endlessly predictable. Vukradin is not, exactly, because he is sincere about it in a way the others are merely performing. He means it. Somehow that makes it funnier and more frustrating simultaneously.

He cast Thorn Whip instead. The thorned vine lashed skyward with what I can only describe as theological audacity, and it *landed*. Critical. Twenty-six points of damage torn from a white dragon hovering thirty feet above a frozen battlement by a bard who had just finished explaining that he had nothing.

*"I hit! I even got a critical!"*

Soma said: *"Way to go, face!*"

He shouted *"26 damage!"* with a delight so unguarded it briefly made the cold seem less cold. The dragon wheeled. Vukradin was triumphant. The domination countdown was still running.

The battle was beautiful, in its way. An overmatched party on a frozen battlement, one round survived, two remaining before the sword's grip on Cryovain becomes something we cannot interrupt. The situation was bad in several interesting directions at once.

I assessed. I waited. Lathander's mark still shimmered on the dragon's chest, visible against the clouds.

The first blow had been mine. The question now was who got the last one.

## Vukradin — The Sword Speaks

The ritual dies in absolute silence.

That is my doing, and I will not pretend otherwise. Twenty feet of perfect, stolen quiet, dropped directly over the summoning circle like a bell jar over a candle. No incantation, no dark words, no whatever-it-is the Carver needs to bind a dragon to his will. I have interrupted it. I am saving the world.

"Interrupting the ritual! I am saving the world! Huzzah!"

Soma does not look at me when she says, "They were intervening, you interventionist."

I choose to receive this as affection.

"Good job, team! You did it."

The Carver does not wait for us to feel good about ourselves. His legendary action carries him sideways out of the silence zone with a kind of supernatural tidiness, thirty feet of repositioning that provokes nothing, costs him nothing, undoes everything. Soma says something about cheating bastards that I privately agree with. He stands outside my carefully placed quiet and the air crackles again around that obsidian sword, and I think: *alright, new plan.*

The new plan is Hold Person.

"Hold Person."

He locks. Rigid. Mid-motion, like a puppet with its strings cut by someone who left the frame standing. I have paralyzed the Carver and it is, I want to be clear, excellent.

"He is paralyzed! Excellent! Well done, team!"

I mean every word of it. I am conducting an ensemble and the ensemble is *performing.* This is what it feels like when the whole band hits the note together. I have the concentration now — Silence gone, Hold Person active — and the Carver is a statue on the rooftop of Icespire Hold and we are, I genuinely believe, winning.

"He's paralyzed."

"Yeah," the GM says, "he is. The sword isn't."

A pause. A very specific kind of pause. The kind where everyone in the room processes something at slightly different speeds and arrives at the same destination with different expressions on their faces.

"Yes," the GM continues, pleasantly, "he casts Dominate Person."

The magical chains around Cryovain — the spectral ones, the ones holding five tons of enraged white dragon to this rooftop — dissolve. The human body I have locked in place remains locked. The sword, which I had not thought to lock because swords do not cast spells, casts a spell.

And then the voice arrives.

Not through air. Through the inside of my skull, cold and contemptuous and absolutely, infuriatingly certain of itself.

*"Coward's magic. You mute the ritual because you lack the strength to shatter the vessel. And now — now I shall have everything I always wanted."*

Soma asks if that was the dragon. The GM clarifies: we hear it in our heads.

I stand there for a moment, processing.

The Carver. The sword. The— oh.

*Oh.*

"The carver... the sword... the... oh, the carver is the sword."

Soma nods. The human was always just a vessel. The sword consumed its wielder long ago and has been wearing him ever since, walking him toward this rooftop, toward this dragon, toward a ritual that my Silence spell interrupted and my Hold Person spell did not, because I cast Hold Person on a husk.

The thing I have been calling the Carver has just called my most clever tactical decision of the evening *coward's magic.*

I think about this. I think about what it means that my first instinct, when faced with a ritual too dangerous to let complete, was to steal its voice rather than shatter its source. I think about the voice's contempt — the precision of it, the way it landed in my thoughts like a stone dropped from a height. *You lack the strength.* And I think about the fact that it is not wrong, exactly, because I do have a Necklace of Fireballs and I chose not to use it and I would make the same choice again, but —

But the dragon is free.

"It's in seething psychotic rage," the GM is saying. Soma tells us to go, not to unlock it, her voice carrying the weight of a 24 on an animal handling check. She has looked into what this creature is and found nothing retrievable there.

"We're gonna talk to it."

This is, I will admit, not a serious proposal. I know it is not a serious proposal. But there is a part of me — the part that talked a tavern full of hostile dockworkers into buying me drinks, the part that once got a merchant guild to apologize to me — that cannot fully extinguish the instinct. *Every conflict is a misunderstanding.* Even this one, presumably. Even a five-ton dragon in psychotic rage, chained until thirty seconds ago, now fixed on us with the specific fury of something that has been helpless and is no longer.

The three-round clock begins.

"Alright," I say, and I hear myself say it, hear the voice that has gotten me through every impossible room by sounding more certain than it has any right to be. "That's fine. Let's kill a dragon. It'll be fine."

I turn to Valphine. I need her to believe this, because if she believes it, it becomes slightly more true. "Didn't you always want to kill a dragon? Isn't this, like, a rite of passage for the director?"

And then I point at Brewbarry, because when you have a Dragon Slayer Sword in your ensemble you do not waste it.

"Crewbury, kill the dragon."

He goes. He hits. He hits again. I tally the numbers with the focus of a man calculating a music budget — 21 and 22, the Dragon Slayer's extra dice rolling across Cryovain's scales like thunder given form, and 43 damage total lands in two blows and Soma says, quietly, "That was worth me sacking around for," and I feel the particular warmth of a conductor watching their finest player hit a passage they have been building toward all night.

And then the dragon breathes.

Forty points of cold. Brewbarry stands in the cone of it and his rage is irrelevant — cold is not slashing, his defenses do not apply — and he takes it, all of it, and the dragon launches itself off the battlements and hangs in the air thirty feet above us and I look up at it and I take stock.

The Necklace of Fireballs is around my neck. Seven beads. One of them would solve this problem in the immediate and create other problems further down — but that is a future concern, and the dragon is a present one, and I have made a promise to Brewbarry, a promise I did not make lightly, and I will not unmake it because the tactical situation has become inconvenient.

"I got nothing, guys. Other than Fireball."

Soma looks at me. "You're actually gonna use it?"

"Nope."

What I have, what is left, is Thorn Whip. A cantrip. A tendril of thorned bramble cast upward at a dragon hovering thirty feet in the air, by a bard who has spent most of this campaign being more useful in the green room than on the stage. The voice in my head — the sword's voice, cold and certain — said I lacked the strength to shatter the vessel.

I cast Thorn Whip.

The dice give me a natural twenty.

"I hit! I even got a critical! Twenty-six damage!"

Soma says, "Way to go, face!" with the genuine delight of someone who has been watching me be mostly decorative for several months and is very pleased to be wrong about that.

"Crushed it!"

I mean it absolutely sincerely. Twenty-six points of damage from a bard who had nothing. The Carver — the sword, wearing its man, standing paralyzed and helpless in the body it consumed — called me a coward from inside my own skull.

My thorns disagree.

But the dragon is still in the air. The sword's domination is still tightening its grip. Round one of three is closing, and I look at what we have — Brewbarry standing in the wreckage of a dragon's breath, Soma quiet with the weight of what she's seen, Valphine tactical and steady, the clock running — and the euphoria of the critical hit does what euphoria always does.

It drains.

"Well," I hear myself say, "now he looks more powerful."

I was feeling good. I am feeling less good. Two rounds left, a dragon above us with a sword's will behind its eyes, and the cold voice of something ancient and contemptuous ringing around the inside of my head where my best ideas used to live.

The clock ticks.

## Soma — The Dragon Unbound

The rooftop hit us like stepping into a held breath.

Below was fortress, stone, the winding stairs we'd climbed through orc quarters Vukradin had immediately started calling "Eastern Homeland Reclaiming" in that way he has of making everything sound like a land rights symposium. Up here was something else entirely. The mountain swallowed the horizon. The cold was the kind that goes past skin into intention — it finds the part of you that's thinking about staying alive and addresses it directly. And in the center of the rooftop, within a summoning circle that had no business being up here except that someone had clearly dragged it up here anyway, Cryovain.

I had seen large things. Old things. Things that made a tortle feel very small and very new. But a white dragon in chains is something specific — not diminished by the chains, just contained. The way a river in flood is not diminished by its banks.

I started reading her before I'd even fully registered the Carver standing at the circle's edge, holding that black sword, channeling something that made the air taste like endings.

Animal handling is not magic. People get that wrong. It's patience, and attention, and a willingness to understand what a creature is before you ask what it's doing. Twenty-four years of both lives stacked behind my eyes, and what I read in Cryovain was immediate and absolute: she was not here. Whatever she had been before this ritual — territorial, predatory, perfectly ordinary in her violence — was buried under something that had no bottom. Seething. Psychotic. The word isn't strong enough. It was the kind of rage that doesn't distinguish between the thing that caused it and the ground under its feet.

"It's not happy," I said.

The GM told me what I already felt: a state of such enragement that she would attack everything around her. Everything. Not the Carver. Not the sword. Not the party. Everything. Walls, air, the idea of being alive nearby.

"Go, guys, don't, don't unlock the dragon."

Vukradin said, "We're gonna talk to it."

Shell sprout.

I didn't say that. I noted it, filed it, and moved on, because Vukradin had already stopped listening to himself and started casting, which was the best possible outcome from where I was standing.

The Silence came down on the summoning circle like a dropped cloth, and the ritual guttered. The bard announced himself to the mountain.

"Interrupting the ritual! I am saving the world! Huzzah!"

"They were intervening," I said, "you interventionist."

He took it as a compliment. He always does.

The Carver moved. Legendary action — slipped out of the silence bubble without provoking anything, which was deeply irritating in a mechanical sense. "Cheat bastard," I said, and then caught myself and clarified, "Cheating bastard, yay, with his legendary actions. Not you," because the GM was right there and I have some manners.

Then Vukradin paralyzed him. Hold Person, clean hit, and the man with the sword went rigid — and Vukradin did what Vukradin always does, which is declare victory at the halfway point.

"He is paralyzed! Excellent! Well done, team!"

And the GM said: "Yes, he casts Dominate Person."

Vukradin said, slowly, "He's paralyzed."

"Yeah," said the GM. "He is. The sword isn't."

The chains dissolved.

The voice came into my head like it had always been there, like it had been waiting since before I was born to say this specific thing in this specific moment: *"Coward's magic, you mute the ritual because you lack the strength to shatter the vessel. And now, now I shall have everything I always wanted."*

I said, out loud, in a voice that came out considerably smaller than I intended: "The dragon says that?"

No. In your heads, the GM told me. In everyone's heads.

I rolled arcana, got a thirteen, and thirteen was enough. The architecture of it assembled in front of me the way a healer assembles the nature of a wound — not magic, just pattern recognition applied fast. The human was a vessel. The sword was the Carver. The sword had been performing a ritual to consume Cryovain entirely, and Vukradin's Silence had interrupted that, and so it had done the next available thing, which was Dominate. Not complete control. Not yet.

"The human is just a vessel," I said.

Vukradin was still working through it. "The carver... the sword... the... oh, the carver is the sword."

Yes. It took him a moment. It was still faster than most people would have managed.

Three rounds, the GM told us. Three rounds before the sword's control solidified. The dragon was partially dominated, still in psychotic rage, and we had a window.

"Wait a minute," I said. "Can we go wail on the sword?"

The sword that had been purple and swirling with something was now just — a black obsidian sword. Whatever it had been channeling, it had spent. The power had already transferred.

Vukradin said, with the serene confidence of someone who has never once second-guessed himself in a crisis: "Alright, that's fine, let's kill a dragon. It'll be fine."

I was still in spider form, which I want to note, in my own defense, made perfect sense earlier in the evening. Eight eyes. All of them technically functional. None of them, as it turned out, particularly useful. "Yeah, sorry," I said, climbing over the separating wall, "I'm blind in spider form, I've got 8 eyes and none of them work." I burned my last Wild Shape charge shifting to bear. No attacks this turn. Everything spent just to be in the right place. You do the math and you make the choice and you stop thinking about what it cost.

It was worth it. That was the only thought I permitted myself.

Valphine put Guiding Bolt into Cryovain — fifteen radiant, and the glow of it painted the dragon's scales in colors that had no business being that beautiful in a fight — and then Brewbarry was there, Dragon Slayer Sword in hand, and he hit her. Twice. Twenty-one and twenty-two, back to back, the dragon-slayer bonus making each strike into something close to the specific language of an ending.

"That was worth me sacking around for," I said.

Then the dragon breathed.

Thirty-foot cone of cold, aimed at Brewbarry, and I watched from bear-shape as he took forty points of it. Stone's Endurance helped. Rage didn't — cold breath is cold breath, not slashing, not the kind of damage a barbarian's body knows how to half-process. He took it full. Then she rose thirty feet into the air, positioned over the battlements, breath weapon range, out of melee, reading the battlefield like she was calculating something.

"A lot of thinking," I said, "in his enraged state."

Dominated, not enraged. The rage was still there — I could still read it, still feel the seething underneath — but the tactical repositioning was the sword's work, not hers. It had noticed we had no longbows. It had noticed the ceiling of our reach. It was managing us.

Vukradin surveyed the situation. "I got nothing, guys. Other than Fireball."

I looked at him.

"You're actually gonna use it?"

"Nope."

There it was. Brewbarry's necklace, which has been on the table as an option since roughly the beginning of our acquaintance, will apparently remain on the table until the heat death of the universe or until Vukradin decides it's a team choice and not a Brewbarry choice, whichever comes last.

Then he cast Thorn Whip.

The vine went up thirty feet into frozen air and struck a white dragon and the dice came up natural twenty and Vukradin said, "I hit! I even got a critical!" and then "26 damage!" and I said, "Way to go, face!" because twenty-six points of thorn damage from a bard who has been telling us he can't do anything is the kind of thing you cheer for regardless of the circumstances.

He said, "Crushed it."

He had. He genuinely had.

Then the dragon took her legendary action — the free disengage, hovering further off, doing nothing that could generously be called dramatic — and I noted, for the record, "That's not very legendary, then, is it? I'd say lame, it's just, you know, the common is not, you know, on the scale of loot, it's the opposite end of legendary."

Three rounds. One down. The clock was still running.

## Brewbarry — Dragon Slayer Strikes

The rooftop of Icespire Hold is all wind and cold and the smell of something ancient and wrong. The dragon fills half the sky. That's the first thing. The dragon fills half the sky and it is chained, and the chains are made of something that isn't quite light and isn't quite darkness, and the Carver stands at the center of it all like he has already won.

Brewbarry runs.

Not toward the Carver. Toward the gap between where he stands and where he needs to be. The wall cuts across the roof and he has to go around it, boots slamming stone, rage already lit and burning clean in his chest. The Dragon Slayer Sword is in his hand. He'd been pointed at the target — *Crewbury, kill the dragon, didn't you always want to kill a dragon* — and now he runs and the distance doesn't close fast enough and that's the only thing in his head. The distance.

He doesn't make it. Not this turn. He stops with the gap still between him and anything he can hurt, and the rage has nowhere to go, and that is its own kind of pain.

Vukradin's Silence falls on the summoning circle. The Carver's mouth opens and nothing comes out and Vukradin announces it to the mountain:

*"Interrupting the ritual! I am saving the world! Huzzah!"*

*"They were intervening, you interventionist,"* Soma says.

*"Good job, team! You did it."*

Brewbarry doesn't say anything. He is watching the Carver's face, and the Carver's face says that this is not over, that this was never going to be over that easily.

He's right. The Carver moves — that elegant, infuriating slide away from Brewbarry's reach, thirty feet of repositioning without a single opening for the blade — and then Vukradin's *Hold Person* catches him mid-stride. Freezes him. The Carver's human body locks up like ice over a still pool, one foot lifted and going nowhere.

*"He is paralyzed! Excellent! Well done, team!"*

Brewbarry lets himself feel it for one breath. Hope. The kind he doesn't trust, because hope is always the moment before something goes wrong.

*"He's paralyzed."*

*"Yeah, he is. The sword isn't."*

The chains dissolve.

Cryovain surges — not free, not really free, but freed from the ritual and poured immediately into something else, the sword blazing purpley and wrong before going black, cold obsidian absorbing the light. And then the voice arrives in Brewbarry's head, which is not how voices usually arrive in his head, and it is the worst voice he has ever heard — not because it's loud, but because it's so certain:

*"Coward's magic. You mute the ritual because you lack the strength to shatter the vessel. And now, now I shall have everything I always wanted."*

Soma says: *"The dragon says that?"*

No. Not the dragon. Not the man. The sword. It was always the sword. The man was just a vessel, same as the dragon was almost a vessel, and Brewbarry stands on a frozen rooftop holding a sword in his hand and thinks: I understand this. He can't say why. Maybe because he has also been someone else's weapon, before. Maybe because the tribe went boom boom boom and he was part of that boom. He doesn't say any of this. He just stands with the understanding and lets it settle.

Three rounds to kill a dragon.

Valphine's Guiding Bolt hits the dragon true — fifteen points of radiant light that doesn't just hurt but marks, paints a shimmer on those white scales that says *here, this is where the blade goes next.* Brewbarry sees it and he moves.

He swings.

The Dragon Slayer Sword is not a subtle weapon. It doesn't ask questions about what it's hitting. It knows what it was made for and it sings — the first hit biting deep into white scales, something in the enchantment catching and surging, the blade knowing dragonflesh when it finds it. Twenty-one. The second blow follows, same conviction, the rage giving his arms more than arms should have. Twenty-two. Forty-three damage in two heartbeats.

*"That was worth me sacking around for,"* Soma says, quietly, and Brewbarry doesn't look at her but he hears it, and it costs him something small and good to hear it — the kind of acknowledgment he is still not entirely sure how to hold.

Then Cryovain breathes.

The cold doesn't give you time to decide if you're ready for it. It is simply there — a cone of frozen air that hits Brewbarry straight on, and his Goliath blood does what Goliath blood does, bracing, absorbing, the Stone's Endurance pulling ten of it away into nothing — but thirty points of cold tears through anyway, and rage is no shelter from this. His tribe never told him about this. His tribe never fought anything with real cold in it. The cold is clean and total and it doesn't care about his history or his shame or the sword in his hand or what he's trying to become.

He's still standing. That's the thing. He takes it and he's still standing.

The dragon beats its wings and rises — thirty feet up, the rooftop suddenly very far below those vast pale haunches, and the Dragon Slayer Sword is suddenly useless, and Brewbarry watches his target ascend into the cold air with the particular frustration of a weapon that cannot reach what it was made for.

Vukradin, from somewhere below that twenty feet of sky, says: *"I got nothing, guys. Other than Fireball."*

A beat.

*"You're actually gonna use it?"*

*"Nope."*

Of course not. *You have my sword, you have my halberd, the beads are yours* — Brewbarry had given them over without hesitation, because Vukradin asked, because that was enough. The beads belong to Vukradin to not use if that's what Vukradin decides. This is not complicated.

Then the thorn whip cracks upward — Vukradin, who can't do anything, critting on a dragon thirty feet in the air — and the party absorbs it in real time, Wade saying *way to go, face* with the exact sound of someone who didn't see that coming at all, and Brewbarry stands battered and frosted on the rooftop with two rounds left on the clock and the dragon bleeding overhead and the Dragon Slayer Sword waiting in his grip.

It needs to come back down.

He'll be here.
