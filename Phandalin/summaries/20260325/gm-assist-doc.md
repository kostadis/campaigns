## Vukradin — The Ascent to Icespire Hold

The mountain had been watching us for weeks.

From Phandalin, on the clear days when the light came in low and golden from the east, you could see it — a white peak at the edge of everything, patient and permanent, the kind of landmark that makes you feel simultaneously small and oriented. I had looked at it from the market square, from the Privy Council steps, from the window of the inn when I was working on a new piece and needed something to rest my eyes against. I had looked at it so many times that when we finally began the ascent, the recognition that landed in my chest had a quality almost like reunion.

"So we've seen it before," I said, more to myself than anyone. "Oh. Interesting."

Soma looked up from the path ahead of her, steady and unhurried as she always is. "We can see it from Phandalin."

"It's like Mount Fuji," I said.

I meant it as a compliment. I meant: it is one of those mountains that is so perfectly itself that it becomes a symbol — not just a peak but a reference point, a fixed note in the chord of a landscape. A mountain that gets painted. A mountain that earns its name. The others received this with varying degrees of enthusiasm.

The path was where music becomes necessary, as a practical matter. Silence invites attention to the wind, and the wind at that altitude has opinions about you specifically and they are not flattering. So I began composing as we climbed — a marching song, something in three-four because three-four wants to move forward, a little push built into every measure. I was still working out the bridge when we hit the first stretch of real difficulty and I had to put the composition aside to concentrate on not falling.

"Brr," I said, which is not a line from any of the songs I have written, but which felt true in a way that the most carefully constructed lyric could not have matched in that moment.

The cold was a living thing up there. It had texture. It came from below as much as above, up through the soles of boots and into the ankles, and it came sideways on the wind with a persistence that felt almost personal. I looked at Brewbarry, who was moving with the particular unconcerned ease of a man whose relationship with discomfort is a casual and largely unthreatening one.

"That won't be a problem for Brewbarry," I noted. "He'll be fine."

He appeared not to hear me, or to need the observation confirmed. Either way, he kept moving.

We found our footing on the path — that is the best way I can describe it, the way the group settled into a rhythm, each person contributing the thing they were best at: Soma's patient attentiveness to the terrain, Valphine's navigation, Brewbarry's absolute immunity to the conditions that were making the rest of us miserable. The exhaustion that should have accumulated on a climb like that somehow did not grip us the way it might have. I like to think the song helped. I was still humming the verse when the fortress came into view.

Two structures. Joined by a stone bridge. Stone and ice and altitude, exactly as ancient and indifferent as it looked from Phandalin, except now we were standing in front of it rather than half a region away. Icespire Hold. I had a moment of the sort of feeling that does not translate well into words — the gap between a thing seen at a distance and a thing seen close up, the way the imagination fills in details that the reality then replaces. I had imagined something forbidding and dramatic. The reality was more than that. It was simply there, the way mountains are simply there, beyond the human vocabulary of threat or welcome.

The alternative route presented itself as a two-hundred-foot wall of sheer ice rising alongside the main path.

"That appears to be the only safe path," I said, studying the routes available to us. Then, because the question seemed natural: "What are the unsafe paths?"

No one answered in a way that made the ice wall sound appealing, and I took this as useful information. We were not climbing the ice wall. This seemed like a reasonable collective decision.

The rock slide was another matter. A section of the trail — perhaps twenty feet of it — had gone wrong at some point, boulders and rubble collapsed across the path in a way that would have stopped a less determined group. 

"There's a rock slide!" I said, spotting it, which is the kind of announcement that is true and yet somehow insufficient to the situation. The others, to their credit, treated it as a problem to be solved rather than a reason to abandon the enterprise, and in fairly short order we had either climbed over or navigated around the worst of it. Soma, at some point during this process, shifted — there is no other word for what Soma does, and I have watched it many times now and still cannot quite describe it adequately — into the shape of a wolf spider larger than a man. A giant wolf spider, specifically, as she herself would later feel it necessary to clarify, with some dignity.

"I'm a giant wolf spider," she said, navigating the rubble with eight legs and complete competence, "not... it's a medium-sized beast, not a... not a little rinky-dink spider."

The clarification landed in the middle of a rockslide on the slope of a mountain at altitude, and I found that I had no adequate response to it. I filed it away as something that would eventually find its way into a lyric, and kept moving.

We came up over the last rise and the fortress was fully before us. And there, on the roof of the main structure — blending into the snow with a kind of casual mastery, white against white, enormous against enormous — was the dragon.

"Oh," I said. The cold had a new quality suddenly. "You can see the dragon."

I had known, in an abstract sense, that there would be a dragon. This was, after all, Icespire Hold. There is a dragon in the name. But abstract knowledge and the actual sight of the creature were in different categories, and my mind was doing the work of moving the information from one category to the other.

"The dragon has a name?" I asked. "Cryovain?"

"Yeah," said Soma, from somewhere to my left, four of her eight legs braced against a boulder. "It's the first I've heard of it."

Cryovain. It was sleeping. Its scales — white, almost translucent at the edges — were barely distinguishable from the ice beneath it. It was the size of a structure. It was sleeping in the way that large, dangerous things sleep, which is to say with complete apparent unconcern about anything that might be observing it.

I looked at it for a long moment. I thought about the marching song I had been composing on the way up, the three-four rhythm, the verse I hadn't finished.

"The obvious path is a trap," Soma said, in her particular tone of patient certainty.

"I don't know," said Valphine, thoughtful. "The gatehouse just seems so obvious."

We stood there on the edge of the approach, the wind working at us steadily, looking up at the fortress and the sleeping dragon on its roof. From Phandalin, on a clear day, you can see this mountain. I had looked at it dozens of times and thought: *that is far away.* Now I understood that far away and here are the same place, separated only by the willingness to climb.

## Valphine — Bypassing the Gatehouse

The approach to Icespire Hold had a certain theatrical quality that I appreciated more than it deserved. The winding path cut straight up to the gatehouse — obvious, exposed, the kind of route that announces itself like a herald with a fanfare. Which is precisely why I suggested we take it.

"Maybe we should just take the obvious path," I said, because someone had to say it, and because watching the others talk themselves out of efficiency has become one of my minor pleasures on long journeys.

Soma, to her credit, had already been thinking further. She always does — methodically, patiently, like someone laying stones. "If we come in from the side entrance, we might actually avoid an encounter or two." She wasn't wrong. She rarely is, on tactical matters. The gatehouse would be watched. Side entrances exist precisely because whoever built the front door expected you to use it.

Vukradin took exactly as long to be convinced as I expected. "Alright. Sold. Do it. Go for it." He commits quickly when the logic lands — it's one of the more functional things about him.

The cliff was perhaps fifty feet of icy rock, which in practical terms means it was not climbable by anything without claws or a rope. Soma looked at it with the calm assessment of someone for whom this is simply a question of which shape fits the problem. "Giant wolf spider, yeah... Skitter up the wall, drop a rope down."

I watched Vukradin's expression shift into the particular configuration that means he has an idea he's very excited about. This is rarely a good sign.

"What if—" he began, with the careful enunciation of someone who knows they're about to be interrupted, "and stick with me for this a minute—I have that spell that can make you see whatever I decide. You think it's real? So we can just have you experience that, and then you will have seen it."

The logic of this deserves a moment's examination, which is more than it merited in practice. He was, as best I could reconstruct it, proposing that Soma could understand spiders more deeply by *believing* she had seen one. As a theological proposition this has some distant merit. As a plan for climbing an icy cliff, it does not.

It didn't work that way. The cliff remained fifty feet tall. Soma remained patient.

"I can't do anything in this campaign," Vukradin said, which is the kind of statement that collapses under its own weight if examined too closely, given that he had just volunteered to solve a physical problem with an illusion. I did not say this. Filing things away without comment is a skill I have been practicing since before his grandparents were born.

Soma's pragmatism cut through cleanly. "I'm not here to piss off the GM." I had no idea what she meant by that, but the effect was decisive, and Soma began to change.

There is something worth noting about the transformation — the way she simply *becomes* the spider, efficient and without ceremony, without the performance of metamorphosis that you might expect. One moment she is Soma: slow, careful, a tortle with a staff. The next she is eight legs and purpose, moving up the cliff face with the absolute confidence of a creature designed for exactly this surface. I watched her go and felt something adjacent to envy. Not for the shape. For the certainty.

The rope came down. We climbed.

Vukradin, characteristically, composed a song for the occasion. "Up the ice, fire climbing, we will go..." floated down from somewhere above me, and I focused on my handholds and thought about whether there was a theological interpretation of this moment that Lathander would find meaningful. Probably. The light was extraordinary up here — the cold amplified it somehow, everything sharp and precise, the snow catching it and giving it back twice over in ways that hurt beautifully to look at. I didn't mind the climb.

When we reached the top and moved onto the bridge, Vukradin surveyed what we had achieved — the gatehouse below us, the position we had taken — and delivered his assessment with the timing of a man who has thought about worst cases rather more than most.

"Now we will be surrounded on the bridge, and attacked from both sides."

The image of it was so precisely calibrated to every tactical error we had avoided by climbing instead that I almost laughed. He had, without meaning to, described exactly what happens to people who take the obvious path.

"Down the rope! Down the rope, everybody! Down the rope!" He was already, apparently, planning the retreat.

I let the silence sit for a beat and then walked forward. The rubble-strewn ground ahead bore the marks of an earthquake — old damage, heaved stone, corridors opened where walls had been. Cryovain had moved through here, that much was clear: a hallway smashed not by time but by something large and deliberate, the stone not crumbled but *broken*, the way things break when something enormous has decided they are in its way.

I catalogued it. The direction of the damage, the scale of it, the way the destruction was oriented. An animal making a path. A large one, moving with the comfort of something that owns this space entirely.

Somewhere ahead, the dragon was waiting. Not for us — it wouldn't know to wait for us yet — but it was there, present in every piece of broken stone, every scorched surface, every silence that felt slightly too heavy to be only cold air.

I find that clarifying.

## Soma — Soma Scouts the Roof

The wall was stone, old and rough, and in spider form that was enough. Eight legs found purchase where two never would have, and I pulled myself up the face of the fortress with the patient, methodical efficiency of something that had been climbing things since before the concept of hesitation. The wind off the peaks was cold and sharp. I didn't rush.

I crested the edge and went still.

The roof opened up before me, and what I saw there made the cold feel like a distant, irrelevant thing.

Cryovain. I recognized the shape of a white dragon even like this — even reduced to this. He was chained at the center of a summoning circle, the markings carved or burned into the stone beneath him, and he wasn't moving. Not sleeping. Not still in the way of a creature that has chosen stillness. He was held. His scales had gone the color of old ice left too long in shadow, and his eyes — something had gone wrong with his eyes. The angles of his face were off. The hollows where alertness should live were empty in a way that wasn't unconsciousness.

*Where's his nose,* I thought, and I meant it literally. Something had been taken from him, or something had been done to him that had taken it by degrees.

The Carver stood over him. Performing something. Moving in the way that people move when they are speaking words they believe to be important, and perhaps they are right to believe that. I held myself against the parapet and watched and did not breathe, which is easier when you have as many legs as I did. He didn't look up.

I watched Cryovain long enough to understand what I was seeing. The stillness wasn't resignation. Underneath all of it — underneath the chains and the circle and whatever had been done to hollow his eyes — there was something seething. The kind of rage that has been compressed past the point where it still resembles rage and has become something closer to madness. He wasn't waiting. He wasn't biding time with any intelligence behind it. If those chains came off, he would not think. He would not choose. He would simply destroy everything within reach, and we would be within reach.

I filed that away. Carefully.

The Carver had not seen me. I confirmed this by remaining exactly where I was and continuing to not die, which is my preferred method of confirming these things. Then I climbed back down.

---

When I told the others what I'd seen, Vukradin leaned forward the way he does when he's already building an argument before you've finished speaking.

"Maybe you should get, like, a plus eighteen on your stealth. You're a spider."

"That would have been embarrassing," I said. "I wouldn't have been a spider for long."

He absorbed this, and moved on, which I appreciated. "Oh, he has chained the dragon and put it in a summoning circle."

"He's looking very, very zombified," I said. "Like, where's his nose?"

The question stood on its own. Nobody had a good answer for it.

Then came the part I had been thinking about on the way down. "I mean, he's in chains. Are they real chains? Are they, like, spectral chains? I worry that killing the Carver or something would, like, make the chains evaporate."

"Well, we're gonna have to kill the Carver regardless." Vukradin, certain as stone.

"I would like to understand," I said, carefully, "you know — is he load-bearing on the dragon kind of thing."

There was a pause that told me my meaning had landed.

Valphine considered this with that particular expression she wears when she's found an angle. "The enemy of my enemy is my friend."

I looked at her.

"I don't think the dragon's gonna see it that way."

"When the enemy of my enemy is released," she said, with the patience of someone explaining something she considers obvious, "then my enemy does not have the resources he's looking for."

"You'll just," I said. "You'll talk him out of it."

She seemed to take this as a reasonable plan. I let it go.

"Yeah, he doesn't look like a sane fella," I added, which I meant as a footnote to the entire conversation.

Vukradin, undeterred by all of this — and I say this with the weary affection of someone who has watched him be undeterred before — offered: "Actually, after we save the dragon, the dragon may be very grateful."

I thought about those eyes. The thing behind them that wasn't waiting for anything anymore.

"Oh boy," I said. "So, there's a dragon down there that's just kind of being held down. Alright. I guess I relay that to the team."

There was more, though. Someone — I had missed something, apparently, in my look at the chains. Someone who understood such things better than I do worked out what had actually been done to Cryovain.

"Yeah," I was told. "He is. The sword isn't."

The Carver had not simply chained a dragon. He had taken one.

"Oh," I said.

From beside me, Vukradin: "Oh…"

We sat with that for a moment. A dragon — Cryovain, who had terrorized Phandalin, who had been a threat and a monster and a problem to be solved — was somewhere inside that fortress, hollowed out, held down, wearing his own rage like a second set of chains. Not freed. Not tamed. Dominated.

I thought about what would be left of him if we broke those chains. I thought about what he would do to the first thing he could reach.

I thought: *this is going to be complicated.*

## Vukradin — Confrontation at the Summoning Circle

The orc quarters hit you before you see them — a wet, charred smell like a fire that gave up halfway through the job, and underneath it something organic and deeply unpleasant. Whatever furniture those orcs had brought in, they'd mostly burned for warmth or out of boredom, and what remained was black-rimmed and collapsed inward. I found myself remembering, almost involuntarily, what I knew of how they'd come to be here in the first place.

"Oh, right," I said, to no one in particular, more to myself than to the party, "and then the dragon came and scared them all out, and then they had to go attack all the people."

Which gave me an idea, the way an unexpected chord change gives you an idea.

"Right," I said. "We need to secure Icespire so the orcs can return safely."

Soma looked at me the way she sometimes does — patient, deeply patient, the way someone looks at a student who is almost there.

"Eastern Homeland Reclaiming," she said.

Exactly. The humanitarian framing was better, actually. More accurate. I filed it away.

Then Valphine found the secret door, because Valphine always finds the thing, and immediately I had the same reaction I always have to a detour when there is a destination already identified.

"Why would you want to go over there?"

The dragon was *right there.* We had just opened the door. The dragon was right there.

"We're going to the dragon," I said, as plainly as I could manage. "It's — it's right there, you just opened the door, it's right there."

Valphine peered through and apparently saw nothing of consequence, because she emerged and said, simply, "Let's go to the dragon."

"We're not fucking around," Soma said, which is not how I would have phrased it, but I appreciated the alignment.

We went to the dragon.

---

I heard the ritual before I understood what I was hearing. The sound of it was wrong in the way that a note struck flat in the middle of a quiet room is wrong — not loud, but *incorrect,* in a way that makes your spine know before your mind does. We came up through the stairs and there was the Carver, in the middle of something dark and deliberate, and there was Cryovain, chained, the great white body of the dragon bound by something I could feel more than see — a magical restraint that vibrated at a frequency that set my teeth together.

Brewbarry didn't need a cue. He went in bellowing, red-eyed and enormous, the rage in him already burning by the time his feet hit the floor.

My hand was already at my voice, the spell already forming, the geometry of it already calculated — *how far, where is the center, does the circle fall cleanly over the ritual's focal point* — and then I cast it.

Silence. Not quiet. *Silence.* The kind that removes all sound from a space entirely, that drops like a dome over the summoning circle and swallows the Carver's working whole.

The ritual sputtered. Choked. The wrong-frequency sound cut off like a string snapped.

"Interrupting the ritual!" I heard myself announce — to the room, to my companions, to the version of this that would become a song later, I suppose. "I am saving the world! *Huzzah!*"

"They were intervening," Soma said, very calmly. "You interventionist."

I accepted this.

Then the Carver moved.

Not stumbled, not panicked — *moved.* With complete precision, a single fluid action, out of the silence zone and into open air without drawing a single attack. Legendary, that footwork. The kind of footwork that tells you everything about what you're actually fighting, if you're paying attention.

I was paying attention.

The arcana knowledge arrived with the clarity of a well-notated score: the black obsidian sword at the Carver's hip was not a weapon. Or rather — it was not *only* a weapon. The man was a vessel. The *sword* was the Carver. The sword had always been the Carver. Everything else was clothing.

I processed this in approximately the time it takes to resolve a fermata.

Before I had finished processing it, I cast Hold Person.

The veteran's body locked. Completely. Mid-motion, mid-breath, the limbs seized and he dropped into stillness the way a marionette drops when you let go of the strings — not collapsed, just *stopped.* There.

"He is paralyzed! *Excellent!* Well done, team!"

And then — "He's paralyzed." 

Said again, quieter, because something had changed. Because I had just looked at what was actually in the room and understood what the paralyzed man meant and what it didn't.

The silence spell dropped.

And the sword spoke.

Not through the man. Not through a mouth. Through the space inside my skull, in a voice that had no interest in being pleasant about any of it.

*Coward's magic. You mute the ritual because you lack the strength to shatter the vessel. And now, now I shall have everything I always wanted.*

The chains binding Cryovain dissolved. Not broke — *dissolved,* like they had never had any confidence in their own existence.

"The Carver —" I started. Stopped. Started again. "The sword — the — oh. The Carver is the sword."

"Yeah," Soma said. "The human is just a vessel."

Of course. Of course he was. And the sword — the Carver — had just skipped the ritual entirely, the slow careful consumption it had been building toward, and done the expedient thing instead. Domination. Now. Not controlled, not refined, just — *seized.*

"It's not happy," Soma said, looking at the dragon.

It was not happy. Understatement as a form of art.

"Don't unlock the dragon," Soma said to all of us, unnecessarily, because none of us were reaching for any metaphorical locks, "go, guys —"

"We're gonna talk to it," I said.

Soma looked at me.

"To the dragon," I said.

She continued looking at me.

We were not going to talk to it.

Someone — I think it was a voice from somewhere outside the moment, the information simply arriving fully-formed — made clear that we had three rounds. The sword had seized the dragon but not completely. Three rounds before whatever was left of Cryovain's mind was gone entirely and we were left with a fully-dominated white dragon in an enclosed space.

"Alright," I said. I straightened. There was a silence in me that had nothing to do with the spell — the specific silence of being genuinely, cleanly frightened, the kind I can't talk my way around. I have learned, over time, not to perform composure in those moments. It wastes energy. "That's fine. Let's kill a dragon. It'll be fine."

I looked at Valphine. She was already calculating, I could see it — the way she always is, measuring distance and divine fire and what Lathander's light can do against a thing this large.

"Didn't you always want to kill a dragon?" I said to her. There was something almost genuine in it beneath the bravado. "Isn't this — like, a rite of passage? For the *director?*"

And somewhere in the back of my mind, in the part that never quite stops thinking about composition, a quiet and slightly horrified note: the black obsidian sword that had been glowing purple and vast with accumulated power was now just — a sword. Black. Still. Like something that had emptied itself out completely to get what it wanted.

It had gotten what it wanted.

## Brewbarry — Battle Against Cryovain

The sword was already in my hand before Vukradin said it.

"Crewbury, kill the dragon."

I didn't correct the name. There wasn't time, and also — he could call me whatever he wanted. He always could.

What I needed was the Dragon Slayer Sword. I'd been carrying it for this. For exactly this. The weight of it felt different now, with the dragon filling the sky above the battlements, all white fury and snapping teeth. I could feel the cold coming off it like a wall. I had the sword. That was enough to move.

Valphine had already put something into Cryovain — a bolt of radiant light that hit like a small sun, and I saw the dragon flinch and reorient, its attention briefly scattered. Good. That was the door. Soma was already repositioning — I caught the moment she shifted form, the spider giving way to something larger, heavier, a bear hauling itself into close range. She burned both her remaining wild shapes to do it. I heard her mark it off without complaint. That was worth me sacking around for. That's what she said. I filed it away. Soma never wastes her breath.

I went straight at the dragon.

The first blow landed clean. The Dragon Slayer Sword connected with something that wasn't just scale — there was resistance, and then there wasn't, and I felt the impact travel all the way up my arms in the best way, the way that means you've done something real. The second blow came right after. Also clean. Both of them, landing the way I always hope they land and rarely do. The sword was made for this. I was made for this. In that moment, neither of those facts felt strange.

Cryovain did not appreciate it.

The dragon's head swung toward me. The mouth opened.

The cold that came out of it wasn't like winter. I know winter. I grew up in it. This was something that winter would run from — a cone of frost that hit me full in the face and didn't stop, pushing through my armor, pushing through the rage that was supposed to keep me standing, finding the places underneath where things are softer and hurting them. I didn't go down. I am not saying it didn't hurt. I am saying I didn't go down, and that felt like the most important fact available to me in that moment.

What I noticed, when I could notice things again, was that the dragon was moving.

It beat its wings once and lifted — thirty feet straight up, pulling itself above the battlements, out of easy reach. Out of my reach. I looked up at it. I looked at my sword. I could calculate the distance, and the calculation was not good. I am not a calculator, normally, but I know when something is outside of what I can do, and a thirty-foot leap at a white dragon while I was still feeling the frost settle into my chest — that was outside.

Vukradin had taken cover behind a section of stone wall, tucking himself against it, looking out through one of the arrow slits. I could see his face and I could see what was on it. He was doing the thing he does when he's looking at a problem that doesn't have clean edges. I recognized it. He doesn't like problems without clean edges. He looked at me, he looked at Soma, he looked at whatever Cryovain was doing forty feet above us.

"I got nothing, guys," he said. "Other than Fireball."

Soma said: "You're actually gonna use it?"

"Nope."

I understood that. The fireball was his. It came from him. He'd made that choice about it already, somewhere private, and the choice was no. I didn't argue. I don't argue with Vukradin about what he keeps for himself. He has kept things back from me that he's told other people, and I have kept things back from him that I've told no one, and we understand each other on this.

But I could see him working. His jaw was tight. He was thinking something through.

What he landed on was a thorn whip.

I want to be honest about my first reaction, which was: a thorn whip. Against a dragon that had just breathed frost on me and was now circling thirty feet overhead with contempt in every wingbeat. A thorn whip is something you use on things that are afraid of thorns. I was not confident this dragon was afraid of thorns.

Then it hit.

Not just hit — it was the kind of hit that lands so perfectly that even the thing being hit seems surprised. The whip caught Cryovain somewhere important and the dragon made a sound that was not the sound of something in contempt. It was the sound of something that had just been reminded it could be hurt.

Vukradin went, "I hit! I even got a critical!"

He said it the way you say something when you didn't quite believe it was possible and then it happened and now you have to reorganize your face around the new reality. I know that feeling. It's a good feeling.

"Crushed it!" he said.

I looked up at the dragon. I looked back at Vukradin. I still had the Dragon Slayer Sword. The frost was still settling somewhere in my chest. We had time — not much time, the window was counting down, the Carver was threading something dark into the edges of this fight, but we had *some* time — and Vukradin had just put a thorn through a white dragon's pride with a piece of vine.

I have seen a lot of things. That was a good one.
