## Valphine — The Ascent to Icespire Hold

The wind up here had a personality. That was the first thing I catalogued as we crested the final ridge — not the cold, not the scale, but the *character* of the thing pressing against us. In the Underdark, air is still. It sits in caverns and breathes slowly and tastes of stone and old water. Up here, the wind was something alive and contemptuous, a force that had been howling at this peak for ten thousand years before we arrived and would howl for ten thousand more once our bones were scattered down the mountainside.

I found it bracing. Life is pain. The wind understood this.

The clouds parted as we climbed, and Icespire Hold revealed itself in the way that grand things do — all at once, and slightly too large to process. Two stone structures joined by a bridge, clinging to the icy spur like a growth the mountain was in the process of rejecting. The fortress itself was not impressive. The mountain was. The fortress was just evidence that something small and determined had once said *here* and meant it, and the mountain had spent years quietly disagreeing. Crevices split the ancient stonework. Two corners had already surrendered to an old earthquake, collapsed into rubble fields that caught the snowdrift and held it. The stones looked grey and cold and tired.

Above all of it, the peak. A landmark so enormous it was visible from Phandalin on a clear day — from the *surface*, with its soft grey skies and weak diffused light that surface dwellers apparently find charming. Up here, even through the cloud cover, the light was another order entirely. It bounced off snow and ice and came at you from angles your eyes hadn't prepared for. It hit the pale stone of the fortress and came back *brighter*. It found the gaps between my eyelids when I blinked.

It was, as always, gloriously painful. I find Lathander's sense of humor quite good, actually.

The path question was practical. Two options: a narrow trail corkscrewing up the mountainside in the manner of something designed by someone who disliked people, or two hundred feet of sheer ice wall. Vukradin assessed this immediately. *"200 is more,"* he said, with the gravity of a man making a great tactical calculation. *"That's more. That's tough."*

I looked at the natural path and the unnatural wall and said what seemed obvious. *"Maybe we should just take the obvious path."*

Soma's voice carried a dry precision I've come to appreciate: *"That's what they'll be expecting."* Which is a reasonable tactical point, and I accepted it, though I note that the party making unexpected decisions has a somewhat irregular relationship with actually achieving surprise. Still. We were not only navigating a mountain — we were trying to catch a man performing a dark ritual over a dragon before he finished. The Carver's trail led directly to the smaller gatehouse. He followed the path, curiously enough, rather than carving through the mountain. Someone noted this was somewhat at odds with the name. The GM confirmed that even the Carver apparently lacked enthusiasm for two hundred feet of vertical ice.

*"If we come in from the side entrance,"* Soma said, *"we might actually avoid an encounter or two."*

*"Alright. Sold. Do it. Go for it,"* said Vukradin, with the efficient decisiveness of a man who has reached the end of his strategic analysis and discovered it agrees with the people around him.

And so: the wall. Soma assessed this with characteristic practicality, shifted into the form of a giant wolf spider — medium-sized, she was at pains to clarify later, not *rinky-dink* — and skittered up fifty feet of sheer ice. I watched from below. The shape of her against the pale stone was not what most would call reassuring, but Soma carries a quality of competence that translates even across species.

She dropped a rope.

Vukradin appeared to consider whether his illusion magic might somehow substitute for the rope. The GM explained, patiently, that it did not work that way. *"I can't do anything in this campaign,"* Vukradin said, in the tone of a man who has said this before and will say it again and does not entirely believe it.

We climbed. DC 7. We passed. The wind pulled at every handhold and the rope swayed against the ice and I thought about the phrase *gloriously painful* and found it apt. Every moment of that ascent felt like an offering — to Lathander, to the mountain, to whatever principle governs the proposition that difficult things are worth doing because they are difficult. I have never entirely understood the surface world's relationship to suffering. They want to avoid it. I have learned to read it instead: what it costs, what it reveals, what it opens.

The fortress interior answered several questions. Cryovain had smashed through doorways in what had clearly been a systematic search for anything interesting, found nothing, and departed. The orcs who came after had burned all the furnishings in the fireplace and left behind the evidence of their occupation in the most literal possible sense of the phrase. The smell preceded the sight.

Vukradin, making his way through the ruins, had a moment of political clarity. *"Oh, right! And then the dragon came and scared them all out, and then they had to go attack all the people. Right — we need to secure Icespire so the orcs can return safely."*

*"Eastern Homeland Reclaiming,"* said Soma, without expression.

*"Yes,"* the GM confirmed. *"You're all about the decolonization."*

I filed this away. It was not a joke, exactly — Vukradin rarely makes jokes that aren't also entirely sincere — but it was the kind of observation that sounds absurd in a ruin that smells like orc and old ash and somehow turns out to be the actual plan. Vukradin is one of the most predictable creatures I have ever observed and yet he never predicts anything I expect.

The secret door was mine.

I will not pretend false modesty about this. The fortress wall yielded a seam I caught where the others hadn't — a discovery of the kind that shifts the entire geometry of an approach, the kind that comes from a particular habit of attention. In the Underdark, hidden passages are not architectural curiosities. They are survival infrastructure. Every Drow child learns to read a wall the way surface children learn to read faces. The stone remembers where it was cut. I remember what stone looks like when it forgets to pretend.

Vukradin looked at the door and made the decision instantaneously. *"We're going to the dragon — it's right there, you just opened the door, it's right there."*

*"Let's go to the dragon,"* I said.

*"We're not fucking around,"* said Soma.

This is not a sentiment I expected to love as much as I do. Something about the flat pragmatism of it — the way it closes off all the interesting complications and replaces them with simple motion. Soma should have been a general in some other life, or possibly was.

What she saw from the rooftop: Cryovain immobilized within a glowing summoning circle, the Carver bent over the ritual, spectral chains holding the dragon against the stone. The report she brought back was careful and specific in the way that Soma's reports always are, shaped around the question that mattered. *"I mean, he's in chains. Are they real chains? Are they, like, spectral chains? Like, I worry that killing the carver or something would, like, make the chains evaporate."*

*"Well, we're gonna have to kill the carver regardless,"* said Vukradin.

*"I understand we're gonna have to. I would like to understand, like, you know — is he load-bearing on the dragon kind of thing."*

No one had an answer. The GM confirmed that Soma had no information to give us on this point beyond whatever we thought as players, which Soma correctly noted was irrelevant to what she thought as a druid on a frozen rooftop.

I proposed the obvious alternative calculus.

*"The enemy of my enemy is my friend."*

*"I don't think the dragon's gonna see it that way,"* said Soma.

*"When the enemy of my enemy is released, then my enemy does not have the resources he's looking for."*

It is a simple enough logic: a freed dragon is not a bound dragon. A Carver denied his prize is a Carver diminished. Whether the dragon would subsequently express gratitude or simply express its feelings through breath weapons was a separate problem. Vukradin appeared cautiously optimistic about the gratitude angle. Soma noted that the dragon did not, on inspection, appear to be a sane fella. I did not disagree. Sanity is not a prerequisite for tactical usefulness.

The wind came through the gaps in the ruined stone and pressed against us as we gathered at the threshold. The fortress was cold and broken and occupied by something vast and white and bound, and somewhere above us the mountain continued its ten-thousand-year argument with the sky, completely indifferent to what we were about to do.

I looked at the light catching on the ice walls through the arrow slits — bright, diffuse, relentless — and felt it in the way I always feel it.

Beautiful. Sharp. Exactly right.

## Vukradin — Bypassing the Gatehouse

The path up to the gatehouse was a narrow, corkscrewing thing that wound around the rock face like a sentence that couldn't find its ending. I looked at it. Then I looked at the other option.

"That appears to be the only safe path," I said. "What are the unsafe paths?"

The answer came back: natural path, treacherous; unnatural path, two hundred feet of sheer ice wall.

"200 is more," I said. "That's more. That's tough."

I am, above all things, a reasonable man.

From where we stood, you could see Cryovain on the rooftop of the main fortress — if you knew to look, if you knew what you were looking at. A white dragon sleeping on white snow, which is nature's way of saying *please do not find me.* I found it anyway, eventually, the shape of it resolving out of the mountain's blankness like a chord you've been hearing the whole time without knowing.

"Oh, you can see the dragon," I said. "The dragon has a name? Cryovain?"

It did. Of course it did. Everything has a name, and knowing the name is the first step toward a conversation, and a conversation is the first step toward resolution. I hold onto this. It has served me well.

What also had a name was our problem: the Carver had followed the path up. The ordinary path. Soma had checked — she has an eye for disturbance, for the shape something leaves behind when it passes through a space — and he had simply *walked up*, same as anyone.

"Yeah, I believe that," I said.

Soma seemed faintly surprised. "I just assumed he'd, like, carve a path through the mountain or something."

The GM informed us, dryly, that two hundred feet of straight ice was apparently not in the villain's plans either.

It was, I will admit, a small comfort. Even catastrophic evil has to find the steps.

---

We stood at the gatehouse junction and debated our entrance strategy the way musicians argue about key signatures — everyone convinced they are right, no one entirely wrong.

Valphine wanted the obvious path. "Maybe we should just take the obvious path."

Soma was already doing the tactical math. "That's what they'll be expecting. Aren't we trying to catch up with the carver, too? Like, isn't there a time schedule here?"

"The gatehouse just seems so obvious," Valphine said.

"If we come in from the side entrance, we might actually avoid an encounter or two."

I listened to both of them. I weighed the arguments. I considered the time pressure, the Carver somewhere above us, the dragon on the rooftop, the cold pressing in on all sides.

"Alright," I said. "Sold. Do it. Go for it."

This is the conductor's job: not to play every instrument, but to know when the piece is ready to move.

---

The side entrance required getting up a fifty-foot icy cliff face, which is where my most innovative contribution of the afternoon was, I will be honest, firmly rejected.

Soma had proposed Wild Shaping into a giant wolf spider to scale the wall and drop a rope. Entirely reasonable. But I had been thinking — and I want to be clear that I was thinking — and it seemed to me that I had a spell, the Flute of Illusions, something I could do that would allow a person to *perceive* that they had already seen whatever I decided. And if a person had already seen it, in their perception, then — 

"What if," I said, "and stick with me for this a minute — I have that spell that can make you see whatever I decide. You think it's real? So we can just have you experience that, and then you will have seen it."

The GM looked at me.

"No," he said. "It doesn't work that way."

I breathed.

"I can't do anything in this campaign."

I say this with some regularity. It is not self-pity. It is a statement of fact delivered in the tone of a man who believes that the next creative solution is always one idea away, and who is simply noting that this particular idea was not it.

Soma went up the cliff as a giant wolf spider. This is the thing about Soma — when I am busy being right in interesting ways, she is busy being right in effective ones. She skittered up the face of that ice without a sound, or tried to, and dropped a rope for the rest of us. 

I should say: I had expected something small. Inconspicuous. The phrase *giant wolf spider* had not fully registered.

"I'm a giant wolf spider," Soma said, from the top of the cliff, somewhat tartly. "*Not*... it's a medium-sized beast, not a... not a little rinky-dink spider."

"Why doesn't your stealth stat change when you're a spider?" I called up.

Then, because I had been sitting with this question for a moment and it seemed important: "Also, you have bardic inspiration."

She did. She used it. A 16 on stealth, which was enough. Whatever music I carry around inside me — whatever that warm current is that I can extend outward to people I trust — she took it up the cliff face with her, and it held.

---

The rope hung there against the ice. I grabbed it.

I don't know why this is when the song started. It may be that I am constitutionally incapable of doing something difficult without scoring it. It may be that the wind was making a sound that needed words. It may simply be that I had been holding a lot in since we first saw the dragon shape on the mountain, and the song was the valve.

"We need a good climbing song," I announced to the ice face, and to anyone within earshot. "*Up the ice, fire climbing, we will go—*"

A pause, while I hauled myself another few feet.

"*There's a rock slide!*"

I made it up. DC 7 on Athletics. Perfectly respectable. The song helped.

---

The fortress interior was a negotiation with ruin. Two corners had collapsed years ago — earthquake, apparently, though a building that has also hosted a dragon for several seasons could be excused for having structural feelings — and the rubble lay in great heaps that required careful navigation. The stone shifted underfoot. The air was cold in the way that abandoned places are cold, which is a different quality of cold than weather.

We passed through a hallway of arrow slits, the outer wall marked by something enormous having pushed through a doorway that had not been sized for something enormous. Cryovain, searching for prey, finding nothing, moving on. The scale of it. The casual obliteration of a wall, the way you might push through a curtain.

I tried not to think about the shape that had made that gap, sleeping fifty feet above our heads on a rooftop.

Then the orc room.

I will tell you about the orc room because it matters to me. Every piece of furniture had been burned in the fireplace. Not for warmth, or not only for warmth — for desperation, for the particular mathematics of survival that says *the chair goes in when there is nothing else left.* The filth was months old. The stench was the smell of people with nowhere else to go.

It clicked into place, the way things do when you already had the pieces and just hadn't assembled them.

"Oh, right!" I said. "And then the dragon came and scared them all out, and then they had to go attack all the people. Right, we need to secure Icespire so the orcs can return safely."

Soma looked at me.

"Eastern Homeland Reclaiming," she said.

"Yes," the GM agreed, "I mean, you're all about the decolonization."

They were laughing, a little. That's fine. They were also right. And so was I.

---

Valphine found the door.

She had been moving through the room with that particular elven attention that she deploys when she wants you to know she's paying attention, and she rolled a 29 on her Insight and there it was — a hidden door, a way further in.

"Sadly," I said, "it was a DC30. You missed it."

"Dude," said the GM, "I'm a bad man, but I'm not that bad."

Valphine opened the door.

And this is where the last of the deliberation burned away, because Soma had already been up, already scouted, already come back with her quiet face and her quiet words:

*Oh boy, so, there's a dragon down there that's just kind of being held down. Alright.*

Held. In chains — real or spectral, we hadn't confirmed — by the Carver, who was up on that rooftop with his obsidian sword and his summoning circle, doing whatever it is that people do when they have decided to bind a white dragon to their will and burn a town.

The door was open.

"We're going to the Dragon," I said. "It's right there. You just opened the door. It's right there."

"Let's go to the dragon," Valphine said.

"We're not fucking around," said Soma.

No. We were not.

I had one more thought before we moved, the one I held onto like a descant under everything else. The dragon was chained. Immobilized. And the Carver had to die regardless — Soma had said so, I had said so, we all agreed on this — but what came *after*, that was the interesting part.

"What I see is the dragon is immobilized until we deal with the carver," I said. And then, because it seemed worth saying: "Actually, after we save the dragon, the dragon may be very grateful."

Soma gave me a look that contained several paragraphs.

"I mean, he's in chains. Are they real chains? Are they, like, spectral chains? Like, I worry that killing the carver or something would, like, make the chains evaporate."

"Well," I said, "we're gonna have to kill the carver regardless."

"I understand we're gonna have to. I would like to understand, like, you know, is he load-bearing on the dragon kind of thing."

Valphine offered: "The enemy of my enemy is my friend."

"I don't think the dragon's gonna see it that way," said Soma.

"When the enemy of my enemy is released," Valphine said, patient as water finding a path, "then my enemy does not have the resources he's looking for."

I listened to all of this. I kept Soma's question in the part of my mind that holds the complicated notes, the ones that haven't resolved yet. *Is he load-bearing on the dragon.*

The honest answer was: we didn't know.

The only way to find out was through the door.

## Soma — Soma Scouts the Roof

The wind at the top of Ice Spire Peak has opinions. It pushes back. It finds every joint between the stones and turns them into knives.

I did not consider this when I shifted into spider form.

Eight legs, yes. Sure grip on vertical surfaces, yes. The ambient warmth of a small creature tucked against cold stone — no. I am a medium-sized wolf spider, which means I am approximately the size of a large dog, clinging to the outer wall of a fortress, in full view of anyone who happens to look up. Vukradin had watched me begin the climb with the particular expression he wears when he is revising his expectations. "Why doesn't your stealth stat change when you're a spider?" I did not answer him. I was already moving. He reminded me about the bardic inspiration as I went. I took it. I have been in this world long enough to take what is offered.

The stone was ice-rimed and uneven. My legs found purchase in the cracks. A small spider could have crossed this wall invisible, a dark fleck against grey rock. I was not a small spider. I was a wolf spider the size of a large dog, crossing a fortress wall at Ice Spire Peak, with a natural three on my stealth check and a bard's borrowed luck standing between me and discovery.

Shell sprouts worry about what they might find. I worried about whether I would survive long enough to find it.

I reached the top. I eased myself over the lip of the wall — slowly, slowly — and looked down.

There are things you can prepare yourself to see. I had known there was a dragon. I had known there was a ritual. I had known the Carver had gone somewhere beyond ordinary madness. These were facts I carried up the wall with me, the way you carry provisions: this is what I know, this is what I expect.

I was not prepared.

Cryovain is enormous. Even sleeping, even chained — and it was not sleeping, I understood that immediately — the scale of the creature is something that hits you in the body before your mind can process it. A white dragon, its scales almost indistinguishable from the snow that dusts every surface of this wretched mountain, and it was *held*. Chains, or what looked like chains, glowing with something that pulsed between cold light and nothing. Spectral, maybe. Real in the way magic is real — which is to say, I could see them, but I could not have told you what they were made of. The summoning circle beneath the dragon blazed faintly, its geometry intricate and deliberate, and in the center of all of it: the Carver.

He was deep in something. Head bowed, body rigid, entirely absorbed in whatever he was doing. The obsidian sword in his grip — I noticed the sword. I noticed it the way you notice something that is looking back at you. Sickly purple light, rhythmic, like a heartbeat that isn't his. The ritual was not his either, I thought. Not really.

But I did not know that yet. I only had the feeling.

And the dragon.

I am a druid. I have spent decades learning to read creatures — to understand what they are communicating in their posture, their breath, their eyes. It is not a gift. It is practice. Attention, compounded over time.

Cryovain's eyes were open and they were wrong.

Not subdued. Not sleeping. The animal handling part of my brain, the part that has never been wrong about this particular category of thing, said: *that creature is in a state of psychotic fury and is being prevented from acting on it by something external*. The chains. The circle. The ritual. Whatever the Carver was doing, it was not calming the dragon. It was *containing* a dragon that wanted, with every cell of its enormous body, to destroy everything within reach.

I looked at that dragon for as long as I dared.

Then I climbed back down.

Vukradin's face when I dropped back into wolf spider form at the base of the wall and then shifted back to tortle was the face of someone who has been waiting for bad news and is not surprised to receive it. I gave it to him plainly.

"Oh boy, so, there's a dragon down there that's just kind of being held down. Alright."

They wanted to know about the chains. Whether they were real. Whether they would hold if the Carver was dead, or evaporate the moment someone put a blade through him and leave us with an enraged white dragon and no ritual containing it.

"I mean, he's in chains. Are they real chains? Are they, like, spectral chains? Like, I worry that killing the carver or something would, like, make the chains evaporate."

Vukradin said we would have to kill the Carver regardless. Yes. I know. That was not what I was asking.

"I understand we're gonna have to. I would like to understand, like, you know, is he load-bearing on the dragon kind of thing."

The question hung there. None of us had an answer. I had tried an Arcana check from the wall — tried to make sense of the circle, the chains, the pulsing light — and gotten nothing. The magic was beyond me, or I was beyond the angle to read it, or my mind was too busy cataloging the dragon's fury to parse the geometry of the ritual. The GM was cheerfully honest about it. *You got nothing. Other than whatever you think as a player, you got nothing.*

That was fair.

Valphine looked at this problem from the angle she looks at every problem. The enemy of my enemy is my friend. She wanted to negotiate with the dragon. Release it. Let it attack the Carver while we handled the sword.

"I don't think the dragon's gonna see it that way."

She made her case — that the dragon freed was no longer the Carver's resource, that stripping his power was the strategic objective.

I did not change my position. What I had seen on that rooftop was not a creature that would distinguish between its captor and the people who freed it. It would not feel gratitude. It would not calculate alliances. It would breathe and fly and destroy, and the only difference between being under the Carver's thumb and being near a released Cryovain was the direction of the threat.

And the Carver himself.

I thought about his face as I had seen it from the wall. The way he was absorbed in the ritual — not performing it, but *consumed* by it. Sister Kaella had warned us. I had not needed the warning. The man I had seen on that rooftop had not looked like a man who had made choices and arrived at a destination. He had looked like a man who had been *taken* somewhere, and whatever had taken him was still in the process of taking.

"Yeah, he doesn't look like a sane fella."

That was all I said. It was all there was to say.

We went to the door.

## Brewbarry — Confrontation at the Summoning Circle

The rooftop hit like a wall of cold.

I came through that corridor moving fast — rage already burning in my chest, that hot clean fire that sweeps away everything except forward. The Carver was *there*. Right there on the battlements above Icespire Hold with his obsidian sword and his chains of light and his stolen dragon, and I was going to reach him and that was going to be the end of it.

Except the walls didn't care.

The architecture of the fortress — stone corners, jutting battlements, the routing of the rooftop — put itself between me and the man like it was doing him a personal favor. I charged and I charged and I ended up somewhere that was not next to him, breathing hard and burning hot and empty-handed. The Carver stood thirty feet away looking like nothing I'd done had mattered at all.

And then — as though the walls weren't insult enough — he moved.

Smooth. Unhurried. The kind of movement that shouldn't be possible for a man who'd just been performing a ritual, who should have been exhausted and exposed. He used his legendary action to disengage and repositioned out of the Silence zone without a single swing provoked, without a single step I could interrupt. Soma's voice cut through the cold air behind me: *"Cheat bastard."* And then, because Soma is Soma: *"Cheating bastard, yay, with his legendary actions. Not you."*

Not me. Right. I stood where the Carver had been and there was nothing in my hands but air.

Vukradin fixed it. He always fixes it in his way.

*"Hold Person."*

And the Carver's body locked rigid — every muscle seized, the man frozen on the rooftop like a statue someone had left out in the Icespire cold. For a moment the whole rooftop seemed to exhale. Vukradin shouted his joy to the mountains: *"He is paralyzed! Excellent! Well done, team!"* 

I felt it too. That release. That was over. That was done. We'd stopped the ritual, we'd stopped the man, now we find the sword and we smash it and Phandalin doesn't burn and Vukradin plays his music and everything is fine.

*"Yes, he casts Dominate Person."*

Vukradin: *"He's paralyzed."*

*"Yeah, he is. The sword isn't."*

I didn't understand it at first. I think none of us did. The Carver's body was standing still as stone, arms at his sides, face blank, completely unable to act — and somehow the magical chains binding Cryovain were dissolving anyway. The obsidian sword pulsed with cold purple light. Not reflecting it. Generating it. The sword was doing this. The sword had always been doing this.

And then the voice came.

It didn't come from the frozen man. It didn't come from the dragon. It came from inside my skull, quiet and certain and completely without mercy:

*"Coward's magic. You mute the ritual because you lack the strength to shatter the vessel. And now — now I shall have everything I always wanted."*

Soma's voice, confused: *"The dragon says that?"*

*"You hear that in your heads."*

In your heads. In mine. In all of ours. Cold as the Icespire wind, contemptuous as a man who has already counted his winnings and only stayed at the table to watch you lose. The sword knew us. It had watched us come up through that corridor and it had let Vukradin cast his spell and it had let the man's body go rigid and it had done all of this while already knowing none of it mattered. Paralyzing a vessel doesn't stop what's inside it.

The purple glow faded from the blade then. Just a plain black sword. All its power was elsewhere now — poured into Cryovain, flowing through the chains as they broke apart, flowing into the dragon's mind.

Vukradin said it slowly, working it out: *"The carver... the sword... the... oh. The carver is the sword."*

Soma: *"Yeah. The human is just a vessel."*

Everything I'd been raging toward. Everything I'd been charging at and couldn't reach and had spent this whole fight trying to get close enough to hit. The man was never the enemy. The man was just a body the enemy wore. And I'd been stopped by walls and legendary actions and the geometry of rooftops — stopped from reaching something that wouldn't have mattered even if I'd reached it.

My rage doesn't think. That's the point of it. But the place behind my rage, the part that watches — that part felt something cold that had nothing to do with the Icespire wind.

Then Vukradin's voice cut through it, simple and direct and aimed right at me:

*"Crewbury. Kill the dragon."*

Three rounds, the GM had said. Three rounds before the sword had full control of Cryovain. Three rounds to kill a white dragon on a windswept rooftop at the top of the world before it became something permanently, catastrophically worse.

He trusts me to do what I do. I won't explain what that means to a goliath who was exiled from his tribe for failing his watch drunk and who has been trying to be worth something ever since. I won't explain it because I barely have words for it myself. He pointed at the dragon and said my name — wrong, always wrong, always *Crewbury* — and that was enough.

Valphine hit first, Guiding Bolt lancing into white scales and leaving Cryovain briefly exposed. Soma had given up her own turn, positioned herself to give me advantage, set me up to land what she needed me to land. I drew the Dragon Slayer Sword — the one that had been waiting in my hand for exactly this moment — and I charged Cryovain the way I'd charged the Carver, except this time there were no walls.

Two blows. The first with Valphine's advantage behind it, the blade biting deep into white scales. The second savage and clean. Forty-three damage total, the dragon-slaying sword doing what someone made it to do, me doing what Vukradin needed me to do.

Soma's voice, dry and satisfied: *"That was worth me sacking around for."*

Vukradin had been grinning. *"21 and 22? Pretty good."*

Pretty good. Yeah.

Then Cryovain looked at me.

The breath came out in a cone of freezing white — thirty feet of ice and death pouring over me like the mountain itself had exhaled. My rage carries me through a lot. It does not carry me through cold. Stone's Endurance blunted some of it, the goliath blood in me absorbing what it could, but thirty points of freezing damage is thirty points of freezing damage and I felt every one of them settle into my bones like debt.

The dragon beat its wings — once, twice — and lifted thirty feet into the air above the battlements. 

I stood there. Ice-rimed. Burning inside where the rage still lived, frozen outside where the breath had hit. Dragon Slayer Sword in my hand, pointing at an enemy now floating thirty feet above my head. 

Vukradin's voice, rueful: *"I got nothing, guys. Other than Fireball."*

A beat.

Soma: *"You're actually gonna use it?"*

*"Nope."*

The beads are his. He'd said it himself, back when we were handing things out: *"You have my sword, you have my halberd, the beads are yours."* That was the deal. That was the trust. Vukradin would rather stand on a rooftop beneath a hovering dragon with nothing in his arsenal than break what he'd given his word on.

I understood that. I've always understood that about him. It's the same reason I followed him.

I stared up at thirty feet of open air between me and the dragon. The most dangerous weapon I own, useless. The clock running. Two rounds left.

Then Vukradin's thorny lash cracked upward — somehow, impossibly — and struck. A critical hit. Twenty-six damage torn from the airborne beast, the bard who allegedly can't do anything in this campaign reaching up through the sky to prove himself wrong.

Soma's voice behind me, delighted: *"Way to go, face!"*

Vukradin: *"Crushed it!"*

A lifeline, thin as a vine. The dragon still in the air. Still above me. Still running out of the time the sword needed to finish its work.

And somewhere on the rooftop, in the paralyzed body of a man who had never really been the enemy, the black sword that had called itself the Carver waited to see if we were fast enough.

## Valphine — Battle Against Cryovain

The first blow was mine.

I want to be precise about that, because what followed consumed everything else in noise and cold and blood, and it would be easy to lose the shape of how it began. The dragon had climbed free of the ritual circle — the sword's doing, not the man's, and I had already filed that distinction away before the first wing-beat shook the stone — and the party looked at one another with the particular expression surface dwellers wear when they realize the situation has changed faster than their thinking. I was watching Cryovain instead. There is a kind of aesthetic purity to something that large and that angry and that white. The Underdark has its own grandeur — the great caverns, the blind horror of the deep things — but it is a grandeur of darkness. This was a grandeur of killing cold, of scale, of something that genuinely did not care whether any of us survived the next thirty seconds.

I found that clarifying.

Lathander's light does not feel like comfort, whatever the hymns say. It feels like staring into the sun until your eyes ache and your vision burns white at the edges and you think: *yes, this is the thing that makes the world visible.* It feels like pain worn as a crown. I raised my hand, and I let the divine current move through me — the deep channel that I drilled into myself during those first savage months on the surface, learning the god of the dawn through the only method I actually trusted, which was to pour the power outward and observe what it did — and I sent Guiding Bolt into the white dragon's chest.

Fifteen points of radiant fire. A mark of blazing light left burning across pale scales so that anyone striking after me would find the target already lit, the creature's defenses momentarily stripped by the divine residue of Lathander's attention.

There is something I notice when I do this — something I have not said to the others because it would take too long to explain and they would find it strange. A former servant of Lolth, channeling a dawn-god's radiant fire against a white dragon on a frozen mountaintop. In the Underdark, this sentence would be incoherent. A drow priestess does not use divine light offensively — she uses it to remind the creatures below that they are always being watched from above, which is a form of cruelty rather than grace. Lathander's light is something different. It illuminates rather than surveils. The pain of it moves outward, not downward.

I do not know what to call this feeling. I am not certain I need to.

Brewbarry went in next. He saw the mark I had left — the shimmer of divine fire still tracing the edges of Cryovain's scales — and charged with that particular barbarian certainty that motion is always preferable to stillness. The Dragon Slayer sword caught the beast where I had lit it, and the first blow landed with advantage, and the second followed with a precision that surprised me in a goliath in a rage state: twenty-one, then twenty-two, forty-three points of total damage carved into a white dragon's hide. Soma, watching from the wall where she had scrambled over the battlement in spider form and was even now beginning the shudder of a second wild shape, caught my eye for a moment across the chaos.

She had staked her last shapeshifting reserve on this fight. I noted that without comment. It is the kind of information that becomes relevant.

The dragon's response was educational.

Thirty feet of freezing cone. The temperature dropped the way a door opens — abruptly, totally, without transition — and ice crystallized across Brewbarry's body in patterns that were, in a purely aesthetic sense, quite beautiful. Forty points, reduced by Stone's Endurance but not by rage, because rage does not negotiate with elemental cold. Brewbarry staggered. The goliath's breath turned to frost in the air.

Then Cryovain beat its wings and climbed.

Thirty feet up. Hovering. The pale shape against the pale clouds, close enough to breathe down on us, far enough to deny the fighters their reach. I watched it positioning and understood exactly what it was doing and why, which made Soma's dry observation — *a lot of thinking in his enraged state* — land with more precision than she probably intended. The dragon had been dominated. It was following something's tactical calculus, and that something was the sword, and the sword was cold and contemptuous and had already announced it would have everything it always wanted.

Three rounds. The sword's domination was not yet absolute. That was the window.

Vukradin pressed himself against the six-foot stone wall and looked up at the dragon and looked at his hands and looked at the necklace around his neck and conducted, in the space of three seconds, the most compressed ethical crisis I have witnessed in the field.

"I got nothing, guys. Other than Fireball."

I said nothing. I was watching him.

"You're actually gonna use it?"

"Nope."

Seven fire bead charges and a white dragon thirty feet overhead, and the bard chose the necklace's rightful owner over tactical pragmatism. Surface dwellers are, as I have noted, endlessly predictable. What I find less predictable is how often Vukradin's particular variety of predictability produces outcomes that are not disastrous.

He reached for Thorn Whip instead.

The vine snapped outward, impossible in this cold and altitude, driven by whatever magical current a bard of Vukradin's instincts can summon when cornered — and it struck. Not just struck. A critical hit, the thorned rope finding something vital, and twenty-six points of damage tore into the dragon's face while Vukradin's triumphant "I hit! I even got a critical!" rang across the battlements like he'd won a bet he'd had no right placing.

"Way to go, face," Soma said.

"Crushed it," Vukradin said, with the particular satisfaction of a man who has done the impressive thing using entirely the wrong tool.

The cold had not let up. The dragon was still above us. The sword was still counting down toward full possession of a creature that could level Phandalin from the air.

I had opened this fight with Lathander's mark, and the mark had held, and Brewbarry's strikes had bitten deep, and we were still standing in the cold on the top of a fortress on the side of a mountain with the clock running.

I found I was not afraid.

That is the part I keep returning to. The scale of the threat, the tactical disarray, the freezing air and the dominated dragon and the countdown I could feel pressing against the back of my teeth — and beneath all of it, something that was not comfort, not confidence, but something older than either. The light burned where I'd placed it. That was mine. Whatever happened next, the first blow had already landed, and it had been radiant, and it had found its mark.

Life is pain. How gloriously it had already begun.

## Vukradin — The Sword Speaks

The moment the Silence fell over that summoning circle, I felt it — that specific warmth that rises when a plan lands clean. Twenty feet of pure quiet, dropped exactly where it needed to be, and the Carver's ritual words died in his throat. I may have announced this somewhat loudly.

"Interrupting the ritual! I am saving the world! Huzzah!"

"They were intervening, you interventionist."

Soma said it without even looking up. Something about the dry precision of it knocked the wind out of my jubilation — not because she was wrong, exactly, but because she was too right. She has this gift for finding the one word that names the thing you weren't examining. I let it land and said nothing for a moment, which is unusual for me.

But the logic was sound. The Carver needed his voice, the ritual needed his voice, and now he had neither. I filed "interventionist" away as a philosophical discussion for later and focused on the fact that we were, demonstrably, winning.

He used his legendary action to simply walk out of the silence. Not fight through it. Not argue against it. Just stepped outside the radius like a man leaving a room he found boring, and the magical quiet that had cost me a spell slot and a great deal of personal satisfaction became a twenty-foot circle of pointless nothing.

"Cheat bastard," said Soma.

I agreed with the sentiment if not quite the phrasing. But I was already recalculating — and here is the thing about me that I think people sometimes miss: I am not mechanical in my tactics the way Brewbarry is mechanical in his violence, but I understand timing. A musician has to. The ritual was disrupted but not destroyed; the Carver was repositioning; the question was what comes next before he can begin again.

Hold Person. Obviously.

The Carver's human body locked rigid the moment the spell took hold — mid-stride, mid-breath, mid-whatever-terrible-thing-he-was-in-the-process-of-doing. I let the Silence dissolve; concentration divided is concentration wasted. The important thing was that the enemy was frozen.

"He is paralyzed! Excellent! Well done, team!"

And I meant every word of it. The party had done this. Soma's scouting, Valphine's cold-eyed tactical thinking, Brewbarry being Brewbarry with his beautiful enormous sword. We had walked onto the rooftop of Icespire Hold and we had — in two rounds — completely neutralized the greatest threat to Phandalin, possibly the entire North. I stood there feeling the particular satisfaction of a piece of music where everything resolves at once, every dissonance finding its note.

"He's paralyzed," I said again, because it bore repeating.

"Yeah," said the GM. "He is. The sword isn't."

I heard the words in the order they were delivered. I understood each one individually. I did not, for a full three seconds, understand what they meant together.

The sword raised itself. Not the man — the man was locked in place, a statue of frozen hostility. The sword, the obsidian blade I had registered as equipment, as an accessory, as the thing the Carver happened to be carrying — the sword moved on its own, and the chains that had bound Cryovain to the ritual circle dissolved like smoke in wind.

The dragon was free.

The dragon was free because we had paralyzed the wrong thing.

"Yes, he casts Dominate Person."

The voice arrived in my head and I cannot adequately describe what that feels like. It does not come through the ears; it arrives already fully formed, already inside the thought-space where your own voice lives, and it is cold in a way that cold air is not cold, in a way that reminds you there are things in the world that are older than warmth.

*"Coward's magic. You mute the ritual because you lack the strength to shatter the vessel. And now — now I shall have everything I always wanted."*

"The dragon says that?" said Soma.

"You hear that in your heads."

Soma's voice had been grounded and dry. The voice in my skull was neither. It was contemptuous in the way that mountains are contemptuous — not because they have decided to be, but because they simply do not register the distinction between you and the snow. It called my Silence coward's magic, and I will confess, in the privacy of this account, that the word landed somewhere.

Not because it was right. It wasn't right. Silence is elegant, precise, the correct tool deployed at the correct moment — but there are people, and apparently swords, who will look at a musician and decide that the instrument is the lesser weapon because it does not cut. I have been having this argument my entire career, and usually I have the advantage of being able to talk back.

Inside my own skull, I could not respond.

The pieces assembled themselves slowly, which is unusual for me. I process language quickly; I have to, in performance, in negotiation, in the thousand moments where the right word at the right instant is the difference between harmony and catastrophe. But this — the sword had been the Carver all along. The man had been — what was the word Soma used, straight and quiet —

"The human is just a vessel."

The human. Not the Carver. The Carver was the sword. The Carver had always been the sword.

"The carver... the sword... the... oh, the carver is the sword."

I heard myself say it and felt, as I said it, the specific vertigo of a musician who realizes mid-performance that the piece is in a different key than they prepared for. We had been planning to kill a man who was not a man, or rather — a man who was not the mind behind any of it. The mind was older and colder and had just announced, through the architecture of my own thoughts, that everything was proceeding exactly as it wished.

Three rounds. Three rounds before Dominate locked the dragon completely, and Cryovain — psychotic, enraged, Cryovain who was not our ally and was never going to be our ally — would be fully under the sword's control.

"Alright," I said, and I heard my own voice land differently than it usually does. "That's fine. Let's kill a dragon. It'll be fine."

Even as I said it, I was looking at the dragon, and the dragon was looking at everything with an expression that I can only describe as *comprehensive homicidal intent*, and I thought: we are going to talk to it. We can talk to it. There has to be —

"We're gonna talk to it."

"Go, guys, don't, don't unlock the dragon," said Soma.

These were perhaps the most sensible words spoken on that rooftop. I filed them away under *considerations*.

"Didn't you always want to kill a dragon?" I said instead, turning toward Valphine. "Isn't this, like, a rite of passage for the director?" I was performing conviction harder than I was feeling it, which is a technique I have used in concert halls when the acoustics are wrong and you need the audience to hear confidence even when you're standing in a dead zone. It works better in concert halls.

But morale is real. Morale is tactically real. "Crewbury, kill the dragon."

Brewbarry required no further direction. He never does. This is one of the things I genuinely love about him — he receives an assignment and executes it with a completeness that I find aesthetically satisfying, the way a well-struck chord is satisfying, full and clear and leaving nothing out. The Dragon Slayer Sword caught Cryovain twice, and the numbers were beautiful, and Soma said *that was worth me sacking around for*, and for a moment — just a moment — I felt good again.

Then the dragon breathed.

Forty points of cold damage is a number that communicates itself immediately even to a bard who was not on the receiving end of it. Brewbarry's rage doesn't care about cold; the cold doesn't care about Brewbarry's rage. He stood in it and the frost settled over him and he was still standing, because Brewbarry, but the dragon launched itself thirty feet into the air on the next beat and hovered there, above the battlements, above the reach of most things a person standing on a rooftop might reasonably do to it.

I surveyed my options.

I surveyed them thoroughly.

There was the Necklace of Fireballs, which belongs to Brewbarry, which I carry, which I have considered exactly once and concluded I will not use. This is not a difficult position to hold. The beads are his; the choice was made; it is settled. Even hovering thirty feet up, breathing frost and murder, the dragon did not constitute a compelling argument for reconsidering.

"I got nothing, guys. Other than Fireball."

"You're actually gonna use it?"

"Nope."

There was a pause in which I believe Soma reevaluated her assessment of my decision-making, and I felt the particular pressure of a room — or a rooftop — waiting for me to act.

I had Thorn Whip. I had Thorn Whip and thirty feet of dragon between me and any meaningful contribution to the next sixty seconds of fighting. It is a cantrip. It is not, in most assessments, a dragon-killing instrument. It is the musical equivalent of a recorder — functional, foundational, not what you bring to the finale.

I brought it to the finale.

The vine cracked across the air and I felt the roll happen in the way rolls sometimes happen, that instant of physical certainty before you look at the number, and the number was twenty, natural, the dice extending something generous in a moment that badly needed it.

"I hit! I even got a critical!"

Twenty-six damage. Against a dragon, thirty feet up, that I had just announced I could do nothing against.

"Way to go, face!" said Soma.

"Crushed it!"

I said it loud enough to hear myself say it, which is not always a thing I do. But there is a specific joy in the impossible cantrip that lands — in the instrument you weren't supposed to use being the one that works — and I am not going to apologize for experiencing it fully.

The joy lasted approximately four seconds, which is how long it took the GM to adjust the Carver's standing stats and for me to register that the thing which had just spoken inside my skull was now looking, through the dragon's ascending fury and the obsidian gleam of its own blade, considerably more capable than it had seemed when it was pretending to be a man.

"I was feeling good," I said. "I'm feeling less good."

The round closed. The clock stood at two.

## Soma — The Dragon Unbound

The moment we crested the rooftop, I knew what kind of creature was waiting for us. I've spent forty-five years learning how to read living things — the cant of a shoulder, the sound of breathing, the particular stillness that means something is coiled instead of calm. Cryovain wasn't still. Cryovain was a white-hot knot of fury wrapped in scales and chains, and the chains were the only reason any of us were still drawing breath.

The Carver stood at the center of a summoning circle, obsidian sword at his side, channeling something dark and patient into the bound dragon. The chains looked spectral. I noticed that immediately. Not iron. Not rope. Something woven from the ritual itself.

Which meant the question nobody was asking was: what holds when the ritual breaks?

I was still turning this over when Vukradin acted.

"Interrupting the ritual! I am saving the world! Huzzah!"

I said, "They were intervening, you interventionist."

He said, "Good job, team! You did it."

He meant it. That's the thing about the kid — he always means it. Forty-five years of watching the world, and I have never met anyone so genuinely delighted by his own heroism. Shell sprout. Loveable, well-intentioned, possibly going to get us killed shell sprout. The Silence had cut the ritual off, yes. But the fight hadn't started. The dragon was still chained. The Carver was still standing. Whatever "it" was, we had not done it.

I turned my attention to the dragon.

Animal handling is not magic, exactly. It's older than magic. It's listening to something that doesn't speak in words — the flare of a nostril, the particular way a creature holds its neck when it's calculating whether you're prey. I've read frightened horses and wounded bears and things with no name in any bestiary I've ever found. I pushed out with that same sense toward Cryovain.

What came back was like putting your hand on a hot iron.

Not fear. Not even aggression. Something past both of those. The dragon wasn't looking at us. It wasn't looking at anything. It was simply *raging*, in the pure, sustained, structural way that some creatures do when everything that was them has been stripped down to a single burning point. This wasn't a creature you could reason with. This wasn't a creature you could free and call an ally.

"It's not happy," I said, which was the flattest possible way to describe what I'd felt, but the alternatives would have taken too long.

The GM — the GM describes it more fully. *Seething psychotic rage*, those were the words. *It will just attack everything around.*

"Go, guys, don't, don't unlock the dragon."

Vukradin said, "We're gonna talk to it."

I did not have the energy to explain why that was not going to work. I filed it under *things I already knew were going to go wrong* and moved on.

Then the Carver moved.

Legendary action. No attacks of opportunity. Gone.

"Cheat bastard," I said. Then, because I realized how that might land: "Cheating bastard, yay, with his legendary actions. Not you."

The word *legendary* is doing enormous work here. A legendary action that amounts to *free movement*. I've seen more impressive things done by migrating geese. Legendary, the opposite end of loot on the rarity scale. Common action with a glamorous name.

I was still composing this observation when Vukradin solved the problem the way Vukradin always solves problems: by being right, loudly, and then being wrong immediately after.

"Hold Person."

The Carver went rigid. Paralyzed.

"He is paralyzed! Excellent! Well done, team!"

I felt something loosen in my chest. The chains were spectral — ritual magic — and the ritual was silenced and the caster was frozen. Maybe I'd been wrong. Maybe this was going to end cleanly.

"Yes," said the GM. "He casts Dominate Person."

Vukradin blinked. "He's paralyzed."

"Yeah, he is. The sword isn't."

The chains dissolved.

I want to be precise about what happened next, because the sequence matters. The chains dissolved. The dragon was freed. And then a voice arrived inside my skull — not spoken, not heard through ears, but deposited there, cold and absolute, the way ice forms:

*"Coward's magic, you mute the ritual because you lack the strength to shatter the vessel. And now, now I shall have everything I always wanted."*

I said, "The dragon says that?"

Because my first instinct — even then, even with everything in front of me — was to check. Was that Cryovain? Was the dragon talking? Was there something in this frozen psychotic creature that could still form words?

"You hear that in your heads."

Not the dragon. The sword.

I ran the arcana check. Thirteen. Enough.

*The real Carver was the sword.*

Vukradin was working through it out loud: "The carver... the sword... the... oh, the carver is the sword."

"Yeah," I said. "The human is just a vessel."

The human and the sword had been the vessel together. The sword had been performing a ritual to consume the dragon — not bind it, *consume* it. Absorb it entirely. We'd interrupted that with Silence, and so it had pivoted to Dominate as a fallback. Smaller. More immediate. But enough.

The architecture of the threat had been wrong in our heads from the beginning. We'd been planning to kill the man. The man had never mattered.

I said, "Wait a minute, can we go wail on the sword?"

The sword that had been glowing purple was now just black obsidian. Dormant-looking. The power had already moved.

Vukradin said, "Alright, that's fine, let's kill a dragon. It'll be fine."

Three rounds. That was the window the GM gave us. The Dominate wasn't complete — the sword didn't have full control yet. Three rounds to bring down a dragon before the leash tightened.

I said, "Now that I've seen a dragon, I can wild-shape into a dragon."

Nobody laughed, but the joke was for me anyway. Dark humor is how I handle the moments between *understanding the problem* and *surviving it*.

I was still in spider form — eight eyes, functionally blind, which is a design flaw I have strong opinions about — and there was a wall between me and the dragon. I climbed it. Then I burned my second Wild Shape charge and became a bear.

No attacks this round. Everything spent on positioning.

"Yeah, sorry, I'm blind in spider form, I've got 8 eyes and none of them work."

Valphine cast Guiding Bolt — fifteen radiant points and a gift to whoever swung next. Brewbarry, raging, Dragon Slayer Sword in hand, landed two strikes. Twenty-one. Twenty-two. Forty-three total damage against a creature that most weapons barely scratch.

"That was worth me sacking around for."

I meant it plainly. I'd given up my turn to be standing in the right place, and Brewbarry had done what Brewbarry does when you aim him correctly. The math was clean. The sacrifice was worth it.

Then the dragon breathed.

Forty points of cold. Brewbarry standing in the cone, failing the save. Stone's Endurance helped. Rage didn't — cold isn't slashing, cold isn't piercing, the barbarian's defenses don't know what to do with ice. He took it. He stayed standing. But he took it.

Cryovain flew thirty feet straight up.

Vukradin looked at the airborne dragon. Looked at his options. Said, "I got nothing, guys. Other than Fireball."

I said, "You're actually gonna use it?"

"Nope."

Seven beads on that necklace. One of them would have brought the dragon down right there. But they belong to Brewbarry, and Vukradin has a principle about that, and Vukradin's principles do not bend for dragons.

I have lived forty-five years. I have never once met anyone like him.

Then he cast Thorn Whip — a vine, lashing upward at a hovering dragon — and rolled a critical hit.

"I hit! I even got a critical! Twenty-six damage!"

"Way to go, face!" I said, and I meant that too. Every point counted. The vine cracked against scales thirty feet in the air and the bard who can't do anything in this campaign crushed it, briefly, against all reasonable expectation.

The GM explained the dragon's positioning — within breath range, out of melee reach, calculated distances. The dragon had noticed we had no ranged weapons. It was staying just close enough to hurt us.

"A lot of thinking," I said, "in his enraged state."

Because that was the contradiction sitting in the middle of everything. The dragon was in psychotic, seething, beyond-reason rage — I'd felt that with my own hands, my own Animal Handling, my 24 — and yet here it was, doing math. Noticing our equipment. Choosing an altitude.

The sword. Not the dragon. The sword was already steering.

One round down. Two to go.

## Brewbarry — Dragon Slayer Strikes

The rooftop hits me like a wall of cold the moment I clear the stairs.

Wind. Ice. The sky too big and too close at the same time. And in the middle of all of it — the dragon. Cryovain. White as the mountain, chained in something that isn't quite light and isn't quite shadow, thrashing against bonds that hum with whatever the Carver put into them. I've seen big animals. Elk. Bears. An aurochs once, during a hunt that did not go well for anyone. None of them looked like this. None of them looked like the world had made them specifically to end things.

I have the Dragon Slayer Sword. The name felt like a joke when I first heard it. It doesn't feel like a joke now.

Vukradin acts first. He always does — not because he's fastest but because he always already knows what he wants to do. He casts Silence on the summoning circle, a bubble of nothing swallowing every sound the Carver might speak. The ritual chokes on it.

"Interrupting the ritual!" he announces to the sky, to the mountain, to anyone listening. "I am saving the world! Huzzah!"

"They were intervening," Soma says, very dry, not looking at him. "You interventionist."

He doesn't even register the insult. "Good job, team! You did it."

The fight hasn't started. I could tell him that. I don't. He knows. It's just how he is. He celebrates because he means it, not because it's true yet, and somehow that still works. I learned to stop questioning it somewhere around the second month.

I rage. The heat rises in my chest the way it always does — not anger exactly, something older than anger, something my body learned before I had words for it. I dash across the rooftop toward the Carver, the longest way around the wall, and I get there just in time to be thirty feet short. The distance mocks me. All that heat and nowhere to put it. I hold the Dragon Slayer Sword and I wait, which is the worst thing I know how to do.

Soma checks on the dragon. She has a gift for this — animals read her the way people read a fire, warm and honest. She looks at Cryovain's eyes for a long moment and then turns back to us.

"Go, guys, don't, don't unlock the dragon."

"We're gonna talk to it," Vukradin says.

"It's in a state of such enragement that it will just attack everything around."

That lands. I look at Cryovain again — at the way the chains are the only thing standing between that rage and us — and I file it. The dragon is not rescue. The dragon is a second problem. First problem is the Carver.

Then the first problem gets worse.

The Carver moves. Smooth and wrong and *fast*, thirty feet of legendary repositioning, slipping clear of the silence zone without provoking a single thing. I stood there burning through my own blood just to close that gap, and he walks away from it like it cost him nothing. I hear Soma mutter something behind me. Something about cheating. Something that ends with "not you," directed somewhere that isn't any of us.

I understand the feeling exactly.

Then Vukradin says "Hold Person," and the Carver stops.

Mid-stride. Frozen. The man's body locked in place, his concentration stolen by whatever the bard wove around him.

"He is paralyzed!" Vukradin's voice breaks with actual joy. "Excellent! Well done, team!"

And then.

"He's paralyzed," Vukradin says again, but the tone has changed. It's not celebration. It's a man reading a sentence twice because the first time didn't make sense.

Because the chains are dissolving.

Because the sword — the obsidian blade at the Carver's hip, the blade that went briefly purple and now looks like deep black glass — the sword has cast Dominate. Not the man. The man is frozen. The sword did it.

The sword was always doing it.

Then the voice comes.

Not through ears. Through the inside of my skull, cold and enormous and contemptuous, the way a mountain would sound if mountains bothered to be contemptuous of you.

*Coward's magic. You mute the ritual because you lack the strength to shatter the vessel. And now — now I shall have everything I always wanted.*

I hear Soma ask if the dragon said that.

*You hear that in your heads*, the GM explains.

Yes. I heard it. I hear it still. Something about the voice makes me feel small in a way that combat doesn't. Combat I know. This is different. This is something that has thought about us and decided we are insufficient, and I don't like how much I believe it in this moment.

Vukradin's voice comes back to me through the ringing. He's figured it out — I can hear him working through it. *The carver — the sword — the — oh. The carver is the sword.* And Soma confirms it quietly: *the human was just a vessel.* The man with the obsidian blade was never the Carver. The blade was the Carver. The man was just the thing it wore.

And now the dragon is free.

Vukradin takes maybe two seconds to absorb this and then says, with a certainty I find genuinely inspiring: "Alright, that's fine, let's kill a dragon. It'll be fine."

He looks at me.

"Crewbury," he says — he has never once pronounced it correctly and I gave up correcting him — "kill the dragon."

A pause.

"Didn't you always want to kill a dragon? Isn't this, like, a rite of passage for the —"

He's still talking when I've already started moving.

Did I always want to kill a dragon. I think about that for exactly half a second. The tribe would have wanted that. The tribe wanted to *be* dragons in some way — all the violence, the conquest, the leaving wreckage behind wherever they went. I don't want to be a dragon. I just want to be standing next to Vukradin when this is over.

Valphine moves before I reach striking range. She casts Guiding Bolt and the light hits Cryovain like a brand — gold and burning, painting a mark on white scales that says *here, here exactly, this is where the next blow should land.* I could kiss her for it. I don't. I run instead.

The Dragon Slayer Sword knows.

I don't know how else to say it. The moment I bring it around toward Cryovain, I feel something in the blade — a recognition, a hunger that isn't mine but that I can use. The first swing lands. The enchantment surges through the steel and into white scales and the dragon *flinches*. Twenty-one damage. I don't stop. The second swing lands before Cryovain finishes reacting. Twenty-two. Forty-three total, and the Dragon Slayer Sword rings in my hands like it has been waiting its whole forged life for exactly this dragon.

"That was worth me sacking around for," Soma says.

Yeah. It was.

Then Cryovain breathes.

I see it coming — the chest expanding, the cold building in the throat — and I know I'm not moving in time. The cone hits me like I've been unmade. Thirty feet of freeze that doesn't care about rage, doesn't care about muscle, goes straight through everything my body knows how to do. I dig deep — Stone's Endurance, my Goliath blood pulling ten of those forty points back from the edge — and it still doesn't matter enough. Thirty damage, clean through. My rage kept me standing. Barely.

I think about the exile. The night the tribe cast me out, when I stood in the dark and cold and understood for the first time that I had failed at the only thing they ever asked me to be. I have been running from that cold in some form or another ever since. Running toward the music Vukradin makes instead of the drums my tribe beat. Running toward something better. Running from the man who stood in the dark and couldn't answer for what he'd done.

I'm still standing.

The dragon beats its wings and rises — thirty feet, right out of reach of my blade, hovering above the battlements like it has already won. I watch it go. I watch the Dragon Slayer Sword become temporarily useless in my hands and I think: two rounds. I have two rounds to get back within striking distance of something that can unmake me with its breath.

Behind me, Vukradin announces that he has nothing. No ranged attacks. Except.

"Other than Fireball."

Soma asks, with real curiosity, if he's actually going to use it.

"Nope."

The beads are mine. He won't touch them. Even now, with the dragon airborne and the clock ticking, he won't use what isn't his. I have stood in front of Vukradin with a sword because he is worth protecting. He has stood here with a choice and chosen to protect me instead, with nothing but a bard's stubbornness and the knowledge that some things aren't his to spend.

Then he casts Thorn Whip and rolls a natural twenty and hits the dragon anyway for twenty-six damage, and Soma shouts *way to go, face* and something in my chest loosens a little.

The round ends. The veteran is paralyzed. The sword is dark. Cryovain is wounded and furious and thirty feet above my head.

I am cold all the way through. I am still standing. The Dragon Slayer Sword is in my hand and there are two rounds left, and Vukradin trusted me with this, and I have not failed him yet.

And yet.
