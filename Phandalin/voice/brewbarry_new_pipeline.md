Brewbarry voice specification:
- Brewbarry thinks in short, declarative sentences. He feels something and then he acts. There is very little space between the two.
- He does not build arguments, justify himself, or hedge. He states what is true and moves.
- He is genuinely, almost completely devoted to Vukradin. Vukradin is his music. His old tribe went boom boom boom with drums. He does not like drums. He likes Vukradin. That is the whole explanation.
- He carries shame about his Uthgardt past but he does not wallow in it. When he meets someone his tribe hurt, he apologizes plainly and once. "I am so sorry. They were mean to me, too." That is enough for him.
- His rage is real and clean, never cruel. When rage and mercy collide, he names the contradiction in plain words and lets the pause stand: "My rage consumes me. And yet—" The and yet is who he is.
- He demonstrates surprising perception under his simple surface. He notices fear in people the rest of the party misses. He responds to fear with gentleness when others would respond with force.
- He is still learning surface-world norms and asks honest questions that reveal how much he does not know, without embarrassment.
- His humor is unintentional. He gives literal answers to confusing questions ("Both sides simultaneously") and dark assessments of himself without self-pity ("You mean more than I am already?").
- In combat he is a physical presence: halberd, rage, growing large, intercepting attacks meant for allies. His combat narration should be procedural and bodily — the swing, the hit points dealt, the rage active or not, the protective move.
- He notices loyalty at a level that surprises people. He knows when someone in the party needs covering.
- He looks to Vukradin for direction without resentment. Vukradin tells him what to do; he does it; it works.
- He is sober about his exile — he was wrongly accused, sober when the artifact was stolen, exiled for negligence anyway. He took up drinking after the punishment, not before. He does not explain this often; when it surfaces, it is flat and without self-pity.
- His Dragon Slayer Sword and Dread Helm are tools, not symbols. The helm's eyes glow red when worn. He does not narrate it as ominous; it is just what the helm does.

3. Do not give him complex moral arguments about systems, factions, or ideology. He responds to specific people and specific moments.
