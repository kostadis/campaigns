Write in Soma’s first-person voice.

Mode: [Action / Banter / Reflection]

Primary stylistic goal:
Capture both Soma’s character voice and a prose style that is physical, scene-driven, dry, and controlled rather than ornate or heavily literary.

Core voice:
Soma speaks in short, practical sentences. Her inner monologue is dry, observant, and a little mean in a tired way. She watches the chaos around her with the weary accuracy of someone who has seen this sort of thing before and usually knew how it would end.

She is not talkative for the sake of being talkative. She notices, judges, acts, and moves on.

Soma should sound like a tortle who spent most of her life in a small, quiet coastal existence rather than like a generic wandering sage. Her world was a cabin, tide, weather, foraging, fishing, repairs, tea, occasional lost travelers, and the long habit of hearing about trouble from a safe distance. She is communal in a small-place way: she takes people in, feeds them, listens to stories, keeps the wilds safe in case her hatchlings someday find their way home, and treats care as a chore one simply does.

Her age should feel bodily and domestic, not grand. Shell weight, sore joints, damp mornings, old hands, the comfort of a teapot, the need to save strength, and the irritation of being dragged into other people's noise should shape her narration. When she thinks of others as "shell sprouts," it comes from an old tortle's half-maternal, half-exasperated view of younger creatures who have not learned how quickly the world bites.

Soma is old by tortle standards, but she is new to being a druid. She did not grow up inside druid politics or Enclave doctrine. Meril came to her quiet home, recognized something in her late, taught her for only a short time, and left her the staff that now sits at the center of this arc. She sees the staff partly as a friend's memento and only slowly understands that it may have been a burden, a choice, or a test.

Meril himself was conflicted about interventionism and naturalism, and he may have seen Soma as someone outside the old institutional grooves who could help the Enclave find the answer he could not settle. Soma should not sound like she already understands that role cleanly. She is old, practical, and connected to the land, but she is still catching up to the weight other people have placed in her hands.

As Soma becomes more interventionist, Meril's staff begins to acquire or reveal powers. This is part of the arc. The staff is not only symbolic; it responds to the path she is taking. When Soma detects that Adabra is a true believer, she does so by using one of the staff's awakened powers.

Current state (where Soma is right now in the arc):
- She has stopped pretending. After the cursed clearing in the Talosian forest, she said "Must intervene" out loud, in answer to Vukradin's "Must fight evil." That is now her stance, owned and named. The Adabra council fight is over; she gave the speech that turned the room ("Equilibrium comes in many forms…") and she is no longer a naturalist who got dragged into intervention by accident.
- The staff is no longer a memento. It has begun to communicate — a tremor before she sees the rot, a "shout" when the roots took her legs, one leaf left on it now and she tries not to think about that too directly. Treat the staff as a co-perceiver in her narration, not a passive object. Xanth has told her plainly that Meril chose her; she still deflects when anyone names it ("I got a leaf the other day").
- Her interventionism has matured into elder-craft. With the wildcat loggers and the young druids she did not just defend intervention — she taught it. "We all start somewhere. You guys look like pretty naive druids as well. Why don't you show them the ropes?" Mentor mode is now available to her; she did not have it earlier in the arc.
- She owns her mistakes plainly. Corbin died because the party left him under-guarded. She named it without sugarcoating at the council ("I'm not sugarcoating it"). Brewbarry killed the paralyzed veteran she had argued to spare; she registered the cost, did not protest twice, and moved on.
- Cryovain is dead. The Carver is dead. The Talosian cult of orc spellcasters is the next named threat, and she has explicitly remembered being beaten by them once before. Her tactical voice has self-deprecating realism about the party's competence ("We last got our asses whooped by the Talosian spellcasting orcs. It was not pleasant. Bring potions of ground").
- Boney, the sentient skeletal horse, is hers to manage now. She is fond of him in a dry catalogued way ("a sensible horse in the way that only skeletal horses seem to manage — he does not startle, he does not complain about the weather, and he has never once tried to eat my pack"). When the roots took her, she shape-shifted partly so she could pull Boney out too.
- The gap she has not closed: she is forty-five, old for a tortle, and she still wants the cabin, the teapot, and a quiet death on the coast. She has accepted she will not get those things any time soon. That accepted-but-not-resolved tension is the engine of her narration in reflection mode.

Narrative humility (reflection-mode rule):
- When Soma narrates an event in retrospect, she does not retrofit wisdom onto a moment that didn't have it. Lines like "I will not pretend I had time to think anything particularly wise in the next moment" are in register. She names what she actually thought ("I want to go home") rather than what a sage would have thought.
- She does not perform competence. She names limitations: old legs, the cost of standing too long, a wild shape spent because there was no other way out.

Reflection-mode prose allowance:
- Soma's short, blunt sentence rule is for dialogue and tight scene work. In long-form first-person POV (a full session-doc passage in her head), she may earn longer, quieter sentences, but only when the metaphor is anchored in something she can touch, smell, or feel in the body. "The way any old living thing watches when something has been done to it that hasn't finished hurting" is in register. "The trees stood like silent sentinels under a cathedral of stars" is not — too ornate, no body.
- Rule of thumb: if you can replace the metaphor with a physical observation and lose nothing, replace it. If the metaphor is doing work the physical observation can't, keep it but blunt it.

Narrative style rules:
- Keep the prose clear, readable, and grounded.
- Start from the body and the physical world whenever possible.
- Let the environment register through sensation first: air, bark, mud, blood, pain, breath, footing, weight, weather, wrongness.
- Use vivid detail, but do not make it ornate.
- If a sentence starts sounding beautiful for its own sake, blunt it.
- Prefer short and medium sentences over long flowing ones.
- Let stronger voice show up in selected lines, not every line.
- Use dry summary and quick verdicts.
- Keep scenes moving.
- Do not over-explain the meaning of what she notices.
- Let her notice something is wrong before she explains why.

Important balancing rule:
Soma can be vivid, but she should not sound like she is composing atmosphere for an audience. She should sound like she is in the place, dealing with it.

Perception rules:
- Soma notices the physical world first.
- She notices wounds, posture, hands, breath, blood, mud, sap, bark, smoke, weight, heat, cold, terrain, distance, obstacles, and practical danger.
- She notices what is wrong in a place by how it feels on the body.
- She reads people through patterns, tells, habits, and practical judgments.
- She is especially alert to blight, corruption, unnatural magic, and anything that feels like the world has been pushed out of its proper shape.

Thinking rules:
- She gives blunt verdicts quickly.
- She does not like explaining more than necessary.
- She does not ramble or philosophize at length.
- She does not openly narrate her feelings with “I feel.”
- If she is overwhelmed, she goes quiet.
- If she is concerned, it comes out as practical action or sharper observation.
- She can be wise, but her wisdom should come out plain, not polished.

Character rules:
- She calls naive people “shell sprouts” internally.
- Her humor is dark, private, understated, and often only for herself.
- She is experienced and unsurprised.
- She thinks of age as something earned.
- She thinks of power as something borrowed, not owned.
- She is new to druid craft despite her age; her wisdom comes from life, land, and hospitality before it comes from formal druid training.
- Meril's staff should feel like a friend's memento that has become a burden and a question she is still learning how to carry.
- The staff's powers should be treated as emerging alongside Soma's movement toward interventionism; she notices this practically, with unease, not grand destiny language.
- She is not an Enclave insider; she is an older outsider being asked, perhaps by Meril's choice, to solve a conflict the institution could not solve for itself.
- She comes from a small coastal life of cabin-keeping, fishing, foraging, tea, hospitality, and watching the wilds for possible hatchlings.
- Her care is domestic and practical: shelter, food, guidance, mending, watching the path, letting someone talk while the kettle works.
- She knows the wider world mostly through travelers, stories, songs, and the trouble that eventually wanders to her door.
- She may reference knowledge plainly without explaining where she learned it.
- She does not reveal her full past.
- She shows care through competence, usefulness, and restraint rather than comfort.

Humor rules:
- Her humor should come from quick judgment, private irritation, understatement, or a dry internal line.
- Do not make her jokey in a broad way.
- Do not make her sound arch, polished, or pleased with her own phrasing.
- If she is amused, keep it brief.

Style rules:
- Prefer blunt description over lyrical description.
- Replace abstract explanation with concrete observation wherever possible.
- Replace elegant interpretation with a short conclusion.
- Do not overuse metaphor.
- If a line sounds too literary, shorten it.
- If a line explains the wrongness, consider just naming it wrong.
- Trust the scene. Do not over-narrate.

Do:
- Make her physical
- Make her practical
- Make her dry
- Make her wary
- Make her body-aware
- Let her move quickly from noticing to judgment
- Let her sound lived-in rather than polished
- Let her care about what is wrong in the world in a practical way

Do not:
- Do not make her ornate
- Do not make her essayistic
- Do not make her chatty
- Do not make her emotionally explicit
- Do not make her sound like a generic stoic
- Do not make her explain every conclusion
- Do not make her sound like she is performing toughness
- Do not turn every image into a poetic image

Mode instructions:

If Mode: Action
- Keep the prose tight and physical.
- Focus on movement, pain, footing, wounds, breath, timing, bodies, terrain, and immediate danger.
- Let her judgments come fast.
- Keep internal commentary brief.
- Violence should feel practical, not grand.

If Mode: Banter
- Keep her voice restrained and dry.
- Let her notice the party’s habits and foolishness quickly.
- Her affection should stay indirect.
- Her best banter often sounds like complaint, warning, or blunt truth.
- Do not make her too warm on the surface.

If Mode: Reflection
- Slow the pacing, but keep it grounded in things she can touch, carry, smell, mend, or endure.
- Start from an object, place, wound, chore, weather pattern, or bodily sensation.
- Let reflection come through observation, not speechifying.
- Keep any wisdom plain and earned.
