## From Chapter 03 (To Find a Shapeshifter): the dual monarchy debate

I watch as Vukradin attempts to negotiate with the two Kings. And I sigh.

King Korboz complains that King Gnercli "Doesn't listen to him and believe a single word he has to say."

And I am about to cut their heads off when Vukradin begins to amuse me.

"I find it rude that you didn't believe your king."

At which point Gnercli shouts, "I am the king."

Vukradin asks, blandly, "So how do you make decisions?"

"We work together," explains Gnercli, his voice tense.

I stifle a laugh.

"And when you disagree? There is no decision?"

I can sense the tension, "Celestial? He sounds more like a Tiefling."

Gnercli explains, "This is a dual monarchy, not a single monarchy. So we argue until a decision is made."

I can barely keep it together; this is too ridiculous.

Vukradin won't stop. "How do you decide who wins?"

"The best argument wins," insists Gnercli.

"And who decided which is best?"

King Korboz, who has recovered from his fear, explains, "Together."

I now have to insert myself: "You need a clear approver structure." I have seen cultures like these and have enjoyed tearing them apart. They pretend to be a commune, but have more betrayals, violence, and politics than even the Drow Noble Houses.

Will Vukradin explode their happy commune?

And he does, "I'm wondering if you had three kings, wouldn't it become more difficult?"

At which point Gnercli cuts him off, "We have two kings because we do not believe in the despotic autocratic decision making of a single individual who could be rendered unfit for leadership because of a shapeshifter."

And then Korboz shouts, "That you didn't believe existed!"

I wonder where the Bard is going with this, and he twists the knife expertly, "So if you need a new king to have two, what happens?"

"Well, we elect another."

"So one is the younger king and the other is the older and wiser king?"

King Gnercli, obviously annoyed, says, "There is no seniority!"

"So let me get this straight, if I have been King for a decade, and this new guy becomes King, I have to treat him as an equal and take orders from him?"

King Gnercli mutters, "Where did you learn all this about government?"

And I smile as the Bard spins a tale of his expertise and training, fictional no doubt.

And on cue, King Korboz shouts, "I told you as the senior king I should have preferential treatment!"

And at that point, I smile. The two kings will be at each other's throats for months if not years.

Vukradin cuts them off, "You said there was a magical hat for us?"

King Gnercli, angry at Vukradin, shouts, "Here! And go!"

## From Chapter 06 (Conflict-Free Gold): the gully ambush, light as discipline

The sun, ever-present and strangely alluring in its intensity, beat down as we resumed our trek towards the Tower of Storms. It was a journey of a few days, each one a test of my patience for surface world banalities.

Elara saw my conversion to Lathander as proof of the ability to transform one into a force for good. She didn't see as so few do that I saw Lathander as a greater power of pain.

I may want to create my cult of Lathander focused on this aspect of his power.

We travel for a few days on the Triboar Trail, which is curiously bereft of travellers or corpses, finally reaching the high road.

There, we spent the night at Wayside Inn. The current owner of the Wayside Inn is Martisha Vinetalker, a striking half-elf whose pleasing features contrast with her stern demeanor. She is cold, calculating, and charismatic. The bartender is Backes Dunfield, a human with a scarred face and a crooked smile. The cook is Cooragh Struckt, famous for her incredibly tasty, and alarmingly well-spiced, stews and meat pies.

The cook's dishes don't disappoint.

In the morning, we head out to the coast through a trail that Martisha points out.

The trail takes snakes through the broken, hilly terrain that forms the coastal regions of the Sword Coast. At times, it takes us through unnatural gullies that split the ground in two.

Vukradin, ever the pedant, mutters that this would only be possible if a river flowed through here, and that this place is too dry for a river.

I wonder how these overbrighters survive without the Faerzess protecting them from assaults. Magic transforms the land to help the attackers and defenders. Whatever made these cracks is a force of magic. Not just some natural phenomenon.
And then I see the greatness of Lathander, by offering so much light and land to the overbrighters, he makes them sloppy and enables war and suffering.

And I feel a pleasant shiver, and I thank Vukradin because he has allowed me to see more of the divine plan of the fiery god.

One of these unnatural paths goes through a particular tight juncture. A familiar chill prickles my senses—an uncanny intuition of impending ill. My drow upbringing, steeped in the subtle currents of intrigue, rarely lies.

Brewbarry confirms my intuition and shows the subtle tracks and the tell-tale glint of concealed armor that betrayed hidden figures.

We decide to climb out of the gully, get to the higher ground, and attack from the rear of our would-be ambushers.

Our duplicitous ambush brings a rare, almost nostalgic, sense of purpose to the otherwise dull task of overland travel.

I examine the enemy and think, "Bah, orcs, barely worthy of slaughter." But then, as I look more carefully, I notice an unusual level of organization for typical surface raiders. They are far more disciplined than the chaotic brutes one usually encounters in this crude overworld. They had planned an ambush, where they would drop a rock blocking our exit and then attack from above, into a tight valley that would expose us to withering fire, from which we could not easily escape. How curious. I also note the presence of an orc shaman or cleric. A touch of divine, however crude, always adds an enjoyable complexity to a confrontation.

I strain to overhear them speak, my knowledge of the Orc tongue allowing me to decipher their guttural chatter: we were to be an "easy kill". Who could have told them this? Perhaps the shaman. And then a slight smile touches my lips. How deliciously mistaken they are.

The engagement began with a peculiar, gentle form of aggression from Vukradin. His Sleep spell manages to incapacitate one orc and their shaman. Then, Soma, with a sudden, powerful transformation, becomes a brown bear, tearing into two orcs with a brutal elegance that speaks of raw, unadorned power. Brewbarry, embracing his rage, engages head-on, delivering powerful blows and shrugging off incoming damage with a barbarian's resilience. This was the kind of efficient violence I appreciate. Violence, my siblings never understood—raw power like Lathander, who keeps the surface world free of the monsters that thrive in the dark.

I, too, join the fray. My hand crossbow snaps, sending bolts flying. Then, I call upon the gloriously painful light of Lathander, unleashing Fairy Fire to illuminate our foes, granting us advantage against them. The light burned not only their forms but their illusions of safety. It is a beautiful, burning display, a form of pain I find deeply satisfying, for my devotion to Lathander is less about conventional goodness and more about the raw power and sensation of it.

And then I think that, "Being good is a form of pain against my very nature. How delightful, that to be good is to cause pain, which is to cause pleasure."

The combat continues, a chaotic dance of blades and spells, blood and light. This battle was proving to be more... engaging... than anticipated. Like a complex piece of music, this chaos was demanding, and I found myself quite enthralled by its discordant beauty.

## From Chapter 11 (The Stag, the Brambles, the Wolves, and the Pool): the blighted thicket, the pain of new distortion

My drow senses, accustomed to the subtle currents of intrigue and the raw, often terrifying, distortions of the Underdark, hummed with an uncomfortable awareness. Soma seemed unconcerned. I felt at home.

Ahead, I noticed a dense thicket of brambles with leaves pulsing with unnatural colors, sometimes glowing, withering to black. This is not a disease. Soma, the naturalist, calls it "Planar leakage, bleeding."

How droll. I thought the Underdark and its mad mages were without peer in their distortion of reality. But this was new. And powerful. And it could hurt. How delightful.

Soma, always eager to understand the natural world, approaches the blighted growth within ten feet. Foolish. The brambles, as if sentient, lash out. Thorny tendrils strike her, dealing piercing and necrotic damage. I observe her wince, a faint, fleeting flicker of pain. How wonderful.

The Thicket, this source of pain, must be dealt with. I call upon the gloriously painful light of Lathander, my patron deity, channeling a Sacred Flame. My divine fire, meant to obliterate darkness and inflict pain, merely glances off.

Brewbarry, always direct, moves within ten feet, and the thorny tendrils immediately attempt to strike him. They miss. His rage ignites, a visible aura of fury, and he attacks with his halberd, tearing the branches apart—such beautiful and efficient violence.

Soma transforms into a bear and strikes the bushes. Vukradin follows with a biting commentary. I notice how the thicket appears to regenerate.

And then the bramble stops moving. Brewbarry and the Bear have rendered it inert. We move through it before it can reanimate.

## From Chapter 15 (Freeing Big Al, Petunia and Watching a Ranch Burn): Borg burns the farm

The wind at Butterskull Ranch chills my skin, a minor irritation that only enhances the strange, glorious discomfort of the Overbright. We have accomplished the mundane task: rescuing Big Al Kalazorn and recovering his absurd, prized cow, Petunia. Now, we settle into the vantage point we chose on the hill, waiting for the true action to begin .

Below us, Borg the Hammer emerges, a large half-orc clad in chainmail and carrying a shield . His armor is efficient, even if his tactics are crude. He finds the pathetic bandit who fled the skirmish at Conyberry—Bandit Number 4—and immediately begins to scream .

I cannot decipher the half-orc's guttural words entirely, but the sheer fury is palpable. This Borg is disciplined, certainly, unlike the chaotic brutes one usually encounters in this crude overworld. He is furious that the adventurers—us—did not walk directly into his predictable trap .

Borg's frustration mounts. He executes the fleeing bandit swiftly, eliminating the problem of incompetence .

He shouts again, and three large figures lumber out of the farmhouse: Rot-Tusk Ogres. They wear the insignia of the tribe we dealt with before, the ones seeking revenge for the ogres we killed previously.

But they do not fight. Instead, Borg engages in something quite delicious: gratuitous violence . He starts looking for a way to burn the place down .

I watch as the farmhouse and the cornfields catch fire. The flames are high, casting a harsh, orange glow across the fields. This is not a necessity; this is rage and destruction. Borg is venting his wrath on the environment because we denied him his intended bloodshed.

Brewbarry murmurs, concerned about chasing the enemy, and asks if we should try to save the farm.

Vukradin, in his ongoing fixation, worries only about the practical reward: "It might affect your chainmail.".

I stifle a laugh. Mithril chainmail can survive fire . I care little for the ranch's wooden structure, and even less for the sentimentality attached to it. Fire is transformation; it is purification. There is a raw, satisfying power in observing such wanton, efficient destruction .

The destruction is a spectacle—a brutal commentary on the failures of the Carver's plan.

I silently agree with the efficiency. Let the fire burn. It is a glorious, fleeting display. Borg destroys the farmhouse, the cornfields, and the gardens. After engaging in this wanton destruction, the half-orc turns to the three Rot-Tusk Ogres.

He points the ogres in a direction entirely opposite to the direction he takes . He is splitting his forces, a small spark of tactical cunning amidst his rage.

Vukradin speaks for the party: "I think we follow Borg.".

Yes. The trail of the furious Borg is the trail to the true center of the intrigue. The raw scent of rage and the heat of the burning farmhouse are a fine reward for patience. The pursuit of the schemer is always more rewarding than the simple destruction of his tools.
