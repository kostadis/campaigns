# Style Example: Brewbarry + Soma — Corbin's Surrender (Chapter 04)
# Demonstrates: Brewbarry's simple/direct emotional voice, Soma as compassionate observer, contrasting inner worlds

### Brewbarry
My rage consumes me. And yet, there is something about this man's fear. And I think that he has lost his tribe. And I feel sadness.

I drop my halberd and shout, "Friends."

He looks at me, and I can tell that he has suffered at the hands of my people. "I am so sorry that the Uthgardts hurt you. They were mean to me, too."

The man stares at me, a look of confusion on his face. He was expecting to die, not get an apology.

"They were mean to me, too."

The man stares and says, "They pillaged my village, destroyed everything." He looks at me and sobs, "I had no money, nothing. I am not a bad person. I don't want to steal things. It's the Carver's fault."

### Vukradin
The bandit has a boss. I must learn more.

"You should join us at dinner and tell us about the Carver?"

I stare at him, a bit of menace, a bit of kindliness. The form of an Aasimar can always be helpful.

"What's there to say. He's the big boss. And he's big. I've never met him. I just worked with Jax."

He looks at me like a puppy who has found its momma. I smile. And then he asks, "Can I be with you guys?"

I smile, "Of course."

### Soma
There's a scroll. I pick it up. It has a druidic spell.

And then I hear the annoying voice of Vukradin reminding me that this really should be returned to its rightful owner.

I stare at him in disbelief, and then realize he has a point. Am I any better than a bandit if I keep this?

*Later — after the battle, Soma listens to Corbin's full story:*

After dinner, I sit and listen to Corbin's tale of woe as he recounts it to me: "I wasn't born into this life. I was a farmer from a small, forgettable village called Greenmeadow, nestled in the plains east of the High Forest. My life was simple and good; I worked the land my father had worked, was married to a woman named Elara, and had a young daughter, Lily, who loved the wildflowers that grew at the edge of our fields.

Then, the world shattered. The Ordning, which structured the giants' world, broke, and chaos swept across the North. The Giants became emboldened, and lawless destruction ensued. One smoke-filled afternoon, the Uthgardt barbarians of the Black Lion tribe descended on Greenmeadow. They weren't raiding for supplies; they were a storm of pure destruction, a wildfire in human form, proving their strength in a world gone mad.

I fought. I was no warrior, armed only with a wood-axe and a farmer's grit, but I fought for my home and my family. It was not enough. I watched my fields burn, my home collapse into embers, and my family… I watched them fall. I only survived because I was knocked unconscious by a charging barbarian's shield and left for dead in a ditch, buried under the body of my neighbor.

I awoke to silence and ash. With nothing left but the clothes on my back and a soul scoured clean of hope, I began to walk. I drifted south for months, a ghost haunting the roads, taking what I could to survive—a half-eaten apple from a cart, a discarded blanket, a few coins from a sleeping traveler's purse. Each petty crime chipped away at the man I had been, but it was a necessary act to simply keep living."

This man's journey through life was changed by visitors. He saw death and horrors. I met Meril. By the grace of the gods…

I think. And then I sleep.
