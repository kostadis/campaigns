## From Chapter 06 (Conflict-Free Gold): the principled rescue, the embarrassing chant

And so we leave for the Tower of Storms. As we round a bend, we spot a small, overturned trade cart, partially obscured by thick undergrowth. Huddled nervously behind it are two figures – a middle-aged human merchant, Elara, and her groom, Jarek. They look visibly shaken and disheveled, their pack horses nowhere in sight.

I approach, and they stare at me with wide eyes, terrified. I take a moment to reflect on our group. Approaching these poor innocents are an Aasimar, a Uthgardt Barbarian, a Drow, and a large Tortle, while the only human looks like a bandit and is wearing armor that he stole from a dead body.

Sensing an opportunity to make some conflict-free coin, I immediately step forward, reassuringly.

The woman, seeing that I am obviously a being of principles and not another brigand, cries out, "Orcs attacked us."

Soma, whose patronizing motherly tone and lack of refinement are obvious, offers in her guttural voice, "We aren't orcs."

I want to scream, "Well, obviously!"

The woman looks at carefully, and asks, 'Well who are you?"

And again before I can masterfully handle the situation, Valphine shouts, "Lathandar!"

The woman looks confused and then mutters, "Oh ,Priestess! The faith must be strong in you!"

Valpine smiles and adopts that look that so many misunderstood of bliss, "He's painful!"

The old woman looks confused and mutters, "I have never heard that teaching…"

Calmed down, she says, "My name is Elara, and this is Jarek, my groom and we were heading towards Phandalin…"

Soma offers to mend the broken cart, and Elara accepts the offer.

Elara, seeing all of the free help we are giving, asks if we could rescue one of her horses that had run down into a field. Brewbarry, ever the eager companion, rushes off to retrieve it. He's a brute, but a loyal one, especially when there's a task involving strength or, in this case, a horse.

As Brewbarry runs off, Jarek finally speaks, "I told you we should travel with some protection."

Curious about the attack, I ask her to tell me what happened. And as she describes the battle, her eyes dart around, as if she is trying to remember something. She shakes her head and sighs. As she finishes her tale, she says, "They moved with unnerving discipline not like the usual pack I have seen and had odd markings... like a swirl, or an eye?" She shakes her head as if it clear it and continues, "No, it's gone now..."

As for Jarek he also describes the eerie coordination of the attackers, and finishes with this odd note, "I think... I heard something... a sort of hum, not really a sound, just... in my head, before they struck. Then it was gone."

Valphine says it's mindflayers, but I dismiss her because she always says it's mindflayers.

Upon Brewbarry's return with the horse, Elara is relieved. She thanks me for our timely assistance and offers a reward. I hold out my outstretched hand, but she reaches out to Valphine and says, "For the Morninglord and whomever you feel is worthy."

I point out to Valphine, "This! This is Fair Trade and Conflict Free gold."

The rest of the group wants to press on, but I insist that we return Elara and Jarke to Phandalin. Although there is grumbling, we do just that.

Upon arrival, we see Ser Kaelen Thorne, who is still in Phandalin and I explain everything to him. He seems pleased that I am not just focused on the big picture, but also doing good deeds, and how that aligns with the broader values of the Order of the Gauntlent. He ends with, "Bringing you into the fold was the right thing to do."

Compliments are always good.

As I am asked to commission music, and want to know the style of the group, I ask, "Can you share with me a cheer of the Gauntlet?"

He stammers and apologizes, saying that my talents would improve anything the Order had done.

This piques my curiosity further. And I insist, so he shouts, embarrassed, " Steal and shield, Heart and band. We stand as one. A steadfast band."

"I am more of a magic user. You have magic users?"

"Yes, we do. This chant is rather embarrassing, that's why we need your genius!"

I smile—finally, someone who appreciates my talent and vision.

And so I head out from Phandalin and reflect on what has happened.

Ensuring Elara and Jarek were safely back in town and getting paid for it is the kind of coin I sought – earned through genuine assistance, not through "barbaric slaughtering". It shows me that I can make the money to build my studio and stay true to my principles.

Like a well-structured song, helping Elara and Jarek helped resolve the discordant tone of this horrible world into a harmony.

I say to no one in particular, "Even in a world of monsters and mayhem, opportunities exist for a principled artist to earn a livelihood without compromising their ideals."

## From Chapter 08 (The Beating Heart of the Storm): destroying the heart, returning the conch

The grim truth of Moesko's defeated form settled heavily upon me. I believe, fundamentally, in the sanctity of earned possessions. All this treasure feels like stolen merchandise, and I'm determined to find its rightful owners. I am a musician, after all, not a common thief, and I just wanna make a music studio, and I want to do it without stealing something! My companions, especially Soma, seem less bothered by such ethical quandaries, but to me, the integrity of our actions is paramount.

When we found the opalescent conch, I immediately recognized it as belonging to Miral the Sea Elf. Our quest had brought us this far, and I intended to see it through with honor. As Valphine probed Moesko's belongings, a concealed compartment yielded a dark, iridescent talisman. It was crudely etched with the stylized, jagged lightning bolt of Talos, the God of Storms. This was significant.

As the others ascended the tower, I noted the octopus armor Moesko wore. My initial thought was that it was splint mail, and therefore useless to us, as no one in our party was proficient in such heavy armor. My mind, however, was already turning to the source of the lighthouse's eerie glow. From below, I had seen the lightning rod, and Soma had connected it to the evil god Talos. Bad things happen in this tower, and the lightning rod is part of the curse. We had to break it.

Reaching the very top, I saw the beacon's pulsating green light spill over the sea. Valphine, with her peculiar fascination, stared directly into it, and for a moment, she was blinded. But not before she revealed the horrifying truth: the light emanated from a human heart, several sizes too large—Moesko's heart, still beating! This was the very source of the lighthouse's corrupted power. I knew then, with absolute certainty, that this heart, no matter how bright, was evil. My convictions solidified. I shouted, "We need to destroy the heart, guys. Like, that was part of the deal, that we were gonna save the lighthouse."

I momentarily wrestled with the problem of replacing the light, but then a forgotten melody, a historical song about a lighthouse and a fire, surfaced in my mind. Lighthouses typically had a fire, not a beating heart. The path was clear.

I wasted no time. With a determined resolve, I descended to the lightning rod and smashed it. Then, I made my way to the top and, with a mighty swing, struck the grotesque heart. It was still beating, a resilient organ of corruption. My first attack missed, but my offhand strike landed. The second swing connected, and the heart, with a final, sickening thrum, was destroyed. The light went out. The air felt lighter, cleansed. "Shall we set a fire?" I asked, feeling the return of a proper order. I then cast a light spell to temporarily illuminate the beacon, as a symbol of our intent, and declared, "I'm more of a musician, not really a swimmer," opting to remain on shore while Soma retrieved wood from the wrecks.

From my vantage point on the shore, the murky water offered little visibility. I heard the commotion as Soma engaged the shark. Trying to assist, I focused my bardic magic. "I can cast Vicious Mockery at a creature if I can hear it," My words, aimed to wound the shark's pride, dealt one point of damage, a slight but satisfying jab. Later, when the sharks proved more numerous, I decided on a different tactic. "Can I try to sleep both of them?" I asked. With a focused strum of my lute, my magic flowed, and both remaining sharks were asleep! This was crucial. If they take damage, they will wake up, I explained, ensuring my companions understood the new tactical advantage.

As Soma brought the chests ashore, I immediately questioned Valphine: "Why are we opening other people's chests? Those aren't yours." This constant urge to loot grated on me. We have to return them! I insisted, looking for any markings to identify the owners. The mermaid statue, the spellbook, the wand of secrets—all were treasures that belonged to others. I'm feeling increasingly isolated in this party, I thought, as the others seemed to embrace the 'finds' more readily.

## From Chapter 11 (The Stag, the Brambles, the Wolves, and the Pool): the vision of counting coins

We faced some wolves. They had an unnatural howl that rooted me to the spot. And their eyes glowed. Others may say it is fear. I say it was magic. The creatures were quickly defeated, and then we took their pelts.

I was very pleased to seize their pelts. Because a dire wolf pelt is, by definition, conflict-free gold, and more importantly, the unnatural magic of this place may make them more valuable to the discerning collector.

After skinning the creatures, we continue deeper into the Whispering Wood, a place that makes the hairs on my arms prickle. It's more than just a strange energy; it's a disharmony, a discordant note in the natural world that offends my artistic sensibilities.

The fear of the wolves, a magical fear, prompted me to reflect on my own personal fears. Adabra said she thought I only did it for the music. It was the music in its purest essence that motivated me. But was it? Was I just afraid to admit that it was about the fans? I saw the fans at Stonehill Tavern so happy, and I realized it is about the fans. I won't be afraid of doing it for the fans. And if it is about the fans, and not my music, should I just stop trying to make them love what I love and instead do what they love?

And then we stumble into a clearing, and there in front of us is a pool of water.

I look carefully and realize that this is something different. The surface reflects a distorted version of ourselves. Valphine asks, "Will I see myself happy and content?"

I look at her more carefully. She looks at the pool, but avoids staring at it. It's almost as if she is afraid of being happy. As I was scared of admitting that I liked to play for the fans, I thus became consumed by my desire to focus solely on the music. And in my pursuit of music, I walked away from what truly made me happy. Was I sabotaging my music? Trying to make it less likable to the fans?

This pond may help me see more clearly. So I look in.

And there I see a version of myself clutching coins constantly. Is this the price of pursuing art or the price of pleasing my fans? Is this a version of me trying to convey a message to me?

I must know more. This is a planar scar, a window into fractured realities. I jump in.

The moment I step in, a dizzying cacophony assails my mind. It's "people, scenes, faces looking at me all at the same time". A barrage of fragmented visions, whispers, and fleeting emotions. This is knowledge, raw and unadulterated, but it's overwhelming. I try to hold onto my composure, but I cannot.

A chill seeps into my very being, a creeping sense of unease that twists my perception. Then, through the swirling chaos, I see a distorted creature pleading, saying, "He saw beyond, but only what he was meant to see. The numbers. Always the numbers." The numbers. My mind latches onto it. Is it the counting of "blood gold", the endless tally of "stolen merchandise"? The cold, hard accounting of a world driven by plunder rather than art? It's a terrifying vision, affirming my convictions about "conflict-free gold" and the "sordid morality" of this world.

The intensity is too much. I need to escape this place. I stumble out of the pool, feeling an immense drain on my energy, and then, without warning, I fall unconscious. The world simply... ceases to be.

## From Chapter 15 (Freeing Big Al, Petunia and Watching a Ranch Burn): the corpses I refused to plunder

The air here, near Butterskull Ranch, feels heavy, not with mist but with malice. We bypass the main trail completely, electing for a stealthy approach from the north. I keep my movements precise, channeling my focus—not on the combat I dread, but on the certainty that this is an opportunity for honest coin. This path, guided by information bought with minimal violence, is the artistic path.

We creep past the charred remains of the barn and smithy. Then, scattered near the outbuildings, I see them: dead bodies. Orcs and humans both. They are already swarming with flies. *Plunder.* My gorge rises. This casual aftermath of slaughter is precisely what I despise.

Others would plunder these corpses, and that plunder would help me get the music studio. And here, far from my friends, I could do this and none would be the wiser. Then I correct myself, "It is not what I do in front of anyone that sets my moral compass. But what I do when no one is looking. I will not scavenge them. That is not who I am and it is not my style."

My gaze is drawn to the fresh wounds on the orc bodies; they look ripped and mangled, injuries that suggest something much larger and more vicious than human bandits. This confirms Borg the Hammer is here, or something big.

Quietly, I slip toward the back of the house. As I get there I see more dead orcs. And I also notice open windows. where the cellar door is. It groans faintly, but my sleight of hand is sufficient. Downstairs, the air is earthy and cool. The cellar is lined with shelves holding peculiar skulls made of butter . But most importantly, there he is: a large figure tied to a chair, a burlap sack covering his head .

I quickly work the ropes.

"Are you Big Al Kalazorn?" I whisper, untying the man.

He is startled, but relieved. He is Alphonse "Big Al" Kalazorn, the man we were sent to find .

"You have a… there's more of you?" he asks, relieved .

"It's a rescue mission," I assure him .

He explains quickly, his voice hushed and hurried, that a band of orcs first raided the farm, but then the rot-tusk ogres, led by the half-orc Borg the Hammer, showed up, killed the orcs, and seized the property .

"I'm thinking that's what's going on. They kept you alive as the bait, huh?" I muse . Borg intended for the quest to draw us into a trap.

I urge him toward the stairs: we must retreat to where Corbin waits. But Big Al pauses.

"I really need my cow," he says, utterly distraught . "She's my prized possession. Without her, you know, the Butterskull Ranch is just another…" .

I look at him. A cow. This is the moment for negotiation. "Okay, we can find her cow. She's not here, you just said she's not here, so we need to leave and go look for her," I decide .

The reward is astonishing. "I got this suit of mithril chainmail that I'm willing to exchange for… You finding my cow," he offers .

This is substantial, legitimate coin. I must verify its source. "How did you come by Mithril Chain Mail, Big Al?" I press .

"I bought it," he insists. He explains that he's a retired veteran and former Sheriff of Triboar, the predecessor to Darathra. The chainmail is clean. It is untainted by blood or banditry. Fair trade.
