# CounterForce — Rimardo, Corrin, and Istus's Surprised Laughter

**What it is:** The campaign's cosmological counterweight to KP (see `KP.md`).
**Where it manifests:** As Rimardo and Corrin — apparently a touring comedy act, actually the echo of an interrupted avatar of Istus from another world.
**How it pays off:** Joke first, dread second. Comedy as prophetic sabotage.
**Status:** First on-screen manifestation queued for the Woodland Manse (playbill clue). First public-act manifestation queued for the Wayside Inn. Forward-planning hook into the Iymrith endgame.

---

## The mechanism

KP is encroaching on Savras's domain by treating prophecy as infrastructure: divination reduced to prediction, destiny reduced to management, the runestone's pursuit of fate converted into a controllable system.

Savras can see the violation, but he cannot easily fight it. **If he answers KP with more prophecy, more prediction, or more divine machinery, he only strengthens the logic KP is using.** A god of prediction cannot out-predict KP.

What bleeds through to fill the gap is not a command and not a conventional avatar. It is a *method* — borrowed from a foreign fate-current that originated on Oerth: **CounterForce**. Savras begins manifesting Rimardo and Corrin because they are the shape of fate fighting back without becoming another system. They are comedy as prophetic sabotage, a foreign fate-pattern that Savras catches without fully understanding why.

The reason he can catch it at all is KP. KP's multiversal optimization has damaged the boundaries between worlds enough for Savras to receive and express this foreign pattern, and the pattern gives Savras the one weapon KP cannot optimize: **absurdity that reveals the limits of control**.

Savras is not manifesting Istus herself. He is manifesting the echo of Istus's surprised laughter.

## The deep backstory: Istus's interrupted avatar

Before Rimardo and Corrin became a comedy problem, they were already a fate problem.

In the Drow Conspiracy (see `KP.md` for KP's role in this), Zuggtmoy, Iuz, Lolth, and the Elder Elemental God all converged on the same hidden prize: a dagger capable of rendering a god mortal for a day. Each power wanted it for obvious reasons. In ordinary heroic structure, the end of that adventure should have been simple. Four adventurers reach the final guardian, discover the owner or protector of the dagger, fight them, kill them, and claim or destroy the artifact.

That is not what happened.

**Rimardo decided the real problem was not the dagger. The real problem was that the dagger's protector had a broken marriage.**

So instead of resolving the climax through violence, Rimardo played marriage counselor. The protector (Lundbeams) was not defeated in the expected way. The protector handed the guardianship of the dagger to Bayard. KP, who was present and deeply invested in understanding the actual nature of the hidden artifact, never found out what the dagger was.

This was the first great surprise.

Rimardo and Corrin did not arrive at this climax neutral. They had reason to want revenge on Lundbeams, and Corrin clearly expected the scene to end in killing. Other voices around them pushed that direction too. The shock is that Rimardo did not simply choose mercy as a tactic. **He reversed his own revenge story because, at the end, he felt bad for Lundbeams.**

That matters because it makes Istus's surprise sharper. Fate had room for vengeance, greed, payment, heroism, mercy, and even foolish improvisation. What it had not comfortably accounted for was revenge becoming empathy through an absurd marriage-counseling bit. Rimardo did not refuse the climax. He changed what kind of climax it was.

On Oerth, Rimardo and Corrin were meant to become a paired avatar of Istus — Rimardo as the voice of fate's legal and theological inevitability, Corrin as the silent hand that makes inevitability happen.

Then they made the wrong, impossible, perfectly timed choice. Instead of becoming a solemn avatar of fate, they became a comedy routine.

This surprised Istus.

For a god of fate, surprise is not a minor event. Istus had not merely seen mortals disobey. Mortals disobey constantly. Rimardo and Corrin did something stranger: **they fulfilled the structure of the avatar while changing its genre.** The divine instrument still existed, but it had become a bit.

And Istus laughed.

That laugh changed her. It did not make her chaotic or turn her into a comedy goddess. It made her realize something more unsettling and more amusing: **the god of fate is also trapped by fate**. If everything has already been woven, then Istus herself is inside the pattern she governs.

The CounterForce offers the impossible exit. Fate can describe the pattern, tighten the thread, and prepare the avatar, but it cannot fully extinguish the human capacity to answer structure with timing, pity, refusal, and a joke that should not work but does. By embracing the CounterForce, Istus discovers how fate can escape fate: not by denying the weave, but by allowing absurdity, reversal, and compassion to become real features of it.

That is what cracks her up. **The loophole in fate is not outside fate. It is hidden inside it, wearing a cheap stage costume and ruining the solemnity of the divine plan.** The CounterForce does not prove that fate is false; it proves that fate is funnier, stranger, and less totalizing than systems like KP want it to be. Timing, reversal, empathy, and laughter became part of the thread.

This is the Oerth fate-pattern now leaking into the Forgotten Realms because of KP's work.

## Rimardo and Corrin in the Forgotten Realms — ontology

**Deliberately unresolved.** Whether the figures the party meets are:

- the real Rimardo and Corrin somehow transposed,
- echoes of them,
- prophetic masks Savras is shaping,
- accidental composites Savras is generating from a fate-current he is catching but not directing,

— is open. The DM should not commit publicly. The right answer at the table is *yes*.

What is consistent:

- **Rimardo** talks like the system. Legalistic, procedural, inevitable. He sounds like a contract clause that has decided to perform stand-up.
- **Corrin** is silent. He breaks the system through timing, misdirection, and the perfect impossible reveal — usually a card flipped, a prop produced, a beat held one count longer than it should be.
- Their comedy has rhythm, setup, and punchline. **Their appearances are not random. Their comedy is a map.**

## Why Vukradin is the receiver

A wizard would see a magical anomaly. A cleric would see an omen. A rogue would see misdirection. **A bard sees structure.**

Vukradin is uniquely positioned to read R&C correctly because he understands performance grammar. He can recognize that a joke that lands too cleanly was constructed, that a callback that closes too neatly was planned, that an audience reaction shaped too well was rehearsed. The R&C clues should preferentially be delivered to him (playbill, stage-vision, recognizing the rhythm of a ritual as ugly percussion).

If Soma or Valphine encounters R&C content first, run it as confusion or omen. The structural read belongs to Vukradin.

## Current arc — staged manifestations

### 1. Woodland Manse — the playbill (queued, not yet on table)

After the party disrupts part of Grannoc's ritual at the Manse — or after the battle, if you want to keep the reveal clean — they find an object that does not belong: a **comedy playbill folded into a triangle and pinned to a storm-marked tree with a tiny dagger**.

Front of the playbill:

> Rimardo and Mr. Corrin
> Predestination, Plunder, and Other Legal Fictions
> Special Woodland Engagement: The Boar Who Was Already Booked
> Woodland Manse
> Attendance: violently encouraged

Back of the playbill:

> One dragon, displaced by larger wars.
> One mountain tribe, displaced by the dragon.
> One forest, made crowded by consequence.
> One lodge, seized by the displaced.
> One storm cult, recruiting from the desperate.
> One thunder beast, scheduled for punishment.
> One ritual, using borrowed mathematics.
> One audience, not yet aware it is participating.
> Balance outstanding.
>
> Signed:
> R. and C.

**Corrin's mark is a tiny dagger hole through the phrase "borrowed mathematics."** That is the tell that points at KP.

The playbill is also the first crack in Falcon's framing of the Lodge — "*One lodge, seized by the displaced*" directly contradicts the Talosian-occupation story Falcon told the party.

### 2. Vukradin's stage-vision (when he touches the playbill or uses music near the Manse)

> Vukradin stands before a rain-soaked stage. The audience is hidden in darkness. Thunder provides the percussion. Rimardo stands in the spotlight, holding a ledger. Corrin waits beside him, silent, holding five cards.
>
> Rimardo says:
> *A monster is only random until someone sells tickets to the charge.*
>
> Corrin turns over four cards:
> **GIANT WAR**
> **CRYOVAIN**
> **FALCON'S LODGE**
> **GORTHOK**
>
> Then Corrin turns over a fifth card:
> **WAYSIDE INN**
>
> Rimardo looks irritated and says:
> *That was supposed to be the second act.*
>
> The vision ends with applause that sounds like rainfall, hoofbeats, and distant orc war-drums.

The vision does not mean the party should immediately abandon the Talos sequence and go to Wayside Inn. It means **the same hidden pattern continues there later**. This plants R&C as recurring figures without derailing the current objective.

### 3. Wayside Inn — first public appearance (next staged payoff)

At Wayside Inn (the High Road inn run by Martisha Vinetalker, where Teega the Smith — Kraken Society compromised — was last located), Rimardo and Corrin appear as a **real public act**. The show is funny, successful, and *professionally threatening to Vukradin*: their material is better than his, their rhythm is sharper, their audience read is uncanny.

During the routine, Corrin's silent tricks should reveal evidence of the next layer of the campaign: Talos residue (if K. is still active), Kraken Society influence (Teega, the Drowned Gate, the Splinter Colony schism), or KP-style planar control. The DM chooses which layer to surface based on where the campaign is when the party reaches the Inn.

**Tone rule:** Joke first. Dread second. The party should first react with laughter, then with the slow realization that the bit understood something it shouldn't have understood.

### 4. Optional Shrine of Savras confirmation

If the Shrine of Savras is later visited, the party finds a **second playbill already waiting for them**. This one is not a clue to the next ritual. It is a confirmation that Savras's prophetic machinery is the channel — even though Savras himself may not understand why these figures from a different world's fate-current keep appearing.

Shrine playbill line:

> Prophecy is what powerful people call a guess after it becomes profitable.

## How to play Rimardo and Corrin

- Rimardo speaks in procedural, contractual, mock-legalistic cadence. He treats prophecy as a billing problem. He is rarely cruel; he is bureaucratically inevitable.
- Corrin does not speak. He produces objects, flips cards, hands over notes, makes eye contact one beat longer than expected. His silence is the timing engine.
- Their jokes must scan as jokes first. Players should laugh before they get worried. If the table is just worried, the dread is undercut.
- They should **never appear as quest-givers**. They are a counterforce signal, not a sponsor. They do not tell the party what to do. They tell the party what is already happening, in the wrong genre, so that the party recognizes it.
- They should **never explain themselves**. The moment Rimardo monologues about being an avatar, the doctrine collapses into exposition. Let the players assemble it.

## Thematic role

Rimardo and Corrin are KP's natural enemy because:

- KP wants prophecy to behave like computation. R&C make prophecy behave like a comedy routine where the punchline is the truth.
- KP treats mortal agency as variance to be modeled. R&C *are* the variance — the moment of pity, the unexpected reversal, the choice no model predicted.
- KP wants information control. R&C broadcast the information KP most wants withheld (the structure of his own plan) and frame it as a setup-and-payoff joke.
- KP cannot interrogate them. He cannot capture them. He cannot dispel them. They are not technically present in any way that admits to capture. They are the echo of a laugh from another world.

Their job in the campaign is not to defeat KP. It is to ensure that when the party encounters KP's infrastructure, **the party has language for what they are looking at**. The playbill names the displacement chain so the party can act on it. The Wayside Inn act names whatever comes next so the party can recognize it. The CounterForce is the campaign's accessible diagnostic for KP's invisible work.

## The Iymrith collision (forward planning)

The endgame collision is Iymrith — see `KP.md` for the structural setup (Aletra's Phase 3 at Iymrith's Lair, the Planar Energy Conduit, Falcon's brass ring). When that arc reaches the table, **Rimardo and Corrin should bracket it**:

- **Before** the Iymrith arc: an R&C appearance whose card-flip or playbill names Iymrith, the Conduit, or the ring as already in motion. The party should recognize the pattern from the Manse/Wayside Inn precedent: this is the next "second act."
- **During or after** the Iymrith arc: an R&C beat that is no longer a joke — or, more precisely, the joke that lands as a horror. Suggested shape: the playbill that lists the displacement chain ends with a final line the party has never seen before, naming whatever loss the Iymrith arc cost them. *Balance outstanding.*

This is forward planning, not session prep. Specific staging waits until the Iymrith arc's shape is decided at the table. What matters now is that **the CounterForce is established early enough at the Manse and Wayside Inn that the Iymrith payoff doesn't feel like an introduction.** By the time R&C appear adjacent to Iymrith, the party should already know what their appearance means.

## Core line

**KP is trying to optimize fate into a controllable multiversal system.**

**Savras cannot fight that system by becoming more systematic. He fights back by manifesting the echo of Istus's surprised laughter: the CounterForce hidden inside fate.**
