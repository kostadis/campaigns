# Chapter 12 Confronting Adabra and learning of the Interventionists. 
### Soma
I feel like a shell sprout, wet from the nest, a child that has been taken advantage of. I trusted Adabra. And she took advantage of my trust to get me to stop the people who were trying to save our plane. 
Vukradin tries to calm me down, but I growl, “I want to chew her out!” 
The bard shrugs. 
As we enter the main square, we head to where she has set up shop, next to Garaele’s house, across from the Shrine of Luck. 

And just as luck would have it, Lyra and her allies also show up. 
Adabra opens her door and smiles. I am furious, so I throw out the blind and broad accusations, “What the hell! What was that!”  
The kind woman suddenly becomes cold. And then, with a very patronizing tone, she speaks slowly, her very words dripping with contempt, “I had such hope for you, Soma. I thought you heard the true whisper of the word. But you let the interventionists,” and she waves her hand at Lyra,” fill your head with the noise. They speak of cleansing and planar infections because they fear what they cannot control. You help them wound the world to make themselves feel righteous.” 
I stutter, “We didn’t help them.” I feel chastised and remember that we didn’t actually complete the ritual, and in some sense, I could argue that the ritual failed because of me, “You could say we interrupted the interventionists.” 
Adabra is confused. She points at Lyra and asks, “Then why are they here?” 
I explain that we saved them and add, “They didn’t deserve to die.” 
The old woman is suspicious and asks if we attempted to stop the ritual. I try to lie. I try to deceive her, but I am too old for those games that the hatchlings play. 

I then look at Vukradin and think that the silver-tongued bard should talk to her. He can convince anyone of anything. 
And he manages to confuse her just enough for her to forget about any betrayal. 

She asks about the Treant, and VUkradin smiles and says, “He’s in stable condition.” 

Adabra thinks he’s alive. I know he’s dead. But in stable condition, the condition of being dead. 
Lyra is furious and screams, “You are insane!” 
Vukradin will have none of it and says, “Let’s calm down. Do you want a beer?” 
I turn to Vukradin, “Can you tell Adabra that not all is lost? Everyone got something from this. The planar rift was not sealed. The interventionists are alive.” 

Vukradin smiles and goes to work. And soon, Adabra and Lyra are no longer about to come to blows. 
When Lyra is about to go on some long-winded rant, Vukradin again manages to deflect her need to speak and says, “Let’s go listen to some music? Wouldn’t that be nice?” 

But this time, Lyra is fed up and shouts, “If you cannot see the evil this woman represents and her fanaticism and its danger, then you are all fools.” 

### Vukradin

I just want to be friends with everyone. I don’t understand why people are so full of hate. I mean, I used to think that the key to everything was to play music that was better than my audience, but now I realize that playing popular music is the way to their hearts. And I just want them to love me. 

And this hate between Lyra and Adabra is making it hard for me to be friends with everyone. I mean I want to work with everyone, except the Zhentarim. 

The Zhentarim don’t quite fit with my personal goals of only making money from conflict-free treasure. 

As I watch Lyra leave, and her agent follow her, I grab one and ask if he could stay and get a drink. 

“I can’t stay now, but we’ll be back later if you are buying.” 

I nod. I know that Spiderman will pay. 
Adabra is still there and mutters, “The interventionists started this war on nature when they chose to reject the multiverse's diversity. Their rituals and cleanses are acts of violence.”

I can’t stand it, and shout, “Boooring.” 

She looks at me. 
“Boring.” 

She glares. 
“Everyone is so serious!” 
Adabra is taken aback, and she decides to try a different take, “You like music?   And isn't music supposed to be an explosion of insight and beauty that reaches from any point in the universe? Is there bad music? There's only music? You understand, Vukradin, what the interventionists believe is that some music is bad, just because they don't like it.”
Valphine smiles that peculiar smile of hers, “It’s bad because it crosses planar boundaries!” 

Poor Adabra, she might have had a point a few days ago, but I have learned that playing music that people love and enjoy is the essential art of music. 
“There is music that is bad,” I explain. 
Soma nods her head in agreement and adds, “Like, there are some planes that are bad for this one.” 
The  Vukradin charm worked its magic. Adabra is smiling at me as she says, “There is hope for you, Vukradin, and perhaps even Soma?” 
Soma shakes her head. 
Seeing the opening, I ask her why he and his fellow adventurers were dragged into this internal feud between the members of the Emerald Enclave.  
What I gather from her talk is that among the Emerald Enclave, calling anyone an interventionist is the worst thing you can call them because every member thinks of themselves as preserving nature, not intervening in nature. 
And then it hits me that I don’t want to be an interventionist. Being an interventionist means I have fewer fans.
And then Adabra starts talking again, it's boring, but I am bored, so I hear say, “Those of us who see the true harm of the interventionists have been reduced to begging for crumbs of resources, for being cast out and treated like we are mad and do not understand what is going on. I was forced, forced! To reach out to any ally I could find, and I thought that you and Soma understood the universal beauty of the multiverse.”  
She stops. 
“I was wrong about Soma, but perhaps I was right about you.”

I look at her and realize she misunderstood what I meant, but why be unpleasant and make an enemy out of a potential fan? I turn to her and say, “I am going to go play some music, will you join us?”  
She sighs, and before she leaves, she asks, “Is the treant still there?”

Soma is trying hard to say nothing. And I realize she’s waiting for me to say something. Should I say a half-truth, “It’s still there?” No, that would provoke too many follow-ups. I will tell her the truth: “I’m not really sure what happened. There was a massive planar tear, and it was right in the middle of that tear in the fabric.” 

Valphine jumps in, “And there was that creature.” 
Brilliant, the comment distracts her from her line of inquiry. After Valphine explains the strange nature of the creature and its behavior, Adabra appears to be in ecstasy and says, “ A symptom of the process, not the cause. It was likely drawn to the energy, a planar magpie. Did it… did you kill it too? Or did you stop to wonder what its purpose was in the grand design?” 
I look at her and say, “We didn’t intervene.” 

Adabra is pleased and hands me five conflict-free gold pieces. 

She continues for some time in some kind of ecstasy, saying words that mean nothing, about planes, and designs, and universalism, and I am bored. Soma later on tells me that she was spouting nonsens that was meaningless. I try hard not to say, “Well, I already knew that.” 

### Valphine
Adabra is a zealot. I have seen her kind and how they worship Lolth, convinced that there is some greater purpose to the universe, that the demonic powers they worship will grant them power, or that by serving them, they are serving some enduring power. 

Fools.

The only such thing is Lathander. A god who, every day, shows the glory of his power by pushing the darkness away. A god so powerful that he keeps the Drow underground. A god who causes so much exquisite pain.  

Vukradin turns to no one in particular and mutters, “If we sell these pelts, she’ll know what we did, but we need the money.” 
As we stand there in the main square, Corbin turns to Vukradin to offer to find a fence for the pelts. But Vukradin is not swayed. The ex-bandit swears it won’t be blood money, but the bard is not convinced.  
The Bard and his need for clean money, as if there is any money that is not dripping in blood. 

We decide to go to Barthen’s provisions. Barthen looks over the goods and offers a paltry sum. He doesn’t see the value that Vukradin sees. 

So we try the Lionshield Coster. There, we get a different reception from Linene Graywind, the 35-year-old woman manager. She looks pretty excited and asks us questions after question, trying to understand where we found it and how we found it. Vukradin is a fool and answers truthfully. This information could be ransomed for gold.  

Nonetheless, Linene offers us more than twice what Barthen offered. That is strange. I recall reading in Neverwinter that the merchants had an interplanar device that enabled them to ship goods, but it somehow stopped working.  
I am tempted to try to bargain some more, but Vukradin agrees to the price. 

He will never own a music studio, given how freely he shares information. 

Linene takes the pelts and says how her bosses will be very interested in this particular planar effect. 

As we leave, Linene asks whether we will perform tonight. Vukradin nods. And then she asks, much to my gratification, if I will play because she feels that it is I who makes the ensemble shine. 

Vukradin is annoyed and then remembers his goal to be beloved by all; why argue when you can say yes and profit? 
At the Stonehill Tavern, our music set is - off. I am, of course, perfect, but Vukradin is just not getting his vibe right. I wonder if it’s because he has lost his edge, and has now just become another people pleaser? 
After a few more songs, which are received well, but not as well as the last time we played, the members of the Emerald Enclave arrive and sit with us. 

They sit at our table after Vukradin invites them. And then he starts with the questions, “So are you sticking with Lyra?” 
The oldest among them drinks from the beer that Spiderman has brought to us and says, “ We've sworn our lives and our honor to the Emerald Enclave, and Lyra was just upset. The quest… her goal was to save the tree and to save the forest, and all that happened was… and that all failed, and she is very …”
