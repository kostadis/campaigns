# Chapter 04 Saving Adabra

## 04-01 Taraskh 1495

### Vukradin 

Corbin stands before Vukradin. Corbin is a gaunt man in his late twenties, with the haunted, hollowed-out look of someone who has stared into the abyss and found it staring back. His hair is a matted, dirt-brown tangle, and a patchy, uncared-for beard clings to his jaw. His eyes, a pale blue, are often distant and unfocused, but can sharpen with a desperate, feral intensity when threatened. He wears ill-fitting leather armor scavenged from a dead man, and his movements are tinged with a weary resignation, punctuated by moments of nervous energy.

Vukradin thinks for a moment and is displeased, “Is that stolen armor?” 

The look on Corbin’s face is enough for Vukradin. He knows. 

Brewbarry pushes, “Vukradin asked you\!” 

Vukradin examines Corbin’s facial features, and the fear in Corbin’s eyes, and the doubt, and finally Corbin answers, “The owner was already dead?” 

The bard is outraged; this is why the world is such a mess. Taking the armor off the dead is the final act of treachery and thievery of a world gone mad. 

“I had no choice.” 

Vukradin notices the sweat on Corbin’s face. He stares impassively. 

“I was a bandit. And it was either wear this armor or wear nothing and die. I don’t want to die.” 

“Well, Corbin, working for us, you will be taking care of our camp. Therefore, no armor is required. Take it off,” commands Vukradin. 

“Mr Brewbarry, can I please keep my armor?” 

Vukradin inwardly sniggers as Brewbarry wears none. 

“I don’t wear any.” 

The despair on the bandit’s eyes upsets Vukradin and so he relents, “We need to get you some proper fair trade armor.” 

The bandit is relieved, sighs, and says,” Well, until then I will wear this to protect myself.” 

A spider suddenly squeaks, moves its arms frantically and points at Corbin as if it is trying to ask a question.

Corbin sees the spider and freaks out. 

Vukradin laughs, “That’s Soma, our tortle.’ 

### Soma  

This spider's body is fun. But I can’t communicate. I mean, I can, but only with other spiders. But seeing the world from this angle, with these eyes, teaches you so much about the natural world and the natural order. I smell things and see things and taste things that I didn’t know existed. It’s an explosion of senses. 

And no more. The time has passed and I am tortle again. 

The bandit is in shock. I look at him and say, “It is true I am tortle. And why do you need armor?” 

The bandit answers back, “Because I am human.” 

I look at his squishy form and think how it's strange that this race has dominated so much of Faerun.

And we continue our journey until we reach a hill, atop of which is a windmill, a set of cairns, and a ruined building. 

Atop the windmill is, quite obviously, a manticore. And soon I can hear the voice of a woman coming from the building, “Help me\!” 

I think about how best to advance but Valphine starts asking questions. 

“What do you do in the overbright when there’s people being attacked by monsters?

“She looks like she’s in need. We would usually try and save the sentient life.” 

Valphine turns to Vukradin and asks, “We were told to bring her back.” 

I know where this is going, “Yes. But it was implied alive.” 

“So we must kill the manticore?” 

And of course Vukradin has an angle, “What if we killed the Manticore, returned to Phandalin, demanded more money, and then came back and brought her to the village?”

I look at him and roll my eyes. “How about we just kill the manticore and save her?”  

And with that, we begin to climb up the hill quietly, trying to avoid drawing attention. 

Behind, I hear Corbin ask Vukradin, “Hey why don’t you leave your musical instrument here, so it won’t get damaged.” 

I want to laugh, but it would be too loud.  

“No.” 

The bandit has this look of terror on his face: “I just wanted to make sure it doesn’t get damaged.” 

He is a little child, lost, and I have to help. 

“Hey Vukradin, I can always cast mending.” 

Vukradin looks at me and says, “Thank you.” 

I stare at him, confused. 

“I am not the kind of musician who thinks that hands are better than magical tools.” 

I smile. The bard needs my help, and guidance. 

Vukradin turns to me, “Let’s go quietly.” He points to the manticore a distance away. I nod. 

And although Vukradin is silent, I am not. 

The manticore perks its head at us and can see us all.

I missed my chance to talk to it.  

### Valphine

The creature roars. I put one of the bolts in my hand crossbow, and target the creature. 

And of course Vukradin has to offer an opinion, “I thought we were going to return those magical items? We are a fair trade group\!” 

I look at him and wonder how his kind even exists, and shoot. 

Was it the distraction or the natural toughness of the creature, but it’s unaffected. 

“That hurt,” it howls. 

“It can speak?” I say to no one in particular. 

The creature shouts, “I am just trying to get a tasty meal\!” 

Vukradin shouts, “Perhaps we can pay you?” 

“It would take a lot\!” 

“We have very little.” 

The creature snarls, “Maybe this will help motivate you.” It directs its anger at me and fires a volley of tail spikes that hit me. 

Then its voice echoes over the land, “Perhaps this will motivate you to find more money?” 

As I examine my wounds, I hear Vukradin shout a command. The spell doesn’t work. 

And I hear the monster laugh. And then it stops laughing, “I see a human. I will happily take him and leave you alive.” 

A fair trade, I would have thought back in a different life. 

Soma, who cares about everyone of us, shouts, “What?”

“I prefer the succulent taste of human meat to the flesh of other humanoids. Let me have him, and I will leave you and this woman alone.’ 

I wonder what Soma will do, and she casts a spell, conjuring a familiar that she orders to fly into the mouth of the Manticore. The creature snaps its jaws shut. 

The goal was to provide it with a tasty morsel. But all it does is enrage it further. 

“That was not satisfying\! Where’s the meat\!\!\! The blood? The tasty crunch\!” 

Soma is charging. I know that look—the look of a mother who won’t let someone touch her clan. 

Seeing everyone advance, I advance. 

Brewbarry has arrived close enough to throw his hand axes. 

The monster looks at the barbarian and shouts, “Maybe you are tastier” after one of the axes hits the wings of the manticore. And it flies and rends Brewbarry with its claws. Brewbarry simultaneously strikes it with his halberd as it flies in. 

Both antagonists are injured, but it is the manticore that flies to the second floor howling in pain. 

“Please do something,” comes a frantic voice from the first floor of the windmill. 

Soma tries to reassure the woman, “Stay calm\!” 

“Do something,” the woman shrieks. 

Soma stares with that look of confusion, “You could run out while it’s distracted?” 

“It could attack me\!” 

I observe Vukradin wondering what to do. I know that look, the look of a gambler. “Should I try again?” 

Soma is not a gambler. She shouts, “He’s unlikely to avoid falling to your spell twice\! I believe in you.” 

And of course, the Manticore is unaffected. And of course Vukradin is annoyed, “Well, I didn’t study maths or chance\! I studied the arts\! I trusted you, Soma\!” 

The tortle looks hurt, “How can I make it up to you?” she says in her matronly voice. 

“I don’t know if I can ever trust you\!” says the aasimar. 

I stare at Soma, who looks visibly hurt.  

I place another crossbow into my handheld crossbow and shoot, and miss. 

Thankfully, Brewbarry is still alive. He climbs to the second floor where the Manticore is hiding, and hits it twice. The creature howls in pain. And being ignorant, attacks Brewbarry, whose rage protects him from the worst of the Manticore’s claws. 

I notice how the creature seems to be flying away, and I wonder where and there in the distance, I can see Corbin. The beast has decided to eat Corbin.

Vukradin and Soma strike her with their spells. And now the killing blow is all mine, and my hand-held crossbow bolt misses again. 

I stare in disbelief at my crossbow, frustrated. 

“YES\!\!\!” screams Brewbarry. He has killed the Manticore. 

### Vukradin

I cut off the creature’s head and then march to the windmill, the bloody head in tow. 

“Ma’am, you can come out now\!” 

Soma tries her soothing tone, “It’s safe now.” It's the tone of a mother to a small child. 

The woman who greets me at the windmill door is clearly past her prime, though her age is difficult to pinpoint. Her form is that of a commoner, neither remarkably tall nor short, with a lean and perhaps slightly stooped posture, as if weighed down by endless burdens or years spent hunched over work. Her hands, though calloused and bearing the faint, persistent scent of herbs and perhaps antiseptic, seem capable of both healing and, you sense, great precision. Her face, weathered by the winds of Umbrage Hill, is often set in a grim, unyielding line, almost as hard as the stone of her windmill. But it's her eyes that truly stand out: a piercing, perhaps cold, gaze that seems to miss nothing, burning with an inner intensity that hints at her fanatical convictions and ruthless determination. Her hair, likely a practical shade of grey or brown, is pulled back simply, betraying no vanity, only purpose. She wears plain, functional clothing, perhaps stained with remnants of her work, emphasizing her pragmatic nature. 

I straighten up, “Are you Adabra?”

Soma says soothingly, “It’s safe\!” 

And, of course, Valphine tries to be helpful, “I could teach you about Lathander\!” 

“Is it dead?” 

Brewbarry, ever helpful, waves the dead tail. 

I am confused that she didn’t guess we had won when she heard the big thump of a body hitting the ground. She explained that she wasn’t certain if it was one of us the creature had picked up and dropped, or the beast itself. And she was very determined not to tempt fate. 

Soma tries to find out something about how long she has been here under the siege of the manticore, and we discover it’s only been a short time. 

I try to get her to come with us, but she refuses. And just then she rushes back into the building and comes out with a vial and announces, “This is my special potion of healing with extra scent and taste that makes it go down sweeter\!” 

Soma, who lived near a city and saw more than her share of rich people use scents when they bathe, mutters, “That sounds like a bath water potion.” 

That doesn’t help us get her to come to town. She’s very determined to stay here. Insisting that she must stay here to craft her potions. 

There is a deeper agenda at play here—an agenda I don’t quite understand. 

### Soma

I am trying to make up lost time as a druid, and this woman has something about her. She looks at me. 

“You are a tortle?” she asks. And I nod. And then she asks me something very odd, “In the great schism, are you on the naturalist or interventionist side?” 

I don’t know what she is saying, but I sense that in my heart I would be with those who want to preserve, not intervene, and say, “Naturalist.” 

Her aura and sense of being change. There is a sudden shift in her body. 

“Very good.” And then she starts to speak with a zeal about events that I don’t quite understand involving a group called the Emerald Enclave \- “The naturalists, like us, think that the meta-verse is part of the natural order. We should accept the divine interventions as part of the natural order.” 

I look at her, and smile, she just wants to keep things the way they should be, “We should accept this as part of the natural order.” 

“Wonderful,” she sighs. “Now, as part of my role in the natural order, I must stay here and continue to craft potions.” 

Vukradin has other plans and tries to convince her to come with us, explaining that it would be safer for the villagers if they didn’t have to hike out in the open with a white dragon flying about. She smiles and says, “The natural order requires that they come here.” 

Then she stops and looks at me, “You are the ones Townmaster Harbin was looking for? I recall he sent a note to his Lordship asking for help. His Lordship promised that help. Are you it?” 

When we tell her yes, she sighs. “In that case I will stay here, safely protected by you.” I'm not sure if she's mocking us or being earnest. 

Vukradin could convince her through his music, “Can you try?” 

He plays a tune, and it is a good tune. Brewbarry appears delighted. 

“He really loves your music?” I tell Vukradin. 

The Aasimar sighs, “I would survey my customers and ask them to give me a score out of ten. I would only accept money from those who gave it a 10/10 rating. Brewbarry was, in fact, the only customer that ever paid me.” 

I stare at him, aghast. He is a child. A naive babe that needs protecting.  

But Adara seems delighted and exclaims, “How wonderful\! This is truly blessed music\! Aasamirs are truly touched by the Celestial planes.”

And yet it doesn’t change her determination to stay. I wonder if I had had that determination, I would still be back home with my family. So I prepare to leave the windmill. 

“Wait,” she shouts. 

And comes back with a note. 

Vukradin takes it. When she permits him to read it, he reads it out loud: “Thank you, Townmaster Harbin, but I will not be leaving my home. Thank you for your help. Please reward these heroes.” 

And then he says, “Wait, here’s a long-winded description of how to take some medicine. Are you healing him?”

Adabra looks at Vukradin, “Yes.” 

With the note in hand, I shrug and begin the long trek back, when of all people Brewbarry asks if we can spend the night here. 

Adabra squeals in delight, “Yes\! I will make a fantastic meal\! But I need help.” 

### Valphine

Corbin arrives at the windmill. He looks even worse than he did before. And now he smells. 

Vukradin again begins the interrogations asking if he robbed Adabra. Even when Adabra insists no one has ever robbed her, Vukradin persists. 

The inquisition of the Aasimar is exquisite. 

Corbin breaks down, “I didn’t want any of this. I woke up with nothing but the clothes on my back after my family was killed. I came to town, and nobody wanted another broken man. So I went into the forest, and then the crew told me to join. And I joined.” 

So he’s another lost soul. Perhaps Lathander can help. But Vukradin has already tasked him with helping Adabra. 

As the food gets prepared, Adabra stands up, “Well let me show you around. Outside here is an old dwarven house.” 

And of course, Vukradin has to start asking if she stole the house. “We’ve encountered a lot of people taking things, thinking they are entitled.” 

Adabra looks at him, hurt. She explains that the house was destroyed before she came here, that she has a deed to the land, and that she has tended to the dwarf cairns for the dead. 

“I am quite human, not a grave robber. That’s why the manticore wanted me\!” 

Soma, who although younger in years, is wiser, looks at the old woman’s casual specism and mutters, “He thought you were just meat. That’s why he wanted you.” 

But then we learn from Adabra, that Manticore’s actually do have a preference for human meat. 

Imagine that. 

“And you were not adequately trained by your druidic masters. Well then let me help you Soma, the Manticore has a peculiar taste for human flesh over all other humanoids.” 

Soma, whose story of her training is still a mystery to me, looks deflated and grouses, “Whatever.” 

The room is silent while Adabra makes a meal of hearty, fresh-baked bread, a vegetable and herb stew made with local fungi, foraged vegetables, berries, and wild greens.

Overbrighters make the best meals. It’s all because of Lathander’s glory\! The sun-god fuels the ground and the food, and it is \-just- tastier. 

### Soma

Soma was annoyed with Adabra. Her druidic master, Meril, had tried his best in his one year near her home. And it was his prompting that led her to follow Vukradin. Yes, there were gaps, but she didn’t have the time to train because she had so little time left. And to be reminded.

After dinner,I sit and listen to Corbin’s tale of woe as he recounts it to me: “I wasn't born into this life. I was a farmer from a small, forgettable village called Greenmeadow, nestled in the plains east of the High Forest. My life was simple and good; I worked the land my father had worked, was married to a woman named Elara, and had a young daughter, Lily, who loved the wildflowers that grew at the edge of our fields.

Then, the world shattered. The Ordning, which structured the giants' world, broke, and chaos swept across the North. The Giants became emboldened, and lawless destruction ensued. One smoke-filled afternoon, the Uthgardt barbarians of the Black Lion tribe descended on Greenmeadow. They weren't raiding for supplies; they were a storm of pure destruction, a wildfire in human form, proving their strength in a world gone mad.

I fought. I was no warrior, armed only with a wood-axe and a farmer's grit, but I fought for my home and my family. It was not enough. I watched my fields burn, my home collapse into embers, and my family… I watched them fall. I only survived because I was knocked unconscious by a charging barbarian's shield and left for dead in a ditch, buried under the body of my neighbor.

I awoke to silence and ash. With nothing left but the clothes on my back and a soul scoured clean of hope, I began to walk. I drifted south for months, a ghost haunting the roads, taking what I could to survive—a half-eaten apple from a cart, a discarded blanket, a few coins from a sleeping traveler's purse. Each petty crime chipped away at the man I had been, but it was a necessary act to simply keep living.” 

This man's journey through life was changed by visitors. He saw death and horrors. I met Meril. By the grace of the gods… 

I think. And then I sleep.
