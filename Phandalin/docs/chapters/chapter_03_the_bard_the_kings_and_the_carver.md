Vukradin 

Vukradin, ever the theatrical one, taunts the grotesque creature before them. “We have it outnumbered! It’s stunned! We shall kill it before it lays a hand on us!” His voice booms, filled with an almost reckless confidence that, at times, borders on delusion.

But the battle is not going as planned. Brewbarry, the stalwart warrior, struggles in vain to pull his halberd free, the weapon seemingly stuck fast in some unseen impediment. Soma's usually potent puff of poison, a sickly green cloud that had felled countless foes, dissipates harmlessly around the creature, leaving it utterly unaffected. Even the nimble mimic, usually so precise, misses its strike at Brewbarry, its attack sailing wide.
“You will not touch us!” Vukradin declares, attempting to weave another of his magical incantations, a spell he’d once described as ‘utterly foolproof’ for its ability to deter. But this time, the mimic, with an unnerving flicker in its multifaceted eyes, seems wise to his magical words. It remains utterly unaffected, its alien intelligence seemingly having adapted to his parlor tricks.
At this point, Soma, usually reserved and focused on the immediate threat, mutters under her breath, a rare show of frustration. “Unnatural magical creations that have no place in the material world.”
Vukradin, with his usual impeccable timing for tangential conversations, turns to her, a glint of curiosity in his eyes. “Wait, you’re a naturalist druid?”
Soma looks at him, her expression a mix of disbelief and annoyance that he would choose this precise moment, mid-battle, to discuss theological taxonomy. “Yes,” she replies, her voice clipped. “Constructs, extra-planar creatures, are all abominations. Disruptions to the natural order.”
Vukradin, unable to resist, presses on, a mischievous glint in his eye. “What about Aasimar’s and Tieflings?” He pronounces the words with a dramatic flourish, clearly relishing the debate.
Soma looks at him, truly confused this time. “What are those?” she asks, her brow furrowed. The question, delivered with genuine ignorance, clearly takes Vukradin by surprise.
Vukradin, momentarily flustered, mutters, “Aasimar are touched by Celestials, Tieflings by the Abyss!” He gestures vaguely upwards and then downwards, as if sketching the celestial and infernal realms in the air.
Soma, however, remains unimpressed by his poetic flair. She looks at him with a steady, pragmatic gaze. “You’re just another creature born into this world,” she states, her voice calm and unwavering. “There is no celestial touching here.”
Vukradin’s face contorts in annoyance. His poetic license, a cornerstone of his self-proclaimed identity, had been challenged. “I am a poet,” he declares, his voice rising in indignation, “and I get to take whatever poetic license I want. And I will not have you deny my allusion.”
Meanwhile, oblivious to the philosophical debate raging behind him, Brewbarry is about to unleash a powerful, albeit desperate, punch with his bare fists when Vukradin, ever observant despite his theatricality, asks, “You have no ranged weapons.”
Brewbarry, realizing his oversight, thumps his head with a meaty hand, a moment of exasperated self-reprimand, and then, from some hidden pouch, pulls out a pair of gleaming handaxes.
And then, with a final, decisive strike, Soma finishes it off, her earlier philosophical musings giving way to efficient, deadly action. The unnatural creature, a temporary blight on the material world, is no more.

### Valphine
I watch as Vukradin attempts to negotiate with the two Kings. And I sigh.
King Korboz complains that King Gnercli “Doesn’t listen to him and believe a single word he has to say.”

“I find it rude that you didn’t believe your king.”

“We work together,” explains Gnercli, his voice tense.

Gnercli explains, “This is a dual monarchy, not a single monarchy. So we argue until a decision is made.”
I can barely keep it together; this is too ridiculous.
Vukradin won’t stop. “How do you decide who wins?”
“The best argument wins,” insists Gnercli.
“And who decided which is best?”
King Korboz, who has recovered from his fear, explains, “Together.”
I now have to insert myself: “You need a clear approver structure.” I have seen cultures like these and have enjoyed tearing them apart. They pretend to be a commune, but have more betrayals, violence, and politics than even the Drow Noble Houses.
Will Vukradin explode their happy commune?

“Well, we elect another.”

As I walk past the two inventors, I realize that they have been overhearing us.
Fiddlestib tells Vukradin, “I am in favor of a bicameral legislature with a unitary executive!”
Dabbledob, trying to one-up her, explains, “I prefer more kings! That way we could have a vote!”
Vukradin tries to create more chaos, like a good Drow, and says, “I think you should be king!”
But the two inventors are more interested in building a tamper-proof voting box. Or rather, arguing over whether such a thing is possible.
