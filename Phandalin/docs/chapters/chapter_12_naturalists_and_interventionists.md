# Chapter 11 Naturalists and Interventionists

### Soma

The air itself tastes wrong as we push through the last line of dense trees and step into the clearing. It’s perhaps sixty feet across, but something here is fundamentally off. My fur, thick and protective in this bear form, prickles with an unease that goes beyond simple predator instinct. The woods surrounding us don’t lean; they *twist*, their bark gleaming like polished obsidian under the faint, sickly light filtering through a canopy that looks less broken and more *fractured*.

Pools of iridescent sap shimmer on the ground, their colors shifting in a hypnotic dance that makes my bear brain recoil. This isn’t the healthy glow of life; it’s an alien sheen. The underbrush, usually a soft carpet for my paws, is blackened and thorny, snagging at my thick hide. It feels like walking through a wound in the world.

At the heart of it all stands an immense tree, a Treant, its limbs bent in impossible geometries, dripping that glowing, unnatural sap into a dark pool. And near it, faint, flickering tears in reality pulse softly, whispering not of power, but of a madness I want no part of. There's nothing fair or right about such power. This place is… a blight—an unnatural disruption.

My keen bear senses pick up movement, and then the distinct, desperate scent of fear. There, trapped amidst the thorny undergrowth, are several figures. Members of the Emerald Enclave, clearly. I wonder what brings them to such a twisted place, but then one of them, a woman whose scent speaks of leadership, shouts.

"My name is Lyra. Help us\! We're out of the Emerald Enclave\! This thing\! It was the Grove's Guardian, but it has been corrupted. We are trying to cleanse her of a planar infection when it turned on us. Please, fight it\! Don't let the corruption spread\!"

Her words reverberate through my mind. *Corrupted.* *Planar infection.* It’s precisely what Adabra and my own deepest instincts have warned me about: these constructs and extra-planar abominations, these disruptions to the natural order. This Treant, this once noble guardian, is no longer itself. It has been twisted, warped by forces that do not belong here.

I cannot speak in this form, so a low growl rumbles in my chest, a bear's affirmation of understanding and simmering defiance. Lyra wonders out loud if I am a druid or a bear. *Let her figure it out on her own*, a dry wit only I can appreciate in this moment.

The thorny brambles, these blighted vines, are creatures themselves, holding the elves captive. I saw them earlier. They won't actively attack unless we get too close, but they prevent spellcasting, binding the Enclave members physically. 

Vukradin, ever the loose pebble, rolls his eyes and tries to *Command* a vine. The vine simply ignores him, much like a stubborn sapling shrugs off a light breeze. He prefers his "command performances" to effective action. He stares at the vine, defeated. He is still wet from the nest and needs to be protected. So I act. 

My eyes fall upon the Treant itself, towering and sinister. Its bark is glossy obsidian, its limbs stretch in impossible angles, and its movements appear as if you blinked and then saw the creature in a different position, but you never blinked.  This is not the ancient, wise guardian of a natural grove.

The Treant then speaks. Its voice is overlapping whispers resonating with an arrogance that chafes against my naturalist beliefs: "Stay out of my grove. If you stay out of my grove and let me be, then I will not attack you". It is making a claim of ownership over a place.

Adabra said that there was a blight and that the interventionist had to be stopped. What is the blight? Where is the blight? 

Is this strange magic the blight? Where are those who are intervening? Are they the Emerald Enclave?

### Valphine

My drow eyes, ever keen to the subtle distortions of reality, watch Soma – still in her bulky bear form – lumber towards the blighted brambles. Her brute force approach is, at least, direct, a refreshing change from Vukradin's performative posturing. The thorny tendrils lash out, predictably. I see them strike her, and though she is a sturdy creature, I note the faint wince, the minor disruption to her composure as four points of damage are inflicted. It is a taste of pain, perhaps not gloriously searing, but present nonetheless.

She swings her massive paws in retaliation, a primal, unrefined attack. The first misses, a clumsy flailing that almost makes me sigh. Then, with a surprising lack of grace, the second connects... with herself. A bear hitting itself in the midst of combat. How utterly inefficient. The Treant, I note, finds this amusing. Indeed.

It is at this point that Lyra, the trapped Emerald Enclave leader, calls out to me, her voice cutting through the unnatural whispers of the grove. "A cleric of a drow that is a cleric of Lathander? Can this be?" she exclaims, her tone a mix of disbelief and perhaps a touch of awe. I meet her gaze, a small, knowing smile touching my lips. Yes, it can be. The surface world is so easily surprised by the obvious. I offer a simple, honest truth, a declaration of my paradoxical devotion: "Glorious lightbrining pain.” My new god's power is a brilliant, beautiful agony, a sensation far more profound than the petty squabbles of drow houses or druids.

Soma, recovering from her self-inflicted embarrassment, manages to land a solid blow, against the blighted vine. Two of the ensnared elves – Lyra and another – are finally freed. Lyra immediately produces four crude stones, a dramatic flourish common among surface dwellers. "We need to complete the ritual," she announces, "on the stones to clear… to seal the rift". She explains what we need to do, tricky and dangerous. And if we fail three times, we failed. Drow rituals were precise, brutal affairs, often with far more immediate consequences for failure. This reliance on a series of successes, with such a generous margin for error, speaks of a certain softness. Still, I think, a ritual implies a structure, a focus of power. Perhaps it is not entirely without merit.

Brewbarry steps forward, his usual gruff demeanor punctuated by a sudden surge of power. He grows in size, his already formidable frame expanding to grant him a fifteen-foot reach. A brute, certainly, but sometimes sheer mass has its uses. He targets another blighted vine, swinging his weapon with typical barbarian abandon. It is a miss. His efficiency could use refinement.

Then, with an almost oblivious confidence, he steps into the Treant's immediate vicinity, the aura of corrupting planar energy that whispers promises of madness. The "doom zone," as Vukradin might call it. He is forced to contend with its insidious influence, and to my mild surprise, his primal will is enough to see it for what it is. The Barbarian is more resilient than he appears, or perhaps his mind is already too comfortably situated in its own unique form of chaos for this additional madness to take hold. "You mean more than I am already?" I recall him saying, a flicker of dark humor that resonates with my own understanding of existence. Good. Less unexpected chaos for me to contend with.

Now, it is my turn. With the blighted vines still holding the remaining Emerald Enclave members, and Brewbarry having failed to make a significant impact, a more… refined approach is clearly required. I lift my hand, calling upon the raw, glorious power of Lathander. The light of the Morninglord, a searing balm to my drow senses, gathers in my palm.

I unleash a Guiding Bolt, a focused spear of pure light, directly at the still-resilient vines. It strikes true, a solid hit. The unnatural pulsing of the brambles visibly flickers, their sickly colors momentarily dulled as the divine energy scorches them. They are weakened now and with the brilliance of Lathander's light still clinging to them, they now offer an advantage to the next attacker.

A tactical advantage. A demonstration of focused, directed pain. This is the beauty of Lathander's power as I perceive it: not a gentle warmth, but a potent, purifying fire that cuts through corruption and illuminates the path to victory. It is gloriously painful in the lights, a sensation that still thrills me to my core. The surface dwellers may not understand my devotion, but its efficacy in battle is undeniable.

###  Vukradin

The grotesque dance continues, a discordant movement in this wretched grove. My *Starry Whip*, a personal composition of biting starlight, lashes out at the corrupted Treant. A satisfying, if not exactly operatic, blow. It connects, which is always preferable to a mere command performance.

Now for this so-called "ritual". Six successful actions required, three failures and this entire composition unravels. The stakes, while not truly artistic, are present. Lyra, still catching her breath, seems to believe that we – this motley ensemble – can achieve what her Emerald Enclave could not. I consider the four crude stones she conjured – aesthetically lacking, but a task is a task. I attempt the ritual, but alas, I fail. A dismal failure. "Failure. I love it. One failure down," I quip, the words tasting of bitter irony. It is, in its own way, a testament to the unpredictable nature of existence, or perhaps simply a commentary on the universe's refusal to appreciate true finesse. As if to punctuate my artistic lament, the planar energies of this blight exact a toll and inflict a dull, throbbing bass note in my mind.

The Treant, this warped guardian, spouts another bombastic declaration of self-importance. "You think I'm wrong\! You think there is something wrong with me\! That I must be changed back\! I am… happy to be powerful and dangerous\!" it booms, its voice an overlapping chorus of whispers, a cacophony of self-delusion. It then, with a predictable lack of dramatic flair, attacks one of the Enclave’s Warriors who miraculously survives with a single hit point. A missed opportunity for a truly tragicomic scene. Lyra, bless her earnest heart, unleashes a Moonbeam, only to be re-ensnared by the Treant's insidious vines. The cycle of futility, truly.

Brewbarry, ever the bumbling but earnest brute, then stomps forward, cleaving another vine and even manages to hurt the Treant itself. He is within the aura of madness, the "doom zone", but shrugs off its psychic caress with an almost insulting resilience. Perhaps his mind is already too comfortably situated in its own unique form of chaos for this additional madness to take hold. Valphine, ever the pragmatic drow, unleashes *Fairy Fire*, illuminating our foe with a rather beautiful glow, and then sends a crossbow bolt at a vine. A decent display of focused pain, if one appreciates such uninspired directness.

Then Soma, dropping her bear form, attempts her hand at the ritual. She focuses her wisdom and… Our first ritual success\! Perhaps there's hope for these crude, terrestrial rituals after all. Still, the planar energies extract their toll with more pain for lingering in the aura. She then conjures another Moonbeam, bathing the Treant in a pale, ethereal light inflicting pain. The beast reels, a promising crescendo\!

It's my turn again. The second enclave warrior, poor soul, is at death's door. Principles demand action, even if it momentarily delays my artistic pursuits. I move to him, performing a quick burst of healing—and he is restored. Enough to pull him back from the precipice, if only for a moment. Then, with a renewed sense of purpose, I address the ritual. Lyra’s ritual requires finesse of hand.  Soma, in her matronly way, offers to help—a testament to her burgeoning understanding of collaborative art, even if the medium is less refined than I would prefer.  A success\!. Two successes, one failure. We are, as the commoners say, in the game.

Brewbarry follows, who, for all of his behavior, seems to have a deep wellspring of wisdom, attempts a ritual... Another success. Three successes, one failure. We're practically proficient\! But then Soma, with a clumsy flourish, tries and fails. Our second ritual failure. Before I can even process the dramatic irony, Soma and the first Enclave Warrior deliver the final, unceremonious blows to the Treant. The corrupted guardian falls, not with a bang, but with a whimper of poison and arrows—a rather inelegant final cadenza.

### Soma 

The tree dies. And the ritual is not yet finished. I see one of the stones. I will do the ritual.

And then I pause. Is helping Lyra the right thing to do? Is she telling me the truth that this is a disruption in the natural order, and that she is trying to set things right? Or is this the natural order, and Adabra is right?  

Vukradin turns to her and asks, “Was your plan to come here with your friends and perform this ritual on your own? A ritual this complicated?” 

Vukradin turns on the charm, and Valphine, with a noblesse d’oblige, turns it on even more. 

Vukradin is upset. 

Valphine turns to him and says, “You surface dwellers are so easy to read.” 

Lyra turns to them both, “I don’t think I thought this through very carefully.” 

Vukradin rolls his eyes, “Understatement of the year.” 

But the druid continues, “There was talk amongst the emerald enclave that we should do something. Some said we should do nothing, saying it was part of the natural world, and others said it was not.” 

I am confused; this is making no sense. “Are you suggesting that this is natural and you are trying to intervene and stop us from stopping it?”  

Lyra stares at me, confused.

I am confused. 

She looks at me and sighs, “The naturalists think this,” and she waves her hand at all of the unnatural effects that surround me,” is natural.”  

Adabra would not lie. They are lying. I am tempted first to kill her. And then I am tempted to attempt the ritual and purposely fail, and then I remember the staff my mentor gave me. A staff that has the power to detect unnatural elements in the natural world. 

And the staff glows, and it tells me that the treant, the pools, and the bushes are all unnatural. This planar rift is not part of the natural world. 

This makes no sense. The interventionists want to protect the natural world from the planar intrusions, and the naturalists wish to safeguard the planar intrusions. 

I want to protect the natural order. And then I realize that I, too, am still wet from the nest. All the time I spent in my forest, I never stopped to learn what was going on beyond. And now here I am. All this time, I looked down at Vukradin when I should have been looking at myself. 

I must learn. 

“What is an interventionist? And what will happen here if we fail?” 

Lyra looks at me, “The interventionists? I haven’t heard that in a while.” She sits down. 

“For a very long time, the question of the boundary of the natural world has been a bitter debate. Some draw the boundary at the prime material plane, some draw the boundary at their forest, and some draw the boundary at the multiverse.”

She looks at me as if I were a shell sprout. And I am. 

“It was the kind of thing that people would argue, but it didn’t really matter. And then the War of the Dragons happened. Severin and his mad men concocted a plan to summon Tiamat into the Prime Material. Some felt that Tiamat was an abomination and must be stopped. Others believed Tiamat’s survival or death was just part of the natural cycle because Tiamat was part of the multiverse, and wasn’t everything part of the multiverse? And that everything would be okay, and we didn’t have to do anything.”

I look at her and remember those days when the Dragons flew and burned. 

“A council in Waterdeep was formed, and the Emerald Enclave attended. Delaan Winterhound was the wisest of us. And we would have stayed out of this war, but Malfire, a hero of the War of the Dragon, convinced him that we must join to save the world. Many of us answered and fought. But those who believed that the boundary of the natural world was the multiverse were furious. They screamed that we were intervening in the natural world and violating our oaths.” 

Tiamat’s defeat has led to an uneasy truce. Those who say we did the right thing point out the harm that we avoided. Those who say we did the wrong thing point out that it was Tiamat’s defeat that led to the War of the Giants. That Tiamat’s defeat inspired the Giant gods to shatter the Ordning.”

She pauses. 

“The naturalists see what you see here and see nothing wrong. I see what I see here and see an alien force corrupting our world.” 

My mind reels. 

Lyra stares at me, confused. 

“This tree ate people,” I declare. “Trees do not eat people.” 

Lyra nods.

“It was a good tree, and it protected this forest, but ever since the planar scar emerged, it became poisoned. It was no longer sustained by the natural light of the sun, but by these planar pools.” 

Valphine adds, “Sunlight is not natural.” 

Both Vukradin and I glare at her. 

And this is where the choice looms, stark and unsettling. Adabra believes the interventionists are the blight, the ones who disrupt. Lyra, the interventionist, was fighting what she believed was a blight, a planar infection. I thought I was a naturalist, but the undeniable reality of this corruption, this motivated blight, twists my understanding. I don't know anymore.

If Adabra is right, then accepting this planar power is natural, and intervening is the blight. But my own senses are screaming that this is unnatural, an intentional harm. If Lyra is right, and this is an unnatural blight, then Adabra's naturalism, in this context, allows for destruction. 

Adabra never spoke of a motivated blight, of a puppet controlled by unseen hands. She spoke of accepting existence. How can I accept this calculated desecration? The sheer wrongness of it fills me with dismay. I must act. I will seek answers, starting with Adabra. Perhaps she can explain this paradox. Or I may find my own path, separate from either faction, guided by the raw, undeniable truth of the living world itself. This is not balance. This is sickness. And sickness, I now realize, must be healed.

Vukradin reminds me of the tormented deer, and my decision is made for me. I attempt to do the ritual successfully. And I succeed. 

And then another failure. And then I try one last time to succeed, and I fail. 

And a screaming, searing pain rips through my head. I look around. Everyone else is clutching their heads. 

The earth shudders, not with natural movement, but with a sickening internal tremor. Around us, the iridescent pools and planar wells begin to grow in size, pulsing with an even more violent, sickly light. My shell prickles with a deep unease. The Whispering Grove is no longer merely 'withering'; a planar rift possesses it.

Lyra is very upset. "This did not work the way I had hoped," she whispers. I watch her, and think how hard it must be not to have a shell to hide into, and then I am confused. 

"Alright, let's get outta here," someone says. The air itself feels poisoned, the very fabric of existence here stretched thin and frayed. We leave, but the image of those expanding wells, the pulsing, unnatural colors, etches itself into my shell.

As we fall back, a shadow detaches itself from the swirling chaos of the rift. It's an inquisitive creature. Phased out, its form shimmering at the edge of perception, its eyes distorted. It moves with an unsettling grace, like a marionette, picking things up off the ground. It seems to be under the control of something. Brewbarry, with surprising clarity that often belies his brutish exterior, voices the thought stirring in my own mind: "He's a puppet that's being controlled."

My gaze tracks it as it moves, collecting ingredients from the corpse of the corrupted Treant. This is not the dance of scavenger birds, or the natural decay of the forest. This is calculated. Purposeful. The creature disappears into the well, vanishing back into the planar tear.

The wells remain disrupting the world. This isn't random. It can't be. My internal struggle intensifies. Adabra, my wise friend and philosophical guide, taught me that naturalists accept these planar shifts as normal. She warned me against interventionists, calling them the blight, the ones who meddle. But what I've just witnessed, what I've felt in this grove, screams otherwise.

I must go and talk to her.
