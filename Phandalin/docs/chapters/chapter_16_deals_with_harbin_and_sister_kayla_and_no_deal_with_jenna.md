# Chapter 15 Deals with Harbin, and Sister Kayla, and no deal with Jenna

## 7-02 Taraskh 1495 

### 

### Vukradin 

Harbin’s accusations are too much. He has crossed the line. 

As I stand there fuming, I hear Ser Kaelen’s voice \- “I am delighted to see your name cleared. I told Harbin that a member of the Order of the Gauntlet couldn't commit such a crime. The accusation was scandalous\!” 

I roll my eyes and repeat, “Scandalous.” 

Ser Kaelen didn’t or chose not to hear me \- “I insisted we must have a trial immediately so we could clear your name\! The integrity of the Order of the Gauntlet was questioned, and that was unacceptable.” 

“Very,” I sneer. 

He then turns to me, “I am very frustrated with the leadership of this town. Harbin has been a terrible representative of Lord Nevermember. He has made scandalous accusations against members of our order. I believe a town meeting is essential to resolve this\!”

I nod.

Harbin, who is standing near me, interjects with a slight tremor in his voice, “Let’s not be too hasty. I think we could have a polite and civil discussion. Perhaps a town meeting? A gathering to discuss some of the events?” 

He then turns to Ser Kaelen with a hint of steel in his voice, “His Lordship appoints the position of the Town Master, and his Lordship has the privilege and only he has the privilege of replacing me.” 

I can’t believe what I am hearing, “No democracy in Phandolin, that’s an outrage\! Absurd\!” 

I feel the power of the people rising in me, “We need a revolution\!” 

Ser Kaene looked pale and whispered, “Perhaps we should avoid the R word?” 

My honor is besmirched. “All I hear are rumors and innuendos that trash my good name\!” I point my finger at Harbin, “He is using blood money\!” 

The Townmaster turns pale and stutters, “I think there’s been a tiny little misunderstanding.” 

And then Brewbarry, to make Harbin feel even more uncomfortable, steps close to him. 

And with that, Harbin finally proposes something I want to hear: a town meeting where we can make decisions with the principle of one resident, one vote. 

But when I ask for such a meeting to be convened, the Townmaster again finds reasons why it cannot be convened now.   

“Such meetings can not be held from one minute to the next. They need preparations, an agenda, discussion on the format, and pre-work\!” 

He reminds me of many agents that couldn’t find me a gig, and this time I won’t tolerate it. “Now or pitchforks. Do you want pitchforks?” 

I look at him and realize I don’t need him.  

I can convene my own meeting. 

As I prepare to call for a meeting to end the Townmaster, Ser Kaelen pulls me aside. 

He whispers, “Your dedication to participatory democracy, power to the people, opposition to authoritarianism is commendable. But we do live in the real world. And in the real world, Lord Nevermember put Harbin in this position. And if we get rid of Harbin, Lord Nevermember will send the army here. So let’s have our democracy but leave the Townmaster as a puppet.” 

I wanted this so badly, and all I can think to say is, “But he’s corrupt\!” 

Ser Kaelen looks at me and mutters, “A privy council that made the decisions for Harbin would be sufficient, I think.” 

“No, we are going to have a town meeting. I will not have you shut me down,” I declare. 

I march onto the town green and start playing my music. Soon, all of the local notables have arrived: Elmer, the owner of Barthen’s provisions, and his two clerks, Ander and Thistle, and  Halia Thornton, owner of the Miner’s Exchange, Linene, the manager of Lionshield Coster, Spider-Man, and  Adabra. 

There are also many farmers and miners that we haven’t met. But the town isn’t that big, and once you get past 20 or so, there aren’t that many more. 

So I start a concert. And of course, they start chanting for “Valphine.” 

As I look over the crowd, I notice someone I have never seen before. She is staring at me. She is in her late thirties with an air of refined, unassuming elegance. Her auburn hair is tied back in a neat, intricate knot, and her sharp, intelligent green eyes seem to assess everything with a merchant's calculating gaze. She is neither overtly beautiful nor plain; her features are pleasant and easily forgettable. She dresses in the high-quality, practical attire of a traveling guild representative or minor noble. Her doublet of dark green wool is well-tailored, her leather boots are sturdy but stylish, and the simple silver rings on her fingers are her only adornment.

I smile at her. After all, she is a voter. But she just smirks. 

With the crowd warmed up, I am about to give my speech about the people and freedom, when someone screams \- “DRAGON\!” 

“You believe that rumor\! It’s just a form of societal control. There is no dragon,” I shout.

But everyone is running away and pointing behind me. I turn to see what they think is a dragon, but it's actually a dragon.

All I can think is, “I thought that was a rumor.” 

The white dragon is diving towards us. I can only imagine it has one thing in mind: food. 

Harbin starts screaming, “This is what happens when adventurers talk instead of doing their job.” 

I look at him, “And when we kill the dragon, it will be even more proof of how incapable you are at doing your job.” 

I stare at the dragon, and think about how every revolution begins with a heroic and impossible last stand. I think of the ballads that will be written about my heroism and how I stared down this dragon and killed it. I begin to consider a few potential couplets and musical elements, and decide that an opera would be necessary to convey the religious and political energy of this moment fully. 

Only one thought remains, “If I kill the dragon, the fates will prove that I am destined for this change\!” 

Brewbarry stands in front of me and shouts, “I’ll protect you.” And as he watches the dragon fly towards us, he asks, “Should we not ask what he wants?” 

I look at my goliath friend and say, “You are welcome to ask.” 

But I have a different plan. As the Dragon approaches, I cast phantasmal force, creating an illusion that he has a peasant in his claws. 

I hold my breath and then see him fly away. The dragon is convinced he has his prey and, feeling the pain of the spell, decides to go away to eat his phantasmal dinner. 

I hear a voice shout, “I wish to make an announcement.” 

Harbin is about to speak, and so help me. 

But Harbin surprises me as he says, “The recent actions of Vukradin demonstrate the need for a more active and engaged community in our decision-making. Although it is not within my power to make him Townmaster, I do believe that after reviewing our charter, a new privy council consisting of myself, Tolbin, Elmer, Halia, Linene, and Adabra, with our new hero Vukradin, will be just what we need in these perilous times\!” 

I am tempted to just seize the job of Townmaster for myself, and Ser Kaelen can see that. He explains that if we were to do that, then Lord Nevermember would see this as an attack on his authority and send troops to deal with us. Even if we were able to stop the troops, the harm to the town would be considerable. 

For a moment, the nobility of the revolution calls, but then I realize that compromise is also noble. 

### Valphine 

It’s been a long day, waiting for the sun to settle. While Vukradin, Soma, and Brewbarry went into town, I had a different plan. I would stay outside of town with Corbin and Locutus. 

At night, I plan to sneak into town, meet with Sister Kayla, and learn what I can. 

I almost had to change my plans when I heard the roar of a white dragon. I told Corbin to watch Locutus while I ran towards Phandalin. But before I got too far, the Dragon flew away. 

But now it’s nighttime. Before I go, I get rid of any sign of Lathander and look as much the part of a drow of the Underdark as I can. 

The old skin feels uncomfortable and comfortable. 

Locutus looks terrified, and I think, good. 

He takes me to an abandoned and decrepit building at the far edge of the town. We enter. 

Inside the building, and out of the shadows, a woman wearing a dark robe and hood steps forward and whispers, “Who is that?” She is clearly pointing at me. 

I explain, “I am his assistant.” 

She turns to Locutus, “Did you kill Borg?” 

Locutus nods, and I show the head. 

And then, before I can react, she strikes down Locutus. Her first two strikes with her rapier miss, but her offhand dagger cuts the orc down where he stands. 

I am ecstatic. This reminds me of the games I played with my sisters in Menzoberanzaan.  

But then something happens that shocks me, “Now, Valphine, we can have a private conversation.” 

I mumble, “Sure?” 

Sister Kayla removes her hood. I stare at her features. She has cold grey eyes, her hair is cut short, and there is a scar near her neck. Under her cloak, she is wearing some armor, and in her hands are the rapier and the bloodied dagger, which are pointed at me, as a reminder of what the consequences of not agreeing with her might be. 

“I have more important business than working for the Carver,” she begins, and then continues after a pause, “The Borg had one job: kill Big Al. Leave no witnesses. If he had done that, my carefully laid plan would have worked. But he didn’t. And so the plot to blame you for the crime of stealing goods from a merchant has failed.” 

I look at her, “Perhaps you should be working for someone else?” 

She smiles, “The Carver’s gang needs new management.”

I nod. 

And then she says, “I can see that you are exactly the kind of person I can work with.” 

I look at her, confused, and then realize she’s assuming I am a racial stereotype. Much like Locutus, who believed I was going to send him into the Underdark. 

She’s assuming I am a duplicitous, backstabbing, and evil Drow.

How droll. 

She continues, oblivious to my reaction, “I will provide you with information in a few days to kill the Carver’s last lieutenant. I know you are wondering why we don’t kill the Carver. Because he has a very unique ability to communicate with his people, he can communicate with us telepathically. I don’t know how. So we have to weaken him sufficiently so that he comes out of hiding.” 

I recall the stories of mind flayers, aboleths, and eldritch monsters that could do this, and I decide that this is a bridge too far. I am about to tell Sister Kayla that I no longer care to help her in her war with the Carver. 

She again doesn’t sense my misgivings and asks, “Do we have an arrangement?” 

I wonder why she would make this offer, and she explains that the Carver doesn’t like failure,  and she needs to disappear. She made a promise but failed to deliver, and now worries that she will meet the same fate as Borg. 

She notes, “If you kill his lieutenants, then the Carver will come out of hiding, and then I can kill them.” Then she pauses, “And by I, I mean you.” She looks at me, “Those people you are with are they your allies, your dupes? What exactly is your role?” 

I smile, “They are my assistants.” 

Sister Kayla believes me, “Interesting. This whole Lathander thing is a front, right? You are clearly a cleric of Lolth.” 

I glare at her, and she smiles as if she knows something.

 “No need to say anything,” Sister Kayla purrs and then, with more steel in her voice, says, “I know I could get a deal with you. Vukradin was too naive and too simple-minded, Brewbarry was in the bard’s pocket, and as for Soma, she is too full of nature.” She points the bloody dagger at me, “But you? I knew you and I could make a deal.” 

I again glare, impassively. 

She smiles, as if she knows something, a secret we share, and then tosses a bag at my feet, “The money Locutus was going to get for killing Borg, consider it a down payment on our future partnership.” 

And before I can react, she disappears. 

### Brewbarry 

The tavern is a happening place. And the beer tastes good, but the woman who smirked on the green during the dragon attack is still smirking and staring at Vukradin.

The bard needs to be protected from his own best instincts. Too many people here want to harm him. 

I ask Spiderman where she is from, and he explains that she is from Neverwinter. And then he explains that she works for the Lord’s Alliance. 

“How do you know?” I ask. 

“They have a stench, and she carries their insignia,” he whispers.   
I look her over, and whatever secrets she has come spilling out. She has a purpose to negotiate with Vukradin. And she thinks him to be an easy mark.

The Lord’s Alliance was hell bent on destroying my people. And I am tempted to exact my vengeance here, but now this is not the place or time. 

She sees me approach, smiles, and asks, “You are?” 

“Brewbarry,” I growl. 

She speaks softly and quietly to settle me, “Brewbarry. You were a big fan of Vukradin’s in Neverwinter and attended all of his performances?” 

I look at her and nod. 

And then she says something I didn’t expect, “I think his music is fantastic,  and I think it’s a terrible shame that so few people know about it.”

I am confused, “And what do you mean by that? What do you suggest we do about this?” 

She says, soothingly, “I would love an introduction. And I’ve noticed how Vukradin has made new friends.” 

My voice becomes threatening, “Really?” 

Once again, Jenna reassures me, “I’ve heard rumors that she’s a friend of the Order of the Gauntlet.” 

I am very suspicious, “And why do you care?” 

She looks startled, “I hear Vukradin is very committed to conflict-free treasure, and he only wants to work with high-integrity individuals.” 

I nod and say, “That should be the way for all of us\!” 

She smirks, “I am not one to judge. My role in life  is to make sure that the ruling… the law and order and the preservation of society is maintained in the face of chaos and the mayhem that surrounds us all.” 

I know this kind, “To preserve your own interests?” 

The woman is taken aback. “I should not be surprised to hear that from,” she pauses, “Uthgartian.” 

Her tone drips with contempt. Her eyes are full of hate as she tries hard to hide what she feels about people she calls, Barbarians. 

I tense, ready to strike her down, but she recovers. 

She continues trying to pretend the moment never happened, “ At the end of the day, this is not about you and me; this is really about Vukradin and his choices, and it is particularly his choice to work for the Order of the Gauntlet. Did he not receive a letter recently?”

I look at her, convinced that she is up to no good, “How would that be any of your business?”

She laughs, “Because I sent it.” 

I am confused, “Why did you ask if you sent it?” 

She sighs, “ Because I still see him working with Ser Kaelen, and it's my understanding that Vukradin is against people whose money is blood money. And I'm sure that even Vukradin, in his infinite musical wisdom, would know that House Margaster is only blood money.” 

She then turns to me, “I was going to approach him, but since you came here, and are his biggest fan, I thought you could make the introduction.” 

I look at her, “Hand over your weapons.” 

And she smiles and drops a rapier, a dagger, another dagger, and a hand-held crossbow on the table. 

She then says in a soft, gentle tone, “ I am not here to commit acts of violence, Uthgartian; I am here to have a pleasant conversation with Vukradin.” 

When I make a point about her weapons, she points at my halberd. I nod and bring the bard to the table.

Vukradin is doubtful at first, but when he hears that he wrote the mysterious letter, he insists. 

Before he reaches the table, I tell him that I disarmed her. 

### Vukradin 

I thank Brewbarry for his actions and remind him that a tavern is an unlikely place for violence. He seems unconvinced and says, “I like your confidence.” 

He tells me that she loved my music and that I should be more famous than I am. 

At that, I am enthused, “Yes, let’s go talk to her.” 

After I sit down, get a beer from Spiderman, and introduce myself, she explains, “I have been looking all over for you.” 

I tell her that I got her letter. 

She looks at me, confused, “I must say, given your well-known position on immoral money, I was shocked to find out that you continue to work for the Order of the Gauntlet.” 

I turn to her, “Define work for?”  

She looks at me, confused, “Ser Kaelen here says that you are a full-fledged member of the Order of the Gauntlet.” 

I sip my beer, “I am friends with many people. I have many fans. I spread my music to all types.” I stare at her, “Are you interested in my music? I am sure your organization would love to hear my music.” 

She seems confused, and I continue, “I want to be friends with everyone.” 

And then she splutters, “But Ser Kaelen insists that you are a member of the Order of the Gauntlet. Am I to understand that this is not an exclusive arrangement with the Order?” 

I stop her, “Is this a trial?” 

She looks at me, perplexed as if this idea that I might not commit to one group is as strange as anything in this world. 

I explain, “I’m  just trying to make organic fair trade gold so that I can start my music studio and spread my music to all of Faerun. That's my goal here, and I'm happy to be friends with lots of people, as long as they're not evil, and as long as we earn our money fairly and justly.”

She smiles, finally, and begins, “ I'm very relieved to find out that you haven't embraced Ser Kaelen's philosophy wholeheartedly and without any reservation, but instead take the very appropriate musical point of view of wanting to work with everyone, to bring your music to everyone.” 

She pauses and thinks for a moment, and continues, “Now I understand why, after receiving that missive about the House Margaster and their funding of the Order of the Gauntlet, you did not abandon them. You were more of a friend to the Order of the Gauntlet than a member.”  
Her tone worries me, “Again, am I on trial?” 

She laughs, and then in a reassuring tone, “  I'm asking this question because I've come to make you an offer, a suggestion, a potential way for you to become… what's the word I'm looking for? More famous.” 

I smile. 

She continues, “I am a member of the Lord’s Alliance, and“

I interrupt her, “What is the Lord’s Alliance?”

Her jaw drops at my honest question. 

I ask her, “Is it like the Zhentarim?” 

She looks even more shocked. 

I explain, “ I've heard of the Zhentarim, but I haven't met them yet, either. Are you with them?”

She stares at me, confused, and then smiles, “ I had heard so much about you that your passion for music had made you oblivious to the outside world, and I see now it is true.” 

Brewbarry grunts at Jenna and asks her about her weapons. 

She looks at him and says, with contempt, “Blue beerrree? Is that your name? Did I mispronounce it?” 

She mispronounces my friend's name, and I don’t know why. 

Brewbarry turns to her, and asks, “GEEENA? Is that the correct pronunciation of your name?” 

I interject, “JINA\!” 

And she laughs, “It is Jenna, but for you, Vukradin, it can be anything.” 

I stare at her, confused, “Why would you want your name to be confused?” 

“Because I only care about your success.” 

Now it hits me, “You want to be my agent.” And then I think for a moment, “I do need an agent.” 

She smiles with her whole face and says, “Exactly. What can I offer you? I  can get you plugged in to all of the top places in Waterdeep and Neverwinter.  I can have you performing in front of the nobility. I’m sure I could even get you an audience to play for a set for Lord Nevermember Himself.”

I nod, showing a hint of excitement, but not too much. 

At that point, I hear Valphine’s Lathanderian Choral Death Chant mix. I try hard not to focus on it. And the crowd that demanded her a few minutes ago, now chants for my name. 

I turn to her, “I need to get back on stage. My friend is bombing.” 

She looks at me, “Before you go, there is this small detail about your, what is it, fair-trade gold.” 

I look at her, “I am a real believer in that.”

She looks hurt, “I don’t want you to abandon that philosophy. But a refinement would be necessary.  The treasure is stolen, but it's stolen from the people, not from a person. And let the adventurers be the first to reclaim it. And you might think this is another theft, and it would be, except that by taking the treasure and redistributing it as purchases, the adventurer enables the community to grow through the stimulus of this new gold. In short, by purchasing goods, you are returning the gold to the **people** from whom it was stolen.” 

I shake my head, “Yeah, no.  This is not a line I'm gonna cross. I gotta go perform.” I look at her and tell her what she doesn’t want to hear, “Sorry, not interested in compromising my morals, I'm strong on this one. We need to return these items to their rightful owners.” 

She protests, “I could have done so much for you.” 

I turn on the charm and tell her, “ Why don't you reconsider this, because I think this is a good arrangement for you. I could really entertain your nobles, and they'd really think highly of you as my agent.”

She looks at me, and I can see her consider it for a moment, but then the mask she wears goes back on. But there is a softening in her glare. She admires me. She won’t change her tune, but she does admire me.
