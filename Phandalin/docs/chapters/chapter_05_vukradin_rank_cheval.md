## 04-01 Taraskh 1495
### Soma

The sun shines, the walk is long and Adabra feels the need to talk. 
“Harbin, bless his heart, wants this area cleared of monsters. And what is a monster to him?”
I want to answer, but whereas Merill would let me think, Adabra has an answer to his question. 
“Anything that disrupts his idea of peace. The manticore, the orcs driven from their homes, they are merely acting as nature intends.” 
She looks at me, as if she wants me to understand something, “It is the meddling that truly upset the balance.” 
I nod. Her wisdom is undeniable. “Let nature take its course. Wolves and deer are going to eat each other. It’s okay.” 
Adabra smiles, “This is exactly what I say, Soma. Some believe we must fight every evil that comes from the outer plane. But where do you draw the line? Do we draw the line at a spirit from the ethereal plane? A fiend from the abyss? Or is it a god’s design? To fight against these is to deny the very nature of existence itself. And when you start denying the right of one thing to exist, you start calling people barbarians.” 
At that, poor Brewbarry sighs, “I was a barbarian!” 
But Adabra assures him he was never a barbarian; he was a nomad. 
I am worried that this will continue for hours, so thankfully, Vukradin decides to play some music, and this time the music is good.  
### Vukradin
Valphine wanted to play her musical instrument along with me. That may not work for me. Although this time, it added a particular color and texture that elevated my composition to art. Perhaps, we can find a way to collaborate as long as she recognizes her role in our duo. 
And at last we are at the doorway of the Townmaster’s home. And once again, he pleads that a dragon ignore him, and I am about to explain once again I am not a dragon but am interrupted by Adabra.
“Harbin, get out here and pay this young man who has rescued me the money that is owed him.” 
As the Townmaster peeks out from the door, I cough and explain that the mission was more involved than he intimated in his note, and that he could pay us more. 
The Townmaster, reflecting the sordid morality of the area, asks why I didn’t pay myself in Manticore treasure as per the lawful agreement with Lord Neverember. 
I am about to express my outrage, but Adabra is furious - “Harbin, we have talked about this before. This young man, Vukradin, is no common sellsword, the kind of whom you and Lord Neverember insist on recruiting. He is an idealist, respectful of life, and willing to sacrifice money for his principles. He wants honest wages, fair trade for work done, and conflict-free gold.”

This is turning out better than I could have hoped. I love this woman. To help her cause, I say, “Well, said.” 
She’s on a roll. “You need more than mercenaries. You need champions who care for the greater whole, whose principles, though perhaps untested, are fundamentally sound. This isn’t just an expense, it’s an investment in the moral fabric of Phandalin’s defense!”

She takes a deep breath and continues, “Those who protect us must do so with a deeper conviction than the possibility of tax-free plunder.” And then with a flourish, “Give this man more coin! And not just this man, but the tortle Soma who shares so many of my concerns about our world, and the nomad who has been so unjustly treated.” 
I notice how she just skips over Valphine, as if she doesn’t exist. Or is it that something about Valphine annoys her? Is it because she is a Drow? 
### Valphine
Lathander’s light will guide me even if this woman chooses to ignore me. If I were a cleric of Lolth, or if I were in my noble house, she would be dead. But I have moved past that world.  
And now the Townmaster and her talk, and they point at Corbin who is standing next to me. 

Finally, the Townmaster explains that in addition to monsters, the town has been under siege by bandits, and he is short of coins. 
Adabra may ignore me, but her look terrifies the townmaster, who suddenly remembers he can help us and offers free room and board in the Stonehill Inn.  
And so we find ourselves once again in the Stonehill Inn. 

Vukradin, whose presence fills a room, approaches the tavern keeper and offers to play some music. 
As Vukradin prepares to play he shouts, “Toblen, what do your friends call you?”
Tolbien laughs, “Spiderman.”

I look at the man, wondering if he is one of us. I do so like spiders. But no. He explains that as a child, he fell into a spider’s nest where spiders were hatching and was covered in baby spiders. He wasn’t afraid, and the sight of him walking calmly covered in Spiders earned him the nickname. 
In a different time and place, my sisters would recruit him. Or kill him. Or possibly delightfully torture him. 
Vukradin sits down to play, and I ask if I could join him. He assents while reminding me that my role is to support him and his music. 
I nod. 
Vukradin’s music is a discordant mess. The brilliance is there, but it’s too complex for this audience. As I listen to it, I realize that the musical elements I've been working on to fuse Lathanderial Choral music and Prayers to Lolth, which I've called *Choralum Lux Maleficarum,** *will be perfect. 
The rich, layered choral harmonies drenched in bright, resonant tones that evoke the warmth and hope of Lathander’s light, interwoven with sinuous melodic lines and subtle dissonances that hint at the enigmatic and sometimes unsettling nature of Lolth’s influence, balance out the complexity of Vukradin’s music. 

Vukradin is at first outraged, and then he sees how the crowd is enjoying the music. And he smiles. 
And I take a chance and sing using my contralto voice, weaving intricate, almost hypnotic lines that feel like a spell or invocation into this music. 

Vukradin raises an eyebrow and builds on my vocals. 
The crowd is silent and watches us perform. 
When we are done, there is applause. 
Vukradin looks at me and says, “Perhaps we should play together.” 
### Soma
As I sit and watch the child Vukradin and Valphine, the oddest of pairings, play music, I think about the forest and my children. 
They are traveling the wide world, and I pray to the gods that they are doing well and have avoided the horrors that have felled the north. 
And as I sit, I hear yet another rumor about the strange goings on in the Neverwinter Wood. 
The Neverwinter Wood reminds me of home, and I am tempted just to go and look at it, or at least convince my group to come with me, but just then, Vukradin overhears another story.  
Later, I would learn his name, Daran Edermath, but he’s known as Silverleaf. He is a tall, lean half-elf with a weathered but noble appearance that speaks of years spent both in the wilds and in battle. His once-dark hair is now streaked generously with silver, falling in a slightly tousled mane that frames a face marked by fine lines and a well-trimmed salt-and-pepper beard. His sharp, emerald-green eyes retain a spark of keen intelligence and quiet wisdom, reflecting a lifetime of hard-earned experience. He wears simple, practical clothing—earth-toned tunics and sturdy leather boots—well-suited for tending what I learn later is his orchard and wandering the countryside. 
Silverleaf tells Vukradin, “You’re an adventurer. I used to be one. Let me share with you a story - West of Phandalin, on the coast, is an old stone lighthouse. Ships are drawn to this gleaming tower like moths to a flame, and are doomed to crash upon its rocks. Their wrecks must be filled with treasure!”
Poor Silverleaf, his pitch was wonderful, but he didn’t realize that Vukradin sees Treasure differently, “No! No! No! No! That’s not treasure.” The boy flops his hands down, “How many times do I have to explain this. You don’t go and rob from the dead and call it treasure!” 
Silverleaf is in shock and apologizes for offending. Vukradin is on a roll, and I feel for the old elf, but at the same time, lessons need to be learned. 
### Vukradin
This is why adventuring is wrong. When is it a monster? And when is it a poor farmer that you plundered? It’s plunder, and there is no blurry line that makes it right under some circumstances. 
But before I can give my speech, I notice someone new that I had missed earlier. He’s in his early thirties, with a steady and capable presence. He’s carrying himself with some confidence that makes him look larger. He’s got dark hair, he’s clean-shaven, and he has a scar above his right eyebrow. 

He’s not a farmer or likes to spend a fortune on and wear chain mail armor.  
I scrutinize him, and I notice an emblem of a gauntlet grasping a sword by the blade. I try to remember where I saw it before, and I recall something. Yes, I remember. It must be the emblem of a legendary maestro who wields their instrument with such finesse that they grasp the ‘blade’—the sharp edge of sound—directly, mastering even the most dangerous notes!
A fellow musician, or at least someone who appreciates my music. 
And then I remember something about a group of fanatics, but the details escape me. 
He introduces himself as Ser Kaelen Thorn. Ser wants to talk in private, I insist we do it in public. He shrugs and sits down, and orders drinks from Spiderman. 
“I have heard stories about you around Phandalin and your goals.” 
I smile, clearly a musician, “You’ve heard about my studio that I am trying to build?” 
“No.”

“I have heard about your unique perspective on treasure. It’s refreshing in a world that only measures worth in plunder.” 
Valphine seems annoyed or mischievous and blurts, “Or dead drow.”

“She’s a good Drow. A cleric of Lathander.”

For a moment I am worried that he might get up and leave, but Valphine may be the missing link in my music, and I am not going to discard her yet. 
Spiderman brings us drinks, and he takes a sip, stretches back and says, “It’s rare to find one who grapples so earnestly with questions of right and wrong. Where sentients just take the armor off the bodies of the dead without a second thought. We steal their armor in a final act of treachery on the living.” He pauses, “But I sense a deeper conviction in you.”

“Valphine.”

Maybe if I tell him what I want, “It would be better if I had a music studio?” 
“I am offering something else. What? Imagine what your voice and story would accomplish with the backing of our order. Yes, you can motivate hundreds, but with our backing, you would be known to tens of thousands.”
