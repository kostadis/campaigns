# Chapter 37 The Intervention

---

## Valphine — Return to Phandalin and Political Briefing

The road back to Phandalin always smelled the same: pine resin, horse dung, and that particular quality of surface-world optimism that I've never quite learned to predict. We had come from Dragon Barrow and the mine with the sort of success surface towns prefer to make public before anyone asks too many questions about the cost. The Underdark taught me to read a court from the smell of it. Up here, everything smells like possibility, which is functionally identical to naivety. I find it charming, briefly, and with the certain knowledge of how fragile it is.

Harbin Wester was waiting for us at the town boundary with the full theatrical apparatus of gratitude: banners, townsfolk arranged in the semicircle that people arrange themselves in when someone important has told them to look festive, and that particular smile he wears when he's already drafting the speech he's going to give about himself. The festival had probably been his idea within the hour of receiving word that the dragon was dead.

To his considerable credit, he praised us warmly. He extolled our bravery, our sacrifice, our decisive role in ending the dragon threat that had hung over the region like a promise of ruin. He mentioned, with the modest pride of a man who has rehearsed modest pride, that it was his own foresight — his establishment of the Privy Council — that had brought such capable heroes to Phandalin in the first place.

I filed it away. Not with resentment. Resentment is a surface-dweller luxury, the emotional equivalent of standing in a rainstorm and being surprised that you're wet. Harbin had done exactly what any competent political creature would do: lashed his name to a victory before the victory had time to acquire its own history. Back in Menzoberranzan, my mother's rivals would have done the same thing before the body was cold. At least Harbin waited until we were in earshot. I found that almost touching.

The party received this with varying degrees of awareness. Brewbarry beamed. Vukradin accepted it with the earnest dignity of someone who does not fully understand what is being done to him. I smiled and clasped my hands and let the light of Lathander fall agreeably on my face. The performance cost me nothing.

It was Ser Kaelen who broke the pageantry — he drew Soma aside with the quiet urgency of a man delivering genuinely bad news, which in my experience means it is either bad news or he wants something. The rest of us drifted after them the way the party always drifts, because we have collectively never managed to let anyone have a private conversation that doesn't involve us.

Adabra, it seemed, had been busy. She had taken the events at the Whispering Wood — specifically Lyra's failure, which we had caused, depending on how generously you framed the sequence — and had been using them as a lever. Ostracizing Lyra from the order was the visible blade. The deeper cut was Soma: Soma as a dangerous interventionist, someone who had disrupted the natural order, who held Meril's Staff without the Enclave's blessing, who was teaching others to interfere rather than observe. Adabra was a true believer, which always makes them more dangerous than the merely ambitious. Ambition you can negotiate with. Faith is inflexible.

I watched Soma receive this information. She absorbed it the way she absorbs most things — slowly, from the outside in, like rain soaking through bark. It would settle into something useful eventually. That is her particular genius, and I mean that without condescension; patience is a form of power I respect entirely, even if I would find it suffocating to practice.

While Kaelen continued, my attention moved where it was more profitably spent. Vukradin asked whether my church was a charity.

The Temple of Lathander's Searing Pain of Justice. My contribution to Phandalin's spiritual life, housed in the restored bones of Tresendar Manor and accumulating, with gratifying steadiness, the wounded, the curious, the desperate, and the few with enough sense to understand that healing is more honest when it does not pretend pain was an accident.

Yes, I explained, it was charitable. We provided healing, food, shelter, community, and care to the broken and inconveniently alive. All of that was true. The doctrine was also true, though less suited to polite summary. Lathander's light is not gentle. It is power made radiant, pain made holy, correction delivered without apology from the sky itself.

Vukradin, to his credit, performed a small and elegant piece of duplicity: he made the transfer of unclaimed property to my church sound like moral hygiene rather than patronage. I admired it. He would almost certainly have objected to that interpretation, which only made the maneuver more interesting. Sincerity, in Vukradin, often wears the appearance of strategy so perfectly that I doubt even he understands the advantage he is taking.

I kept my expression entirely neutral. Inside, something that was either amusement or theological vindication observed that Lathander had arranged this quite tidily. Lost property flows toward the church. The church grows. The church heals people, which is genuinely good, which is genuinely painful against my very nature, which is to say genuinely pleasurable, which is to say that the Morninglord has arranged things so that goodness costs me something, and I find that an entirely satisfying way to worship. If there is a god listening, they have an excellent sense of irony.

It was, naturally, at this moment that Bryn and Giles came around the corner.

I had known they were conducting a fundraiser. A public religious parade through town, apparently. I had perhaps not inquired too closely into the precise method.

They were scarred. Sunburnt in the specific pattern that only comes from extended exposure combined with repeated contact between skin and something fibrous. Self-flagellation is one of those practices surface folk condemn most reliably when it is sincere enough to make them consider what their own comforts are protecting. They were smiling the way converts smile when they have done something they believe was worth the cost, which is to say: beatifically, with the particular radiance of people who have just proven something to themselves. The town watched them pass. The town's expression was varied.

I regarded them with the satisfaction of a priestess watching doctrine acquire flesh. This was Lathander's light stripped of pastoral decoration. Not the sentimental sunrise painted in temple glass for people who want divinity to resemble a kind grandfather. This was power and pain meeting in public, hurting the body, clarifying the will, and still serving a charitable purpose. My congregation understood more than I had perhaps credited them for. They had gone into the streets of Phandalin and demonstrated that healing need not be soft, that faith need not be comfortable, and that a worthy god may burn and still be worshipped.

The fact that most of the watching townspeople looked unsettled was, I thought, somewhat beside the point. The Underdark never celebrated Lolth's gifts with comfort, either. The surface world would adjust. It always does, once it understands that the pain on offer comes with genuine healing attached.

I looked at the faint burn marks tracing Bryn's forearms and felt the clean satisfaction of an arrangement beginning to hold. Lathander was attentive. The pain was landing where I had directed it. Whether Harbin Wester and his council would recognize this as spiritual growth, public service, civic liability, or some mixture of the three remained to be seen.

I folded my hands and let Harbin's festival continue around me, and watched, and waited, and filed everything away.

## Vukradin — The Road to the Druid Council

The road out of Phandalin smells like pine resin and cold mud, the kind of morning where the mountains are still wrapped in cloud and the light hasn't quite committed to being light yet. I have my flute in my hand — not playing, just holding it, the way some people hold worry beads — and I am thinking about Adabra.

I like Adabra. This is inconvenient, given everything.

She has always been direct with me. Fair, too, in her way. The last time we spoke about the grove, she listened to what I told her, accepted it, and paid me five gold pieces for the trouble, which is not how villains behave in songs or in life, generally speaking. We simply mean very different things. She thinks Soma carrying Meril's Staff is a violation of something sacred and structural. I think Soma is perhaps the most careful person I know, and that sacred structures are occasionally wrong. This seemed to me, at the time, like exactly the kind of disagreement that would improve once I had the chance to speak with her properly and remind everyone that they were all trying to protect the same land. I know what words can do when they are chosen well. I also know what happens when an audience decides it likes you. I was once a much purer artist than I am now, and purity, while very satisfying to the person practicing it, pays terribly. Give the audience what it needs, let them believe it was what they wanted, and suddenly everyone is much happier to hear.

Then Thomas found us on the road to the council, and the hoping became somewhat harder. We were going there to defend Lyra, which is the sort of sentence that sounds noble until you notice how often noble sentences are just weather reports for political traps.

He materialized the way Lyra's people do — not from nowhere, exactly, but from the specific nowhere that well-trained agents occupy when they want to seem casual about it. He had the look of someone delivering bad news they'd been rehearsing. One of Lyra's trusted agents, carrying Lyra's anxieties, which are considerable and usually well-founded. He told us Adabra was moving to ostracize Lyra.

Ostracize. Not debate. Not challenge. *Ostracize.*

I turned this word over a few times while Thomas kept talking. Ostracism is not a philosophical disagreement resolved through patient dialogue. Ostracism is a verdict. It means Adabra has already done the arithmetic, already knows the votes, and called this council not to discover an outcome but to announce one. That is a different kind of gathering than I had been preparing for.

The detail that landed heaviest: Reidoth was away. The nominal head of the local Emerald Enclave chapter — absent. And Adabra had called the council during his absence.

I have sat on the Privy Council. I know exactly what it looks like when someone calls a meeting they intend to win before it starts. You pick your moment. You pick your audience. You make sure the people most likely to complicate your majority are occupied elsewhere. It is not dishonest, precisely. It is just the architecture of a predetermined conclusion.

"Even if Reidoth were present," Thomas said, with the particular weariness of someone who has thought about this extensively, "he could not stop Adabra from speaking."

No. Of course not. But presence matters. Presence is a kind of pressure, even when it cannot be translated into procedural obstruction. Reidoth standing in that glade, saying nothing, watching — that changes the texture of what Adabra can do. His absence gives her cleaner acoustics for her purposes.

We talked about reaching Reidoth. We kept it as an option, held in reserve, not dismissed — the way you keep an alternate chord progression in mind when you're not sure how the main one is going to land. Maybe. Maybe it becomes relevant. We did not send for him yet.

What I kept turning over, walking that road with the flute still unplayed in my hand, was the timing. Not the ambition — I understand ambition, even ambition I disagree with — but the *precision* of the timing. This was not opportunism. This was a scheduled event. Adabra had looked at the calendar, identified the window, and placed her move there. That is something closer to certainty than confidence. She believes she has the numbers.

Soma was ahead of me on the path, her shell catching the grey morning light, Meril's Staff in hand. The staff that is the whole of this argument, the artifact around which all of Adabra's parliamentary maneuvering has been constructed. The leaves on it have been diminishing. Soma knows this. She carries it anyway, and she carries it because Meril trusted her with it, and because something that powerful needs someone careful rather than someone credentialed.

I believe that. I believe it plainly and without reservation.

The question — the one I was walking with, the one Thomas's warning had sharpened into something with an actual edge — was whether believing it would be sufficient. Whether the council we were walking toward could be moved by the truth of it, or whether it had already been arranged to reach a different conclusion.

When we talked through our strategy, Soma said the thing plainly, in that steady way of hers: she was not going to apologize for interventionism. Not soften it or reframe it into something more palatable to druidic conservatism. She had intervened because the situation required intervention. If that was the argument, then that was the argument.

I heard her. I did. And then, because I am very good at words and very accustomed to being useful with them, I made the small, fatal translation from *Soma should say this* to *I can make Adabra's case look weaker before Soma has to say it*. It felt natural at the time. Helpful, even. If Adabra had arranged the council, then surely the first thing to do was loosen the arrangement. Find the weak beam. Press there. Let the audience hear the creak.

It did not occur to me, walking that road, that I might become one more thing standing between Soma and the moment she needed. That realization would require a much less comfortable glade.

The mountains were still in cloud. The road wound on ahead through the trees. I turned the flute once between my fingers and thought: *read the council when you get there, Vukradin. You don't know the acoustics yet.*

But I had a feeling. And the feeling was that this was going to require every word I had.

## Soma — Adabra's Accusation

The staff told me what I needed to know.

I did not like it.

The glade was already full when we arrived. Druids settled in the grass. Hands on knees. Eyes forward. The air smelled of damp leaves and old bark. No kettle. No roof. No polite little pause where everyone pretends this will be simple.

Adabra did not waste time making anyone comfortable.

I held Meril's staff and used its power of Resonance to see if she was controlled by some alien power. 

I pressed it toward Adabra and held the question steady in my mind: was she deceived, was she controlled, was she lying to herself, was something wearing her conviction like a coat.

The staff answered plain.

No. No. No.

Vukradin muttered it before I could say anything myself. "Yeah, no, she's a true believer."

There it was.

I had been watching Adabra since she opened her mouth. She did not raise her voice. Did not wave her hands. Did not dress anger up as grief. She stood there steady as a fencepost sunk deep.

I knew that kind of steady. Coastal folk get it when they have tied the same knots for forty years and think the sea owes them agreement.

It does not.

"I will be brief," she said. "I am not here to argue philosophy with you. I'm here to tell you what I found."

I believed her. That was the problem.

She had walked to the sacred grove in the Whispering Wood herself. Three weeks ago. Her own feet. Her own eyes. The treant was dead. Roots collapsed. Crown black. The grove ruined around it.

Not wounded. Not sleeping. Dead.

She said it like someone setting stones in a row. No extra weight. No wasted breath.

I looked down at the staff in my hands.

For a long time it had just been the thing Meril left in my cabin. A good staff. A friend's staff. Something to keep near the door with the walking sticks and old line. I did not know it would become a question everyone else expected me to answer.

The leaves were more than before.

I noticed. Of course I noticed. You notice when something old in your hands begins to wake up. You notice when the net mends itself in the night. You notice when an old tool changes in your grip.

Meril had come to me late. Stayed longer than most travelers. Took tea. Taught me names for things I had been doing by instinct for years. Then he left me this staff and moved on.

Old shell, new druid. No use pretending otherwise.

Adabra wasn't finished.

"The last time I spoke with this party, I believed the ritual had failed. The tree had still lived. When I heard there was a planar creature collecting materials near the grove, I was — I will say this plainly — pleased. I called it a planar magpie. I said it was the world working as it should. I gave the bard five gold pieces for what he told me."

She didn't look at Vukradin when she said it. That was almost worse.

"That day, I believed him."

I have known shell-sprouts who lied to my face and I've known shell-sprouts who lied to everyone including themselves and I've known shell-sprouts who told the truth and watched people not believe them anyway. Vukradin has a gift for making things sound true. It's not a flaw, exactly — it's the thing that makes him useful and frustrating in equal measure. But Adabra had walked to the grove. She had pressed Lyra's people for answers. She had spoken with others who were there that night. She had found the gap between what was reported and what existed, and then found more gaps where the stories should have joined. And then she had come here.

"I'm not asking you to condemn anyone today," she said. "I'm asking you to look at what I found before you listen to anyone tell you what it means."

Falcon was sitting beside her. I noted that the way I note a change in weather — not judgment, just fact. Falcon, who has been watching us since before we were worth watching. Falcon, who has opinions about how the balance should be kept and who should keep it. Falcon, sitting next to Adabra in the council circle, while she laid out a case against us with the calm precision of someone who has had weeks to be certain.

The staff was quiet in my hands.

Vukradin tried. He is nothing if not persistent, my bard shell-sprout, and he has survived worse audiences than this one. He leaned into the cross-examination the way he leans into most things: charm held back, just enough space for logic.

"Between when you heard this account — which you claim was false — and when you saw that the treant was dead. How much time between those two points?"

Adabra: "Two weeks."

"Two weeks. A lot can happen in two weeks. How do you know that what you heard from me wasn't true, and that the treant died within that two-week period before you personally went and saw that it had died? You don't know that."

It was a reasonable point. It was the kind of reasonable point that sounds convincing right up until you look at the woman it's aimed at and realize she's already considered it, walked around it, and decided it doesn't change what she found.

Adabra didn't answer that moment — she didn't need to, not yet. The council would weigh it. Maybe some of them would find purchase in it.

Then Thomas stood up. And Lyra. And they said the quiet part in full sentences, out loud, in front of everyone:

"Lyra asked us to go deal with the metastasized treant. We tried, we failed, the party showed up, they killed it."

No shame in Thomas's voice. None in Lyra's either. Not defiance exactly. Just: *this is what happened*. They had failed to save the metastasized treant, the party had killed it, and the killing had stopped a planar infestation from spreading farther. The same register Adabra had been using the whole time. The same weight.

I held the staff a little tighter and said nothing.

The thing about true believers is that they are hard to beat with facts, because they have facts too. Adabra went to the grove. Fact. The treant was dead. Fact. The grove was ruined. Fact. Vukradin had given her a story that did not match what she found. Fact. We killed the treant because something worse was coming through. Also fact.

Facts do not always sit together nicely. Anyone who has kept a small house knows that. The roof can need mending and the fish can need cleaning and the guest can need feeding all at once. None of those things stop being true because your hands are full.

I am old. Old enough to know nature is not one thing with one answer. Old enough to know letting rot spread is also a choice.

But I was new to this. New to councils. New to being looked at like I had stolen something holy. New to holding Meril's staff while people who had worn the Enclave longer than I had worn the word druid decided what it meant.

And the staff kept answering me.

That was the part I did not like to say out loud. The more I stepped in, the more it woke up. The more I stopped waiting for the world to settle itself, the more power came into my hands. Not owned. Borrowed. Maybe offered. Maybe expected.

Maybe Meril knew.

Maybe he had stood between these two ideas too long. Let things be. Step in. Trust the wilds. Protect them. Maybe he had not found the answer. Maybe he thought some old tortle from a quiet cabin, who had spent more years keeping paths safe than arguing doctrine, might see what the rest of them had handled too often to see clearly.

Or maybe he just left a friend a staff.

Both could be true. That was the problem with old friends. They leave you simple things that get heavier later.

Adabra would call that the problem. She isn't wrong that there's a problem. She's wrong about what it is.

I could not prove that to her.

The staff had already told me so.

## Vukradin — The Emerald Enclave Schism

The staff had already told us the worst useful thing: Adabra was not enchanted, deceived, or consciously lying. She believed every word she was saying.

That should have simplified matters. It did not.

A liar gives you something to expose. A puppet gives you strings to cut. A true believer gives you a circle full of people nodding along because conviction sounds very much like truth if no one interrupts it in time.

So I interrupted.

Soma had said what she meant to do. She was not going to apologize for interventionism. She was not going to pretend the party had been passive observers when we had, in fact, intervened. She meant to stand there and say the difficult thing plainly.

I heard her.

And then I did what I do when a crowd starts leaning the wrong way: I stepped toward the lean.

Not to convince Adabra. That was not the move. Adabra was rooted. The move was to make the council hear the weakness in her case before Soma had to carry the whole weight of it. If Adabra had arranged the council, then the arrangement needed loosening. A little pressure at the right joint. A little dissonance. Give the audience permission to doubt the first melody they had been handed.

This was, I believed, helpful.

I was very nearly wrong in an instructive way.

Then Falcon stood up.

I knew Falcon. Or I thought I did — the comfortable retired adventurer at his comfortable hunting lodge, the man who had once asked us to protect that lodge and whose fire we had watched from a distance when it burned. There was a version of Falcon I had assembled from available evidence: melancholy, yes, and perhaps a little embittered, but fundamentally on the right side of things. People who build hunting lodges and invite heroes in for meals are not, in my experience, the kind who turn on you in council circles.

I had not updated this model recently.

"I came because someone finally sat long enough to listen," he said. His voice had the particular weight of a man who has rehearsed his grievances until they feel like philosophy. "You can keep cheering your victories. I've seen what comes after them."

This, I thought, was a general statement. A mood. The kind of thing a retired adventurer says when the joints ache and the trophies have gathered dust. I was still being charitable.

Then he pointed at me.

"She's right! She's right about what needed to happen. You all wanted it simple. Well, this is what simple looks like. Trees are dead because of men like *him.*"

The circle. The entire circle, looking at me.

I want to be precise about what I felt in that moment, because I think precision matters here and I am, above all things, a precise person. It was not embarrassment. I have been on stages, I have been reviewed badly, I have played to audiences that did not want to be played to. Embarrassment is something I have developed a tolerance for. What I felt was closer to — bewilderment. A specific bewilderment, the kind that comes when someone changes the key signature mid-phrase and doesn't tell you, and suddenly you are hearing a song you thought you knew as something entirely different and you cannot find where you went wrong.

Trees are dead because of men like him.

Men like me.

I pulled Falcon aside. I needed to understand, yes, but I also needed him to stop making me the evidence. There is a difference. I am at my best when I am finding the note that lets everyone come in together. I am not at my best when someone points at me in public and tells the circle I am the rot in the tree.

"What's going on here?" I asked him. "Like, I thought we were… Why are you pointing me out in front of this group? What did I do?"

He said: "You intervened, and in your intervention, people died. Adabra sees it clearly."

There it was. Adabra had given him a song simple enough to sing while wounded. Intervention leads to death. Regret becomes wisdom. Everyone still fighting the fire becomes responsible for the smoke.

I like Adabra. I do. She had been fair to me once, and I do not discard that lightly. But she had found Falcon at his most hollow and handed him a frame that made his pain feel principled. He was wearing it like a good coat.

"Didn't you used to intervene?" I asked. "Like, what… why the change of heart?"

"And look what it got me."

I looked at Falcon. I thought about the lodge. The comfortable life. The years of reputation and the stories people told at festivals about the great Falcon of the hunting lodge. I thought about all of it, and I thought about the fact that he was standing in this circle pointing his finger at me while wearing boots that adventuring had paid for.

"As far as I can tell," I said, keeping my voice even, keeping it musical, keeping it in the register where things land cleanly, "it got you a beautiful hunting lodge, and lots of fame, and glory… and a whole life built on a lie. Got you a lot, that intervention. But now, now… you're gonna screw me over."

He started to speak. I did not let him finish.

"I could tell everybody about what you did. About how you intervened."

This is not a threat I am proud of. I want to note that. I am not a man who thinks of himself as making threats. But when someone attacks me, something in me reaches for the nearest sharp instrument, and unfortunately I am usually carrying several in my mouth. I had done nothing Falcon had not done. I had done it for the same reasons he once had. And he was standing there in the council circle using me as his confession booth, pointing at me as his evidence of the world's wrongness, and I found that I was angry in a way that was clean and simple and not particularly musical.

"Ever since I spoke to Adabra," he said, dismissively, "I have learned the error of my ways."

"Well then," I said, "you should have no trouble repenting in front of the group and explaining the errors *he* made."

I meant it as a calling of the bluff. What I had not accounted for was that Falcon would call it back.

He stood. He addressed the council. And what followed was — I will give him this much — a genuine confession. He told them about his adventuring days. About the dragons he had failed to fight. About the lodge that burned while heroes stood by. About the baubles and the money and the way nature had eventually torn it all down regardless. He wept, a little, I think, or something close to it. "Once again, nature tore it down," he said. "Once again, the Falcon Lodge was burnt. Once again, I asked heroes to intervene, to protect my lodge, and what did they do? They did nothing, and my lodge burned down."

It was, objectively, a very good speech.

It was also, objectively, still entirely about him.

I had wanted him to confess that his attack on me was hypocritical. What he had done instead was confess that his entire life was tragic, which is not the same thing, and which — somehow — made him more sympathetic to the council rather than less. I had handed him a spotlight and he had stepped into it beautifully and used it to make himself the protagonist of a story about his own failure, and the circle was watching him with something close to compassion, and I was still standing there being the thing trees had died because of.

I am a musician. I know when an audience has turned.

"I'm going back to Phandalin," I said. "You all can stay. I do not like the personal insults. I'm not interested in helping Lyra anymore."

Nobody tried to stop me. That was its own information.

Behind me, as I left the circle, I heard Brewbarry — loyal, enormous, dear Brewbarry — ask the remaining party the only question that had ever truly been in his register: "Do we kill all these people, or do we leave?"

And Soma, patient as tide and twice as inevitable, pulled him back from it. "Without the druids, the land is even more screwed than with half of them."

I was already away from the circle. The trees were easier to look at than the council, which was a relief.

I had one thing left I could do from here. Sending is not the sort of spell bards are expected to have unless they have made a few unusual choices, and Magical Secrets is a wonderfully literal name for the sort of choice I had made. I reached for the particular resonance that connected me to Reidoth — the old ranger, the man who actually understood what these woods were before they became anyone's ideology — and I sent my voice toward him across the distance between us, the way a melody travels through open air. I told him what was happening. The schism. The council. Adabra's faction and Falcon's confession and the circle that had turned before anyone had voted on anything.

Reidoth needed to know. Someone sensible needed to know.

Then I walked back toward Phandalin, with the sound of the council fading behind me, and I composed nothing. Some things don't want to be songs yet. Some things need to sit and get cold first before you can find the key they're actually in.

## Soma — The Druid Council Debate

The moment Vukradin walked away, every eye in the circle came to me.

Right.

Just me, then.

My knees hurt. My hands hurt. The staff sat in my grip like it had always belonged there, which was a lie. I had not been a druid long enough for anything to always belong anywhere. I was an old tortle with a late lesson, a friend's staff, and too many people looking at me like I had answers stored somewhere under my shell.

I am not naturally a speaker. I am a watcher. I sit with things. Weather. Tracks. Sick soil. A traveler talking too loudly over tea. You wait long enough, most things show you what they are.

Then you act.

Usually without an audience.

Meril never made speeches when he stayed with me. He made decisions. Quiet ones. He taught me names for things I had already known in my bones. Then he left me the staff, and I thought that was the end of it.

Shell sprout thinking, maybe. Even old shells can sprout in the wrong places.

Aldric Stone Path stood there with his arms folded and his jaw set. Weathered man. Mountain man. The kind of man who has said the same thing for years and been treated like wind through grass. Now someone had finally listened, and he had fastened himself to her for it.

I understood that.

Adabra had listened to him. That mattered.

But listening isn't the same as solving.

"I don't know how you don't see those as a problem as well, sir." I kept my voice level. Not soft, not hard — the voice I use when I'm telling someone a plain fact they've been avoiding. "She's bringing in yet another problem — these planar anomalies, these infestations, these blights on the land. Adabra says let nature take its course and get obliterated, basically." I let that sit for a breath. "What does Adabra say the solution is?"

Aldric opened his mouth. Closed it.

I was not trying to shame him. I wanted the answer. If there was one, I wanted it plain. I had spent too many hours watching the staff lose leaves and wake up new powers in my hands to pretend I knew everything.

Old in the body. New in the craft.

Bad combination some days.

Adabra herself stepped into the silence. Her voice was careful, measured — the voice of someone who has given this argument many times and believes it completely.

"The displacement is downstream of the intervention. The answer is to stop the cascade by withdrawing. The ecosystem finds equilibrium without help."

Equilibrium.

I had heard that word a lot lately. People like it. Smooth word. Clean edges. Sounds like a pond going still after someone stops throwing rocks.

That was not what we had seen.

I thought of the planar rift at Falcon's Cabin. Soil gone grey. Grass dying in rings. Talosians with their hands in the earth like they were teaching it to rot. That was not a pond settling. That was a wound under a bandage you were too polite to lift.

"Equilibrium comes in many forms." I heard my own voice come out quieter than I expected — not because I was uncertain, but because I wanted them to lean in. "One of these equilibriums could be the end of everything, of all life." I met Adabra's eyes. "That's not the equilibrium I'm here to protect."

Aldric sat down.

There it was.

Adabra noticed. I saw her notice. Her face did not change much, but something behind it shifted. Like a knot pulling tight.

She had lost something.

Not enough.

Then the centaur arrived.

I had not expected a centaur. Maybe I should have. Xanthopoulos knew the high valleys. His people saw what moved through mountain passes before lowland folk finished arguing about whether anything was moving at all.

Still. A centaur arriving in the middle of a druid council makes a person pause.

He was deliberate in the way that old creatures are deliberate. Not slow — considered. He looked at the circle. He looked at Adabra. He looked at me. He did not come in carrying a banner for either side, which was probably why the council had to make space for him. Pure interventionism, pure naturalism — he had the air of someone who had watched both become follies when held too tightly.

Then he looked at the staff.

The way he looked at it made something tighten in my chest that I didn't have a name for.

"I know whose staff that is," he said. "I know what it means that Meril gave it to her. He did not give it for sentiment. He gave it because he… picked her."

The staff was warm against my palm.

I knew its knots. Knew where it caught on my belt. Knew how it resisted when the ground went uneven. I knew it the way you know an old tool after enough mornings carrying it.

That did not mean I knew what it meant.

But Xanthopoulos said it out loud, in front of everyone, in a tone that expected it to be believed.

"It's just a staff," I said. "Isn't it?"

It came out wrong. Too much question in it.

No one answered. That was its own kind of answer.

Then Xanthopoulos told us about Corbin.

"I have news. Corbin is dead. Sister Kayla walked out. She's gone to the wind."

The circle went quiet in a different way.

I stood there and let it hit.

Corbin had been a farmer before he was a bandit. A shell sprout who had made bad choices and maybe had started to understand the weight of them. Sister Kaella was a killer in priest's cloth. We had left him guarding her because we were moving fast and thought fast was the same as right.

It is not.

They left a farmer turned bandit with a deadly assassin because they were in a rush to intervene.

"And Boney stood by and did nothing?" I heard myself say.

Not the right thing.

I knew that.

Boney is a skeletal horse of uncertain moral standing, and I had nowhere useful to put the thought, so it came out sideways. Happens when the kettle boils over and there is no cup under it.

Adabra, of course, was already moving.

"See, this is the price of interventionism. Dead bodies everywhere, by careless, careless intervention."

Xanthopoulos cut her off with the same flat calm he'd used on everything else. "I am not on your side. I'm not on theirs. I'm telling you what my people saw and what happened."

The circle was watching me.

I could have walked around it. Found softer ground, more forgiving language, let the silence do the work. I've done that before — let things settle into ambiguity because ambiguity is survivable.

"This was — we were planning on our —" I stopped. Started again. "I can't sugarcoat it. The ask was, do I sugarcoat it, or do I fess up?" I looked at Adabra. "I'm not sugarcoating it."

She wanted excuses. Something grand. Something she could pull apart in front of everyone.

I did not give her that.

But I also wasn't going to give her Corbin's death as a trophy.

"The interplanar treant?" I said. "Yeah, that's — I'm not going to cry for that. That treant was going to bring a lot more manifestation and horror with it."

"We've seen things — if left unchecked, the Talosians are starting to blight the land itself." Grey soil. Dead grass. Wrongness underfoot. I could still feel it in my soles. "Given that the Falcon's Cabin was overtaken by Talosians — and it's weird, you know, the Falconer asked us to intervene, but now he's an anti-interventionist. He's mad that we failed to intervene, and yet…"

I let it trail off. The contradiction was self-evident. I didn't need to finish it.

Corbin was dead because we moved too fast. That's true.

The blight would have spread to three valleys if we hadn't moved at all. That's also true.

Truths do not line up neatly because you are tired. Anyone who has kept a cabin knows that. Roof leaks. Fish spoil. Guest needs feeding. Storm coming. All true. Same day. Same two hands.

I looked at the staff.

Meril had been torn by this. I knew that now, or near enough. Let the wilds be. Protect the wilds. Wait. Act. Old fight. Older than me, probably. He had left the staff with someone outside the Enclave's habits. Someone late to the craft. Someone who knew more about keeping a patch of land safe than winning a doctrine fight.

Maybe he thought I could see it because I had not spent my life polishing the wrong answer.

Maybe he just left a friend a staff.

Both could be true.

The staff was heavier than it had been when he left it.

Or maybe my hands knew more now.

That was the only argument I had. I was fairly certain it was the right one.

---

## Brewbarry — The Emerald Enclave Council Verdict

The whole time I stood there, I was thinking about how easy it would be.

Not in a bloodthirsty way. I want to be clear about that. But I had already offered — before the speaking started, before the council settled in with their serious faces and their druid opinions — I had already looked around the glade and made the calculation. Everyone in the circle. Not a difficult problem. The Dragon Slayer on my hip, the rage that lives just below the surface. Done. Thirty seconds, maybe less. Soma had laughed, or something close to it, and we had moved on.

The point is: I had the solution. I just wasn't allowed to use it.

So I stood at the edge of the glade and watched words do the work instead.

---

Lyra spoke first. She stood before the council and explained what had happened at the rift — the failing ritual, the planar damage spreading through the forest like a sickness in wood, the treant that died when the intervention collapsed. She spoke carefully. She had prepared this. You could tell.

I hadn't been there for all of it, but I had been in enough places like that — Axelholm, the mines, the sites where the corruption sat in the ground and twisted everything it touched — to know what she was describing was real. The dwarves we found there weren't dwarves anymore. Not fully. Whatever had gotten into the stone had gotten into them too.

We said as much. The party made the case — the Talosian blight on the land, the things we'd seen, the twisted bodies that used to be people. The forest didn't know the difference between natural rot and this. The forest just died.

And then someone mentioned the Falcon's lodge.

I don't know who said it. Maybe Soma. But it landed. The Talosians had taken the Falcon's own lodge from him — his home, his seat, the thing that defined him — and that was what sitting idle got you. That was the cost of the philosophy Adabra was defending.

---

Adabra's response was long.

She was not a stupid woman. I want to say that, because what followed was not the speech of a stupid woman. It was the speech of someone who had thought about this for years, who had a framework for the world that was internally consistent and deeply felt, and who was absolutely furious that reality wasn't cooperating with it.

She talked about the natural order. About the arrogance of intervention. About cycles and balance and the kind of patience that watches a burning forest and calls it sacred.

I understood maybe half the words. But I understood the argument. I'd heard something like it before, in the tribe — the elders who said that what happened to us was meant to happen, that suffering was just the mountain testing its children, that you didn't push back against the world as it was. I'd believed it for a while. Then I stopped believing it.

I didn't say any of this. I just stood there, at the edge, and watched.

---

The council deliberated. Not for long.

When the presiding druid spoke, it was with the tone of someone who had made up their mind before Adabra finished her second sentence and had simply been waiting for the polite moment to say so.

"Adabra, the case you've made just does not hold. Yes, it is unfortunate that Corbyn died, but he was a criminal. And the party made the decision — Soma made the decision to save the forest from the dragon. That was the right decision."

Adabra's face did something complicated. I watched it happen.

The druid wasn't finished.

"Aldric over here admits freely that if we were to listen to you, the orcs — what's going on — is perfectly normal. And the Falcon here depends on money that these interventionists saved him, and he needs them to save him. All you managed to do was offend the bard."

That last part. I felt something warm move through my chest. All you managed to do was offend the bard. I filed that away. I was going to tell Vukradin that exactly as it was said. He would appreciate it. He would probably make a face and say something modest. I would tell him anyway.

The druid delivered the final piece with a kind of simple clarity that reminded me of how Soma talks when she's done arguing and has arrived at the thing that's actually true.

"The purpose of the Druids Enclave is not to save the multiverse, the purpose of the Druids Enclave is to save the forest. And to say that to just sit there and wait, and say that it's all part of some divine, cosmic plan — it makes no sense whatsoever."

---

The moderates had sided with Soma and Lyra. It was strange, watching a council decide a thing without anyone bleeding for it. But it had happened.

Adabra left.

She didn't say anything when she went. She just — went. Seething. The word that fits is seething. She walked out with the particular posture of someone who is not done, who is in fact far from done, who is simply choosing a different battlefield.

I watched her go and thought: that woman is still out there. That was not a person who had accepted a verdict. That was a person who had been outvoted.

Lyra was restored to full good standing within the Emerald Enclave. The council said the words. I watched Soma — steady, patient, present through the whole thing — and at the end she lifted her staff and brought it down once, deliberate, a punctuation mark in wood and magic. Small gesture. It said everything.

---

Then one of the druids leaned forward and said, with genuine regret in his voice, as though this was the actual tragedy of the evening:

"Is there anything you could do to get the bard to come back? We were told he plays very good music. We were very much looking forward to listening to him play."

I nearly laughed. In the middle of all of this — the politics, the philosophy, Adabra's fury, the fate of the forest — the council's deepest disappointment was that Vukradin had left early.

Honestly. Fair.

Soma gave them what comfort she could. "He's… you're gonna have to come see him in Phandalin, or when his studio opens."

Then she asked about Adabra. The question came out careful, the way Soma asks things when she already knows the answer might not be comfortable. "What do we do about Adabra? I'm not gonna bring up… she's still a druid at heart, right? Like… well, you let us know if she comes back, and, you know, in greater numbers."

In greater numbers. That was the honest fear under the polite words. Adabra hadn't lost. She had been outvoted. Those are different things.

And then the druid said the last piece, the one that stayed with me:

"Her behavior was absolutely disgraceful. Her attack, her personal attacks on Vukradin crossed the line."

I thought about the nights I'd stood outside while Vukradin played, just to hear it from a distance because the rooms were too small for me and the sound carried anyway. I thought about following a bard into the mountains because he was the first person who looked at me like I was something worth following. I thought about what it costs to be the thing people aim their contempt at, and how Vukradin just — absorbed it, and kept playing.

Her personal attacks crossed the line. Yes. They did.

A political victory. Won with words. No blood, no thirty seconds, no Dragon Slayer.

I will never fully understand how that works. But I was glad it did.

---

## Valphine — Counseling the Fallen Ranger

The others dispersed by degrees: Soma toward Xanthopoulos, because old authority recognizes its own even when one party is pretending to be only tired; Brewbarry toward the edge of the glade, where he waits when no one has yet authorized the simpler remedies. I remained where I was and watched Falcon.

There is a particular stillness in a useful person who has concluded he is useless. I had seen it in Menzoberranzan among nobles who discovered, too late, that they had been serving another house's design; among soldiers who understood that their courage had been converted into someone else's advancement; among victims who mistook the inevitability of pain for the absence of choice. Falcon had that stillness. His eyes had stopped calculating, not because there was nothing left to calculate, but because he had mistaken exhaustion for judgment.

Nihilism is wasteful in anyone. In a ranger, it is misused discipline. Here was a man trained to track, endure, judge distance, read weather, choose the hour of action, and he had allowed Adabra to teach him that his life had a single pattern: he intervened, and ruin followed. She had not created his wound. She had put a collar on it and called it philosophy.

I decided I wanted him.

Not sentimentally. Falcon was damaged, but damage is not the same as worthlessness. A wounded man with skill, guilt, and no doctrine strong enough to hold him is a resource in search of a hierarchy. Lathander, to his credit, is very good with such material.

So I approached without ceremony. Ceremony is useful when power must be displayed. This required pressure, not display. I placed myself where he would have to acknowledge me eventually, and waited until his reluctance tired of itself.

Then I looked at him properly.

There was no performance in his grief. No fashionable despair, no decorative ruin, no small plea disguised as self-loathing. He believed the indictment he had made of himself. He had arranged every failure into a private liturgy: intervention, consequence, loss; intervention, consequence, loss. Adabra had given that liturgy a respectable name.

That meant pity would be useless. So would contradiction. Men like Falcon do not need to be told that they have misread the evidence. They have told themselves that already, rejected it, and mistaken the rejection for wisdom.

I needed a different pressure point.

He looked at me with the familiar hesitation surface dwellers reserve for drow when they wish to appear civilized about their discomfort: the small pause, the recalculation, the disciplined refusal to stare at white hair, grey skin, and whatever tavern-bred education had taught them to expect from Menzoberranzan.

"How did you become a cleric of Lathander?" he asked. And then, because he was being honest enough to name the obvious: "You're… you're a drow."

I have been asked that question in many tones. Suspicion. Accusation. Academic curiosity wearing manners as a veil. His was simpler than that. He wanted to know how an apparent contradiction had survived itself. I respect practical questions.

"I was a denizen of Menzoberranzan," I told him. "And after the demons invaded and rampaged the town — came up to the Overworld, and wow, just the vividness of the sun beating down on us. It was a glorious moment."

I let the words stand. They were inelegant, but accuracy sometimes arrives in common cloth.

What I did not explain, because surface language is badly made for certain forms of holiness, was what the sun actually did to me.

The demons came first. Menzoberranzan, with its necessary cruelties and exquisite corrections, came apart under a violence even the great houses had mismeasured. That city had taught me hierarchy, secrecy, leverage, the proper uses of victims, the discipline of controlled pain, and the cost of failing to see the knife before it moved. It had taught me that life is not sacred merely because it persists. Life is material. Rank, will, and power decide what may be made of it.

It had not taught me the sky.

Then I reached the surface.

I had heard the words, of course. Warmth. Dawn. Hope. Light. The vocabulary of people who have been injured by a thing so gently and so often that they have mistaken its restraint for kindness.

They were wrong.

The sun hurt.

Not as a punishment. Not as instruction administered by a superior with appetite and witnesses. Not as a little private art performed on the body in measured increments. It hurt because its nature was to hurt. It laid itself across my skin and remained there, radiant, indifferent, inexhaustible. Every nerve understood the theology before my mind had finished arranging it. I had known pain below. I had cultivated it, refined it, invited it, made of it discipline and private pleasure. But this was larger. Cleaner. It required no matron, no priestess, no house sanction, no consent, no audience. It simply existed above the world and inflicted itself on everything beneath it.

That was when I understood Lathander.

Not as comfort. Not as mercy. Those are the explanations surface dwellers use when they wish to make power less embarrassing to worship. I understood him as the most astonishing god I had ever encountered: power incarnate, radiating pain openly, constantly, universally, and receiving devotion for it. Still they built temples. Still they lifted their faces. Still they called him good.

Lolth taught pain through hierarchy, appetite, fear, and the careful expenditure of lesser lives.

Lathander radiated it.

For a daughter of Menzoberranzan, that was not gentleness. That was majesty.

I became a cleric because the pain was worthy of worship. The light was power without apology, power made visible on the skin, power that did not conceal the wound it gave and did not diminish itself in order to be loved. How could I not kneel to that?

Falcon was silent for a moment. I watched the account enter him, not as doctrine, not yet, but as a possible replacement for the one Adabra had fitted around his throat. She had taught him to interpret pain as proof that action corrupts. I offered him a cleaner hierarchy: pain as proof of contact with power, guilt as useful pressure, suffering as material not yet properly consecrated.

"I don't even know why I ever supported Adabra," he said, and his voice had changed. The blankness was cracking into something more honest and, in its way, more painful. "We need to go deal with those orcs. I probably owe the bard an apology."

Progress.

He looked at me with the particular helplessness of someone who knows what needs to happen but has lost the vocabulary for it. "You have a way with words. Maybe you could convince him that I didn't mean harm?"

I considered him. A man who had aimed himself at worthy targets for much of his life, then bent himself around the wrong authority and mistaken the resulting distortion for wisdom. Not ruined. Warped. That distinction matters. Ruin is decorative. Warping can be corrected, provided sufficient pressure is applied.

"You know," I said, "get a little bit of thrill and sensation. The pain is a good way to kind of, like, really get a kick in your life."

It was inelegant. It was also true.

What I meant, though I did not burden him with the fuller formulation, was that the pain of seeing himself clearly was not a sentence passed against him. It was the useful part. The living part. A man who feels the injury of his own wrongdoing has not yet become doctrinally dead. Lathander does not soothe that pain away like a nervous attendant. He illuminates it until one can no longer pretend not to see where it begins, what it has touched, and what it demands.

As for the apology to Vukradin, I advised a gesture with substance. Not an apology made entirely of air. A musical instrument of real value, perhaps, something worthy of the bard's vanity and skill. Or a room in Falcon's retreat, a studio where Vukradin might work: space, silence, and material respect. Words are useful. Gifts, properly chosen, are words with weight.

Falcon received this as men often receive mercy when it has been disguised as a task. He nodded. He had been given something concrete, something he could carry without needing to become articulate all at once.

He asked me to mediate the conversation when the time came. He lacked the language, he said.

I found I didn't mind.

Across the glade, Xanthopoulos was taking his leave of Soma, and I caught the end of it: a quiet remark, weighted with the care of someone who does not spend judgment cheaply. That perhaps Meril had made the right choice in selecting her.

A precise thing to say. More precise still in its recipient.

I turned back to Falcon.

One correction at a time.

---

## Soma — The Aftermath and Next Steps

The fight was done.

The grove went quiet after. Too quiet. Leaves moving. People breathing. Someone shifting their weight in the grass like their knees had finally remembered they were old.

Mine had remembered a while ago.

Lyra straightened herself. Slow. Careful. Like dignity was a shawl that had slipped off one shoulder and she had to put it back where it belonged. She offered to buy us drinks at the Stonehill when we got back to town.

Good offer. Simple. Useful.

Vukradin was not there to hear it. Still felt like some of the gratitude was aimed at him. Gratitude does that. Finds the person whether they stayed for it or not.

I did not argue. My feet hurt. There was something dried on my forearm. Sap, blood, mud. Could have been all three. I left it alone.

---

We did not leave the grove. Not yet.

The council's words still sat in the air. The ground felt packed down by more than feet. I settled where I was and let the shell-sprouts talk.

Orcs, mostly.

They had opinions. Several each. They shared them all at once.

Privy Council obligations. Phandalin's perimeter. Duties. Roads. Responsibilities. People say responsibility louder when they have only recently noticed they have one.

The orcs were a real problem. The council was a real problem. The question of which real problem to address first was, apparently, a problem that required considerable volume.

I would have liked tea.

No one had tea.

---

Neverwinter won.

Or Neverwinter won and the orcs might be handled along the way, which is the kind of plan that sounds tidy until the road puts teeth in it. I have seen travelers make that kind of plan before. They stop at your cabin, drink your tea, tell you they are only passing through, and then the storm comes down before sundown.

Some lessons need weather.

I said nothing.

---

Somewhere in all the talking, while the grove started becoming trees and dirt again, I understood Falcon a little better.

Not all of him. Enough.

The way he had looked at Vukradin. Not like a man with one clean grievance. Like a man who had been moved and only later noticed his feet were not where he remembered putting them.

I had seen that look before. Lost hunters get it when they realize the path they trusted has been bending them away from home for hours.

Vukradin does not know he does it.

That is probably the honest version. He speaks and people shift. Their certainty. Their courage. Their shame. Their angle on the whole thing. He calls it music. He calls it truth. Maybe it is both. I have not caught him in a clean lie.

But there are people around Phandalin who made choices with him in the air. Some of them have started counting backward. Some of them are wondering how much of the choice was theirs.

Resentment grows slow. Like rot under a floorboard. By the time you hear the creak, it has been there a while.

The kid does not see it yet.

Silver tongues have that problem. You spend so long meaning well that you miss when people start feeling handled.

I did not say it. Not then.

What would I say? Your gift makes people feel managed even when you are trying to help?

He would listen. That was the trouble. He would take it in and carry it around. There would be a time for that.

Maybe.

---

Xanthopoulos had said it near the end. Quiet. Almost to himself.

*Perhaps Meril made the right choice.*

I had heard it. I don't think he intended me to hear it as much as I did.

The staff rested against my side. Familiar weight. Slight warmth. More leaves than last month. More power with them.

I did not like how neatly that lined up.

Meril had trained me late. Old shell, new druid. Maybe he saw an old tortle outside the Enclave's habits and thought I might understand the thing he had not settled: let the wild be, or protect it before waiting becomes another kind of harm.

That is a heavy thing to leave in someone's hands.

*Perhaps Meril made the right choice.*

Seven words. I turned them over in my mind like a smooth stone. Not looking for anything. Just feeling the weight.

Not *good enough*.

Not *useful enough*.

*The right choice.*

I am old. I have seen much. I have spent plenty of years suspecting Meril saw something in me I could not see plain. I was not going to make a song out of seven words from a centaur who did not waste them.

But I kept them.

Somewhere careful.

The company was loud. The grove was quiet again, but not empty. Phandalin stood somewhere behind us. Somewhere northwest, there were orcs and a road toward Neverwinter.

The staff was warm against my side.

That was enough for one night.
