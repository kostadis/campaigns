# Chapter 40 — Unraveling the Storm God's Secrets

## Vukradin — Pre Battle Observations At The Circle Of Thunder

The hill had been climbing for most of the afternoon, and then it stopped. The treeline thinned and there it was — a ring of standing stones against a sky that hadn't decided whether to storm. The clouds above the henge moved the way clouds don't move: inward, circling, held in place by something that wasn't wind.

Two half-orcs stood among the stones. Twelve twig blights ranged around them like a second ring, bark-dry and motionless. The half-orcs were moving — not marching, not chanting. Dancing. Slow, deliberate steps that traced a pattern I couldn't fully see from the treeline but could feel the shape of: something being called toward. Something being opened.

Corbin, I thought. Then I pushed the thought somewhere useful.

Valphine was the one who named it. She watched for a moment, head tilted, that particular stillness she gets when she's cataloguing rather than reacting. "Summoning ritual," she said. Quiet, certain. The way she identifies things she's seen before.

I looked at the dancers again with that frame on them. "Oh, yeah, you would recognize that in this," I said. "Animate Dead." Not a question. The motion had that quality — purposeful vacancy, movement in service of something absent.

Soma watched. Brewbarry watched. Neither of them saw it the way Valphine did, and I was moving through the same fog — you don't spend your early years in Phandalin studying Talosian summoning rites. What I felt was the thing underneath the recognition: we were already late. Whatever they were calling, they had been at it for a while.

"I wonder who they're summoning," I said.

"I mean, you'll find out," came back at me from somewhere to my left, dry as bark.

"No, we won't."

There are promises you make in a treeline. I meant it as one.

The tactical question resolved itself quickly in my mind into a single clean answer: Silence. You cast it on the chanters, you cut the ritual at its voice. I had used it before, at the lighthouse, at worse angles. "Alright, I'm gonna cast Silence," I said. "And I, once again, have no, like, connection to the board, so — but I am casting silence on whoever it is is chanting." I kept my voice level. "And if that is unknown, then the two spellcasters."

Except they weren't chanting. That was the thing. We waited, someone went back through the logic of it, and the answer came back clear and slightly absurd: the ritual had no vocal component. They were dancing. Twelve twig blights and two half-orcs turning slow deliberate circles in a stone ring, silent as a held breath.

"Is there no vocal component to the ritual?" I asked, already hearing the answer.

There wasn't.

"Awesome," I said. The word landed flatter than I intended.

I filed the Silence away. "I mean, if there's no vocal component, there's whole person — that's also an option." Hold Person. Different spell, same interruption. The ritual, we determined, would end only when the dancers stopped. The standing stones couldn't be disrupted from the outside; the movement had to cease. Which meant the people making it had to cease.

Brewbarry was watching the half-orcs step their slow circles in total silence. "So they're like, what, a pantomime club?" he said.

I looked at him. I looked at the henge. "Okay, guys," I said. "Do you want me to go with Silence or Hold Person?"

Before anyone answered, someone caught the gap: we hadn't rolled initiatives yet. The observation arrived with that particular mild embarrassment of a procedural thing almost skipped over in the press of the moment.

We rolled. I drew the short straw on the tie-breakers — except I didn't. I won them. Several, one after another, my name settling ahead of the others in the order like the situation had opinions. I had no strong feelings about going first. I had feelings about what going first meant I would have to do.

"Alright," I said, once the order was settled and clear. "So I've not cast Silence — because we're following initiative order, alright?"

The hill was still there. The stones were still there. The dancers had not stopped.

## Brewbarry — The Battle At The Circle Of Thunder

Twelve of them.

Vukradin said "Two twigs and an anchorite — the start of a joke," and he wasn't wrong, but twelve is not a joke. Twelve is a wall of dry, clacking wood and too many limbs, and they came fast. I was already moving.

That's the thing about the rage. People think it makes you stupid. It doesn't. It narrows everything down to what matters — where the threat is, where the gap is, where you can put your body to do the most good. The hill was steep and the standing stones were wrong, humming at a frequency that made my back teeth ache, and the two half-orcs at the center of it all were still dancing, still chanting, and I wanted to be the thing that made them stop.

The lightning bolt came before I reached them.

Vukradin had already said it, dry as old bark: "It's nice that we're all lined up in a perfect line there — that's convenient for him." He wasn't wrong about that either. The bolt split the air between us and I got my shoulder turned into it and the current raked through my scale mail and I was still standing because I am Brewbarry Root Smasher Ogalakadu and I have stood inside a dragon's breath, and a half-orc in a boar mask is not a dragon. Valphine took it worse. I saw her flinch — something going tight across her face that she would never call pain but that looked exactly like it. The invisible weight of Lathander's favor pressing on every swing, and it still turned against her twice. Some days the gods are funny.

Vukradin didn't waste the opening. He spoke and the two anchorites stopped dancing. Just — stopped. Mid-step, mid-chant, arms out, frozen like figures in a story someone stopped telling. She stopped them. Both of them. I've seen him do things with words that make no sense to me and probably never will, but this I understood immediately: they were objects now. Problems with a shape. I moved.

The handaxe is not my preferred statement. I am a big man with a big sword and I know how to use it, but the blights were between me and the anchorites and the blights needed clearing, so I pulled the rage up from wherever it lives in my chest — it feels like a door opening, every time, like something I've been keeping in a small room finally being let out — and I threw.

The first throw was embarrassing. The axe spun wide and I had a half-second of pure goliath shame before Valphine pushed something of her certainty into my hand, metaphorically speaking. Something steadied. The axe was already back in my hand — it comes back, that's the trick, that's what I've been calling the Elemental Cleaver, because naming things makes them more real — and I adjusted and threw again and the blight I hit came apart like kindling. The momentum carried: one blight dead, the kill gave me a heartbeat of something clear and sharp, and I used it to take a second. Two down. One more and I missed, and by then the battlefield was moving around me in all directions.

Boney bit a paralyzed anchorite. The skeletal horse that Soma has somehow convinced is a willing member of this party bit a frozen man in a boar mask and I respect that completely.

Prutha ran through blights like they were grass. He put a javelin into the second anchorite and then charged in with his greataxe and I watched him and thought, briefly, of myself ten years younger, when violence was still simple, before the exile made it complicated. The anchorite he hit was already staggered. Not down, but close.

Soma became an elk. I have seen this before and it still takes a moment. One second: Soma, tortle, shell, old and unhurried. Next second: enormous antlers, enormous mass, crossing the ground between them in a breath. She hit the anchorite like a wall of forest deciding to move. He didn't have a choice about it.

Then she turned and hit one of the standing stones.

I thought: is that part of it? The henge was wrong, humming, tainted — Talosian hands had been on it, doing whatever ritual left a deer split open at the center with its heart replaced by something black and glassy and cold. Breaking a stone felt right. Felt like undoing something.

Valphine was moving through the twig blights like a woman who finds this situation aesthetically appropriate. The radiant presences she called up swirled around her and the blights caught in them just — went. She crossed the field and the light went with her, sweeping the dry things from the hill in waves, and I kept count because counting kept me sharp.

The anchorites never got free. Vukradin said, somewhere to my left, "Stopping a man in his tracks — both men — that is a very powerful thing," the way a man says something he already knew but is pleased to have confirmed.

At the end, Valphine said her battle cry. She said: *"Die ye then."*

She hit the last anchorite and he went down and the chant that had been threading through the air for however long we'd been on this hill — it stopped. The clouds kept moving for a moment, like they hadn't been told yet. Then they peeled back. Slow. Reluctant. Like something exhaling. The pressure in my ears dropped. The ozone smell faded to wet leaves and old rain. The stones went quiet.

No thunderclap. Just the end of it.

I stood in the sudden silence and looked at the two bodies. Young, both of them. Younger than I expected under the boar masks. One with storm-cloud tattoos spiraling up his neck, each one with an open eye. The other with crossed boar tusks down both forearms. They'd been someone's devoted. Someone had put that ink in their skin and told them this mattered, and they'd believed it, and now they were dead on a hill in the Neverwinter Wood with their masks rolled free of their faces.

I didn't feel bad about it. I felt the way I always feel after a fight: present, tired in the right way, aware of every part of my body. Alive.

Vukradin called it: "Huzzah!"

I exhaled.

## Soma — Aftermath And Ideological Clashes

The ozone went first. Then the pressure in my ears released — not all at once, but in slow degrees, like something enormous exhaling. The clouds peeled back from the henge in a reluctant spiral, and the standing stones, which had been humming through my boot soles since we crested the hill, went inert without ceremony. No thunderclap. Just wet leaves and old rain, and the ordinary smell of a forest after weather.

I stood still for a moment and let the quiet settle.

It's a druidic site. That was the thing I kept returning to as I walked the circle. Valphine had asked it and gotten confirmation — anchorites don't build henges, they borrow them. Someone had stood here before Talos claimed it. Someone had read the standing stones as sacred in a different register entirely, and whatever ritual they had meant to conduct here had been slowly replaced, glyph by glyph, with something that killed deer and summoned storm and hummed with a frequency that made my back teeth ache.

The deer was at the center. I didn't approach it at first. I noted it from the edge of the circle the way you note a thing you will have to reckon with in a moment.

When I did walk over, the smell hit me: blood, cooled, and something underneath that had no name yet. The animal had been split throat to belly, a clean and deliberate cut, and where the heart should have been there was a hollow of black, glassy material. Not stone. Not metal. Cool to the touch, faintly humming the way the stones had been humming a minute ago. I put two fingers near it and pulled them back.

I have seen a blighted grove. I have seen what happens when something reaches into a living system and begins redirecting it. This was that, except it had been done to a specific animal, at a specific moment, for a specific purpose. The deer had been chosen. The thought sat in me like a stone.

The anchorites were younger than I expected. I looked at their faces after the boar masks rolled free — young half-orcs, the kind of young where the face still carries some softness under the tattoos. One had storm clouds and coiling spirals up his neck, each spiral containing an open eye. The other had crossed boar tusks repeated down both forearms in a different hand but the same ink. They had been marked by someone who knew what they were doing. Someone who had wanted them to carry Talos on their skin.

I did not grieve them. I want to be honest about that. But I noted the youth, and I noted the tattoos, and I thought about what Adabra had called me at the council — interventionist, as if that were a diagnosis — and I thought about what it had taken to get here, and whether the intervention had been clean.

It hadn't been clean. It rarely is.

The argument started almost before the blood had dried.

Vukradin moved toward the bodies with the particular energy he gets when a principle is at stake. Holy symbols of Talos, he said. We need to return them. To the people who owned them.

Prutha looked at the dead priests. Pointed. *We found owners*, he said. *We give to them.*

I watched Vukradin attempt to reason through this. He was persuasive — he always is — but Prutha's logic was not wrong so much as it was complete in a way Vukradin's wasn't.

"You're trying to reason with an orc," Brewbarry said, which prompted Vukradin to ask whether that was racist, which prompted Brewbarry to explain that he wasn't saying orcs were evil, only dumb. "I'm dumb, but he's dumber. It's like dumb and dumber."

I didn't intervene. That's the honest account.

While the three of them cycled through this, Prutha destroyed the symbols. I watched him do it with the same focused attention he brings to everything. Then Valphine placed one of the holy symbols on the skull of a fallen anchorite and smashed it with her mace, and the sound it made against stone was flat and final.

"God. Valfina, we need to have a conversation about conflict-free goals," Vukradin said.

He was not wrong that it was a conversation worth having. But the symbols were already in pieces, and the argument had already happened in the order it always happens with us: the principle is stated, the chaos proceeds, and then we discuss the wreckage.

Then Prutha said something quiet, and I paid attention.

"My great-uncle said dawn comes for everyone. Even these ones. I did not think dawn would feel quiet."

He was looking at the bodies. His own kind, wearing someone else's faith on their skin. There was nothing performative in it — no theater, no flourish. He stood there with the weight of what he'd said settling around him, and Valphine answered him simply: *Dawn is powerful.*

He grunted and waved his axe. But the grunt was different. It had something in it.

I have seen a lot of converts and a lot of grief, and I have learned not to confuse one for the other too quickly. But Prutha was carrying something real in that moment, and I let it stand.

That was when I felt it. The staff had been trembling since the cursed clearing — a low, persistent movement in the wood, like a vine testing a trellis. Now it stopped. I looked down.

Near the crown, where the three leaves curved outward, there was a fourth bud. Tight, green, the size of a thumbnail. It hadn't been there before the fight. It hadn't opened. The staff had simply added it, quietly, without announcement, the way a tree puts out a new shoot in spring because the conditions have been met.

The staff goes quiet. That was the thing I noticed most — the trembling had stopped, and the quiet was not absence but a different kind of presence. Something had been registered. Something counted.

I stood with that for a moment longer than I needed to, shell sprout.

Then Brewbarry was asking about the leather thong with the boar tusk — *sorry, a leather thong?* — and Valphine's face had gone very interested in the way it goes when something aligns with her theology, and she was already talking about putting it in her sacraments.

I looked at the fourth bud. The henge stood around me, inert now, its original purpose obscured beneath someone else's use of it, and somewhere in the center of it a deer lay open with a hollow where its heart had been.

The storm was over. The quiet was real. I wasn't sure yet what we had earned, or what it had cost, but the staff was still.

## Valphine — Exploring The Caves Of The Circle Of Thunder

The tunnel smelled the way the Underdark smells when it's trying to pass itself off as something harmless — stone cold, mineral-flat, a hint of something decomposing at the far edge of the air. I followed Brewbarry in, which meant I was already second-guessing the decision before my eyes had finished adjusting.

Soma had remained outside with Boney. The tunnel mouth was too narrow for an elk, which meant the tortle in elk form was standing watch in the wood while the rest of us walked into a cave because Brewbarry was thirsty. This is what passes for a plan among the Protocol Shell-Crackers.

"More dungeon than refrigerator," Vukradin muttered, somewhere behind me. "Just, you know, FYI."

I could hear Prutha's footfalls just ahead of his breathing — faithful, uncomplicated, following as she always follows now. Another convert who hadn't yet learned to be complicated about it.

"It's a wine cellar," I said. I didn't actually believe that, but Brewbarry's entire demeanor brightened when I said it, and sometimes that's reason enough to say a thing.

"It's perfect," he said. "But I'm with you, man." He meant it the way he means most things: entirely, without irony, at full volume.

The first cave had a boar in it.

This is something I should probably have anticipated, given that we had just dismantled an orc cult whose iconography consisted almost exclusively of boar imagery. Brewbarry spotted it and announced that the meat was over here, that they had clearly been preparing for a barbecue, that there was a bowl. He was delighted. Vukradin, for reasons I still find difficult to fully account for, decided the correct response was to insult it.

He looked at the animal — a living creature, rooting in the cave dirt, doing nothing to any of us — and opened his mouth to deliver what I can only assume he considered a devastating rhetorical blow. The boar stared back at him with small, flat eyes and registered nothing. No flinch. No wounded pride. Complete indifference. Vukradin looked briefly as though he'd been the one mocked.

Then the boar charged.

"That wasn't very nice," Brewbarry said afterward, with the kind of moral conviction he usually reserves for more significant moments. "Why would you insult the boar? The poor boar. That's mean."

"You're okay with boars," Vukradin said, "but orcs are just inherently evil."

"No," Brewbarry said, entirely serious. "I wasn't saying the orcs are evil. I was saying the orcs are dumb. Sorry. Different things."

I watched this exchange happen. I had questions. I asked none of them.

What I did instead was let the guardians that circle me finish it. They had been circling since the ritual, radiant and faintly impatient, and the boar — already diminished by Vukradin's second attempt, which had landed — took the full weight of them. Lathander's light doesn't apologize for what it does.

"You now have radiated pork meat," I observed.

"Perfect for barbecue," Brewbarry said, already moving. "Now all we're missing are drinks. Let me resume my search."

Vukradin made a small, self-vindicating gesture. "It was coming right for us."

"It was," I said. Because it was. After the insult he'd weaponized into something sharper, anyway.

The second cave was more interesting. The lintel above the entrance was carved — a boar pursuing a stick figure, which struck me as either symbolism or a warning, depending on who was being asked to read it. Several boars were penned inside, alive and unsurprised by our arrival. Brewbarry said, "I'm tempted to say this is boring," and waited for someone to appreciate it. I gave him nothing. Some gifts are better deferred.

The satchel was in the corner. Dark leather, sealed with wax, and the wax was stamped with a circle and a single vertical line through it — Talos's mark, simple as a scar. My fingers found it before the others had finished looking at the boars.

"We should destroy the symbol," I said.

"Do you want to see what's inside before you—"

"Destroy the symbol. Then open the satchel."

The wax broke cleanly. A good seal, actually. Someone had pressed it with care.

Inside: three letters, the same hand throughout, the ink slightly smeared at the margins from being folded in haste. I read them in order.

*Your storm holds. Grannoc's does not. He is reckless; he will summon before the Lodge is ready.*

So Grannoc was a name, and the name meant recklessness, and the Lodge — Falcon's Lodge, presumably — was part of a sequence. We had known the Talosian network was coordinated. Here was the coordination, in ink.

The second letter was the one that settled cold in my chest.

*The Chosen is pleased with the boy from the eastern clan. Continue feeding him with stories. Do not yet tell him about the leash.*

"Holyish," Vukradin said quietly, reading over my shoulder.

Someone is being cultivated. Someone young, from the east, being fed a narrative someone else designed. The leash not yet revealed. I know what that looks like from the inside — the House does not announce its arrangements until you've already agreed to all the terms. I read that sentence three times.

The third letter was the shortest.

*If the henge falls, the manse summons in its place. If the manse falls, I summon myself. The boar comes. The storm comes. You are not to fail twice.*

We had just taken the henge. Which meant the manse was next — had always been next, by design. And whoever signed these letters without signing them at all had already accounted for our success.

There was a fourth fragment, this one from the firepit in a different cave — half-burned, barely saved, a different hand entirely. Sharper. Less ceremonial. Brewbarry read it aloud, because someone had to.

*…dawn cannot rise where the storm already lives. — K.*

K. A single letter, signing nothing, claiming nothing. I had seen that signature referenced before, in pieces of intelligence that had never quite assembled into a face. I filed it where I file things I do not yet know how to use.

Vukradin was already moving toward the exit — impatient, or processing, or both. He walked quickly when his mind was full, which meant he walked quickly often. I let him go ahead.

I almost missed it. Almost.

The floor of the cave, at the far edge of the torchlight: a seam. A square cut too clean to be natural, too faint for someone in a hurry to bother noticing. Vukradin had just stepped over it without registering it at all — his mind elsewhere, cataloguing implications, composing the debrief he would give Soma later. He was already three steps past it.

I crouched. The compartment opened on the third careful press, hinged from within. Inside: a single vial. Dense, the color of polished steel catching light, sealed with a stopper that had never been opened. I turned it over in my fingers. The magic in it was unmistakable — deep and defensive, the kind that settles around its carrier like a second skin. Something that would make a person nearly impossible to put down.

I held it for a moment. The Underdark upbringing is very clear on what to do with things you find that other people didn't notice: you keep them. It's not a rule so much as a reflex, worn into you over years of learning that advantage has a shelf life and visibility is a liability.

But Vukradin was going to be walking into whatever came next — the manse, or the person behind these letters, or the thing the third letter meant when it said *I summon myself* — without this.

He would refuse it on principle if I told him. He would deliver a careful, sincere speech about conflict-free acquisition and the ethical provenance of magical resources, and then he would leave it in the cave, and then he would possibly die because of it. I am a servant of Lathander, who teaches that suffering is sacred — but there is a difference between suffering that illuminates and suffering that is simply avoidable and stupid.

I closed my fist around the vial. I stood. I walked to where Vukradin had paused near the tunnel mouth, gesturing to Brewbarry about something, his pack open at the flap where he'd been rearranging after the letters.

I tucked the vial into the inner pocket of his pack, behind the folded notes, where it would be cold against his back and feel like nothing unusual. He didn't turn around.

He carries it now without knowing. Lathander's light lands where it's needed, not where it's asked for. I've started to believe that.

## Vukradin — The Final Cave And The Map Fragment

Narux's cave was the smallest of the three, and it smelled like him — orc-sweat and old vellum and something faintly charred, as if lightning had moved through the space so many times that even the stone had absorbed the habit.

I let the others go in first. Brewbarry stepped past me, looked around, and said what he always says when a room disappoints him.

"There's nothing in this cave. Let's beat it."

Valphine found the shield first — buried under a folded cloth, unadorned but solid, the kind of thing you don't notice until it catches the torchlight at the right angle. She passed it toward the center of the room without ceremony. Unassigned. Another item in the ledger of things that belong to someone we haven't decided yet.

The vellum sketches were worse. Narux had drawn Gorthok the way a child draws a nightmare — four legs, a silhouette the size of a granary, tusks trailing what had to be lightning. The annotations were in Orcish, and Valphine translated the ones that mattered: *feed before unleashing*. *The leash holds only if the stones sing.* There was a leather cord beneath the sketches, looped and knotted, and I did not touch it. Whatever it was for, I already didn't want to own it.

Then the map fragment.

Torn along one edge, soft at the corners from handling. Neverwinter Wood laid out in a dead man's hand — three sites marked with cult semantics I had to work out by looking at them together. The Circle of Thunder, the henge we had just finished clearing, was X'd through. Not in defeat. In *advance*. They had expected to lose this place. It was a decoy, or a distraction, or a sacrifice — some term that amounts to the same thing, which is: we were never the threat they were afraid of.

The Woodland Manse was double-circled. Active. Grannoc, presumably, already moving through whatever Narux had started here.

And Falcon's Hunting Lodge was the target. Both lines converged on it. The whole plan pointed there.

Brewbarry looked at the lodge marker and arrived, inevitably, at his own conclusion. "That's where they hide the drinks," he said. "Hidden in plain sight."

I did not have the energy to explain that this was not what hidden in plain sight meant.

"Okay," I said, because we were all looking at it and someone needed to say something. "We knew that we wanted to go to those places anyway. Those are the next places we have to go."

I pulled out the running list — the one I keep, partly for myself and partly because no one else will. I crossed off the entry. Horse with no rain. Done. The Circle of Thunder — what we had gone there to do, what we had told ourselves we were doing for Corbin — was finished. "I'm going to cross off the horse with no rain," I said. "We've completed it. And amazingly enough, the map leads us to the next two locations."

Both entries were marked in red.

"They are marked in red," I started, "because — " and I paused, because I knew how it sounded — "I have not yet gotten an apology."

From Falcon. From Falcon the hunter, who had been rude to us at the druid council, who had every reason to be rude to us at the druid council, as I was about to remember in a way I didn't especially enjoy.

The conversation that followed was one of those conversations where everyone already knows the answer but is waiting for someone to say it in a way that doesn't make them feel terrible. Valphine offered to be the one who smoothed the way, to talk where I might only push — and I appreciated it, because it meant I didn't have to ask. She had apparently already laid groundwork at the druid conclave — proposed a musical instrument to Falcon, some gesture of good faith, and he had said yes to it. I had not known this. I made a note.

And then came the part I had been not-thinking-about.

"Okay, guys — Falcon, the hunter, wants us to go to these places, but you know, he was pretty mean to us at the druid council, he doesn't really like us very much..."

I was working up to something, a defense, an accounting. Then the thing I had glossed over in the briefing reasserted itself. The Woodland Manse. The orcs. The timeline.

We were supposed to deal with the Woodland Manse. We didn't. Then the orcs attacked Falcon's lodge.

"We didn't deal with that," I said, and I meant it as a statement of fact, but it came out sounding like a plea. "It can't be in five places at once, you know? There's only so much we can do."

Falcon had put his faith in interventionists. That was the word — *interventionists*. And the intervention had not come, and the blowback had been considerable.

"His girlfriend died?" I heard myself say.

Brewbarry said, "Excuses, excuses," with the cheerful blankness of a man who has never once in his life felt accountable for a consequence he didn't personally cause with his hands.

I turned the map fragment over, looking at the torn edge, the ink bleeding slightly where the vellum had gotten wet at some point. Narux's planning. Narux's dead hand pointing at Falcon's lodge, at the Manse, at all the places we had not yet been. The light-footed boots — reward, promised, contingent on work we hadn't done before the damage was done.

Valphine was already thinking through the broker angle. That was something. Falcon was ready to give the apology, apparently — he'd had time to process it, or Valphine had helped him process it, or both, back in some conversation I hadn't been present for. The red markers on the list didn't have to stay red.

I set the map fragment down on the vellum sketches, the torn edge lined up with nothing, pointing nowhere in particular. The leash coiled underneath. The shield still unassigned.

There was only so much we could do. I knew that was true. I had said it aloud and I believed it.

I just kept finding, when I looked at the red markers, that believing it didn't move them.
