# Chapter 14 Freeing Big Al, Petunia and watching a ranch burn

## 4-02 Taraskh 1495 

Soma

Vukradin halts us when we spot four brigands, easily recognizable as Carver’s disposable crew. My sigh is internal, a slow puff of air against the inside of my shell.

“I intend to parley,” Vukradin announces, foolishly.

“You are going to send Corbin, the terrified one?” I ask, though I already know the answer. Corbin, the lost boy, is dispatched as our emissary. He tries to smooth-talk his way in, but his fear is too loud. Predictably, the bandits respond by drawing steel.

Seeing the danger to my bale, I transform into a Brown Bear. I run forward, my powerful form covering the distance. My claws connect with the first bandit. I can feel the blood and life being ripped from him. He dies immediately.

From somewhere I can feel Brewbarry become big. He leverages this new power with primal fury, dispatching another bandit instantly. Good. 

Meanwhile, Vukradin finally uses his magic for utility, putting one bandit to sleep. The last one flees—no one races after him. Too often we have seen people escape only to pursue them and kill them. Killing is necessary, but too much of it disrupts the balance. 

The combat is done. We have a captive, the perfect source of information.

I growl. I wish to speak, but I cannot. Vukradin ties up our prisoner. 

Vukradin wakes the bandit. The bandit opens his eyes, and then I can see the terror that he feels. He looks around and sees that he is surrounded by the menacing bulk of Brewbarry, the heavy shadow of my bear form, and Vukradin’s own dramatic presence.

“Where is the Carver?” Vukradin demands.

The bandit, whose name is Skippy, cracks instantly, terrified and smelling strongly of fear. “I don’t know where the Carver is, but I do know that the Carver has an ambush for you prepared at the Butterskull Ranch”.

Vukradin demands to know how the Carver knew. He always thinks there are spies. 

“It’s boringly predictable,” Skippy whimpers, explaining that the Carver knew that knocking out the ranch would generate a quest, causing adventurers like us to walk right into his trap. He knows that Harbin uses the quest board to hire out adventurers. He further reveals that the ambush is led by Borg the Hammer, a half-orc enforcer. 

Vukradin wants to know how the Carver is so well informed. Skippy is tempted to keep silent, but he looks at the blood on my claws and reconsiders. He tells us that one of Carver’s henchmen is Sister Kayla. She is the Spymaster and runs their spy network in Phandalin.

We need to go quickly. But Vukradins wants to return the prisoner to Phandalin. 

This will take days of travel, and the Borg may lose patience. Vukradin is insistent; he will not allow this poor prisoner just to sit there defenseless. 

Corbin suddenly offers to guard him. And as he eyes the captive, he asks the ridiculous question: “Vukradin, can I take his armor? If I take his armor, is it conflict-free armor?”

I pinch the bridge of my snout. The ongoing debate over loot is tiresome. This armor is clearly "stolen merchandise" if acquired through threat or capture.

Vukradin, naturally,  defends his principles. “We all have our challenges, things that we have to face,” he declares dramatically. “You can’t take the armor. And we have to face the Borg.” 

Corbin wants to hide in his shell. How do these humans survive without a shell? 

But then, perhaps because he has no shell, he comes up with a good idea, “What if I take care of him here?” 

Vukradin is dubious. But Corbin is insistent. The former bandit promises to take care of Skuppy. He promises to take care of him. I can see that Corbin wants revenge. Vukradin notices it as well and tells him that Skippy better not be dead by the time he returns. 

### Vukradin

The air here, near Butterskull Ranch, feels heavy, not with mist but with malice. We bypass the main trail completely, electing for a stealthy approach from the north. I keep my movements precise, channeling my focus—not on the combat I dread, but on the certainty that this is an opportunity for honest coin. This path, guided by information bought with minimal violence, is the artistic path.

We creep past the charred remains of the barn and smithy. Then, scattered near the outbuildings, I see them: dead bodies. Orcs and humans both. They are already swarming with flies. *Plunder.* My gorge rises. This casual aftermath of slaughter is precisely what I despise.

Others would plunder these corpses, and that plunder would help me get the music studio. And here, far from my friends, I could do this and none would be the wiser. Then I correct myself, “It is not what I do in front of anyone that sets my moral compass. But what I do when no one is looking. I will not scavenge them. That is not who I am and it is not my style.” 

My gaze is drawn to the fresh wounds on the orc bodies; they look ripped and mangled, injuries that suggest something much larger and more vicious than human bandits. This confirms Borg the Hammer is here, or something big. 

Quietly, I slip toward the back of the house. As I get there I see more dead orcs. And I also notice open windows.  where the cellar door is. It groans faintly, but my sleight of hand is sufficient. Downstairs, the air is earthy and cool. The cellar is lined with shelves holding peculiar skulls made of butter . But most importantly, there he is: a large figure tied to a chair, a burlap sack covering his head .

I quickly work the ropes.

“Are you Big Al Kalazorn?” I whisper, untying the man.

He is startled, but relieved. He is Alphonse “Big Al” Kalazorn, the man we were sent to find .

“You have a… there's more of you?” he asks, relieved .

“It's a rescue mission,” I assure him .

He explains quickly, his voice hushed and hurried, that a band of orcs first raided the farm, but then the rot-tusk ogres, led by the half-orc Borg the Hammer, showed up, killed the orcs, and seized the property .

“I'm thinking that's what's going on. They kept you alive as the bait, huh?” I muse . Borg intended for the quest to draw us into a trap.

I urge him toward the stairs: we must retreat to where Corbin waits. But Big Al pauses.

“I really need my cow,” he says, utterly distraught . “She's my prized possession. Without her, you know, the Butterskull Ranch is just another…” .

I look at him. A cow. This is the moment for negotiation. “Okay, we can find her cow. She's not here, you just said she's not here, so we need to leave and go look for her,” I decide .

The reward is astonishing. “I got this suit of mithril chainmail that I'm willing to exchange for… You finding my cow,” he offers .

This is substantial, legitimate coin. I must verify its source. “How did you come by Mithril Chain Mail, Big Al?” I press .

“I bought it,” he insists. He explains that he’s a retired veteran and former Sheriff of Triboar, the predecessor to Darathra. The chainmail is clean. It is untainted by blood or banditry. Fair trade.

We rush him back to Corbin at our remote camp. We secured the primary objective, but the contract demands the cow.

Soma is immediately practical. “Hey, this is where Speak with Animals might come in handy. I could ask the pigs if they've seen the cow,” she says.

I agree. Within the hour, thanks to her efficient magic, we find the prize: Petunia, bearing the crucial stamp, “Big Al’s Cow.” She is whisked off to safety.

Now, the true objective begins. We retreat to a nearby hill where we can observe.

“I don't think we need to be near… that near the farmhouse, right? Like, we can watch it from…” I mutter, selecting a spot roughly 250 feet away . We wait patiently, prioritizing the hunt for Borg over a direct frontal assault . We are here to track the villain back to his source, not merely crash his party. This caution, this patience, is the price of honest adventuring.

###   Valphine

The wind at Butterskull Ranch chills my skin, a minor irritation that only enhances the strange, glorious discomfort of the Overbright. We have accomplished the mundane task: rescuing Big Al Kalazorn and recovering his absurd, prized cow, Petunia. Now, we settle into the vantage point we chose on the hill, waiting for the true action to begin .

Below us, Borg the Hammer emerges, a large half-orc clad in chainmail and carrying a shield . His armor is efficient, even if his tactics are crude. He finds the pathetic bandit who fled the skirmish at Conyberry—Bandit Number 4—and immediately begins to scream .

I cannot decipher the half-orc's guttural words entirely, but the sheer fury is palpable. This Borg is disciplined, certainly, unlike the chaotic brutes one usually encounters in this crude overworld. He is furious that the adventurers—us—did not walk directly into his predictable trap .

Borg's frustration mounts. He executes the fleeing bandit swiftly, eliminating the problem of incompetence .

He shouts again, and three large figures lumber out of the farmhouse: Rot-Tusk Ogres. They wear the insignia of the tribe we dealt with before, the ones seeking revenge for the ogres we killed previously.

But they do not fight. Instead, Borg engages in something quite delicious: gratuitous violence . He starts looking for a way to burn the place down .

I watch as the farmhouse and the cornfields catch fire. The flames are high, casting a harsh, orange glow across the fields. This is not a necessity; this is rage and destruction. Borg is venting his wrath on the environment because we denied him his intended bloodshed.

Brewbarry murmurs, concerned about chasing the enemy, and asks if we should try to save the farm.

Vukradin, in his ongoing fixation, worries only about the practical reward: “It might affect your chainmail.”.

I stifle a laugh. Mithril chainmail can survive fire . I care little for the ranch's wooden structure, and even less for the sentimentality attached to it. Fire is transformation; it is purification. There is a raw, satisfying power in observing such wanton, efficient destruction .

The destruction is a spectacle—a brutal commentary on the failures of the Carver's plan.

I silently agree with the efficiency. Let the fire burn. It is a glorious, fleeting display. Borg destroys the farmhouse, the cornfields, and the gardens. After engaging in this wanton destruction, the half-orc turns to the three Rot-Tusk Ogres.

He points the ogres in a direction entirely opposite to the direction he takes . He is splitting his forces, a small spark of tactical cunning amidst his rage.

Vukradin speaks for the party: "I think we follow Borg.".

Yes. The trail of the furious Borg is the trail to the true center of the intrigue. The raw scent of rage and the heat of the burning farmhouse are a fine reward for patience. The pursuit of the schemer is always more rewarding than the simple destruction of his tools.
