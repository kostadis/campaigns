## **Brewbarry — The Dragon's Mountain Fortress**

The mountain fortress of Icespire Hold didn't look like somewhere you'd bring a dragon to heel. It looked like somewhere the mountain itself had given up trying to keep the cold out.

We found Cryovain inside a summoning circle on the rooftop battlements — a great ring of burning light, geometric and wrong, the kind of geometry that doesn't belong to any stonecutter or architect I've ever seen. The dragon stood at the center of it. Or was held at the center of it. The distinction mattered, and I wasn't sure which was true.

The Carver was doing something to him. That's the only way I know to say it. The obsidian weapon, the mental amplifier, whatever Vukradin wanted to call it — it was working. Cryovain's eyes had a film over them, a glaze I recognized from men I'd seen after too many days without sleep or water. Presence, but not quite home. He was fighting it. Or he had been. Hard to say how much of the dragon was still left in there.

Cryovain was enraged. Of course he was.

He launched off the stone and climbed the air in one beat of those enormous wings — thirty feet up in an instant, and suddenly my halberd was swinging at nothing. I stood there for a moment with the weapon raised and the wind from his ascent hitting me in the face like an open hand.

Valphine sent a bolt of divine light up after him. It carved a bright line through the cold air and missed. The mountain swallowed the light.

Then Soma did something, and a column of silver moonlight descended out of nowhere, out of sky that didn't have a moon positioned for it — the kind of thing I have learned not to question about Soma. The moonbeam found the dragon and the dragon made a sound that I felt in my back teeth.

That opened something in me. I won't dress it up. I am Goliath. I was built for this.

I grew.

There is no cleaner way to describe what happens when the rage comes properly. I am already large. And then I am more. My reach extended, the battlements shrank, the thirty feet of air between me and the dragon became manageable. I brought the halberd up and then I brought it down, and then again, and then again — each strike finding the place where scales meet scale, where the neck joins the shoulder, where size means you can put weight behind every swing that would stagger a lesser creature. Cryovain was not a lesser creature.

But I hit him hard enough to matter. Several times.

I felt each impact as much as I delivered it. That's the honest account. The cold bites through everything when you're that exposed. Dragon breath is not like standing in bad weather. It reorganizes you. The first time it hit, I understood in a bodily way — not a thinking way, a bodily way — that I was at the edge of what I could absorb. The second time it hit, I was somewhere past that edge, running on a kind of forward momentum that isn't quite decision-making.

My body was doing the math. The math was not comfortable.

Soma, somewhere behind me, was saying don't kill, don't kill — he was just a victim. I heard her. I was not sure what to do with the information in that moment. Cryovain had just breathed ice through me twice and the Carver's influence was still inside him, still pressing, still trying to make the ritual stick. The Carver needed three more rounds. I had fewer rounds left than that if I took another hit like the last one.

This is what the tribe never understood about me. They thought the rage was the absence of thought. They were wrong. The rage is very clear. The rage is math.

The dragon came down. The halberd went in. I don't remember the exact sequence — that's also the honest account. What I remember is the sound the circle made when it broke. The burning geometry guttered and went out, and the Carver's essence — whatever it was, whatever had been reaching through that obsidian instrument into the mind of a white dragon — dissipated. Like breath in cold air. Like it had never been more than that.

## **Valphine — The Fall of Cryovain**

The breath comes without warning — or rather, it comes with every warning, because it always comes, because dragons are not subtle — and Cryovain turns that vast pale head and *exhales*, and the world becomes cold and white and wrong. Brewbarry is in the path of it. Of course Brewbarry is in the path of it. I watch the frost pour over him like a wave and I think, with the calm detachment of someone who has already calculated the outcome, that he should not still be standing after that. And yet.

He is still standing.

I have spent enough time around Goliaths now to know that this should surprise me more than it does. It doesn't. I file it under *things that are true about Brewbarry* and move on.

Vukradin is already moving — not in the way he usually moves, which is toward a conversation no one asked him to start, but with real intent, bardic inspiration threading through the space between thought and action like a needle pulling silk. Whatever he does, it lands. The crack of it finds Cryovain somewhere that matters. Above us, Soma's moonbeam has been burning steadily through all of this, patient and relentless, a column of silver fire that does not negotiate.

They have done their part.

The dragon is still standing. Not by much.

I have missed this creature several times today. A fact I am aware of with complete precision, because I track failure the way I track everything: clinically, without sentiment, and without looking away. Lathander's light does not always land where I aim it. I have made my peace with this. The pain of the miss is itself an offering — I mean that literally; I have constructed a theology around it and I stand by the construction — but there is a difference between offering and *purpose*, and purpose has not yet been served.

I raise my hands.

I call the radiance.

The sacred flame comes down.

The number is 3\. Not an impressive number. Not a number that features in triumphant accounts. Three is the number of a thing that barely existed, a flicker, a candle, the minimum viable expression of divine will. Three. I have spent the better part of this fight producing more ambitious numbers than 3 and watching them accomplish nothing, and now here is this small, undeniable, sufficient 3\.

The dragon collapses.

*"It's 3, so he died,"* and yes — yes, that is precisely correct. The dragon died because 3 was what was needed, and 3 was what I had, and there is something almost funny about that if you are the kind of person who finds the gap between effort and outcome funny. I am. I do.

*"And the Carver's essence dissipates, and it too is gone."*

I stand in the silence after that and I look at what remains: the vast cold body of something that was, a moment ago, a threat of cosmological significance, now simply a very large dead thing on a very cold roof. The Carver is gone — not fled, not retreated, *gone*, unmade by the same stroke that unmade its vessel. Two deaths for the price of one flickering, modest, wholly undramatic 3\.

Lathander's will is not always legible. I have made my peace with this also. But sometimes — not often, not as a matter of habit, but *sometimes* — you are given a moment that is not subtle at all, that is in fact the most direct possible statement a god could make, and the statement is this: *I was waiting for the right moment. That was it.* All those misses, all those small humiliations of wasted light, and then this — the final breath of a dying dragon extinguished by the smallest, truest flame I could produce.

I exhale.

The cold bites at my skin, and I let it.Soma — The Fate of the Veteran

The man was already down. Already still. Vukradin's Hold Person had taken the fight out of him completely — he lay there on the stone like something a wave had deposited, a veteran soldier reduced to a pair of open, terrified eyes. You could see him in there. That's the thing nobody mentions when they talk about paralysis. The body stops. The person doesn't.

"Don't kill, don't kill\! He was just a victim\!"

I heard myself say it and I knew, even as the words left my mouth, exactly how much weight they carried. About as much as a dry leaf. I've been traveling with these shell sprouts long enough to know when a decision has already been made by the time someone's asking for opinions.

Still. I put myself on record. That matters to me, even when it changes nothing.

The debate happened the way these things always happen — fast, slightly chaotic, everyone talking past the actual question. Was he a victim? Probably. Most people who end up working for something like the Carver are victims at some stage of the process. That doesn't mean they're safe. That doesn't mean you turn your back on them. But it also doesn't mean you take a halberd to a man who can't move his hands.

I tried to do it differently. I used the bear claws — not to kill, never to kill, just to weaken him further, to remove the possibility of threat without removing the man entirely. Then I moved the moonbeam over him. More pressure. Less life in him. The logic being: if there's less to fear, there's less reason to finish it.

That logic did not survive contact with Brewbarry.

He's not cruel. I want to be clear about that. There is not a drop of cruelty in that Goliath. What there is, is a very long tradition of how combat ends, and a battle-rage that does not consult the options quietly once it's been running. He looked at the paralyzed veteran on the stone and he made the calculation the way he always does — the man was a threat, threats are finished, this is what finishing looks like.

The halberd came down.

"I win, you lose — tradition upheld."

He said it the way you'd say *the sun rises in the east.* Not triumphant, exactly. More like... confirmed. A fact of the world that he was present to witness.

I stood there for a moment. The veteran's eyes, which had been the only moving thing about him, weren't moving anymore.

I thought about saying something else. I ran through the options the way you run your tongue over a sore tooth — not because you expect it to help, just to assess the damage. What was there to say? That I objected? I had objected. The objection is sitting in the air right where I left it, preserved perfectly, completely ignored.

"I… apparently I am."

I don't know what I meant, exactly. Apparently I am what — present? Complicit? A druid who just put a moonbeam on a paralyzed man so a Goliath could finish him more efficiently? Yes. Apparently. The universe does not care about intentions. I know that. Meril knew that. The moonbeam was mine.

Brewbarry had already moved on. He has a gift for that — not callousness, just a very clean relationship with what's done being done.

"I hope this will inspire a song in your new music studio."

He said this to Vukradin, I think. Or to the air. It was the kind of thing Brewbarry says at the end of things — an offering toward the future, a reason to keep going. The music studio is his north star as much as Vukradin's. I find that quietly touching, even now.

"I just want to celebrate our victory."

Yes. Of course. There's a dragon dead on this rooftop, and a creature that was trying to enslave it, and the Carver's vessel cooling on the stone. By any accounting that should be worth something. I didn't argue.

Brewbarry declared himself thirsty. There was no drink. He remained thirsty and undeterred.

I noted my position for the record and accepted that the record would be filed somewhere no one would ever read it.

*I'm on the record as not being pro-killing, but that's okay.*

That's what I said, out loud, to whoever was listening. It came out lighter than I felt it. I've learned to do that — state the thing plainly, let it sit, don't add weight to it by explaining. The plane of existence is safe. The Carver is stopped. A man who probably had no real choice died faster than I wanted him to.

The universe is still standing. I'm still standing. Brewbarry is standing and thirsty.

That's the accounting. I don't have to like it to accept it.

## **Vukradin — The Spoils of Victory**

The treasure was a block. That is perhaps the most honest way to describe it — not a chest, not a pile, not the scattered artful coinage you see in illustrations. A block of fused ice and residium and gold and gems and trade bars, all melted and refrozen together into something that had lost its individual components entirely. Three thousand gold, more or less, by estimation. None of it spendable. None of it separable, apparently, without the services of a planar theorist, because Soma identified — with the flat certainty she reserves for things that are absurd and true simultaneously — that the ice had been fused through some kind of planar trickery.

So: a fortune I could not spend, welded into a frozen mass by magic I did not understand, atop a tower I had just helped clear of a dragon and a body-snatching alien intelligence.

I stood there looking at it and thought: *yes, this is approximately how things go for me.*

The obsidian sword was there too — the Carver's weapon, the instrument of the domination ritual, the thing we had spent the better part of the last several hours trying to stop from completing its work on a white dragon. I picked it up. I examined it. And then I noticed the crystal in the pommel, and my heart did something complicated and involuntary.

It was a mental amplifier. The gem could project music — *directly into the minds of an audience.* No room acoustics to fight. No back-row listeners who couldn't quite hear. No bad nights where the lute is slightly out of tune and nobody tells you until afterward. Just thought, translated directly into experience, received by every mind in range with perfect clarity.

I stood there holding it and understood, in my bones, what this instrument could do. What *I* could do with it.

I said: "It seems like this was sent by the devil to tempt me, yes. Yes, save me."

The GM said something about how a lot of care and attention had gone into the selection of that phrase — *mental amplifier* — and I was already three steps ahead, because I had already named it.

"It is absolutely the Bluetooth sword. Done."

There was protest. There is always protest when I name something correctly on the first attempt. I accepted the clarification — "Magical Bluetooth swap, got it, sorry, thanks for clarifying" — because I am a reasonable person and I am capable of updating my terminology when presented with additional product information. The underlying concept remained sound.

But here is the thing. Here is the thing about the gem.

It had to go back to its owner.

I know how this sounds. I know exactly how this sounds, standing on a frozen battlement holding the single most useful instrument for my particular professional ambitions that I have ever encountered, freshly looted from the corpse of an alien entity that had been trying to use it to enslave a dragon. I know. And I said it anyway, because it is true: the gem belongs to someone, and that someone is not me, and until I can establish through proper documentation that the item has no rightful owner or that the rightful owner consents to transfer, I cannot keep it. That is the principle. That is what I said I would do. "Handcrafted for my personal quest," I told it, because I am aware of the irony even when I cannot act against it.

"Save me," I had said.

I meant it.

---

The main block of treasure was the next problem. Three thousand gold, mostly unspendable, and already I could feel the shape of the argument forming: *who does it belong to?* The gold a dragon hoards over a long life does not start out belonging to the dragon. It starts out belonging to somebody. Merchants, probably. Villagers, probably. People the dragon killed or displaced or simply robbed by existing in a radius that made trade impossible. The fact that it had then been fused into a residium-ice block by planar magic did not change the underlying ownership question. It just made it harder to divide.

I said this. I framed it as a criminal analogy because I think those land better than philosophical abstractions: scratching the serial number off a stolen item does not make it yours. That is what the ice was. Scratched-off serial numbers. The moral situation was unchanged.

Soma looked at the sky and said: "Look, the universe is safe, right? Like, or, you know, the plane of existence is safe, that's what matters most, nothing else."

She is not wrong. She is almost never wrong about the large shape of things. But the large shape of things and the specific ethical obligation are not the same question, and I was operating on the specific ethical obligation, which required me to declare the gold stolen goods and begin the documentation process.

"I am enraged," I said, "at all the corruption and evil that is stinying me from my music studio."

*Stynying.* I meant *stymying.* It came out wrong because I was genuinely upset and because Soma's cosmological calm has a way of making my particular frustrations sound small, which they are not, they are enormous, they are everything I have been working toward for years.

Harbin Wester would try to claim credit. The Lord's Alliance would try to claim the gold. Some functionary in Neverwinter would produce documentation explaining why the frozen block of dragon treasure was, actually, technically, the property of the Crown, and I would be left having saved the world and earned nothing for it except a sending stone message from Ser Kaelen telling me to come celebrate in Phandalin. "Conflict-free triumph," he had said. He had studied my vocabulary. He had absorbed it and fed it back to me in twenty-five words. I did not know whether to be moved or suspicious, and I was not sure those were different reactions.

"He's about as close as someone we can actually trust as anybody," I said.

Beat.

"No, I don't trust him. He's as close as anybody."

That sentence contains everything I currently believe about the people around me.

---

And then the statue.

The situation was we had to make, we made a choice, and the particular individual we wanted to return the blue quartz mermaid to was no longer available for us to return it to.

We chose the dragon. We chose to come here, to stop the Carver, to save Icespire and by extension Phandalin and by extension whatever cosmological safety Soma was gesturing at with her plane-of-existence framing. We made the right call. I believe that.

"Well, then that, you know, fuck them," I said. "We gotta go. Her next of kin? And they're in Neverwinter."

But while I was saying it, there was a voice underneath the words — not a voice from outside, just the particular internal noise that shows up when you are doing the right thing and paying a price for it simultaneously. *You could have your music studio. All you have to do is compromise a few principles here and there.*

"You don't get it," I said.

Which is true. The voice doesn't get it. That is the whole point of having principles — they are not negotiable tools you deploy when convenient and set aside when costly. They are the thing you are. The moment you start treating them as items in a cost-benefit analysis, you have already lost them. I know this. I have always known this.

"I just wanted my goddamn music studio," I said, "and I ended up having to go save the world."

The block of gold sat there, unfissured, inseparable, belonging to someone we had not yet identified.

The gem sat in the pommel of the sword, perfectly designed for a purpose I was not permitted to use it for yet.

The Carver was gone, and the world was, apparently, safe, and the plane of existence was intact, and the right owner of the blue quartz mermaid was unavailable. 

I just wanted my goddamn music studio.

## **Soma — Unwelcome Claimants**

The moment Sylvine opened her mouth, I knew exactly how this was going to end.

Not because I am wise. Not because Mielikki whispered it into my ear. Because I am forty-five years old, and I have seen this particular performance staged by this particular kind of creature — smooth voice, hands clasped, eyes wet with convenient devotion — more times than I can count on both hands and my shell. She hadn't even reached the question mark before I had the whole script mapped.

*Are you Vukradin the Bard? The one who returns the treasure to those that it has been stolen from?*

Shell sprout. Shell sprout with backup.

The backup being Runa, who stood behind her sister with the expression of someone waiting to remember her cue. They had the look of women who had rehearsed this approach on the road — who had heard enough about the famous, principled, treasure-returning bard of Phandalin to know exactly which door to knock on.

I give her credit for the detail. *Returns treasure to those it has been stolen from.* That is a precise hook. That is a hook designed and tested for a specific catch. Someone had done their research.

Vukradin, for his part, gave them nothing to work with.

"No," he said, with the flatness of a man who has been awake too long, "I haven't done very well with the return."

A lesser con artist might have recalibrated. Sylvine just pivoted without losing a syllable of warmth, turning to her sister like they were sharing a private miracle.

"Runa, didn't I tell you that that saint, Vukradin, would come and get our treasure for us? I knew it, I knew that if all we had to do was pray to the gods, a kind soul like Vukradin would come here and deal with that dragon, and give our treasure back."

And Runa: "Blessed be\! Blessed be\!"

I did not roll my eyes. I am above rolling my eyes. I noted the performance with the professional detachment of someone who has watched the same river flood the same valley and knows better than to stand in the same spot twice. The *saint.* The *kind soul.* The gods invoked as collateral. The treasure presented not as a claim to be proven but as a thing already returned, already ours to hand over, the paperwork mere formality.

Brewbarry said, beside me, with what I can only describe as genuine admiration for the audacity: "Well, that's the quickest we've ever found treasure's owners before."

Yes. Remarkable speed, that. Almost as if they'd been waiting.

I watched Vukradin's face while Sylvine performed. He's getting better at reading people — the exhaustion has burned off the naivety, if not the idealism — and I saw the moment he decided. Not doubt. Not consideration. Just refusal, arriving fully formed.

"Lady, I don't know who you are, I don't know how you think that all 3,000 gold that this dragon hoarded over its long life is somehow yours, but I don't really believe you. You're probably gonna try to steal this, like everybody else steals. You're one of them. Why don't you just get out of here?"

I've heard him angrier. This was the tiredness talking — not hot anger but a kind of flat, accumulated exhaustion with the whole category of person standing in front of him. The phonies. He's been counting them.

Sylvine switched registers instantly. One beat of theatrical injury: "I am hurt. You are a thief\!" And then she held it just long enough for Vukradin to respond—

"I am tired of all these liars and cheats in this land. Get out of here. There's nobody left worthy."

—and then she dropped it entirely.

That was the tell, if anyone needed one. Wounded pride takes a moment to recover. Wounded pride lingers, escalates, demands an apology or at least a witness. This woman let it go in under a second because the injury was never real to begin with. It was a stage prop. She set it down when she was done with it.

"Second plan\! We kill them all\!"

There it is. I had been waiting for it since the question mark.

What followed was the kind of fight that happens when people who trained together try their training against people who have survived worse things without training at all. Brewbarry announced the terms of engagement with characteristic precision — "Try not to die too quickly, I want to enjoy this" — and then made good on the invitation. When the last veteran went down, he issued his verdict with the gravity of a man recording something for posterity.

"Root Smasher stands, you don't. That's the story."

One of the veterans — not quite dead, not quite committed to seeing the fight through — found his way to a practical conclusion: "Screw this shit. There's more money to be found elsewhere."

Career advancement. Yes. Sensible.

I watched him go.

I am on the record as having no objection to that particular outcome.

## **Brewbarry — Skirmish in the Fortress**

The moment Sylvine Wintermoon stopped performing and said *"Second plan — we kill them all,"* the rage came up clean and simple, the way it always does. No hesitation between the feeling and the body. Just the halberd in my hands and the red at the edges of everything.

Valphine was already moving. Her spirit guardians bloomed outward, divine light with teeth, and the bandits who'd been creeping toward us through the fortress corridor got their first lesson about walking into a cleric's radius without asking first. I heard them take it. I watched them not enjoy it.

Then one of them reached her. Got through the guardians, got a blade in, and the light went out — the concentration broken, the aura gone. I noted this the way you note weather. The math had changed. Fine.

Soma went bear. There's a sound to that shift — I've heard it enough times now to not flinch at it — and she put her weight into the nearest veteran hard enough that he staggered. Good. One number smaller.

Then someone made a grab for the treasure slag. I saw it from the side: a bandit's hands on Vukradin's, trying to pull the hoard piece free while the fight had his attention split. Vukradin held. Of course he held. I've seen him keep hold of a principle while getting stabbed in an argument — a man who can do that doesn't lose his grip on gold he's already decided is complicated.

And then Vukradin sang something, or said something, or whatever the shape of his magic was in that moment — and two of the veterans stopped moving. Just stopped. Held where they stood, faces caught between one expression and the next, the kind of frozen that means their minds are still running but their bodies have taken a vote and declined to continue.

I looked at them standing there. Paralyzed. Alive inside, somewhere, trying to get out.

*And yet* — there was no and yet. Not today. Not after the dragon. Not after the Carver. Not after all of it. The mercy was used up somewhere further back up this mountain, and these men had walked into a fortress with weapons and a plan, and the plan had been *kill them all.*

I cut them down. That's the full account. The halberd makes a certain sound when it connects with something that cannot move to absorb the blow, and I made that sound until the ledger was closed, and then I looked at what was left standing.

One bandit. Looking at his two colleagues on the floor. Looking at Soma's bear. Looking at me.

He made a decision. It was, honestly, a good decision. He said something about better opportunities elsewhere and he left. I let him. The rage doesn't care about fleeing men — it cares about the ones who stay and keep trying.

The fortress went quiet.

Vukradin's message to Ser Kaelen happened while I was still coming down from it — that transition from the red edges back to the ordinary colors of the world, which always takes a moment longer than I expect. I heard him say, *"The dragon is dead, so is the Carver. We're heading to Neverwinter. Spread the word."*

I thought about the dragon. About fighting *with* it and then fighting *against* it and then watching it fall. About what that actually costs a body, mine and everyone else's.

"Fighting a fucking dragon," I said. "That was conflict."

Nobody argued the point.

## **Vukradin — A Message from the Gauntlet**

The spell takes twenty-five words. I know this the way I know how many beats a phrase wants before it lands — exactly twenty-five, no more, and the clock on the battle is still running when I start composing.

I will be honest with you. I drafted it in my head three times while Brewbarry was still finishing things. The first draft ran long. The second draft was too spare — it felt like a battlefield dispatch, and I am not a soldier, whatever the Order pin on my collar implies. The third draft I actually sent:

*The dragon is dead, so is the Carver. We're heading to Neverwinter.*

Clean. Factual. Leaves nothing open that I did not intend to leave open. I am moderately proud of it.

Ser Kaelen's reply arrives like it was hand-calligraphed by committee.

"Praise Torm\! Your righteous victory brings light to the north. The gauntlet awaits your arrival in Phandalin. We shall celebrate your conflict-free triumph together."

I stand there on the battlement with dragon frost still melting off my collar and read those words again. *Conflict-free triumph.* He put my vocabulary in his mouth and handed it back to me like a gift. Like he had been taking notes. I am not sure whether to be flattered or to feel like I just caught someone reading my letters.

There is a second message underneath the warmth, of course. Not *Neverwinter.* Not *yes, go where you said you were going.* The gauntlet awaits your arrival in *Phandalin.* We shall celebrate in *Phandalin.* And then, because even a Sending cannot contain him, the short version of a longer argument: *Well done. Come to Phandalin quick. Issues with the Emerald Enclave.*

Issues. With the Emerald Enclave. After everything else, now that.

I hear myself say it out loud before I have decided to. "He's about as close as someone we can actually trust as anybody." The words sound fine until they land, and then I hear what they actually mean, and I add the correction immediately: "No, I don't trust him. He's as close as anybody."

Which is to say: the bar has gotten very low and he has cleared it. Which is to say nothing.

Somewhere nearby, someone mentions the copper wire — the material component for Sending, which means we have been passing voice over copper wire this whole time, which means the world invented the telephone and forgot to tell anyone. I say *yes, per phone*, because it is true, and also because the alternative is to keep thinking about what *conflict-free triumph* means in the mouth of a man who has never once, as far as I can tell, done anything without a second purpose.

What does he want us to see when we arrive? What has he arranged? The Emerald Enclave — that is Adabra's world, and Adabra has been cooling on me for weeks, and I have not asked myself hard enough why.

I just wanted my goddamn music studio, I think, not for the first time and certainly not for the last. And I ended up having to go save the world. And now the world is saved, provisionally, and Ser Kaelen is waiting in Phandalin with open arms and a press release already written, and he used my words, and I do not know whether he loves me or is managing me, and I am not sure the difference matters.

Brewbarry's voice cuts through all of it with the efficiency of a man who has no patience for ambiguity when there is a clear action available.

"I heard 'celebrate,' say no more. Let's go."

I look at him. He is covered in dragon blood and is already thinking about the drink he does not have. His face is completely at peace. The reply said *celebrate* and that is all the information he required. I find, against all expectation, that this is the most comforting thing anyone has said to me since the battle ended.

We are going to Phandalin. Ser Kaelen will be there, ceremonial and over-composed, and I will smile and shake hands and let him have his celebration, and I will listen for what he does not say. That is fine. I have twenty-five words too, and I know how to use them.
