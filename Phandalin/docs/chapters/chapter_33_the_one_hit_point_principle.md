# Chapter 33: The One Hit Point Principle

## **Brewbarry — Aftermath of the Battle**
The word came up the way it always does — sideways, in the middle of something else entirely.
Someone said *goodberries*, and I stopped.
"Good berries," I said. "I remember something about good berries."
I sat with that for a moment. The memory was old, and I don't usually go looking for old things. But this one had teeth. It had caught on something and wouldn't let go.
"I think I like them."
Nobody made much of it. Why would they. A big man remembering a small fruit. But the thing the word had pulled up — that was the part I hadn't said yet. That was the part I hadn't told anybody.
I don't know why I said it then. Maybe it was the fire. Maybe it was that we'd just survived something that could have gone differently, and surviving has a way of making a man want to square his ledger. Maybe it was just that Vukradin was there, and Vukradin is the person I tell things to, even when I don't entirely mean to.
"So I haven't told anybody for this," I said. "But, after the artifact was stolen, I found one of these berries, and they were good, and they healed you."
Vukradin nodded, easy, like he was following along. "And you were like, not hungry anymore for the whole day."
"Yeah," I said. "And that's why they thought I was… I was asleep on the job, and didn't take care of things."
He looked at me with that look he sometimes has — the one that means he thinks he already knows the end of the story. "And really, you were just drinking your wine?"
"No," I said. "I was sober, and I had a... I had a..."
The word for what I'd had didn't come easy. A full belly. A stillness. The kind of quiet that a goodberry gives you — not drunk, not asleep, just *settled*. And I had been sitting with that feeling, unaware of everything, and while I sat with it something was stolen, and the tribe looked at me and they saw a man who had failed his post, and they made their judgement, and that was that.
Vukradin had stopped moving. "Whoa," he said. "You're never sober."
I stood up. I am not small. Standing up when I want to make a point has a certain effect.
I patted my belly — the good round belly that has held so much wine and so much ale and so much of everything this world offers in a cup — and I stretched, and I said it plainly.
"I'll have you note that I did not drink before the loss of the artifact in my banning from the Uthgartian tribe."
He was quiet for a moment. Genuinely quiet. Vukradin is not often genuinely quiet.
"Oh," he said. "I guess I never knew you in those days... I just knew when you were coming into my club, and drinking, and drinking, and telling..."
He didn't finish that sentence. He didn't need to. He'd met me already exiled. Already a man who had found the cup and found it good and decided that if his tribe was going to punish him for something he hadn't done, he might as well become the thing they'd accused him of being. That's a thought I've never put into words before. It sat in my chest like a stone.
"Yes," I said. "But since I discovered drinking, I think that was wasted youth."
Vukradin stared at me. "Sobriety was wasted youth."
"Yes, yes, I…" I nodded, very slowly, with great conviction. "Instead of drinking when I should have been, I was sober."
He laughed. He couldn't help it. Neither could I.
But underneath the laugh, something else. A punishment that came for a man who wasn't guilty. An exile earned by a piece of fruit and a druid's magic. All those sober years with the tribe — the drumbeats and the rituals and the foul spirit they called drink, the thing that has no flavour and no colour and is *more like the thing that makes you drunk, in condensed form* — all of that, and the world I'd actually wanted was on the other side, and I never knew it. And by the time I found it, I'd already been cast out.
I don't wallow in things. I'm not made that way. But I held that thought for a moment longer than I usually hold thoughts, and I let it be what it was.
Then Vukradin started talking about the necklace again — the necklace of fireballs that is *absolutely not his necklace*, he is merely *holding* it, as a kindness, for the unknown and presumably very concerned rightful owner — and I came back to the present because that is where I live.
"You have the necklace," I said, with the patience of a man who has explained this several times. "You have my sword, and my halberd. You have nothing to fear. This is your necklace."
He gave me the look.
"We are going to go face dragons. We need fireballs."
"No," he said, in the voice he uses when he has thought of something clever. "It's… Dragon's probably immune to fire."
I considered this.
"Good point," I said.
The argument collapsed completely. I accept this. He is usually right about things, even when he is making my life more difficult. That is, I think, what it means to follow someone. You know they're going to win the argument, and you still want to see what argument they make.

## **Soma — The Centaur's Revelation**
The centaur came back to himself like a wave pulling back from shore — gradual, then all at once. One moment he was folded against the earth, flanks heaving, and then my hands were on him and the warmth moved through the spell the way it always does, steady and purposeful, and he rose.
He was enormous standing up. I hadn't quite registered that while he was down.
"Well, this is an important day," Xanth announced to the trees, to us, to the general air around him. "You have healed me!"
The enthusiasm was immediate and total, as if the injury had been a brief administrative inconvenience and we were now back on schedule. He looked around at the party with the bright-eyed appraisal of someone cataloging a market stall, and landed on Vukradin.
"You are Vukradin. Vucravinios, as we say in Centaurian."
"Yes, yes, yes," Vukradin said, with the practiced weariness of a man who has had his name made classical before. "But if I use that name, people start talking to me in Greek, and then I'd say, well, actually, I don't know Greek, only my French goddess knows Greek."
The centaur absorbed this with equanimity. "My name is Xanth. You can call me Xanth. My real name is Xanthopoulos. Most people can't pronounce it."
And then he turned to me, and something in his face changed — warmth, gratitude, something almost reverent — and he said, "Soma, that, that is, I could kiss you."
"You're allowed," I said.
It came out before I'd thought about it. Which is usually how the best things come out. He laughed — a centaur's laugh is a whole-body event — and the last of the battle's tension dispersed into the air around us.

He knew about the dragon before we'd finished asking. Of course he did. He lived here. He knew the shape of the disruption the way you know the shape of water damage in a room you've lived in for years — not by measuring it, but by feeling where the floor gives.
"Oh, my friend Vukradein, you know nothing," he said, when Vukradin tried to parse what half-orc spellcasters had to do with white dragons.
Xanth explained it the way someone explains something that should be obvious — with patience, but with audible effort at patience. A white dragon had come down into the Icespire Mountains. The orcs had been living there. The dragon had, as dragons tend to do, made its preferences about occupancy quite clear. The orcs moved. Into the valleys. Into the places where people lived. One creature pushing out another, the pressure redistributing itself down through the landscape until it became someone else's emergency.
I had already understood this, the way I understood most things about displacement. Every creature finds its niche, or takes someone else's. The centaur and I were in agreement before he'd finished the sentence.

It was when he noticed the staff that things shifted.
He'd been working his way through assessments of the rest of the party — Valphine's relationship with light ("deliciously painful for me," she'd said, and Xanth had filed this under *they make them all kinds* with admirable composure) — when his eyes caught it.
"Oh, what's that?" A pause. "Yes. You have Meryl's staff?"
I looked at the staff. It's a stick. I've been carrying it since Meril left it in my house. It's old and it has one leaf on it, which I mentioned because it seems relevant to any thorough description of the object.
"I do? He… it's just an old stick he left in my house."
"Oh, no, this is not Meryl, this is not a simple staff, this is a powerful staff."
"I got a leaf the other day," I offered.
"Oh, yes, I was looking at it. It used to have more leaves than... but it's one little leaf."
He said it without condescension, which I appreciated. Just observation. The staff had more leaves once, and now it has one, and the history between those two states is apparently significant, and I'm the one holding it. Meril left it in my house and apparently knew what he was doing, which is more than I knew when I took it in.
Xanth was quiet for a moment, looking at it with the expression of someone seeing an old friend's handwriting.
"Meryl must know you. I will help you, Soma. Soma, what do you want? Meryl and I were old friends."
The staff felt heavier. Not a burden — more like the feeling of understanding something you've been holding for a long time without understanding. I've carried heavier things without noticing them. This was different.
What did I want.
I thought about it honestly. The world needed mending in the obvious places right now — a dragon, a carver who was no longer walking like a living thing, orcs in the wrong valleys, people in the wrong danger. My ambitions aren't complicated.
"I don't know," I said. "We're just kind of trying to keep the world a safer place right now, trying to make sure everybody's got a place to live, trying to… trying to keep peace among the land."
A pause.
"I don't have big aspirations."
I mean it. I don't want a throne or a legacy or a name in the histories. I want the weight to come off the world a little. I want the next generation of creatures — all the shell sprouts, all the ones who haven't been born yet into whatever mess we leave them — to have ground to stand on. That's the whole of it.
Xanth looked at me for a long moment with an expression I couldn't quite categorize, and then he nodded, as if the answer was correct.

The Emerald Enclave came up, as it always does — someone mentions druids and eventually you get to the infighting.
"The Emerald Enclave's gone a little bit not so..." Xanth began, searching for the diplomatic framing, then abandoning it. "There's idiots who are saying one thing, idiots who are saying the other thing, the orcs are going around, they can't decide whether the orcs are an intervention or naturalist, they're accusing each other of everything."
"Yeah," I said. "They can't even agree on terms. Tell me about it."
The weariness in my own voice surprised me. I've been in enough of those arguments to know that they resolve nothing and cost time, and time is the thing in shortest supply when the world is actively on fire. Xanth knew it too. He'd been living next to this particular version of ideological paralysis long enough to have formed strong opinions about it.
"Exactly," he said. "You must go deal with a dragon. I think that is the most important thing."
Yes. That.

The animals, as I had cause to mention: very chatty.
Xanth was explaining how he knew about Borg's death — through the network of living things, the quiet constant information-sharing of the forest that city-dwellers consistently underestimate — and Vukradin was mildly incredulous, and I said what was simply true.
"You know, Vukradin, the animals talk a lot."
"Soma, thank you." Xanth turned to me with the relief of someone who has finally found an ally at a long dinner party. "These city dwellers, they think, oh, no, there's nobody here, there's, like, animals, there's leaves, there's some birds, there's everything."
"The animals are very chatty," I confirmed.
Something settled between us then — the easy recognition of two people who have spent enough time listening to know what's being said. Xanth named the Carver's lieutenants one by one, Borg and Sister Kaella and Zax, and Vukradin noted with some satisfaction that two out of three were accounted for, and I listened to the shape of what remained.
One unaccounted for. The one nobody saw. The one who walked without rest, without breath, without soul — though I didn't have those words for him yet, not at this moment. Just the outline of something wrong, moving in the mountains.

When Xanth reached the question of the dragon's hoard, I watched the particular expression cross his face that I had already come to associate with encountering Vukradin's ethics. Bewilderment, genuine and total.
"Why? The dragon has a hoard."
Vukradin had refused it, or was refusing it, or had explained in his particular way why the treasure was not his to take, and Xanth looked at me as at the nearest available interpreter.
"Soma, the little one, does he not? The one with feathers, doesn't he understand these things?"
"He's… different," I said. "We're all a little different."
The most diplomatic answer I had, and the truest one. Vukradin is different. So is Brewbarry, who earned his exile one goodberry at a time. So is Valphine, who finds light deliciously painful and has arrangements with spymasters. So am I — a tortle carrying a dead man's staff that apparently has opinions about its new owner.
We are all a little different. Xanth accepted this and moved on, as reasonable creatures tend to do.

## **Valphine — Interrogating Sister Kaella**
The medicine has its own intimacy, if you know how to use it. I crouched over Sister Kaella's bound form and worked my fingers along her jaw, her throat, the pulse points where unconsciousness lives. Not healing — not Lathander's gift, not yet. Just the older knowledge. The Underdark kind. You learn, when you grow up in a place where people are kept alive for purposes other than their comfort, exactly how much pressure returns a mind to its body without returning it to its strength.
She came up slowly. Eyes first. Then breath. Then the particular stillness of someone who is awake and assessing before they let you know they're awake.
I appreciate that quality in a prisoner.
"Hello, Sister Kayla."
She looked at me — at the party, at the tent walls, at the very specific fact of her continued existence — and the first thing she said was: "Thank God you're alive!"
I won't pretend I wasn't charmed by that. She was talking about herself, of course. But the construction was elegant.
Vukradin, characteristically, stepped in to correct the framing. "You're the one who should be lucky he's alive." The aasimar and his moral ledger — always balancing, always presenting the account. I let him have it. He wasn't wrong, technically.
But Kaella had already run the calculation Vukradin was still setting up. "Oh, no, I… there was the other possibility. Jax swings, and I'm alive, and Jax is now going to torture me to death for several, you know, painfully long, protracted periods."
She said it the way an archivist reads from a record. The dispassion wasn't bravado — it was genuine. She had already grieved the version of events where Jax won. She was now operating entirely in the probability space where he hadn't. Efficient. I find that sort of thing genuinely restful.
"We can still do that," I said. "If you'd like."
A beat. She continued — "Yes, but it's an if. Whereas with Jax, it was a certainty, so now there's at least a—"
"Or if I'd like."
That one landed differently. I felt it land. There's a quality to silence when a room reassesses you — a small, specific texture — and the room had it. Vukradin's particular species of discomfort. Soma's watchfulness. Brewbarry, bless him, probably hadn't tracked the shift at all.
And Kaella: "I didn't know you had it in you."
No sarcasm. Genuine surprise, genuinely acknowledged. I noted it, filed it, and felt something that was — not pleasure, exactly. Recognition. The particular warmth of being seen accurately.
I pressed forward before the moment could calcify into sentiment. "So now you need to tell us more about this dragon. More about the Carver's plan."
What followed was Kaella in full. The Carver, she told us, had gone completely mad — heading for Icespire Hold, intending to possess the dragon and visit vengeance on everyone and everything in reach. Vukradin, being Vukradin, asked whether that would actually be bad, and who, specifically, would bear the brunt of it. Soma pointed out, with the patience of someone who has explained obvious things to clever people before, that parties who assassinate an organization's muscle tend to make lists. Kaella agreed, and then added herself to the list with the quiet resignation of someone who has accepted her own significance.
The confession came out piece by piece — that she had not, strictly speaking, been loyal to the Carver the whole time. That after the party killed the muscle, and after the Carver accused her of intelligence failures, her life expectancy had become suddenly, sharply contingent on the Carver's death. She said "strictly speaking" the way someone uses it when the truth requires a small corridor of precision to move through without catching on anything.
Vukradin found this inadequate. He wanted to know why she hadn't simply come to them. Why the arrangement with me. Why the secrecy.
"Well, she was the only one who showed up."
He pressed. She let him press for a moment, the way you allow a river to run out its strength against rock, and then she delivered it cleanly:
"And Vukradin… I had noticed that you had a difficulty in dealing with morally gray environments."
I watched him receive that. He receives insults like a man who has strong opinions about whether they count as insults. This one, I think, landed in the category of *technically accurate assessment delivered by someone who has forfeited the right to make it*, because what followed was: "You'll do really well in prison. There are a lot of morally gray people there, you can hang out with them for many years."
She smiled. Not unkindly. "As long as I'm alive, there's always hope."
"Keep hoping, babe."
She had been about to say something about drow clerics of Lathander and the importance of darkness and light before Vukradin cut across her. I found myself mildly regretful. I would have liked to hear how she finished that sentence. She had looked at me when she said it — not at the party, not at the tent walls. At me. Deciding I would understand something the others wouldn't, or wouldn't want to.
She was right. That's the thing about Vukradin's moral clarity — it illuminates everything in the same flat light. I was built for gradients. Kaella knew it. She had built her plan around it, constructed her entire contingency on the assumption that there was someone in this party who could hold an arrangement without needing to explain it to their conscience every morning.
"And Valphine, Valphine, we had an arrangement."
"Yes," I said. "We have an arrangement."
She nodded. Soma filled in the rest, briefly, accurately: *value of life.* Two words doing the work of a philosophy. I appreciate economy.
The directions to Icespire Hold came without further persuasion — she was past the point of withholding information and had moved into the stage of negotiating which prison would be most convenient. Her preference was Axholm. She made a reasonable case. She also mentioned, with the timing of someone who has been sitting on a punchline, that the Carver was heading to Icespire Hold as well. The same location. At roughly the same time.
She watched us absorb this.
When Corbin was assigned to her keeping, I watched his face.
I've learned to watch faces the way other people watch weapons — you can see what's coming if you know where to look. The insight rolled clean, and what I saw in Corbin's expression was not cruelty, exactly. It was something older than cruelty. Something unresolved. A specific kind of hunger that belongs to people who have been made to be small by other people and are now presented with the chance to be otherwise.
There was a history there. Whatever it was, it lived in the space between them like a held breath.
Kaella started screaming the moment we were clear of the tent.
Vukradin called back something reassuring about Corbin being a friend, a former colleague, that the two of them would get along perfectly. I said: "I've been teaching him some of our sacraments."
I did not explain which sacraments. Some things are better left as questions.
I walked on. Behind us, the screaming continued.
Life, as I have always found, is pain. The distribution of it is simply a matter of arrangement.

## **Vukradin — Planning the Journey to Ice Spire Hold**
The two paths to Ice Spire Hold were not complicated mathematics. Fifty-five miles through mountain terrain as the crow flies. Cutting directly would cost one level of exhaustion. Swinging through Phandalin first would cost two. You could dress it up in heroic language — *rally reinforcements, shore up the town's defenses, make our farewells* — but what you were really doing, if you went home first, was arriving at a dragon's stronghold already half-dead.
I am many things. I am not that.
The second consideration — and I did not say this aloud, not precisely — was what it would look like to my opponents on the Privy Council. I had only recently learned I *had* opponents, which I will admit surprised me more than it should have. Of course I had opponents. A man who refuses blood money and says so at volume, in public, at council meetings, will accumulate opponents the way a traveling musician accumulates drink tabs. But knowing this in the abstract and feeling the specific weight of *they will use every delay against you* are different things. A detour through Phandalin was a detour through their rhetoric. I could already hear it: *Vukradin abandoned his post. Vukradin left the dragon problem to fester while he came home for a hot meal.* No. We would go directly.
That left the problem of arriving exhausted, which I solved with what I consider a masterwork of diplomatic persuasion, even if Xanth made it easier than it should have been.

Xanth had opinions about everything, which I respected, and delivered them in three connected stories before arriving at any point, which I found charming the first time and merely characteristic thereafter. He had already warmed to Soma in the way that people warm to Soma — with the helpless adoration of someone who has just realized they are standing near something genuinely good — and the sight of Meril's Staff had affected him in ways he didn't entirely explain, or perhaps explained at length and I stopped following.
"Xanth," I said, when the moment seemed right. "I have a proposition for you."
He regarded me with the particular expression of a person who has heard many propositions and found most of them unworthy.
I laid it out. We needed to reach the foothills. He was fast. His hooves would carry us further and faster than our feet, and he would be spared the mountains entirely — his limits, honestly stated, and I had no intention of insulting him by asking him to climb rock faces. Take us as far as the area near the gold mine. That was all.
Xanth looked at me for a long moment. Then he looked at Soma. Then, with the air of a man who has decided that some companions are worth inconvenience, he agreed.
"Soma, that, that is, I could kiss you," he said, which was technically addressed to Soma but seemed directed at the universe generally. And then, turning back to me: "Vucravinios. You are asking me to be a horse."
"I am asking you to be a *centaur,*" I said, "which is considerably more distinguished."
He was not entirely convinced. But he went along with it, which is the outcome that matters.

The transport arrangement, once we worked it through, had a certain ungainly poetry. Xanth would carry myself and Valphine on his back. Soma — and I will never stop finding this remarkable, not because I don't understand wild shape but because understanding a thing and watching a tortle become a bear are entirely different experiences — would carry another rider. Boney the horse, who does not tire, would handle the rest. It was not elegant. It was, however, functional, which is what I needed it to be.
The indignity of being cargo. I have performed for worse audiences.

Kaella was the last piece of business before we could leave, and it was the piece I spent the least time examining.
We had her. She was alive, which was more than I had expected after the fight, and she had told us what we needed to know about Ice Spire Hold — or enough of it, the rest being inference and risk. The question was: what do you do with a captured spymaster when you're about to march into a dragon's stronghold and cannot take prisoners with you?
Corbin answered before I finished formulating the question.
"That will be my pleasure," he said, when we told him to watch her.
Then: "Can I beat her?"
I want to be clear that I heard this as a procedural question. A question about parameters. He was asking, I thought, for guidelines — how much force is appropriate if she attempts escape? What are the rules of engagement for a prisoner guard?
"Until she has one hit point," I said.
Kaella started screaming. Not escape-screaming — indignation-screaming, which is a different register entirely, higher and more pointed. She was calling Corbin a lunatic, and then she was calling all of us various things, and I said, with complete sincerity: "Not a lunatic! He's a friend! He was part of your crew! I'm sure the two of you will get along perfectly."
Valphine said, very quietly, "I've been teaching him some of our sacraments."
She said it the way she says most things — with the particular serenity of someone who finds human suffering aesthetically interesting. I have learned not to ask follow-up questions about Valphine's sacraments.
The screaming continued as we walked away. I told myself this was the most humane available option, which I believe to be true, and I did not think about what Corbin's expression had looked like when he said *that will be my pleasure*, which I believe I was right not to examine too closely.
As long as she stays above one hit point. I was quite clear about that.

The long rest, at least, was simple.
We settled in. The night was cold in the way that nights near mountains are cold — a specific, personal cold, as though the air has decided you individually and has views about your presence. I kept my instrument near. I did not play anything substantial, just ran my fingers across the strings in the way you do when you're thinking without quite thinking, chord shapes without resolution.
Tomorrow: a centaur ride, a bear, a skeleton horse, and fifty-five miles of mountain between us and a chained dragon and a man who walks without rest or breath or soul.
I have, in my career, played in worse venues. Usually I get a greenroom.

## **Soma — The Unnatural Trail**
The wrongness came to me before the trail did.
I've walked old-growth forests that have forgotten human feet. I've passed through groves where the soil remembers every death it's fed on. I know how a healthy place feels — the texture of it, the pressure it puts back against you when you move through it. This was the opposite of that. A negative space. The land pressing inward instead of outward, registering something it could not process.
I didn't say anything to the others. What would I say? *Something is wrong with the air here.* They would have looked at me the way shell sprouts always look when you say a thing they can't verify.
Then we found the footprints.
Human in shape. Perfectly spaced. The trail ran straight through the terrain — over rocks, not around them, through obstacles that any living body would naturally avoid. There was a rhythm to it that made my jaw tighten. Not the rhythm of someone marching with discipline. Something else. The rhythm of a mechanism.
Vukradin said it first, because Vukradin is quick when he wants to be: something about a construct, not a person.
"Or a guy who's under some sort of, like, demonic spell or something," I said. "Like, who's possessed."
I wasn't guessing. I was naming what my skin had been telling me since we turned onto this path.

The stone giants came next — a gathering of them in the defile, speaking to each other in low resonant tones that I felt more in my sternum than heard with my ears. Brewbarry and Valphine understood the words. Something wrong with the rocks. The giants had noticed that much.
One of them spoke about the trail-maker without much interest. *The little one with the obsidian sword.* That phrase lodged in me.
Vukradin asked whether they'd tried to stop him.
"He is a little one," came the answer. "Is of no concern of ours."
I had spent enough time watching the party fail to move immovable things to know that arguing with a stone giant's philosophy was like arguing with weather. But I said it anyway.
"I wouldn't be so sure."
Not a lecture. Not a warning with teeth in it. Just — *I wouldn't be so sure.* The kind of thing you say when you've watched enough forests die to know that a disturbance doesn't wait for you to decide it's your concern.
Vukradin tried the diplomatic angle — *is there a way we can help you?* — and the giants did what giants do. They pointed down the path. Their version of help was for us to stop filling their valley with small-creature noise and go deal with the problem ourselves.
"You would no longer be here and not talking to us."
I almost admired it.

The staff told me the rest.
I pressed Meril's Staff against one of the displaced rocks — the ones that shimmered faintly, sitting at wrong angles, as if reality had tried to put them back and couldn't quite remember where they'd been. The moment the staff made contact, I understood.
Psychic energy. Not magical distortion in the way a spell leaves a mark — something deeper than that. Something that had moved through here and bent the world around itself without meaning to. Without noticing. Whatever had walked this path had been so thoroughly emptied of ordinary selfhood that it warped the fabric of things simply by passing through them.
"That guy is possessed," I said. "There's… nobody walks like that. Possessed by, I don't know."
I genuinely didn't know. That was the part that sat cold in my chest. I know possessed creatures. I know the signature of a will that isn't its own. This was something past that. Past possession, past corruption, past the kind of thing you can cast out with the right words or the right fire.
The rocks phased along the trail — there, not-there, there again, like a wound that hadn't decided yet whether to close.

The giants brought their philosophy to bear on the evidence: *Hekaton has said, and the failure of the breaking of the sundering has shown, that giants must take care of giant things.*
Brewbarry and I both said it, almost at the same moment.
"He will soon."
The giants didn't move. Of course they didn't. They had chosen their stillness the way you choose a wall, and nothing we said was going to change the architecture of a decision that old. Still — they gave us two potions before we left. Which was something. Maybe they heard us, even if they didn't act on it.

Vukradin asked one last question before we left the defile. Whether anyone had been with the Carver.
"No," the giants said.
Traveling alone. That landed differently than I expected it to. A thing that warps rocks and walks through obstacles without noticing them, traveling alone through mountain passes, heading toward a dragon.
I thought about the obsidian sword as we moved away from the giants — the words *level-draining* sitting in the back of my mind the way a bad smell sits in a room after you've opened the windows.
"Obsidian Sword, though," I said. "Do we know anything about that?"
Brewbarry and I answered together, more or less: level-draining.
Vukradin said he didn't want to come anywhere near it.
Nobody disagreed with that. Not even me, and I have walked toward most things in my long life without much fuss.
This one, though. This one I was watching my feet for.

## **Brewbarry — The Stone Giant's Pass**
The mountain narrows itself like it's trying to squeeze you out. That's what the pass felt like — a place that wanted fewer people in it, ideally none.
I understood the feeling.
The rocks were wrong. Not fallen-wrong or cracked-wrong, just wrong in a way that made the eyes slide away from them, shimmering at their edges like they couldn't fully decide to exist. I've seen a lot of mountains. My people were mountain people, in the sense that mountains were where we ended up when everywhere else had asked us to leave. This kind of rock wasn't anything I'd seen before. It wasn't wind damage or freeze-thaw. It was something that had walked through here and left a mark the stone couldn't shake off.
The Carver's path cut straight over everything. Not around. Not through the easier terrain. Straight over, because whatever was walking didn't need the easier terrain. The footprints in the compacted earth had a rhythm to them like a millstone: even, mechanical, relentless. I looked at those prints for a moment longer than I meant to.
Then I heard the giants.
Stone giant speech sits below hearing and above feeling — it's the frequency of things that don't move unless they choose to. My mother taught me the language when I was small enough to sit in her lap and still have room to spare. She said you learn it with your chest, not your ears. She was right. I heard the rumbling before I understood it, and I understood it before the others knew anyone was speaking at all.
They were troubled. That was the word I kept finding for it, in their language and in mine. The rocks were wrong and the giants knew it, and it had made them gather in the pass like something enormous and immovable that had, nonetheless, decided to worry.
There were several of them. The word "several" feels inadequate. There were several of them the way there are several mountains when you look at a range — you understand the number but the scale defeats the counting.
I told the others what I was hearing. We approached.
The giant who spoke for them delivered information the way they deliver everything: like setting a boulder down. Final. Present. Not up for discussion.
"The little one with the obsidian sword came through this path."
Vukradin took that in. "Did you try to stop him?"
"He is a little one. Is of no concern of ours."
I know that voice. Not from stone giants specifically — from every authority I've ever met that decided something small enough to ignore was small enough to let burn. I didn't say that. I stood behind Vukradin and listened.
Soma said, quietly, "I wouldn't be so sure."
Vukradin tried a different angle. "Is there a way we can help you?"
The giant pointed. Down the path. The direction we'd already been going. The meaning was plain as the stone his finger was made of.
Vukradin, admirably, pushed it further. "So, how would us dealing with him help you?"
The giant considered this with the patience of something geological. Then:
"You would no longer be here and not talking to us."
I almost laughed. I would have, if we hadn't been asking these beings for passage, possibly for their help, and absolutely for their indulgence in not simply stepping on us. But I recognized the sentiment. My tribe had said gentler versions of it to me more than once, toward the end.
Vukradin pressed on. The dragon, he wanted to know. The white dragon. Didn't that concern them?
The giant who answered had clearly been waiting for someone to ask about history so he could decline to let it change anything.
"When Am broke the sundering, some thought that it was time for giants to seize power over the little ones. Those giants failed, and they were tempted by — and Am was tempted by the power of Tiamat and her plans to rule the world with her dragons. We have learned from Hekaton that now the war with the dragons is over, and the war with the little ones is over."
Valphine, dry as the inside of a tomb: "So you're not interested in fighting dragons."
"Yes. The dragon can stay where the dragon lives, and the dragon does not bother us."
Soma and I said it at the same time. "He will soon."
The giants looked at us. Not moved. Not concerned. The way a cliff face regards the weather.
I know what it is to have a philosophy built from disaster. My exile wasn't a lesson I chose — it was a lesson I survived, and I built rules around it afterward to keep the same thing from happening again. The giants had done the same thing, just with more blood and more centuries and enough failed wars to forge an ironclad theology out of the wreckage. *Giant things. Giant concerns.* Hekaton had said, and they had decided Hekaton was right, and nothing a collection of little ones said in a mountain pass was going to shift what Hekaton had settled.
I understood it. I didn't agree with it. Both things were true at the same time. Both sides simultaneously.
Valphine tried a different approach. Evidence. "The corrupted one seeks to corrupt the dragon."
"Do you have any proof?"
And then Soma pressed Meril's staff against one of the phasing stones.
I wasn't sure what I expected. The stone didn't glow or crack or speak. But something surfaced in it — the truth of what had happened there, readable if you knew how to read it. Psychic residue warped into the rock itself, following the exact line of the Carver's path. The giants leaned in, the way mountains lean in, which is to say the weight of their attention shifted by a degree that felt enormous when you were standing at its base.
They acknowledged what they saw. And then:
"Hekaton has said, and the failure of the breaking of the sundering has shown, that giants must take care of giant things."
Vukradin tried one more thing. Just one more. "Was there anybody else with the little one?"
"No."
The Carver had come through alone. Alone, carrying something made of obsidian, and the rocks had not recovered from where his feet had touched them.
We talked about the sword afterward — Soma and I putting words to what we were both thinking. Level-draining. That was the word. The kind of weapon that takes something from you that healing can't put back. Vukradin said he didn't want to come anywhere near it, and nobody argued, because nobody had anything intelligent to add to that.
The ascent was what the ascent was — the mountain asking for something physical in return for passage. I gave it what it wanted. I have Goliath blood and the Gauntlets of Ogre Power and a life's worth of climbing things that tried to stop me, and the pass got what it deserved in that negotiation, which was nothing. I hauled myself up and kept moving and tried not to look too long at the places where the rocks were still phasing, still uncertain, still marked by the footsteps of something that no longer needed to think about where it walked.
Before we left, one of the giants — without ceremony, without a word — pressed two potions into my hands. Greater healing. Hill giant strength. Wordless. The gift of someone who has nothing left to say but still, at the last moment, remembers what it means to hand something to a traveler going somewhere worse than where they are.
I held them for a moment. The giants' patience had run out long before their generosity, apparently. Or maybe they were the same thing. Maybe the potions were just another form of *you would no longer be here and not talking to us*, delivered with something that looked, if you were being charitable, almost like care.
I put them in my pack.
The pass widened ahead of us. The phasing rocks thinned, and then stopped, and then there was just mountain — old and cold and indifferent in the normal, honest way that mountains are. The kind of indifference you can trust.
Behind us, the giants said nothing.
The Carver's footprints kept going forward, even and mechanical, ahead of us in the stone.

## **Valphine — The Glacier of Whispers**
The Glacier of Whispers announced itself before we could see it properly. Something in the air changed — a pressure behind the eyes, a frequency the cold had no right to carry. I noticed it the way I notice most things: catalogued, filed, assessed. The others felt it differently. Brewbarry rolled his massive shoulders as if shaking off a blow that hadn't landed yet. Soma went still in the way she does when the world is speaking to her in a register the rest of us can't quite hear.
Then we crested the rise and I saw the ice.
Violet. Not the pale lavender of certain mushroom caves in the Underdark, not the bruised blue of deep glacial pressure — this was a deliberate, saturated violet, the color of something that should not be a color for ice. And in it, faces. Dozens of them, perhaps more, pressed against the inner surface of the glacier from whatever space exists on the other side of frozen stone. Mouths open. Eyes wide. Every one of them screaming in absolute silence.
I stood there for a moment longer than was strategically necessary. I noticed that about myself.
I know what it is to be trapped in darkness. The Underdark does not offer the mercy of unconsciousness — it keeps you precisely aware of your imprisonment, aware of every wall, every weight of stone above you, every absence of light. I left. Most of those who serve Lolth do not. What they become, pressed into that service for decades, for centuries, while something vast and cold and indifferent decides whether they are useful or merely dead — I know the shape of that. I recognized something in those faces that I would rather not have recognized.
I have spent some time cultivating the discipline of moving past recognition into analysis.
The Carver's footprints crossed the glacier in a straight line. That was the detail that settled everything into clarity. Not a path. Not the wandering track of someone picking their way across treacherous ice. A line. The same inhuman geometry we had been following since the foothills — over rock, over ice, never around any obstacle, as if the concept of *around* had been removed from whatever was making these decisions. Each print carried a faint psychic residue that the violet light seemed to feed on.
The whispers arrived when I was still studying the footprints. They did not ask permission. They simply appeared in the space behind conscious thought, the way certain prayers do, the way certain threats do.
*He walks without rest, without breath, without soul.*
Not metaphor. Not poetic warning. A technical description.
I carry the knowledge that there are forms of possession which unmake the host entirely — where the driver inhabits the body but the original inhabitant is simply gone, not imprisoned, not screaming somewhere in the architecture of their own skull, but *absent*, the way a room is absent of furniture. This was that. The footprints confirmed it. The whispers confirmed it. Soma had said it with her quiet druidic certainty — *that guy is possessed* — and she was right, she was usually right about such things, but she had said it with the concern of someone who hoped there might still be a person inside to save.
I did not think there was a person inside to save.
Something surfaced in me then — not memory exactly, more the sensation of a door I had mentally labeled *later* opening without my permission. My priestly training. There had been something, some particular doctrine about this region, about Lathander's light and its relationship to psychic resonance, to the whispers that cling to old wounds in the landscape. I had considered it peripheral at the time. The kind of local legend that priests embroider their sermons with when the fundamental theology isn't sufficiently interesting. I had paid it the attention it deserved, which was partial.
Standing at the edge of a haunted violet glacier, with silent screaming faces pressing against the ice on all sides and a psychic impression of something soulless walking through everything that had ever lived here, I understood that my teachers had not been speaking in metaphor.
Lathander's light, specifically invoked, specifically directed — not as comfort but as *ward* — could hold the psychic pressure at bay. Grant the mind a clarity that the glacier's resonance could not penetrate. For ten minutes. A window.
*This is just some new thing that I can do?*
I had said it aloud before I considered whether to. The GM — the circumstances — had seen fit to hand me a tool I didn't know I'd been carrying, and my first reaction was taxonomic. *Is this new, or did I simply not know?* There is a distinction. I find the distinction matters to me more than it perhaps should, this question of what I have always been capable of and simply failed to examine.
The glacier did not give us time for extended philosophical inquiry.
We crossed. The party made their peace with the whispers through whatever resource each of them calls on under pressure — Brewbarry through some combination of stubbornness and skull thickness, Vukradin through the particular furious clarity that his principles produce when under assault, Soma through that deep rootedness that I have never fully understood and have stopped trying to deconstruct. I kept Lathander's blessing held in reserve, present in my hands the way a weapon is present, unused but drawn, watching for the moment the glacier found a crack in someone's resolve.
It didn't come. The party held.
I emerged on the other side with the faces still burning in my peripheral vision. Not literally — they were behind us now, receding in the violet ice, their silence permanent and uninterruptible. But certain images settle into the archive and do not fade on request.
I know what it is to be trapped in darkness. I do not think I will forget what it looks like from the outside.

## **Vukradin — The Drake's Lair**
The young one lay still in the snow, and I knew before I reached it what the Carver had done.
You see, most creatures — most *people*, even — will walk around a thing in their path. A nest. A territorial beast. A mother's lair. You go around. You respect the inconvenience of life. But the Carver had not gone around. He had walked in a straight line, as something walks when it has already decided that nothing in front of it has standing to matter, and where the young drake had been sleeping the snow was black and the eye sockets were hollow and the cold had settled into the stillness with that particular finality that means: this happened quickly, and with no hesitation, and nothing was afraid. Only the victim, I suppose, and only briefly.
I stood there longer than I meant to.
Above us, the mother circled. She had come home to this — her young dead, the pass filled with strangers, the thing that had done it long since vanished — and she was describing the same arc over and over, those wide, searching loops that are not really hunting, that are something older and worse than hunting. Her cries came back to us off the stone as echoes, so that the grief arrived twice, staggered.
Soma said it quietly, to no one in particular: *"Poor Mother Drake. It was one of your kids that got, you know…"*
"Mothers are resilient," I said.
And Soma — Soma who is always listening to the thing underneath the thing — said simply: *"We are."*
Two words. I felt them land.
I looked back up at the circling drake, and I thought: this creature does not know who we are. She does not know we are not responsible. She does not know we are, in some sense, on her side — that the same creature who killed her young is the reason we are here, cold and improperly dressed, in a mountain pass that smells of necrotic energy. She only knows she came home and the world had been made wrong, and now there are strangers standing over her dead.
And I thought: we do not need to add to this.
"We don't need to fight her," I said.
Brewbarry, to his eternal credit, nodded. He understood immediately.
"Save it," I said. "Save it for the real guy."
There is a thing I have noticed about violence: it is very easy to use it as punctuation. A creature circles, presents a threat, and the combat instinct fires before anyone has asked whether the situation requires it. The Carver had killed without hesitation; I saw no particular reason to prove that we shared the reflex. There was a creature in pain above us. There was a child lying still below. The appropriate response was not another addition to what the Carver had already done here.
I raised my instrument.
I want to be precise about this: I did not play something cheerful. I did not play something that tried to explain or fix or reframe. A grieving mother does not need to be persuaded that things will be alright. She knows they won't. The young is dead. That is the situation and it will remain the situation. What I played instead was something that simply... named the shape of it. The Moonlight Sonata has a patience to it — it does not rush toward resolution, it sits with the difficulty of a thing and lets the phrases fall like stones settling into water. It does not argue with grief. It says: yes, this is heavy, here, I'll hold the other end.
And then, without me asking — which is the only way Brewbarry does anything, I've noticed — he joined in. I don't know exactly what he contributed, because I was watching the drake, not him, but I felt it. Two voices in the cold. Two unlikely figures standing in a mountain pass over a dead young drake, making the only offering we actually had.
The circling slowed.
She came down in widening descents, the way a bird comes down when it has decided something is safe but has not quite finished deciding. She landed some distance from us, and she looked at the young one, and she lowered herself to the snow beside it.
She let us pass.
I kept playing until we were well clear of the lair, until the echoes stopped coming back, until the mountain had swallowed the sound. Then I put the instrument away and walked on without saying anything, which is unusual for me.
The Carver had made a straight line through that lair without breaking stride. We had stopped. We had played. We had let a mother have her grief without adding to it.
I am a politician now, apparently, which means I am supposed to think about what things cost and what they return. But some things you do not do because they are useful. Some things you do because the alternative is to be the kind of person who keeps walking.
I know what kind of person keeps walking in a straight line through a living thing's home.
I would like, very much, to remain a different kind of person.

## **Soma — Carving a Path**
The wall stopped us cold.
Not metaphorically. Literally. Boulders the size of houses, seamed with packed ice, filling the path from cliff face to cliff face like the mountain had simply decided we were done. I looked left. I looked right. I looked up, briefly, in case there was something obvious I was missing. There wasn't. Just more rock. More ice. The wind off the glacier cutting through every gap in my shell that I'd forgotten I had.
The others were doing their own calculations. Brewbarry sized it up the way he sizes everything up — as a thing to be hit. Vukradin was probably already composing something about it. Valphine looked at it the way she looks at everything that inconveniences her: like it had personally offended Lathander.
I crouched down.
This is the part nobody sees, when I do what I do. The part that isn't dramatic. I pressed my awareness down through the frozen surface, through the ice, through the cold-hardened dirt beneath it, looking for the seam. The thread. There's always a thread — some place where the stone is sitting against other stone rather than fused to it, where centuries of settling have left the barest memory of give. The mountain isn't solid. Nothing is solid if you know how to listen.
I found it. Barely. A thin tongue of workable earth running beneath the base of the largest boulder, deep enough that the ice hadn't locked it completely.
*Enough,* I thought. *Maybe.*
"I'm trying to carve a structure out of it," I said, to whoever was listening. "Use Mold Earth to pull things around — move the stones aside."
I didn't explain further. There wasn't much else to say. Either the path was there to be made, or it wasn't, and talking about it wasn't going to change which one was true.
I pressed my hands flat against the stone and let the working begin. This is the part that feels like borrowing. Like reaching into something that belongs to itself and asking — not demanding, never demanding — *can I?* The earth doesn't always answer the same way twice. On good ground, in living forest, it's almost eager. Up here, on a frozen mountain that has never cared about anything smaller than a glacier, it was more like asking a sleeping giant to shift. Politely. With no guarantee it would bother waking.
The stone moved. Grudgingly. An inch, then another.
Vukradin called out something about bardic inspiration — that particular kind of energy he broadcasts when he wants you to know he's paying attention.
"Well, I don't need it," I said, without looking up. "I got it on its own."
Whether that was confidence or stubbornness I couldn't have told you. I was forty-five years old and I had moved earth in harder places than this. I had been cold before. My hands know stone the way they know my own shell — not as something foreign, but as something continuous. The same stuff. Rearranged.
The boulder shifted another inch.
I am old, and I have seen much, and I am not going to die today. Not on a mountain. Not before we find out what comes next.
My hands stayed flat against the stone. The cold moved up through my fingers and into my wrists and I let it, because that's how this works — you have to be of the same temperature, the same stubbornness, the same patience as the thing you're asking to change.
The mountain had not answered yet.
I waited.
