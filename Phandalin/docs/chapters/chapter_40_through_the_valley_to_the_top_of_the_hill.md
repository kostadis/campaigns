# Chapter 39 Through the valley to the top of the hill


---

## Soma — Recap And Battle Setup

The gully was about sixty feet across. I clocked that first — width, depth, the angle of the far ridge. Ten orcs on the opposite side, and one anchorite of Talos with claws where fingers should be. The light was wrong on her. Not blight-wrong, not the wrongness I'd learned to read through the staff, but wrong in the way that something has been dedicated to cruelty for long enough that the cruelty shows in the posture.

I thought of Corbin, briefly. Couldn't help it. He'd be at Boney's flank right now, nervous, trying to prove something. But Corbin was dead, and we were here partly because of that, and I wasn't going to let the thought sit any longer than it took to name it.

Vukradin was already talking. He had launched into something about Lathander — I caught the shape of it, the cadence he uses when he's trying to reach people who weren't planning on being reached. Three of the orcs nearest the anchorite shifted. Not friendly. Not close to friendly. But the particular posture that means *I am listening instead of swinging,* which is more than I'd expected from orcs under a Talosian caster's authority.

Then Valphine let the radiant light bloom from her hands.

The anchorite's reaction was immediate. You didn't need to speak Orcish or Common or any language at all to read what happened next — she named Valphine a false cleric and the order went down the line of orcs like a stone through water. The three that Vukradin had reached with his words were still hesitating, but the rest had made their decision.

Vukradin said something about having cast the whole speech in Orcish. There was a long beat.

"I made a point of talking in orc," he said, and the tone was a man discovering he'd paid for a very specific service that the room didn't require.

Valphine told him she spoke Orcish too. He received this information with the particular stillness of someone calculating exactly how many of his special abilities he had just spent unnecessarily.

Orcs on the ridge. Anchorite with claws. Three neutrals who might hold if nobody did anything else too dramatic.

The battle for the Circle of Thunder was about to begin.

## Brewbarry — The Battle For The Circle Of Thunder

The orc came out of nowhere, screaming for Talos.

I heard it before I saw it — that rising battle-cry, the kind that's meant to rattle you, and I felt Vukradin shift beside me. The big one, the one they'd call Orc 2 if this were a ledger entry and not a gully full of killing, had his greataxe up and was charging straight at Vukradin with everything he had.

And then something strange happened.

Vukradin did that thing he does — that thing I still don't fully understand, where the air around him gets a little heavier, a little more intentional, and the world sort of bends to accommodate his will. The orc swung. The blow that should have connected just... didn't. It went wide. I felt the advantage of it pass into me like heat from a fire, a certainty in my hands I don't usually have until I'm already bleeding. Vukradin's voice, calm behind me: *We'll give Brewbarry advantage on his next attack.*

That's Vukradin. That's always been Vukradin. He takes a hit that was meant to hurt him and turns it into something useful for me.

I didn't have long to feel grateful.

The anchorite hit us all at once.

Lightning doesn't feel like much in the instant it touches you — it's the half-second after, when every muscle in your body seizes and your teeth crack together and the light burns a white line across your vision. The bolt came down the gully and found Vukradin, found Xanth, found me. I heard the centaur curse in words I didn't know, the kind of raw syllables that mean the same thing in every language. I was still standing — barely — but the hit had reorganized something inside me, pulled the air out of my chest and replaced it with a kind of ringing absence. The anchorite stood at the far end of the field looking pleased with himself.

That was a mistake on his part.

Xanth got his longbow up fast — two shots, both of them high, lost in the trees. He looked disgusted with himself in the way only someone who genuinely expected better can look.

Then Soma moved.

I have watched Soma transform before. It never gets less strange. One moment there is a tortle elder, patient and heavy-shelled, and then the air warps and there is something enormous where she was — a giant elk, head down, already moving, already building speed before the form has fully settled. She hit the anchorite like a wall of forest. The ram drove him into the dirt and kept going, and when the dust cleared he was on his belly, prone, and Soma's voice came out of that massive throat with something like satisfaction: *On your belly.*

The anchorite had a moment to consider what it meant to insult a druid.

Around me, the orcs were finding their angles. One went for Vukradin again. Another tried Soma's elk form and missed. A third — one of the ones that was supposed to be neutral, which is a thing I find funny, the idea of a neutral orc in a fight — launched a javelin at the elk and got through. I saw Soma absorb it. She didn't go down, but she was eating damage now, which meant the clock was running.

Then Boney arrived.

Boney is a skeleton on a horse. I mean — Boney is a skeletal horse who carries a sword and fights like he knows what he's doing, because apparently that is a thing that happens when Soma is involved. He came through the middle of everything, found the prone anchorite, and hit him hard with that longsword. Two-handed, because Boney doesn't carry a shield. Of course he doesn't. He's a skeleton. I found myself briefly grateful that I didn't have to explain this to myself anymore.

The anchorite was still breathing. Not for long.

Orc 7 decided to charge into the cluster of us, which was brave, if unwise. My body moved before I decided to move — the halberd was already coming up, the first swing gone wide, and then Vukradin's gift was still in my hands, the advantage still warm, and the second swing connected with everything I had behind it. The orc went down hard and didn't get up.

From somewhere to my left, Vukradin's voice carried that particular note of someone who is trying to sound irritated but is actually just amused:

*"How are we gonna win them over if you're slaughtering them?"*

I didn't have an answer for that. The orc had charged. The sword was already out. What exactly was I supposed to do.

Valphine spoke for me, which was fair: *"But he came to attack us."*

And then it was my turn.

I raged. There is something that happens in that moment — not loss of self, not quite, but a simplification. The gully got cleaner. The anchorite was prone. He had lit up Vukradin with lightning. He had lit up Vukradin.

I moved through the two orcs between me and the anchorite. They both took their swings. Neither of them connected. Sometimes the world wants you to get somewhere, and it steps aside. I brought the halberd down on the prone anchorite — once, with the weight of Bless behind it, with the certainty of Vukradin's gift still singing faintly in my muscles — and the hit was clean and total. I could feel how little was left in him.

The second swing missed. But then Soma pointed out the obvious thing — he was prone, which meant I had advantage — and Valphine rolled again.

Eighteen.

The anchorite died.

Vukradin: *"Alright!"* And then, because he is who he is: *"No more killing things, let's convert everybody!"*

I stood over the body and thought about that for a moment.

Orc 9 came at Boney next, which I hadn't expected — something about the skeleton offended him, maybe, or maybe Boney just had the look of something valuable. I saw the swing coming and I stepped into it. The hit landed on me instead. While raging it felt like being tapped on a bruise — real, but containable. Boney lived. I figured Soma would want that.

On the other side of the gully, Orc 10 threw a javelin at Valphine and missed completely, the kind of miss that has a story behind it. Orc 11 got through the elk's side for something.

Then Orc 2 — the first one, the one Vukradin had redirected — apparently decided he'd been patient enough with all this fighting. He put his greataxe down. Not dropped it. Set it down. Deliberate. And then he said, in the way that men say things when they've run the numbers and don't like the result:

*"Give me money and I let you live."*

I looked at Vukradin. Vukradin looked at the orc.

*"The followers of Lathander don't make such demands."*

The orc picked his greataxe back up. Of course he did. He swung. And I heard, underneath the impact, underneath Vukradin's grunt of pain, the orc's whole logic laid out in the way he pulled back: *I don't want to kill you, man, I just want the money.*

Xanth's second round of shots went high. Both of them.

And then Soma, who had been taking hits in elk form and absorbing them one by one, made a decision. She stepped back, took the attacks of opportunity that came with moving — one of them connecting, one glancing off nothing — and then she dropped the form. Just let it go. The elk vanished. Soma was a tortle again, smaller, standing in the middle of a gully full of orcs, and she climbed onto Boney's back like it was the most natural thing in the world.

*"So we get the abomination and the delicious meat away from the guys,"* she said.

Boney moved. Orc 9 swung at him as he went. Missed.

And that was how it sat — the anchorite dead in the dirt, the orcs still circling, the gully still loud with the smell of lightning and blood and something that might have been possibility, if Vukradin had anything to say about it.

He usually did.

## Valphine — The Rout Of The Talosians

The anchorite went down hard. Brewbarry's axe found him before the man had finished a prayer, and I watched the last breath leave a fanatic of Talos with the same clinical attention I bring to most deaths — cataloguing it. The grammar of that moment: a priest of the storm-god, prone, exposed, cut down by a Goliath who was running through javelin fire to get there. Clean. I had assessed the tactical situation and directed Brewbarry toward the more dangerous caster first. In Menzoberranzan, you kill the spellcaster. You kill the spellcaster before anything else. Some lessons survive the border crossing.

I am not without sympathy for what Talos offers. I understand the appeal of a violent god — the permission structure, the theology that places destruction at the center rather than at the margin. In House Sotorra, cruelty was doctrine. Not worship exactly, but something adjacent: the acknowledgment that power expresses itself through pain, and that the god who sanctions your pain is the god worth serving. I have known that grammar. I left it behind not because it was cruel but because it was *small* — rationed through hierarchy, spent carefully, always in danger of running out. Talos is the same operation. Storms end. The sun does not.

Still. The priest had believed in something. That is worth noting.

Vukradin was already speaking in rounds, his voice working the gully below with something that is, I think, the most honest magic he does. Universal Speech is a strange faculty — not persuasion exactly, more like the removal of a barrier, the linguistic distance collapsed so that the argument can reach the person directly. He rolled a twenty-six. I watched the orcs on the ridgeline go through several stages in rapid succession: hostility, calculation, the particular slowing that happens when an idea enters a mind that wasn't expecting one. By the time he finished the passage, he had moved seven of them away from the fight. He had been speaking to Lathander the entire time before he corrected himself to say it. The slip was interesting. I filed it away.

My own contribution was less elegant.

The orcs speak their own language and I speak it too — one of many things from my education I have never bothered to discard. I called on them to kneel before the power of Lathander. I said it with the register one uses for commands to inferiors, which in my experience functions as a reasonable default. What I had not sufficiently accounted for was the compound liability: drow, undead skeletal horse, unstructured presentation, mid-combat exhortation. The orcs did not find the combination inspiring. One who had been neutral reconsidered his neutrality in my direction and returned to hostility. I was informed, with some diplomatic candor, that *orcs don't like drows.* This is not untrue. And I was informed that *this was not the best organized religious ceremony.* This is also not untrue.

The truth is that a drow cleric of Lathander leading a disorganized warband that includes a skeletal horse and a barbarian in a dread helm with glowing red eyes does not read as a coherent theological proposition to an orc who was already uncertain. Surface religious ceremonies have *structure* for a reason — ritual is the scaffolding that makes unfamiliar power legible. We had offered none. We had offered combat and a persuasive aasimar bard, which is better than nothing but not quite a creed.

*We gotta work on the spectacle a bit,* Vukradin said, which was rueful enough that I nearly said something kind in response. I didn't.

Brewbarry sent one fleeing orc off with a swing that missed — on purpose, we agreed, in the way that certain misses are on purpose, or at least that is how I have elected to record it. The halberd said *who has the violence*. The miss said *you are not worth the expenditure*. The orc understood both messages and ran. That is a reasonable outcome.

Orc nine did not receive the same consideration. I told Brewbarry to stomp him, and Brewbarry did, with the kind of directness that makes him an effective ally and a suboptimal negotiating partner. The non-lethal call arrived a moment too late. Someone noted it. Then: *too late.* Then: *alright, next time.* Vukradin was rueful again. I said, dryly, that Brewbarry would say *oops, sorry*, which is approximately what happened. The orc was dead. We had not intended it. These things accumulate.

Orc ten was handled more precisely. I called the target, declared the strike non-lethal with enough lead time to matter, and Brewbarry put the orc down cleanly, unconscious. Fifteen points of force through a non-lethal strike. One hit. The orc crumpled and stayed. Good. I had wanted at least one of them awake for afterward.

Then the flight.

There is a specific quality to the moment when a warband decides to stop being one. It does not happen in unison — it cannot, because each individual is performing a private calculation, and those calculations do not complete simultaneously. What you observe from the outside is a cascade: one orc decides the arithmetic no longer works, turns, runs. The next sees him go and revises his own figures. Then another. The whole structure dissolves not because anyone surrendered but because everyone, separately, concluded that the available options had narrowed to survival.

Orcs one, four, five, and eight. Then eleven. Then six — who *had* been receptive, who had been moving toward the argument, who looked around at the field and the fleeing companions and the bright light that is deliciously painful for me and decided that life was too short. He ran. I watched him go.

Vukradin called out to orc three as the man bolted: *see the way to Lathander!* The invitation was genuine, which is one of the more interesting things about him. He converts by reflex. He cannot watch a man run away from an argument without wanting to give him the last line. Orc three ran anyway.

What I felt, watching the gully empty, was not triumph. Something more complicated than that — the specific satisfaction of seeing a violent hierarchy disassemble, knowing I understand every structural reason it held together as long as it did and every structural reason it could not hold any longer. The anchorite was the keystone. Brewbarry removed him in the first exchange. Without the spellcaster, without the holy sanction, the orcs were a warband without architecture. Even the one who had believed — even the one who had thrown his javelin at the skeletal horse because an undead abomination offended his god — eventually ran.

I have known worse hierarchies than this one. I have served inside them, been assessed by them, been found insufficient by them. There is a particular flavour of satisfaction in watching the machinery of a smaller power come apart, when you are standing in the light of a larger one.

The gully was quiet. One orc unconscious. The rest: fled or dead.

Vukradin had been speaking to Lathander before he corrected himself.

I did not say anything about that.

## Vukradin — The Conversion Of Prutha

The last orc standing looked at me with the specific expression of a man who has just watched nine of his fellows die and is working out the arithmetic of the situation. He was holding his greataxe. I was watching his eyes.

I said everything I had to say to him. The words came from the same place they always come from — not performance, not calculation, but the genuine conviction that there is a true frame available to any moment and I am reaching for it. The sun above the gully. The Drow cleric wreathed in impossible light. The dead anchorite of Talos cooling behind us. I laid it out for him. The picture was honest. It required no embellishment.

He set down the greataxe.

He looked at Valphine — this radiant, painful, sunlit figure that no orc who grew up praying to the Stormlord could have anticipated encountering in a forest gully — and the working-out in his eyes resolved. He spoke in halting Common, his voice somewhere between wonder and practical assessment.

"Talos offer power. Lathander — more power. Lathander all-powerful."

Then he rushed toward Valphine and announced himself a follower of the Drow Cleric of Lathander.

I said, honestly: *"Wow. We've got a follower!"*

Because that was exactly what we had.

His name was Prutha. The party began debating conversion names before I could object — which I did, loudly, the moment Valphine suggested *Thrall.*

"Harsh, man, harsh. Wow." I let the word sit there. Then: "You know, I object. You know, he doesn't know what thrall means, but I know what thrall means. That is not appropriate for our new friend."

Prutha kept his name. As it should be.

What he told us next required more than just the one moment to take in. His great-uncle had been his clan's shaman. The Talosians killed him deliberately, early, to break the clan's resistance and make recruitment easier. They called the old man weak, abomination, thunder meat. And then they stood in front of what remained of his family and offered the terms Talos always offers: fight, or be what his great-uncle was.

Prutha had fought. Three skirmishes. Never killed by choice.

I thought about Corbin — briefly, painfully, the way I always think about Corbin now, a flash of the face and then the weight settling. I had pushed a man into something similar and called it recruiting. I wasn't going to do that accounting here, in the middle of a gully. But the thought was present.

Then Prutha reached into his clothing and produced a small bone whistle.

He explained that in his clan, the orcs had greeted the sunrise each morning by blowing a bone whistle. Eastern tradition, his great-uncle's tradition. He held it up. He looked at it for a long moment.

And then he blew it.

One note. Simple and thin and very old.

And then he began to cry.

Valphine read him with a sharpness I could not have matched — later she told me her read was simply that he'd realized how long it had been since he last blew it. That was what the tears were for. Not the dead uncle. Not the three skirmishes. Not the men who died in the gully behind him. The whistle. The gap between the whistle then and the whistle now.

I found I had nothing to say for a moment.

Then Prutha gave us the line his great-uncle had apparently lived by, the thing the old shaman had passed down before the Talosians named him thunder meat and put him in the ground:

*"Dawn comes for everyone, even those born in shadow."*

The table went quiet.

"Wow," I said. "It's quite poetic."

I meant it completely. I said to Prutha — honestly, reaching for the right thing to say to a man who had just blown a whistle for the first time in what seemed like years: "You're my first."

And then the thought arrived, the one I voiced aloud because it was obvious and true: "He wants to play the whistle at the dawn, but with Lathander, it's like it's the dawn all the time. So, like, he could play the whistle all the time."

The theology here was not complicated. Lathander is perpetual dawn. The whistle greets the dawn. The math resolves. I was genuinely pleased with how cleanly it fit.

Valphine reached for a Religion assessment of the situation and came back with a result she described only as "oh my god" in a tone that suggested the dice had not cooperated. Despite this, some kind of genuine insight arrived anyway — the notion that the whistle ritual could be woven into her own evolving practice, that Lathander was not moving further from her for it, but closer. I thought about Valphine's church, which the party had described diplomatically as a masochism cult, and then thought about the bone whistle and the weeping orc and the words about dawn and shadow, and I thought: well, this is how religions acquire depth. It is not always tidy.

I turned to Prutha and delivered what I had to give him.

"Lathander blesses you today, my child."

He looked at me. "What does that mean?"

"Lathander blesses you today, my child."

He thought about it. Then: "He thinks that's awesome." He had, apparently, lots to think about.

"Lathander — power. Beyond doubt. Lathander all-powerful." He turned back to Valphine and pledged himself again, firmly, in case anyone had missed it the first time.

Then he kicked the fallen anchorite's body several times to make a point about theological transition.

I disapproved of the corpse-stringing that followed — when Valphine and Brewbarry hauled the anchorite up as a warning, Prutha was visibly enthusiastic in a way that landed somewhere I preferred not to examine — but I didn't intervene. I had a follower. He had just blown a bone whistle and wept and recited a line of genuine beauty in his second language. I was prepared to allow him the kicks.

He laid out his terms for the path forward: orcs no longer following Talos, we spare. Committed Talosians are another matter entirely.

Convert or die. A fluid approach to religion, yes. But it worked for the anchorite in the other direction, didn't it? The Talosians had offered exactly that framing to Prutha's great-uncle, and the only difference between that moment and this one is which door leads somewhere worth walking through.

I asked about the orc who was still unconscious somewhere behind us. Whether he might be revivable, convertible, another Prutha waiting to emerge.

The answer was uncertain. We pressed on.

Someone — I will not say who — made a joke about whether Corbin would have liked Prutha.

The DM's response was that it was cold.

He was right. It was cold. And also, yes. Corbin would have liked Prutha very much. He would have recognized something in the whistle, in the terms offered and refused and survived through compromise, in the great-uncle's line about dawn and shadow. He would have been moved by it.

I was moved by it. I was carrying that, along with everything else.

## Soma — Arrival At The Circle Of Thunder

The last thing Xanth did before he left was make everyone uncomfortable.

He stood there, that enormous centaur, and looked at the staff the way you look at something you already know the history of. Not like he was admiring it. Like he was remembering. Then he turned to me — tapped me, actually, on the shell, his hand broad enough to cover half of it — and said, "This one, this one was blessed by Meril, and I know that she will do great things. Peace, Beauty."

Shell-sprouts. The whole lot of them were watching me after that.

I did not say anything. There is not much to say when a centaur you met two days ago understands something about you that you are still arguing with yourself about in the dark hours before dawn. The staff sat in my grip, quiet. Three leafs on the thing. I have been watching those leaves since Xanth told me Meril chose me on purpose, which is the kind of information you receive and then carry at arm's length while you figure out what to do with it.

Xanth looked at the staff one final time. Just looked at it. He was doing something with that look — measuring, or saying goodbye to it, or confirming something for himself — and yes, it made everybody feel a bit awkward. Vukradin broke the silence first, because Vukradin always breaks the silence first.

"Hooves off," he said, appreciating the phrase as Xanth did exactly that.

I watched the centaur go until the trees took him. Then: "You call that hooving. I'll show you hooving."

Nobody asked what I meant by that. Good. I wasn't sure I knew.

Prutha filled the space that Xanth left. She has that gift — or it is something else — of knowing exactly how to push forward when standing still feels like a waste of everyone's time. "We go kill other Talosian clerics," she said, and that was the plan summarized. Vukradin fussed with the name of the quest for a moment, the way he does when he needs to feel like things are organized properly: *Xanth the Centaur has offered to take us to the lair of the evil half-orc spellcasters.* Yes. And now Xanth had done his part. So.

We walked.

The hill rose out of the treeline before the storm did, which meant I saw it first as a shape — ninety feet of slope, trees gripping the sides of it like they were holding on. And then the clouds. They were already there above the crest, the kind of grey-green build that means something is gathering itself on purpose. Not weather. Intent.

The prepared description came all at once when we cleared the trees: a ring of standing stones at the summit, ancient and deliberate, and inside the ring — two figures moving. Dancing, in the loose sense of the word, in that way that is less celebration and more negotiation with something that does not speak. And around them: small, twitching, many-limbed shapes, capering.

"They're doing, like, a fire dance around it?" I asked.

"I guess you could say that."

Twig blights. Twelve of them, once we counted. I know twig blights. Every druid knows twig blights — they are the forest's bad faith argument, the thing that wears roots like a costume. Intelligence of four, which means instinct and malice and nothing else. Not convertible, I was told, as if I had been about to suggest a conversion.

The staff moved. Not visibly. It is never visible. But there is a difference between holding a piece of old wood and holding something that is paying attention, and standing at the edge of that clearing, looking up at the henge and the storm and the dancing figures, I felt very clearly that the staff was paying attention.

Meril, I thought, and did not finish the thought.

Whatever he had brought here — whatever he had walked toward in his last years, whatever had drawn him to this region and this particular rot in the world — it was up that hill. The Circle of Thunder had been there the whole time, waiting. And here I was at the treeline with one leaf left on a borrowed staff, arguing with Vukradin about a necklace.

"We do!" I said, because we were already into it. "I keep telling you we do."

"And I keep telling you, we don't, we're not allowed to use that, it's not ours. I am holding on to this necklace, you are not — I don't trust you with it."

I did not have time for this. I also did not want to say that what I was actually thinking about was not the necklace at all but the way the staff had just shifted in my hand, subtle as a change in tide direction, some dormant quality of it waking up in response to whatever was saturating the air at the top of that hill.

"Hey, look over there!" Tactically useful, also accurate. "Okay, they're not even — they're not clustered enough for Fireball. They're not even clustered for it."

Vukradin had something cone-shaped. He mentioned it. Valphine offered Spirit Guardians, which was sensible. "Spirit Guardians plus Dash," Vukradin said, and something about that combination appealed to him in a planning way that I recognized. "Anyway, I've got, like, Thunderwave or something, I think that works pretty well, right? Like — Thunderwave, oh..."

The hill was still there. The storm was still building. The figures in the henge were still moving.

I held the staff and looked up at the standing stones and thought three leaves. At least there wasn't a fourth. 

We stayed at the treeline and studied what we were walking into.
