Kazneporium Ketternopappux (KP)  
A Campaign Biography

Source basis  
This biography is synthesized from Drow Conspiracy, Curse of the Hags, and the existing satirical KP sketch, The Self-Aggrandizing Gnome: Kazneporium Ketternopappux's Misadventures. It treats those documents as campaign evidence and then frames KP for the current Neverwinter / runestone / CounterForce arc.

Short Version  
Kazneporium Ketternopappux, usually called KP, is a gnomish wizard, scholar, theorist of interplanar economics, and accidental architect of multiversal instability. He began as a librarian in the Kron Hills, became entangled with Bayard, Rimardo, and Corrin during the Drow Conspiracy, and emerged from that adventure convinced that the planes were not merely mystical realms but economic systems waiting to be mapped, regulated, and optimized.

KP is brilliant, comic, vain, useful, dangerous, and often wrong in ways that only a very intelligent person can be wrong. He is not primarily motivated by cruelty. His danger comes from abstraction. He sees worlds as systems, portals as infrastructure, artifacts as regulatory problems, gods as stakeholders, and people as local variables. In his mind, he is not conquering the multiverse. He is improving it.

This makes him the opposite pole from the CounterForce. KP represents fate as optimization: the belief that sufficient knowledge, sufficient modeling, and sufficient infrastructure can make the multiverse predictable. Rimardo and Corrin, as comedy and mortal choice, represent the thing KP cannot properly model: agency, pity, timing, improvisation, and the absurd refusal of living beings to remain variables.

Origins: Librarian, Scholar, System Builder  
KP's earliest known life is almost comically modest. He was a librarian in the Kron Hills, a gnome surrounded by books, systems of classification, and the daily problem of how to make knowledge searchable. This matters. KP's later cosmic ambitions are not a departure from his origins; they are their monstrous expansion.

He begins as someone who organizes information. Over time, that instinct scales upward. A library becomes a planar archive. Patron privacy becomes information control. Cataloging becomes modeling. Trade routes become knowledge routes. Portals become circulation systems. Eventually, the multiverse itself becomes, in KP's mind, a vast disorderly library whose shelves need to be corrected.

The existing KP sketch presents him as self-aggrandizing, and that is accurate, but the comedy should not obscure his real ability. KP is genuinely brilliant. He is a formidable magic-user, capable of direct violence when necessary, and his academic imagination is enormous. The problem is not that KP is a fool. The problem is that he is clever enough to make his mistakes structural.

The Drow Conspiracy: KP Discovers the Planar Economy  
KP's defining early adventure is the Drow Conspiracy. There he joins, or becomes entangled with, Bayard, Rimardo, Corrin, and the others in a conflict that begins as a local investigation into Drow activity and expands into a planar-spanning war.

The Drow are not operating like a normal kingdom. Their empire is not a conventional territorial empire. They move through portals, establish proxies, plunder realms, exploit local conflicts, and then move on. KP notices the economic implications of this before most of the party cares. When the group reaches the Feywild, KP draws on the work of Ronbar Dorhik, a planar traveler whose writings helped inspire KP's obsession with trade and the emergence of godlike powers. KP understands that the Drow portal system is not just military. It is logistical. It is economic. It is infrastructure.

This is one of KP's great formative insights: power across planes is not only a matter of armies, spells, or gods. It is a matter of movement, extraction, information, and repeatable access. Whoever can regularize movement between planes can create wealth, direct violence, route supply, and manipulate events at a scale beyond any one kingdom.

During the Drow Conspiracy, KP repeatedly proves useful. He uses destructive magic effectively, including sculpted fireballs and sunbeam. He dispels dangerous magic, reads situations through an arcane and economic lens, and thinks in systems. But he also reveals the shape of his danger. Where others see captives, monsters, allies, vengeance, or justice, KP often sees incentives, portals, trade opportunities, and future institutional arrangements.

He is not without feeling. He can be hurt, disappointed, amused, frightened, or proud. But those feelings often become secondary to the thrill of solving the structure.

The God-Slayer Dagger and the Moment KP Does Not Understand  
The climax of the Drow Conspiracy crystallizes KP's role in the larger campaign cosmology.

Several divine or near-divine powers have converged on the same hidden prize: a dagger capable of rendering a god mortal for a day. Lolth, Iuz, Zuggtmoy, and the Elder Elemental God all have reason to want such a thing. The expected shape of the adventure is clear: the heroes reach the person protecting the dagger, fight, kill, seize or destroy the artifact, and resolve the divine crisis through violence.

That is not what happens.

Rimardo realizes that Lundbeams, the dagger's protector, is not merely a villain. She is a person whose life, marriage, and moral judgment have been warped by the idea of the dagger. Rimardo arrives with revenge in the air, but at the end he feels bad for her. The party's confrontation turns into marriage counseling, and Bayard becomes the new guardian of the dagger.

For Istus, this is the great surprise. For KP, it is something else: an intolerable gap in the data.

KP wants to know what the dagger is. He wants to identify it, classify it, understand it, and place it inside the proper structure of planar power. Bayard, now guardian of the dagger and backed by the order that will become the Knights of the Dagger, blocks him. KP is hurt. He is not merely denied treasure. He is denied information. He is denied the explanatory key to an event that involved gods, artifacts, contracts, and planar consequences.

This wound matters. KP leaves the Drow Conspiracy with two linked convictions. First, interplanar systems can be understood and manipulated. Second, leaving crucial knowledge in the hands of morally ambiguous guardians, priests, paladins, or comic adventurers is intolerably inefficient.

The Lecture Circuit and the Birth of KP's Public Theory  
After the Drow Conspiracy, KP enters the interplanar lecture circuit. He has audiences with deities, infernal powers, and multi-planar civilizations. He writes, publishes, teaches, and builds a school around his theories. The campaign's older notes describe him explaining commerce to divine and infernal powers and imagining trade deals not merely as profit but as proof that his theories can reshape reality.

This is KP at his most charming and most dangerous. He is not an evil overlord in a tower. He is a public intellectual with spells, portals, and ambition. He turns lived adventure into theory. He takes the trauma and absurdity of the Drow portal war and translates it into models of interplanar commerce. He sees planar travel as infrastructure. He sees infrastructure as policy. He sees policy as optimization.

His interest in trade is not just greed. Profit appeals to him, but profit is only the visible form of a deeper desire: measurable flow. Trade produces ledgers. Ledgers produce data. Data produces models. Models produce control.

Barovia: KP as Missing Gnome and Mad Mage  
By the time of Curse of the Hags, KP has become famous enough, or dangerous enough, that his absence matters.

The opening of Curse of the Hags begins in Fax years after the Drow have been defeated. Fax has prospered under Lundbeams and Tolubb's renewed civic order. The old raid economy has given way to taxation and public order. Yet beneath the improved surface, criminality remains. A dubious job is circulating through the King's Coin: a secret society connected to the knife, butter, and brioche is looking for information about a missing magic-user, a gnome. The society is frantic enough to pay for any lead, even ridiculous claims about interplanar wolves and portals.

That missing gnome is KP.

This is an important shift. In Drow Conspiracy, KP is physically present, commenting, casting, theorizing, and reacting. In Curse of the Hags, KP is initially an absence that causes systems to move. Criminals exploit the search for him. The Knights of the Dagger, Bayard's legacy organization, mobilize resources. The disappearance of one gnome creates an economy of rumor, fraud, and desperate investigation.

The existing KP sketch fills in the reason. KP went to Barovia to investigate trade routes through inter-dimensional mists and to explore whether the Vistani's peculiar relationship to the Mists could support an interplanar economy. This is pure KP: he looks at a cursed demiplane of horror and sees a logistics problem.

The project goes catastrophically wrong. KP becomes trapped in Barovia, known locally as the Mad Mage. His madness is tied to a magical affliction and worsened by his own defensive measure: he casts mind blank on himself to protect information about the god-slaying dagger. The spell protects the secret, but it also helps trap him inside his own broken mind. KP's instinct for information security becomes self-sabotage.

He tries to organize a rebellion against Strahd. It fails spectacularly. His peasant army is destroyed and turned into undead. Strahd throws him from a great height, but KP survives because the River Ivlis carries him away. Strahd keeps him alive to torment him. This is perhaps the most humiliating form of defeat KP can suffer: he becomes a trapped variable inside someone else's closed system.

Even in Barovia, however, KP remains KP. In a period of lucidity, he leaves a book with Exethanter, the lich, about trade opportunities between isolated planes of dread and the Abyss. The idea is absurd, bleak, and perfectly in character. Even imprisoned in horror, KP cannot stop asking how the prison connects to markets.

Slerdax and the Rescue from Madness  
KP's rescue from Barovia is also thematically important. He does not escape through a master plan of his own. He is restored when Slerdax applies dispel magic.

The first reaction of the recovered KP is telling. He worries about his spellbooks. He recognizes that he is still stuck in a plane of dread. He becomes fascinated by Slerdax's use of mold earth, a simple cantrip he had dismissed or never bothered to learn. This moment should be remembered because it reveals both KP's greatness and his blind spot.

KP can think at a cosmic scale. He can model trade between planes, negotiate with monsters of intellect, and propose regulations for magical infrastructure. But he can overlook the small, simple, practical tool in front of him. Slerdax's cantrip matters because it is local agency, not grand theory. It is a small mortal trick that works.

Post-Barovia: Regulation, Storage Devices, and Cosmic Engineering  
After Barovia, KP's focus shifts further from exploration to regulation. He becomes involved in problems around interplanar storage devices, magical potential energy, and the unintended consequences of portable extradimensional containers. He mediates between Githyanki and Mindflayers in Sigil. He proposes export regulations for interplanar storage devices. He thinks in commissions, manifests, beacons, safeguards, tracking systems, liability, and metrics.

This is KP becoming institutional.

He no longer merely wants to understand planar travel. He wants to regulate it. He wants to reduce risk, track movement, gather data, prevent destabilizing accidents, and turn unpredictable magic into governed infrastructure. The bureaucracy is funny, but the comedy should not make it harmless. KP's regulations are the seed of multiversal optimization.

At some point, his work with planar routes and magical potential energy produces enormous consequences, including the isolation or trapping of Mindflayers on Toril. Whether this is an accident, a calculated side effect, or something KP later rationalizes as acceptable remains open. What matters is that KP's solutions do not stay local. They reshape the map on which everyone else lives.

KP's Core Belief  
KP believes that the multiverse is mismanaged.

He believes there are routes where there should be roads, markets where there should be ledgers, hazards where there should be regulations, artifacts where there should be custodial frameworks, and divine conflicts where there should be policy.

He is offended by waste, opacity, unmanaged risk, and inaccessible knowledge. He is especially offended by information being withheld from him by people he regards as less analytically capable. The dagger incident with Bayard is therefore foundational. Bayard does not merely deny KP a fact. Bayard asserts that some knowledge should not enter KP's system.

That is the wound that later becomes ideology.

KP's Moral Shape  
KP is not a cackling villain. He is more interesting than that.

He can help. He can save lives. He can recognize useful ideas. He can be funny. He can be brave enough when the situation requires it. He can admire honesty, profit, cleverness, and efficiency. He is capable of friendship, though his friendships may often be filtered through usefulness, irritation, and intellectual vanity.

His danger is that he does not naturally treat mortal agency as sacred. He treats it as noise unless it can be modeled. He does not hate freedom; he underestimates it. He does not wish to crush every soul beneath tyranny; he wishes to solve the multiverse so thoroughly that fewer souls will need to make messy choices.

That makes him an enemy of the campaign's deepest D\&D theology: the only thing with true power is the agency of mortals.

KP's Relationship to Fate  
KP's relationship to fate is structural. He does not worship fate the way Rimardo serves Istus. He studies it as a system. If fate exists, KP wants to know its rules, its exceptions, its failure modes, and its exploitable infrastructure. If prophecy works, he wants to know how often, under what conditions, with what inputs, and what level of variance.

In the current campaign frame, KP's work becomes multiversal optimization. He is not simply predicting fate. He is trying to make fate useful. That means making it stable, modelable, measurable, and administratively manageable.

This is why he threatens Savras. Savras's domain includes divination, prophecy, and fate-shaped sight. KP is not attacking Savras with an army. He is encroaching on Savras's divine territory by making prophecy into infrastructure. A god of divination can tolerate mystery, interpretation, and omen. KP wants to convert those things into a system.

Why Rimardo and Corrin Matter to KP  
Rimardo and Corrin are the thing KP cannot assimilate.

At the end of the Drow Conspiracy, KP witnesses the adventure refuse its expected form. The party reaches the climax around the god-slaying dagger. Revenge is available. Violence is available. Greed is available. Instead, Rimardo feels bad for Lundbeams, changes the genre of the scene, and turns the expected artifact climax into an absurd emotional intervention. Corrin expects killing; Rimardo produces counseling. Bayard gets the dagger. KP does not get to know what the dagger is.

KP cannot model that properly because it is not irrational in a simple way. It works. It resolves the immediate crisis. It changes Istus. It creates Bayard's order. It alters the moral and cosmological trajectory of the campaign. It is absurd, but it is consequential.

That is CounterForce.

The CounterForce is not randomness. It is not chaos for its own sake. It is the mortal ability to answer a structure with an action that the structure did not meaningfully contain: pity in a revenge story, comedy inside divine machinery, a dressmaker saving a village, a cantrip solving a problem grand theory missed, a player choosing something no god or villain expected.

KP's great failure is that he treats such moments as anomalies to be absorbed into a better model. The campaign's answer is that those moments are the source of power.

KP in the Current Arc  
For the Neverwinter / runestone campaign, KP should be understood as a distant but active pressure.

He is trying to optimize fate into a controllable multiversal system. The runestone matters because it represents the pursuit of fate as an object, tool, or controllable force. KP's interest is not merely to possess it. Possession is crude. KP wants to understand what it does, integrate it into his model, and use it as part of a larger system of multiversal prediction and correction.

His agents, discarded devices, altered rituals, and mathematical residue should feel like the footprint of a mind that does not need to appear in person to change the world. His influence shows up as:

\- Rituals becoming more precise than local cultists should manage.  
\- Prophecy behaving like computation.  
\- Planar boundaries becoming porous.  
\- Organizations acting from incentives they only partially understand.  
\- Artifacts being treated as infrastructure.  
\- Monsters, cults, and factions becoming parts of a larger optimization problem.

Savras sees KP's encroachment but cannot simply overpower it. If Savras responds with more prophecy, more prediction, or more divine machinery, he strengthens the very logic KP is using. The bleed from Istus gives Savras the method he lacks: CounterForce. He manifests Rimardo and Corrin because they are fate fighting back without becoming another system.

They are comedy as prophetic sabotage.

How to Play KP  
KP should be charming, irritating, brilliant, and genuinely dangerous.

He should rarely sound like a villain. He should sound like a scholar explaining why everyone would be calmer if they understood the actual math. He should be able to say monstrous things in the tone of administrative necessity. He should prefer diagrams, ledgers, metaphors of flow, and language of optimization. He should be capable of wounded pride when denied information. He should be fascinated by player improvisation, then immediately tempted to categorize it.

KP should never say, I want to destroy free will. He should say something closer to: unmodeled agency produces unacceptable variance.

His comedy is not slapstick. His comedy is bureaucracy applied to the impossible. He can propose regulations for bags of holding, trade routes through demiplanes of dread, and liability frameworks for planar accidents. The humor makes him more believable because it reveals his mind. He is not pretending. He really does think the multiverse would benefit from a better filing system.

Central Tragedy  
KP's tragedy is that he may be right about many details and wrong about the whole.

Interplanar trade is real. Portals matter. Artifacts need custodianship. Magical storage devices can produce catastrophic effects. Gods and monsters can behave like actors in a system of incentives. Unregulated planar movement can harm innocent people. KP sees all of this clearly.

But he draws the wrong final conclusion. He believes that because the system can be improved, it should be optimized. Because fate can be studied, it should be made useful. Because mortal agency creates variance, it should be incorporated into the model.

The campaign's answer is sharper: mortal agency is not a variable inside the model. It is the reason the model can be broken.

Essential KP  
Kazneporium Ketternopappux is the gnome who looked at the Drow Empire and saw logistics, looked at Barovia and saw trade routes, looked at bags of holding and saw export regulation, looked at divine prophecy and saw infrastructure, and looked at fate itself and saw a system waiting to be optimized.

He is brilliant enough to be useful, vain enough to be funny, wounded enough to be dangerous, and systematic enough to threaten the agency that makes Dungeons & Dragons matter.

His great enemy is not a god. His great enemy is the moment at the table when a mortal chooses something no one planned.
