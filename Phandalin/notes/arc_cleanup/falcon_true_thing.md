# Falcon's True Thing
*Notecard — the crack, not the argument*

---

## The Person

**Her name:** Senne.

She was killed by Iymrith — the Doom of the Desert. Not in the lair. Before. Iymrith found them first.

Falcon survived. He was not with her when it happened, or he was and ran, or something in between that he has never found language for. He took the grief and turned it into a plan: he would go to Iymrith's lair and kill the dragon himself. He walked into the desert with nothing but purpose.

He arrived and found her already dead.

The Storm King's Thunder — Hekaton's alliance with the Lord's Alliance — had already ended her. Someone else killed the dragon that killed Senne. Falcon stood in a dead ancient blue dragon's lair, revenge denied, catharsis taken, and he stole some treasure and went home.

He built the lodge. He built the legend. He called himself the dragonslayer. Senne is not in any version of the story.

**What she loved:** She kept a small kit of pressed flowers — wherever they camped, she found something growing and added it. She was not sentimental about it. She didn't dry them carefully or store them with reverence. She just picked them and pressed them between the pages of whatever book was closest. By the end she had ruined three books. She thought that was funny.

**The ring:** The brass ring inscribed with the Elvish word for "lightning" is from Iymrith's hoard. He took it because Iymrith killed Senne with lightning. He couldn't take the dragon. He took the word. He doesn't think about why. He just still has it.

---

## The Grief's Shape

This is not clean loss. It is impotence, twice.

He couldn't protect her. And then he couldn't avenge her. The catharsis was taken before he arrived. He stood in that lair and had nothing to do except steal.

And then the War of Dragons: more people dying because of dragons. Cities burned. Communities shattered. And again he was just watching. He couldn't protect anyone.

Adabra's framework offers him something: *stop trying*. The trying is what costs people. The reaching is what burns them. The interventionists who fought Tiamat — look what it cost. Look at the aftermath. Look at the War of the Giants that came right after, because Tiamat's defeat created a vacuum the Giant gods rushed into.

He knows the framework is wrong somewhere. Iymrith died because someone intervened — and it wasn't him. But that knowledge has no place to go. So it stays buried under the anger.

**What the anger is for:** It gives him something to do with his hands. Without it, he is a man who ran, then arrived too late, then stole, then lied about it for twenty years. The anger at interventionists is the only version of the story where his grief is justified rather than just pathetic.

---

## The Crack

**What doesn't work:**
- Arguments about interventionism vs. naturalism — he's not there for the ideology
- Sympathy for his loss — he's heard it, it slides off, he knows what it's for
- Pointing out that Adabra is using him — he knows
- Appealing to self-interest — his self-interest is to stay angry
- Noting that intervention killed Iymrith — he knows, he can't go there, he'll shut down

**What works:**

Someone asks: *"What was her name?"*

Not: "I understand you lost someone." Not a frame. Not a play. Just the question — addressed to the person behind the grief, not to the position. Because no one has asked. Adabra used the grief. The party has been working around it. But the name question means she existed, as a person, not as a cause.

Falcon goes still. He's not going to weep. He's not going to break theatrically. He goes still, and then he says her name.

**What that moment does:**
- It doesn't flip him. He stays in Adabra's bloc.
- It takes the air out of his anger — not permanently, but for this scene, the performance stops.
- The moderates see it. They saw a man named instead of a position.
- Adabra sees it too. She will not forgive Vukradin for it.

---

## In Play

The party has to find it. It cannot be telegraphed. The ingredients are visible if they're paying attention:

- **Vukradin knows about the confession** — Falcon told him privately about the lair, the legend, the ring, the companions who died. Vukradin knows people died. He may not know there was one person who mattered differently.
- **"Lightning"** — if anyone asks why he kept the ring, why that word, why lightning specifically, Falcon won't answer directly. But the question is the opening.
- **The move:** Vukradin stops trying to win the room and starts talking to Falcon as a person. The bard who can argue his way through anything choosing instead to ask. That is what registers — because it is not a technique.

**The recognition trigger:** You know the player has found it when they stop trying to move momentum and start treating Falcon like someone with a history. It could be Vukradin. It could be Soma, who understands choosing action anyway. It could be Brewbarry, who is blunt enough to just ask. Whoever asks: the answer is the same. *Her name.*

---

## After the Scene

Falcon doesn't become an ally. He remains in Adabra's bloc. But he's cracked, and she knows it.

Later — levels 7-9 — if the party encounters him again, he is carrying something new: the knowledge that someone noticed. He won't acknowledge it. He won't thank them. But he is no longer fully in Adabra's orbit, and that is the beginning of her isolation from her own people.

The lodge, when they return to it: if someone looks, the brass ring is not where it used to be. He's moved it. That's all.

---

## DM Logistics

- No roll. Either a player asks the right question or they don't.
- "The right question" = anything addressed to *her* rather than to the argument. Players don't need to know her name or history. They just need to ask toward the person.
- If no one finds it: fine. It's not required for either branch. The difference is Branch A clean vs. Branch A with a splinter.
- If a player asks about the ring directly — "Why lightning?" or "What does it mean?" — treat that as finding the crack. He kept it because it was the word for what killed her. He can't say that. The question forces him to know it.
