# Dragon Mythology — Foundation Note
*World canon for the Phandalin campaign*

---

## A Note on Color and Alignment

Dragon color is biology. It determines breath weapon, habitat preference, physical capability. It does not determine character.

Green dragons can be caretakers. Gold dragons can be reshapers. Iymrith is a blue dragon and she is neither — she is a scientist. Claugiyliamatar is a green dragon and her relationship to the garden is complicated in ways that have nothing to do with her color.

Biological determinism — the idea that what a creature *is* fixes what it *does* — is rejected in this campaign across all species. A goblin can be a scholar. A drow can worship Lathander. A dragon's color tells you what it looks like, not who it is.

This matters practically: the party cannot assume. Every dragon has to be read on its own terms.

## The Root Admin

All dragons share one core belief, regardless of color or alignment: **dragons are the root administrators of the planes.**

This is not ideology. It is not religion. It is simply what dragons know to be true about themselves, in the way water knows it flows downhill. The planes exist. Dragons are their ultimate authority. Everything else — mortals, gods, fiends, celestials — operates within a structure that dragons sit above.

What differs between dragons is not the belief. It is what they do with it.

**Good dragons — the caretakers:** Root access means responsibility. The garden exists. Dragons tend it. Mortals, civilizations, ecosystems — these are the garden's contents. A good dragon's timescale is geological. They do not hurry. They do not panic. The end-game is obvious to them, and there is no rush: the garden will eventually be well-ordered, because they are patient and they are permanent. They protect. They wait. They correct when necessary.

**Evil dragons — the reshaper:** Root access means freedom. The garden exists. Dragons can reshape it. Why tend what you can redesign? Evil dragons see the same root authority and draw the opposite conclusion: if you are the administrator, the system exists to serve you. Reshape reality to meet your needs. The garden is not a trust. It is a tool.

Neither position is wrong from within its own logic. They are two expressions of the same underlying truth: dragons have power that mortals can barely perceive, operating on timescales mortals cannot grasp.

## The Banking Trope

Dragons accumulate wealth. This is universally known. What is less universally understood is *why* — and what that wealth actually does.

Dragons are bankers. The hoard is not greed in the simple mortal sense. It is capital. It is leverage. It is the instrument through which a dragon participates in the material plane's economy without needing to be present in it. A dragon's hoard is a financial institution. It lends. It contracts. It accrues.

The most powerful dragons are also the most powerful financial actors in their regions — and they operate across centuries, which means the interest compounds in ways that reshape civilizations.

**The trope inversion:** "The dragon stole the princess."

What actually happened: a lord borrowed from a dragon banker. The debt became inconvenient — perhaps unpayable, perhaps politically embarrassing. The lord could not simply default; the dragon would collect. So the lord invented a narrative. The dragon became a monster. The "rescue" was debt-cancellation by murder. The treasure "recovered" from the dragon's hoard was simply the cancellation of obligations that could not otherwise be discharged.

This pattern repeats throughout the history of the Sword Coast. The great dragon-slayings that made heroes famous are, examined closely, debt-cancellation operations. The heroic narratives are the cover story.

This maps directly onto what was done to Jewish moneylenders in the 12th century: borrow, then murder the creditor, then blame the creditor for the borrowing. The dragon is the Jew of the fantasy world — powerful, financially sophisticated, and vulnerable to the narrative that their power is inherently predatory.

**Good dragons know this.** It is why they are careful about whom they lend to and how they structure their arrangements. It is also why they have largely withdrawn from direct participation in mortal economies — the caretaker model requires distance.

**Evil dragons use it.** The vulnerability of being the banker is a problem to be solved through power, not restraint.

## Tiamat: The Ultimate Banker

Tiamat is the logical endpoint of the banking model: **she will lend to anyone.**

No moral judgment on borrowers. No assessment of whether the purpose is good. No concern for downstream consequences. She is pure financial power — the bank of last resort for anyone who needs resources badly enough to pay her terms. She has done this for millennia. She has lent to gods, fiends, mortal empires, and desperate individuals.

Her terms are always repayable. Just not always in ways the borrower fully understood when they signed.

**The War of the Dragons:** Severin's desperate gold-gathering that destroyed communities and bankrupted societies was not simple villainy. It was a man trying to service a debt to Tiamat — to complete the terms of a contract that demanded a ritual, a hoard, a precise arrangement of resources. The cascade of destruction was what happened when mortal civilization tried to generate dragon-scale capital on a human timeline. The Interventionists who defeated the ritual disrupted the contract. Tiamat did not receive what was owed.

**Current status:** Tiamat tried to enter the prime material plane. The Interventionists stopped her. Zariel subsequently tried to remove her from Avernus and failed. She is stuck — but a banker does not need to be present to have power. Her debts are still owed. Her loans are still outstanding. Her terms still apply.

She watches. She lends. She waits. She is very good at waiting.

## Iymrith's Deviation

Iymrith is a scientist. This is the thing that makes her genuinely dangerous and genuinely strange among dragons.

She studied greed. Not practiced it — *studied* it. Centuries of research into what hoarding actually *is* as a magical and metaphysical phenomenon. The half-dragon pixies, the Ascore experiments, the artifact program — all of it was directed at understanding the deeper structure of draconic accumulation.

What she discovered: **pure greed — gold treated as an end in itself, never deployed, never lent, never circulated, just accumulated — creates a metaphysical anchor to the material plane.**

Not the banking model. Not debt. Not leverage. Something prior to all of that. When a hoard carries enough accumulated desire — the weight of everyone it was taken from, the covetousness of every theft, the compressed wanting of centuries — it becomes a soul anchor. As long as the hoard exists with that greed intact, the dragon who holds it cannot be permanently removed from existence. Kill the body: the soul returns. Banish the spirit: the anchor pulls it back.

**This is why Iymrith never spent her hoard.** Not because she was simply greedy. Because she understood that spending it would discharge the accumulated desire — would make the gold move, would clean it of its metaphysical weight. The moment the gold circulates, the anchor weakens. She cultivated pure stasis. Pure hoarding. Gold that never moved.

**What this makes her:** Unique among dragons. Every other dragon is a banker or a caretaker. Tiamat lends. The metallic dragons tend. Iymrith did neither. She turned the thing mortals mock dragons for — hoarding — into a precision instrument of immortality. She is not the most powerful dragon in combat. She is potentially the most permanent one that has ever existed.

**What Zariel miscalculated:** She brought Iymrith to Avernus thinking she could control her. But Avernus is just a waiting room for a dragon with an anchor. Iymrith cannot be destroyed there. She can only be tormented. Zariel built a laboratory to torment her. Iymrith waits. She is very good at waiting. She learned it from Tiamat.

## The Restitution Mechanic

The Gold Anchor is made of pure accumulated greed. The antidote is pure restitution.

Not debt-discharge — that is a financial operation. Not debt-cancellation — that is a mortal convenience. **Return.** Give back. Make the gold move back to everyone it was taken from. Clean it of its accumulated desire completely.

When every piece of the hoard has been returned to its rightful owner — or their descendants, or their communities, or some honest accounting of where it came from — the greed dissolves. The gold is no longer sitting. It is no longer carrying the weight of theft. It is clean. And clean gold cannot anchor a soul.

**Why Vukradin is the instrument of this:** "Conflict-free gold" is not a financial principle. It is a metaphysical diagnosis. Wealth without theft. Value without exploitation. Gold that carries no accumulated desire because it was never wrongly taken. Vukradin has been saying this for years without knowing he was describing the exact condition that breaks Iymrith's immortality.

The warriors cannot sword their way to this answer. The druids cannot nature their way past it. The clerics cannot pray it away. Only the character who has spent the entire campaign insisting that gold carries moral weight — that it is never neutral, that it remembers where it came from — can see that the anchor is made of moral weight and can only be unmade by removing it.

## Implications for the Campaign

**Adabra's grievance is partially correct.** The Interventionists disrupted a financial system — the dragon-banking network, Tiamat's contract with Severin — and the disruption caused the cascade. She is wrong about the solution (non-intervention), but the mechanism she observed is real.

**Tiamat lending to Adabra is a loan.** Not recruitment. Not ideological alignment. Credit extended in the form of draconic power. There are repayment terms Adabra has not read carefully.

**The "dragon stole the princess" history of Phandalin.** Falcon's lodge, Lady Alagondar's sword, the lighthouse salvage — these can all be traced back through the debt-cancellation pattern if the party looks closely enough. The dragon-slaying history of the Sword Coast is not what the bardic tradition says it is.

**Iymrith's research program is the key to the epic tier.** She was not trying to rule. She was trying to become permanent. Those are different ambitions, and they require different responses.
