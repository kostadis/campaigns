Woodland Manse \- Rimardo and Corrin Clue

Revised Campaign Placement  
The party has defeated Cryovain and has defeated the agents of Talos at the Circle of Thunder. In the most recent session, the party learned that the next decisive location is Woodland Manse.

The second ritual is at Woodland Manse, and its purpose is to summon Gorthok the Boar.

Important correction: Gorthok is not being summoned to destroy Woodland Manse. Woodland Manse is the ritual site. The intended target is the orc force that has taken Falcon's Lodge.

Campaign Causal Chain  
This beat should preserve the full displacement chain:

1\. The War of the Giants created chaos across the North.  
2\. Cryovain was displaced by that wider chaos.  
3\. Cryovain moved into the Icespire Mountains.  
4\. The orcs of the Icespire Mountains were displaced by Cryovain.  
5\. Those orcs moved into Neverwinter Wood.  
6\. Some orcs were absorbed by the Cult of Talos.  
7\. Some became scattered warring bands.  
8\. One group stayed together and assaulted Falcon's Lodge.  
9\. The orcs at Falcon's Lodge refused absorption into the Cult of Talos and therefore became a threat to be destroyed.  
10\. Their first attempt to call Gorthok was disrupted at the Circle of Thunder.  
11\. Their second attempt will be at Woodland Manse.

This makes the Talos plot more than a generic cult attack. It is a local war among displaced populations, opportunistic cult recruitment, and the absolutist logic of a destruction cult. The Cult of Talos absorbs those who submit to storm and ruin. Those who refuse are not merely enemies; they are proof that destruction has limits. That proof must be destroyed.

Purpose of This Beat  
Woodland Manse should become the hinge between the local Talos cleanup and the deeper KP/Istus conflict.

On the surface, the party is stopping a Talos ritual to summon Gorthok against the orcs at Falcon's Lodge. The cult wants those orcs destroyed because they refused to obey. Some displaced orcs submitted and were absorbed into Talos's storm theology; the Falcon's Lodge group stayed together outside the cult's control. To a destruction cult, that refusal is intolerable. Underneath that, the party discovers that the Talos agents are no longer acting like ordinary storm fanatics. They are trying to make destruction predictable. They are using ritual geometry, timing, and storm magic together.

That is what creates the cosmological problem. Istus herself does not simply appear in the Forgotten Realms or act openly inside Ao's order. Instead, KP's multiversal manipulation creates a cross-world fault. KP is encroaching on Savras's domain by trying to optimize fate itself: prophecy reduced to prediction, destiny reduced to management, and the runestone's pursuit of fate converted into a controllable system.

Savras can see the violation, but he cannot easily fight it. If he answers KP with more prophecy, more prediction, or more divine machinery, he only strengthens the logic KP is using. What bleeds through from Istus is not a command and not an avatar in the ordinary sense. It is a method: CounterForce. Savras begins manifesting Rimardo and Corrin because they are the shape of fate fighting back without becoming another system. They are comedy as prophetic sabotage, a foreign fate-pattern from Oerth that Savras catches without fully understanding why.

The reason they are manifesting is KP. His multiversal optimization has damaged the boundaries enough for Savras to catch and express this foreign pattern, and the pattern gives Savras the one weapon KP cannot optimize: absurdity that reveals the limits of control.

Thematic Frame  
Talos provides violence.  
Orc disobedience provides the immediate motive.  
KP-style logic provides precision.  
Woodland Manse provides the ritual apparatus.  
Falcon's Lodge provides the target.  
Savras manifests the foreign fate-pattern through comedy.  
CounterForce gives fate a way to escape itself.

Rimardo and Corrin become the warning label.

Deep Backstory: Istus's Interrupted Avatar  
Before Rimardo and Corrin became a comedy problem, they were already a fate problem.

In the Greyhawk campaign, Zuggtmoy, Iuz, Lolth, and the Elder Elemental God all converged on the same hidden prize: a dagger capable of rendering a god mortal for a day. Each power wanted it for obvious reasons. In ordinary heroic structure, the end of that adventure should have been simple. Four adventurers reach the final guardian, discover the owner or protector of the dagger, fight them, kill them, and claim or destroy the artifact.

That is not what happened.

Rimardo decided the real problem was not the dagger. The real problem was that the dagger's protector had a broken marriage.

So instead of resolving the climax through violence, Rimardo played marriage counselor. The protector was not defeated in the expected way. The protector handed the guardianship of the dagger to Bayard. KP, who was present and deeply invested in understanding the actual nature of the hidden artifact, never found out what the dagger was.

This was the first great surprise.

Important table detail: Rimardo and Corrin did not arrive at this climax neutral. In the Drow Conspiracy summary, the Lundbeams problem is framed as personal. They had reason to want revenge, and Corrin clearly expected the scene to end in killing. Other voices around them pushed that direction too. The shock is that Rimardo did not simply choose mercy as a tactic. He reversed his own revenge story because, at the end, he felt bad for Lundbeams.

That matters because it makes Istus's surprise sharper. Fate had room for vengeance, greed, payment, heroism, mercy, and even foolish improvisation. What it had not comfortably accounted for was revenge becoming empathy through an absurd marriage-counseling bit. Rimardo did not refuse the climax. He changed what kind of climax it was.

Istus was genuinely surprised. Not because mortals had acted irrationally, and not because adventurers had refused a fight. Fate can account for cowardice, mercy, confusion, greed, heroism, and stupidity. What surprised her was that Rimardo had found a third category inside the expected binary of violence or failure. He solved the god-killing dagger problem through domestic repair.

For a god of fate, that kind of surprise matters.

On Oerth, Rimardo and Corrin were meant to become an avatar of Istus. Not a conventional heroic avatar, but a paired instrument: Rimardo as the voice of fate's legal and theological inevitability, Corrin as the silent hand that makes inevitability happen.

Then they made the wrong, impossible, perfectly timed choice. Instead of becoming a solemn avatar of fate, they became a comedy routine.

This surprised Istus.

For a god of fate, surprise is not a minor event. Istus had not merely seen mortals disobey. Mortals disobey constantly. Rimardo and Corrin did something stranger: they fulfilled the structure of the avatar while changing its genre. The divine instrument still existed, but it had become a bit.

And Istus laughed.

That laugh changed her. It did not make her chaotic or turn her into a comedy goddess. It made her realize something more unsettling and more amusing: the god of fate is also trapped by fate. If everything has already been woven, then Istus herself is inside the pattern she governs.

The CounterForce offers the impossible exit. Fate can describe the pattern, tighten the thread, and prepare the avatar, but it cannot fully extinguish the human capacity to answer structure with timing, pity, refusal, and a joke that should not work but does. By embracing the CounterForce, Istus discovers how fate can escape fate: not by denying the weave, but by allowing absurdity, reversal, and compassion to become real features of it.

That is what cracks her up. The loophole in fate is not outside fate. It is hidden inside it, wearing a cheap stage costume and ruining the solemnity of the divine plan. The CounterForce does not prove that fate is false; it proves that fate is funnier, stranger, and less totalizing than systems like KP want it to be. Timing, reversal, empathy, and laughter became part of the thread.

This is the Oerth fate-pattern now leaking into the Forgotten Realms because of KP's work. Savras is not manifesting Istus herself. He is manifesting the echo of Istus's surprise: Rimardo and Corrin as the avatar that became a comedy act. Whether these are the real Rimardo and Corrin, echoes of them, prophetic masks, or something Savras is accidentally assembling remains unresolved.

Why Woodland Manse Works  
Woodland Manse is the right ritual site because it is already part of the Talos network in Neverwinter Wood. The cult can use its corrupted natural power, hidden approaches, and existing storm rites to make a second attempt after the Circle of Thunder failed.

The Manse is not the victim. It is the summoning engine.

Falcon's Lodge is the intended victim. The orcs there are not targeted because they are innocent, nor simply because they are rivals. They are targeted because they refused the cult's authority. The storm offered absorption; they answered by holding the lodge.

The Talos agents are trying to force the old Manse, the surrounding trees, and the storm above it into a single destructive circuit. If the ritual completes, Gorthok is summoned not as a random beast but as a directed siege weapon: the living thunderbolt that will smash the orc-held lodge.

The Woodland Manse Setup  
When the party reaches Woodland Manse, the area should feel wrong before combat starts.

The storm does not simply rage overhead. It waits. The trees creak in sequence. Rain falls harder in some places than others, as if the weather has been divided into ritual zones. Copper pins and blackened iron nails have been driven into roots, lintels, old walls, and standing stones. Talos symbols have been carved over older natural markings, but the newer marks are too measured and too evenly spaced.

The agents of Talos are trying to turn Woodland Manse into a summoning diagram pointed toward Falcon's Lodge.

Useful clues at the site:  
\- Copper rods hammered into roots and ruined walls.  
\- Talos prayers rewritten as timing instructions.  
\- A storm map showing Woodland Manse as the ritual source and Falcon's Lodge as the target.  
\- Boar tusks wrapped in wire and hung from branches like conductors.  
\- A blackened brass ring etched with mathematical notation.  
\- Orc blood, hair, or stolen trophies used as sympathetic links to the Falcon's Lodge group.  
\- A repeated phrase: if the circle fails, the appetite answers.  
\- Evidence that the Circle of Thunder ritual was the first attempt to tune the storm.  
\- Evidence that Talos-aligned orcs identified the Falcon's Lodge group as disobedient holdouts who refused the storm.

What the Ritual Is Doing  
The Talos agents are trying to summon Gorthok the Boar directly at Woodland Manse, then aim him at Falcon's Lodge.

Their goal is not only to call a monster. Their goal is to bind Gorthok's first charge to a target. The ritual uses the Manse as the thunder engine, the storm as the summoning medium, and stolen orc tokens as the scent.

If the ritual succeeds, Gorthok arrives already aimed. Falcon's Lodge is marked as prey.

If the ritual fails, Gorthok may still partially manifest, but uncontrolled, wounded, incomplete, or enraged. This gives the party a spectrum of outcomes rather than a simple win/loss switch.

Encounter Feel  
Run the encounter as a storm-summoning interrupted mid-performance.

Possible battlefield effects:  
\- Each round, thunder marks a line of charge through the battlefield one round before Gorthok or the ritual strikes there.  
\- Ritual circles around the Manse empower Talos agents until disrupted.  
\- Destroying or removing conductor tusks weakens the summoning.  
\- Recovering or destroying the stolen orc tokens breaks the link to Falcon's Lodge.  
\- A character who disrupts the ritual rhythm can delay Gorthok's arrival.  
\- Vukradin can identify the ritual's rhythm as a kind of ugly percussion.  
\- A successful performance, counter-rhythm, mockery, or musical interruption can impose disadvantage on a ritual check or suppress a storm effect.

The Rimardo and Corrin Clue  
After the party disrupts part of the ritual, or after the battle if you want to keep the reveal clean, they find an object that does not belong: a comedy playbill folded into a triangle and pinned to a storm-marked tree with a tiny dagger.

Front of the playbill:

Rimardo and Mr. Corrin  
Predestination, Plunder, and Other Legal Fictions  
Special Woodland Engagement: The Boar Who Was Already Booked  
Woodland Manse  
Attendance: violently encouraged

Back of the playbill:

One dragon, displaced by larger wars.  
One mountain tribe, displaced by the dragon.  
One forest, made crowded by consequence.  
One lodge, seized by the displaced.  
One storm cult, recruiting from the desperate.  
One thunder beast, scheduled for punishment.  
One ritual, using borrowed mathematics.  
One audience, not yet aware it is participating.  
Balance outstanding.

Signed:  
R. and C.

Corrin's mark is a tiny dagger hole through the phrase borrowed mathematics.

What the Party Learns  
The clue should give the party four actionable pieces of information:

1\. The Talos agents are following an escalation plan, not simply raging through the woods.  
2\. The target of Gorthok is Falcon's Lodge and the orcs holding it because they refused to obey or be absorbed by the Cult of Talos.  
3\. Someone has given the Talos cult methods that make their storm magic more precise than it should be.  
4\. The orc crisis is not isolated. It is the local result of a larger chain of displacement caused by the Giant War and Cryovain.

Vukradin's Vision  
Because this is a performance-clue rather than a pure prophecy-clue, Vukradin is the best recipient. When he touches the playbill, hears the ritual rhythm, or uses music near the Manse, he receives a fractured stage-vision.

Vision:  
Vukradin stands before a rain-soaked stage. The audience is hidden in darkness. Thunder provides the percussion. Rimardo stands in the spotlight, holding a ledger. Corrin waits beside him, silent, holding five cards.

Rimardo says:  
A monster is only random until someone sells tickets to the charge.

Corrin turns over four cards:  
GIANT WAR  
CRYOVAIN  
FALCON'S LODGE  
GORTHOK

Then Corrin turns over a fifth card:  
WAYSIDE INN

Rimardo looks irritated and says:  
That was supposed to be the second act.

The vision ends with applause that sounds like rainfall, hoofbeats, and distant orc war-drums.

Meaning of the Vision  
The vision does not mean the party should immediately abandon the Talos sequence and go to Wayside Inn. It means the same hidden pattern will continue there later. This plants Rimardo and Corrin as recurring figures without derailing the current objective.

Gorthok Escalation Options  
Use Gorthok as the immediate ritual threat at Woodland Manse and the looming threat to Falcon's Lodge.

If the party acts quickly:  
Gorthok does not fully manifest. The party fights Talos agents, storm effects, and perhaps a partial manifestation: thunderous hoofbeats, spectral tusks, or a single devastating charge through the battlefield. The link to Falcon's Lodge is broken.

If the party disrupts the ritual but not cleanly:  
Gorthok manifests in unstable form. It is present, but the ritual bindings are flawed. It attacks the party, Talos agents, and anything carrying the orc sympathetic tokens. The party may need to stop the beast before it finds the trail to Falcon's Lodge.

If the party fails to disrupt the ritual:  
Gorthok manifests fully and is aimed at Falcon's Lodge. The party must race, track, magically intercept, or warn the orcs before the thunder beast reaches the lodge.

Moral wrinkle:  
The orcs at Falcon's Lodge are not innocent, but they are also displaced people. Some of their kin joined Talos. Some became marauders. Some tried to survive together and took the lodge. The Cult of Talos is a destruction cult, so the orcs who refused obedience are a theological problem as much as a tactical one. Gorthok is not just a monster attack. It is the cult using divine violence to erase a refusal.

Battlefield Beats for Gorthok  
\- The ground trembles before Gorthok arrives.  
\- Lightning crawls along roots and ruined stone.  
\- Boar-shaped shadows appear in rain curtains before the true beast manifests.  
\- The Talos champion or lead ritualist shouts that Falcon's Lodge has already been judged.  
\- Gorthok's first appearance should be a charge, not a static arrival.  
\- If Vukradin disrupts the rhythm, Gorthok's charge veers or lands one round late.  
\- If the orc tokens are destroyed, Gorthok loses the scent of Falcon's Lodge and becomes harder for the cult to aim.

Possible Cultist Lines  
The storm does not forget who fled the mountain.

Cryovain drove them down. Talos offered them thunder. They refused.

The weak took the lodge. The disobedient will be broken.

The boar knows the scent of those who refused the storm.

Circle failed. Manse answers. Gorthok comes.

Rimardo and Corrin's Role  
They are not quest-givers. They are a counterforce signal.

Publicly, they are a comedy act.  
Privately, they are the echo of Istus's interrupted avatar: an Oerth-origin fate signal being manifested through Savras's prophetic domain because KP has disturbed the boundaries between worlds. Their exact ontological status is deliberately unresolved: they may be the real Rimardo and Corrin, echoes, avatars, comedic masks, or an accidental composite generated by Savras's divinations.

Rimardo talks like the system: legalistic, procedural, inevitable.  
Corrin silently breaks the system with timing, misdirection, and the perfect impossible reveal.

Their purpose here is to show that KP's influence is not just planar weirdness. It is the conversion of living contingency into predictable process. Rimardo and Corrin are not proof that Istus is manifesting in the Forgotten Realms; they are evidence that KP's work has made the boundary porous enough that Savras is accidentally giving form to the echo of Istus's surprised laughter through jokes, timing, interruption, and performance. KP is the reason the signal is getting through.

In this beat, their clue makes the displacement chain visible while also revealing the cult's destructive logic. The orc crisis is not a random monster problem. It is consequence cascading down from cosmic and political events, then being weaponized by a cult that treats refusal as something to annihilate.

How to Keep the Tone Right  
The clue should be funny before it is frightening.

The party should first react with: Why is there a comedy playbill here?  
Then: Why does it know what just happened?  
Then: Why does it understand the whole chain better than anyone local?

That rhythm is the point. Joke first. Dread second.

Optional Shrine of Savras Use Later  
If you still want to use Shrine of Savras, move it later. It can become the place where the party confirms that Rimardo and Corrin's appearances are not random.

Possible later use:  
At Shrine of Savras, the party finds a second playbill already waiting for them. This one is not a clue to the next Talos ritual. It is a confirmation that Savras's prophetic machinery is manifesting Rimardo and Corrin, even though Savras himself may not understand why these figures, from a different world's fate-current, keep appearing. What he is actually catching is not Istus's direct will, but the echo of the moment when her intended avatar became a comedy routine and made fate laugh. The reason Savras can catch it at all is KP's damage to the boundaries between worlds.

Shrine playbill line:  
Prophecy is what powerful people call a guess after it becomes profitable.

This lets Shrine of Savras preserve its thematic value without contradicting the current table direction.

Why Vukradin Matters  
Vukradin is uniquely positioned to understand this. A wizard might see a magical anomaly. A cleric might see an omen. A rogue might see misdirection. A bard sees structure.

He can recognize that Rimardo and Corrin's jokes have rhythm, setup, and punchline. That means their appearances are not random. Their comedy is a map.

Future Payoff  
The next major appearance should be at Wayside Inn.

At Wayside Inn, Rimardo and Corrin can appear as a real public act. The show is funny, successful, and professionally threatening to Vukradin. During the routine, Corrin's silent tricks reveal evidence of the next layer of the campaign: Talos corruption, Kraken Society influence, or KP-style planar control.

The joke lands first.  
The dread arrives second.

Core Line  
KP is trying to optimize fate into a controllable multiversal system.

Savras cannot fight that system by becoming more systematic. He fights back by manifesting the echo of Istus's surprised laughter: the CounterForce hidden inside fate.
