# Calmer

Calmer was a 5'2" cleric of [[St. Cuthbert]], assigned to the party by [[Terjon]] to maintain institutional oversight. Known for his copper-red hair, hazel eyes, and flat nose, he projected the appearance of a zealous religious figure. His unwavering loyalty to the Church and Terjon drove his actions, but his chronic over-eagerness, noise-making, and doctrinal intrusions rendered him a liability in the field — a manifestation of [[pious-social-dysfunction]].

He repeatedly insisted on leading missions, often triggering dangerous events — such as alerting Lubash the ogre or falling into the moat during the first Moathouse excursion. His attempt to “comfort” the traumatized prisoner [[Voss]] devolved into a lecture on St. Cuthbert’s enemies, requiring [[Sequoia]] to physically intervene. He took pride in his puns, treating his name as a conversational gift — a behavioral marker of [[pun-as-character-defense-mechanism]].

Calmer worked in coordination with Sequoia and had the ability to Turn Undead, which proved useful in combat. He served as an informant for Terjon, reporting to him as per their original agreement, and was treated as an [[expendable-asset-tactic]] by [[Zephyr]], who sent him alone into the pool room to retrieve treasure while a giant crayfish attacked him.

During the ambush, Calmer was nearly killed by [[Albreth]]. He also expressed strong opposition to adding certain individuals, such as [[Gurdy]], to the party.

Calmer was killed by [[Thalsor]] during the Black Earth Compound battle, who threatened to mutilate his corpse to prevent resurrection. The party recovered his intact body via Sequoia’s gaseous form infiltration on Planting 24, 579, preserving the conditions necessary for potential resurrection under [[corpse-integrity-as-resurrection-condition]].

His death remains unreported to Terjon or the Church of St. Cuthbert. Whether he will be resurrected, and what institutional response may follow, is unknown. Calmer’s arc exemplifies the cost of blind loyalty in a world that rewards pragmatism over piety.
