# Expendable-asset-tactics

A strategic approach in which low-status individuals — clerics, mercenaries, cultists, non-humanoids, or ideologically compromised allies — are deliberately deployed as sacrificial tools to achieve tactical or symbolic objectives, with no expectation of survival or long-term value. This tactic extends [[prisoner-as-commodity]] by applying the same utilitarian logic to those nominally aligned with the operator, rather than captives.

Calmer, the cleric of St. Cuthbert, was an early example: his clerical status and apparent loyalty made him appear reliable, but his tactical incompetence rendered him ideal for expendable roles. Zephyr sent him alone into the pool room to retrieve treasure while a giant crayfish attacked — a move that confirmed both the tactic’s effectiveness and its moral bankruptcy. Calmer’s death was tolerated, even exploited. The party’s recovery of his corpse suggests a chilling pragmatic compromise: the asset is expendable in life, but its potential for resurrection or symbolic value must be preserved — creating a duality of disposability in life and utility in death.

The Fire Temple Warlock and his bugbear ally exemplify the institutionalization of this tactic. Both were sent into a high-risk confrontation with no contingency for survival. Their theatrical declarations and rapid collapse confirm they were never intended to win — only to provoke, distract, or trigger a reaction. This is not isolated cruelty, but systemic efficiency: the Fire Temple, the Church of St. Cuthbert, and even Sheernobb’s bureaucracy all employ this method — not out of personal malice, but as normalized operational doctrine.

The true horror is not the death — it is the quiet, institutionalized acceptance of it as normal. This tactic thrives under leadership-by-assertion-vs-leadership-by-competence, where authority is maintained through the controlled sacrifice of subordinate roles, and clerical assignment becomes a tool of influence rather than service.
