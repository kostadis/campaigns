# Poisoned Hospitality as Predatory Ritual

A ritualized form of predation in which food and drink—symbols of safety, care, and community—are weaponized to lure, incapacitate, and consume victims. In the case of Aunt Emma, the plum pie was not merely poisoned; it was a ceremonial offering, part of a broader pattern of using domesticity as a trap.

This ritual mirrors the structure of human feasts but perverts them into acts of consumption. The act of sharing food becomes a test of vulnerability: the victim must lower their guard to accept the offering. The hag’s cookbook, *The Hag's Guide to 101 Recipes Of Children*, confirms this is not an isolated act but a codified tradition.

This concept extends [[prisoner-as-commodity]] by adding a layer of symbolic violation: the betrayal of trust is as lethal as the poison. It also parallels [[public-execution-as-political-theater]] in its performative nature—though here, the audience is the victim themselves, who is made complicit in their own demise.

**Key elements**:  
- Illusory benevolence  
- Edible trap  
- Ritualized preparation  
- Consumption as completion  

**Implication**: Any seemingly generous offer in wilderness or fey-influenced zones must be treated as a potential ritual trap.
