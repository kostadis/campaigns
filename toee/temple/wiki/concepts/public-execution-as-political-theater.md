# Public Execution as Political Theater

Public Execution as Political Theater is a method of governance in which lethal punishment is performed openly, without trial or justification, to instill fear and reinforce authority. This tactic is exemplified by Madame Selentis’s execution of Elfar Twell at the Gallows Table — his crime remains unknown, but his death is a message: defiance will be met with visible, irreversible consequence.

Unlike Zelt the Rat’s criminal enforcement, which relies on economic coercion and hidden violence, Selentis’s theater is ritualized and symbolic. The absence of due process, the silence surrounding the offense, and the public display of the corpse all serve to dehumanize the victim and elevate the executioner. This concept extends beyond mere brutality — it is a form of non-bureaucratic statecraft, where control is maintained through spectacle rather than law.

It contrasts with Y’dey’s disappearance, which relies on mystery, while Twell’s death relies on visibility — both are tools of control, but with divergent psychological effects.
