# Pious-social-dysfunction

A condition in which religious devotion so dominates an individual’s cognitive framework that it impairs social judgment, situational awareness, and tactical adaptability. Calmer exemplifies this: his piety is genuine, but it manifests as noise, doctrinal intrusion, and inappropriate timing. He attempts to comfort Voss with a sermon, interrupts critical operations to assert leadership, and objects to Gurdy’s presence on ideological grounds.

Unlike true zealots who inspire loyalty, Calmer’s dysfunction alienates allies and endangers missions. His behavior is not malicious — it is unselfconscious. He believes he is serving St. Cuthbert, but his actions serve only his own need for validation. This concept distinguishes between authentic faith and performative religiosity — Calmer is neither hypocrite nor heretic, but a victim of his own internalized dogma. His death is not punishment, but consequence.
