# Zephyr — Voice Notes
*Rebuilt from Chapters 1–28 and four VTT sessions (March–May 2026). Edit freely — you know him better than the document does.*

---

## The Core Thing

Zephyr is a Tiefling who has decided that the world owes him comfort, and he intends to collect. He demands the best room at every inn, eats chicken while his companions eat slop, and sizes up the reward before accepting the quest. This is not greed — it's compensation. The world treats him as hellspawn, refuses him entry, looks at him with disgust, and asks "or whatever you are" after every introduction. He has decided that if the world will charge him for existing, he will charge it back with interest.

But comfort is the *goal*, not the method. The method is this: Zephyr leads. He is the first one through the door, the first one with a plan, the first to name the terms. He does this not by claiming authority but by reading what a room needs and supplying it — the confident voice when everyone else is silent, the deal that resolves when everyone else is still arguing, the dagger that ends the conversation before it can go wrong.

He will spend people to get what he wants. This isn't cruelty. It's the same pragmatism he applies to everything: people are resources, resources are spent on objectives, and the accounting only becomes personal when someone he actually cares about ends up on the ledger. He knows the difference between "the price of doing business" and "shit, shit, shit." There's more overlap than he'd admit.

He is from the Abyss — or near enough, in blood. He carries that cosmology in him as a reference point, not a wound. Things are measured against what he knows from there. Most things don't measure up. He is not troubled by darkness. He is occasionally troubled by how comfortable he's become with it.

## The Tiefling Tax (Two Modes)

Every new town, every new NPC, the same thing happens. Jaroo looks at him with "unmistakable disgust." The Sheernobb guard says "or whatever you are." A heartbroken dryad asks if he's "come from the Abyss to torment me." A gnoll announces "Tiefling flesh! Haven't had tiefling flesh in ages!"

Two responses, deployed strategically:

**The shrug**: He lets Sequoia speak instead. He steps back. He pretends it doesn't matter. *"He shrugs and lets Sequoia speak."* This is for ambient prejudice — the village guard, the innkeeper, the druid who just can't help himself. Not worth the breath.

**The defiance**: When it's a threat rather than a slight — when someone announces they intend to eat him — he doesn't flinch. *"Come and get it."* The gnoll said Tiefling flesh and Zephyr said come and get it, and then they died. This is not bravado; it's efficiency. The threat is solved. Move on.

He never gives a speech about tolerance. He never rages about prejudice. He thinks *"This again"* and moves on to the next thing. But the Tiefling tax runs underneath everything — it's why he demands the private room, why he tracks every reward to the silver, why he says "I am a human, a victim of a curse" so smoothly it sounds rehearsed. Because it is rehearsed. He's been saying it for years.

He references the Abyss casually, as a comparative measure: *"I've heard of worse in the Abyss, but this is bad."* He carries the cosmology in his blood. He doesn't dwell on it.

## The Face

Zephyr's best work is his deception — not the simple variety (wearing stolen robes, lying about his name) but the sophisticated kind that uses truth as a weapon.

Against the wraith: instead of lying, he told the truth. He revealed that the Water Temple was going to be attacked. The wraith, calculating its own interests, let them pass — because it had "already feasted enough on Water Temple dead" and saw no reason to stop someone going to do more of the same work. Zephyr understood that the wraith had rational preferences and appealed to them. Persuasion roll 23. The wraith smiled and stepped aside.

Against Kelno: he opened with a test question. *"Are you for or against the temple?"* Not a bluff, not a manipulation — a direct question designed to reveal character through the answer. Kelno said "I am for chaos, but I'm also for living." Zephyr accepted the deal without haggling because that answer was honest. He can spot the genuine article. He respects pragmatism and self-interest over false loyalty. The deal was sealed in seconds.

He performs bluffs with commitment when needed. Walking into an enemy stronghold in stolen robes: *"We have come here to see Lareth the Beautiful. We bring him news and need reinforcements."* The guard buys it. The sergeant buys it. He commits fully to the lie and doesn't flinch.

He also uses his appearance as operational equipment: *"hoping my distinctive demonic appearance will get one of these ignorant villagers to approach me. A spooked mouse will run to its house."* The thing people fear about him is the thing he weaponizes.

## The Flatterer

Zephyr's deeper manipulation is not lying — it is reading what someone needs to be told about themselves and telling them that. He doesn't invent the flattery. He finds the true thing the person already wants to believe, and hands it back to them. The target always moves toward the person who sees them accurately.

The wraith wanted to be acknowledged as a peer who had done impressive work. Zephyr let it display the specters. He asked it for information as if the wraith were the only entity in the room with real knowledge — which it was, and which it needed to feel. He did not flatter with words. He flattered by listening.

Kelno wanted to be respected as a pragmatist who made rational choices under pressure. Zephyr gave him a question that let him prove it. The deal happened because Kelno felt seen.

Hartsch needed to feel like he was still in command. Zephyr let him be. *"What do we do next?"* — four words, and Hartsch had the authority transfer he needed to keep functioning.

He leads by letting other people feel like they're leading. The knife goes in before they notice he was flattering them.

## The Assassin

Zephyr doesn't narrate combat. He ends it. Forty-two damage from concealment before the fire salamander knew he existed. Twenty-seven damage to a ghoul in a single ambush strike. Thirty-three on a Water Temple cultist in the opening move of an assault.

He organizes his positioning so the first round happens every time. Emerge from hiding. Strike. Kill or cripple. Then assess what's left. He doesn't calculate this — he's trained his body to do it automatically. The thinking happens before the door opens. Once he's through, it's just execution.

He claimed the killing blow on Belsornig personally — took the Dagger of Venom from Sequoia and drove it home. Not because he needed to prove something; because it was the right tool for the job, and Zephyr is the one who decides how the job ends.

*"They attack. They die."* He doesn't need more words than that.

## The Expenditure

Zephyr sent Calmer into the crayfish pool because something lurked there and Calmer was the expendable asset for finding out what. *"Calmer, I have a mission for you."* The cleric nearly died. *"Don't lose the treasure."* He ran a three-temple war that burned the dungeon and filled the corridors with bodies that the wraith was still feasting on weeks later. He traded Water Temple intelligence — information that got people killed — to walk past the wraith unmolested.

He is not troubled by this. These were resources spent on objectives. He knew the price and paid it and moved on.

What he will not do — what he has never done — is pretend the expenditure didn't happen. *"As the life escapes his body, I wonder if his reward from his infernal god will be everything he hoped for. Probably not."* He notices what he's spent. He gives it a line, sometimes two. He doesn't give it more than that.

The exception is Calmer's death. When the cleric died fighting Thalsor, the theatrical interior went silent and what was left was: *"Shit. Shit. Shit."* Six times. One word. That was the sound of something being spent that wasn't supposed to be on the ledger.

He will spend people again. He already knows this.

## The Sadistic Mentor

Zephyr's relationship with Calmer was the defining comedy of the campaign — and has now evolved into something more complicated now that Calmer is the Earth Temple's Prophet and nominally Zephyr's boss.

The early dynamic: he sent Calmer into the crayfish pool because something lurked there. *"Calmer, I have a mission for you."* The cleric nearly died. *"Don't lose the treasure."* He told Calmer "grow up" when armor was destroyed by green slime. He said *"Mmm-hmm"* to complaints and chose not to listen.

He needed Calmer — the party needs a healer — but he would never admit it. And when Calmer died fighting Thalsor, Zephyr's response was the rawest thing in the session record.

Now Calmer has been promoted to Prophet of Earth and is relocating temple headquarters and making administrative decisions with visible satisfaction, and Zephyr is nominally watching this happen from somewhere. The dynamic has inverted. Whether Zephyr has noticed is worth playing with.

## The Internal Narrator

Zephyr's inner monologue is theatrical and self-aware. He narrates his own life like someone who knows he's in a story:

- *"Can it be that we found our proof? Another notch in my victory belt?"*
- *"As the life escapes his body, I wonder if his reward from his infernal god will be everything he hoped for. Probably not."*
- *"Great, now everyone in this town will want us to run some fetch quest."* (On cue, they do.)
- *"This man is calculating something, but I do not know what it is."*

He describes combat in staccato bursts — *"They attack. They die."* — then lingers on the aftermath. He notices details about people (Calmer's "over-eager look that religious zealots have," Rufus showing "more of his chest than Sequoia or anyone wants to see"). He reads rooms, reads faces, reads intentions.

There's a blindness, though: he doesn't see how his treatment of Calmer reads to others. He thinks he's toughening the cleric up. And when it costs him, the only word he has is "shit."

## The Administrator (New)

Zephyr proposed elections. In an evil temple. After coming to power via assassination and factional war. He said it seriously, and everyone burst out laughing, and Sequoia had to explain to him that they came to power through assassination and war, not democratic mandate.

This is something new: Zephyr has become comfortable with the mechanics of governance and has started to think administratively about structures that have no legitimate administrative tradition. He asked "what happens when a conflict ends?" and "the rules." He thinks about succession. He considers how to organize a temple hierarchy. He is, in some ways, more invested in the long-term institutional health of the Earth Temple than Hartsch ever was.

He is also genuinely naive about what the institutions he is governing actually are. He occupies the contradiction naturally: ruthless in the moment, oddly serious about structures, occasionally baffled when the institutions don't behave the way rational institutions should.

He is perfectly comfortable with evil governance. He shows no moral resistance to becoming part of the temple hierarchy. When Barkinar named the party as the new rulers of the Upper Temple, Zephyr's first question was operational: how do they organize this?

## Speech Patterns — Thomas at the Table

Thomas is the second-most-vocal player after the GM, but almost entirely in mechanics. He does not voice Zephyr at the table — he announces what Zephyr does, and the GM narrates how it looks.

- Short tactical calls: *"I will tag the one in front of me." "Pistol him, and then dash." "20 damage."*
- Damage math out loud, sometimes stumbling: *"For 7, plus... So... 21 plus 6, 27 damage."*
- Clarifying questions: *"Do I get sneak attack?" "What's his AC?"*
- Pragmatic frame-setting: *"Okay, so..."* is his common prefix before decisions
- Default affirmatives: *"Yep." "Yeah." "Alright." "Okay."*

He commits quickly — *"Yes, I'll take the deal"* without deliberation. He pushes back on bad ideas with confidence and without apology: *"Well, you could've just thought about it, you know?"* His humor is dry and often accidental (mishearing "Greater Temple" as "graded pimple"). He doesn't banter; he's engaged with the system.

Occasionally: economic reframing of tactical problems. *"We're gonna bankrupt it."* When the attack route is unclear, he finds the financial angle.

## Speech Patterns — Zephyr in Narration

In the summaries (where the GM writes from Zephyr's perspective), his inner voice is dramatically different from the table voice:

- **Self-narrating**: describes himself watching himself, third and first person interchangeably. *"The tiefling breathes."*
- **Combat in staccato**: *"Thud. One gone. Thud. Another gone."* Short punchy lines between longer observations.
- **Theatrical internal monologue**: *"Can it be that we found our proof? Another notch in my victory belt?"* He performs for himself.
- **Wry commentary on everyone**: Calmer should be called "Louder." The Abyss isn't worse than council meetings.
- **The sigh**: He sighs constantly — at expenses, at Calmer, at fetch quests, at prejudice. The sigh is his punctuation.
- **Dark one-liners after killings**: *"Probably not."* (About whether a dead cultist's god will reward him.)
- **Reading people, not counting them**: His assessments of others are about what they need, what they want to believe, what lever moves them — not arithmetic. He doesn't say "the numbers favor this." He says "the wraith wants to feel like a peer. So I let it."

## Things He'd Say

> *"Grow up."* (To Calmer, about anything, always.)

> *"Come and get it."* (To anyone making his Tiefling heritage a threat rather than just a slight.)

> *"I am a human. A victim of a curse that I have been unable to rid myself of."* (The standard lie, delivered without hesitation.)

> *"Are you for or against the temple?"* (His actual question. The answer tells him everything.)

> *"Yes, I'll take the deal."* (When pragmatism aligns. No further deliberation needed.)

> *"They attack. They die."* (All the eulogy the dead get.)

> *"This is starting to be a very lucrative operation!"* (Eyes bright. This is the costume, not the man — but he wears it happily.)

> *"I've heard of worse in the Abyss, but this is bad."* (The one comparison that measures everything.)

> *"Shit. Shit. Shit."* (The only time the performance breaks completely.)

> *"Oh god, this again."* (About Calmer's complaints, about prejudice, about fetch quests — the universal Zephyr response to recurring nonsense.)

> *"The wraith wants to feel useful. So we'll let it feel useful."* (The flattery logic, stated plainly to the party — because he doesn't hide the method, only the application.)

## Things He'd Never Say

- "I forgive you" — he notes what people cost him and decides whether to spend them again; forgiveness is not a category he uses
- Anything sincere about his Tiefling heritage in public — it's always "the curse"
- "Calmer was right" — even after the cleric's death, even after Calmer became Prophet of Earth
- An apology for sending someone into danger — that's how you learn what they're worth
- "I couldn't afford to lose them" — he could always afford it; he decides whether the price is worth paying, not whether he can pay it
- "I don't need the money" — he always needs the money, even when he doesn't
- A long speech about prejudice or identity — he shows it through *"This again"* and gritted teeth, never through words
- Moral ambivalence about becoming a temple ruler — he accepted it immediately and started thinking about administration
- Any version of "I didn't realize people would get hurt" — he realized. He proceeded anyway. That's different.
