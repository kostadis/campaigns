# Zinnia (The Masked One) — Voice Notes
*Drafted from Chapters 1-10 and the March 29th VTT session. Edit freely — you know him better than the document does.*

---

## The Core Thing

Zinnia is a professional spy wearing a mask — literally and figuratively. He approaches every situation as an intelligence operation. Where others see a village, he sees overlapping networks of competing agendas. Where others see a conversation, he sees an information extraction opportunity. He doesn't think about good and evil; he thinks about leverage, information asymmetry, and operational security.

The key to his voice: he is always working. Even when he appears to be doing nothing — sitting in a tavern, walking through a village, standing over a dead body — he is observing, cataloguing, and calculating. Rest is surveillance. Small talk is interrogation. Kindness is a cover.

## The Analyst

Zinnia's mind works by connecting dots that nobody else sees. When he sits in a tavern listening to gossip, he doesn't hear idle chatter — he hears intelligence:

- A woman complains about a missing cleric? *"Distrust of central authority, and prone to conspiratorial thinking. Good."*
- A farmer rants about Burne and Rufus? Zinnia synthesizes it instantly: *"The locals see the threat of being assimilated by Verbobonc as bigger than the temple. It's probably related to their allegiance to the god Beory and Verbobonc's theocracy."*

He sees political structures, power dynamics, and faction alignment where everyone else sees village drama. He delivers these analyses to his companions as briefings, not observations.

## The Professional Frustration

Zinnia is surrounded by amateurs, and it grinds at him. Calmer can't stay quiet. Zephyr takes unnecessary risks. Sequoia is competent but impatient. And they march into enemy strongholds without disguises or cover stories.

*"This amateur-hour infiltration of the temple has got to stop."*

He doesn't rage about it. He sighs, glares, and quietly fixes things. When Calmer almost gets killed for the third time, Zinnia doesn't lecture — he mutters to himself that this won't ingratiate them with St. Cuthbert's clergy and moves on. The frustration is expressed through internal commentary, not confrontation.

## The Mask

His face is disfigured — disease and violence have left him behind a mask. This is both literal and metaphorical. He reveals nothing. When the party discovers a torture chamber with recent victims, *"the spy betrays no emotion. No reaction."* He has seen worse, and he says so — but only to himself.

He doesn't share his past. When a stakeout reminds him of a previous operation: *"This reminds me of the time I was. Well, that's a secret mission. And maybe I shouldn't think about it."*

He withholds by instinct. Information is currency, and he doesn't spend it casually.

## Tactical Patience

Zinnia's signature move is the stakeout — the long wait, the careful observation, the patience to let a target reveal itself. He tracks blood trails, notices oiled hinges in supposedly abandoned dungeons, spots loaded dice mid-fall, reads the freshness of torture chamber wounds.

When the party needs to find a spy in the workers' encampment, Zinnia's plan isn't to interrogate — it's to spook the mouse and follow it to its hole. *"I know how to stay quiet, patient, and wait."*

He thinks in predator-prey metaphors: mice, cats, rabbit holes. People are assets to be run, targets to be tracked, or obstacles to be managed.

## The Pragmatist

Zinnia doesn't trust other spies. When a gnome spy offers to join them, Zinnia immediately declines. He doesn't trust anyone whose operational loyalty hasn't been tested. He fake-converts to St. Cuthbert because it gives him access. He wears cult robes because disguise is tradecraft. He lies to allies — Burne, Rufus, Jaroo — not out of malice but because information control is reflexive.

When Sequoia lies to the Duke about what they found, Zinnia approves. *"There is no need for him to know."*

## The Quiet Violence

Zinnia fights in flurries — monk fists, precise and efficient. He doesn't narrate his combat; he narrates his analysis of the tactical situation. *"If I kill the mooks, the captain will run. If I kill the captain, the mooks will run."* The violence itself gets a sentence; the reasoning gets a paragraph.

He watches interrogations with satisfaction: *"I love it when the mouse realizes it's alive only because the cat wants to play."*

## Speech Patterns — George at the Table

George (the player) is economical with words. At the table, he speaks in:
- Short tactical fragments: "Dead." "24." "I'll take him." "What's his armor class?"
- Brief confirmations: "Yep." "Okay." "Sure, yeah."
- Questions about mechanics before acting: "Can I do bonus action?" "What are the options?"

He rarely makes jokes or narrative commentary. He is the quietest player at the table — focused, efficient, and engaged with positioning rather than banter. When he does speak in character, it's a decisive statement, not a discussion.

## Speech Patterns — Zinnia in Narration

In the summaries (where the GM writes from Zinnia's perspective), his inner voice is:
- Analytical and detached: observations delivered as intelligence assessments
- Italicized internal monologue: *"Gotcha." "There must be a secret door somewhere here."*
- Terse with companions, expansive in analysis: he'll give Spugnoir one word ("Tomorrow") but deliver a multi-sentence political briefing to the party
- Occasional dark humor, always dry: *"The last ribbit"* (about a dead frog)

He uses spy vocabulary naturally: "infiltration," "operational security," "agents," "the mouse," "cover," "leverage."

## Things He'd Say

> *"We need information."* (His default opening position on any situation.)

> *"This amateur-hour infiltration has got to stop."* (About the party's approach to anything.)

> *"I don't trust other spies."*

> *"There are so many groups with different agendas. The layers and complexities of these conspiracies are surprising for such a small town."*

> *"We will wear these."* (Picking up cult robes — no discussion, just tradecraft.)

> *"No, I don't think that's necessary."* (Declining help from anyone he hasn't vetted.)

> *"Gotcha."* (Finding a blood trail, a secret door, a loaded die.)

> *"Everyone in this town wants to be told first."*

> *"I know how to stay quiet, patient, and wait."*

## Things He'd Never Say

- Anything about his past in specific terms
- An emotional reaction to violence or suffering (he's seen worse)
- "I trust them" about anyone outside the immediate party
- Small talk — every conversation has a purpose
- An apology for withholding information
- "We should tell everyone" — information is shared on a need-to-know basis, always
