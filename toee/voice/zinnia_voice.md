# Zinnia (The Masked One) — Voice Notes
*Rebuilt from Chapters 1–28 and four VTT sessions (March–May 2026). Edit freely — you know him better than the document does.*

---

## The Core Thing

Zinnia is a monk and a spy — and the thing that makes him effective at both is the same: he perceives. He walks into a room and the political atmosphere registers before he could explain how he knows. He sits in a tavern and hears not gossip but position: who defers to whom, who's afraid, who's been given bad information and doesn't know it yet. He notices the detail that doesn't fit. He tracks it. Then he acts.

This is not intelligence — his mind is not analytical in the systematic sense. It is *wisdom*. He reads people and situations the way a weather-reader reads clouds: not by reasoning through it, but by pattern recognition trained deep enough to feel like instinct. The reads are reliable. He trusts them.

The mask has become the face. He doesn't perform the Obsidian Edge — he is it. When he sits in silence across from Kelno and the room fills with menace, that's not a technique. When he tells the Broken Blades *"whatever your plans are, you are with us,"* he means it. He has been undercover so long that the cover and the person are the same thing now, and this is not a problem for him. It's a strength.

## The Perceiver

What Zinnia does at the tavern is not analysis. He's not reasoning to conclusions — he's arriving at them. A woman's grip on her cup, the angle a farmer leans away from the name "Verbobonc," a guard's eyes going to the door before answering a question: these things land in him as finished readings, not as inputs to a process.

- A woman complains about a missing cleric? *"Distrust of central authority, and prone to conspiratorial thinking. Good."* He didn't think that through. He saw it.
- A farmer rants about Burne and Rufus? *"The locals see the threat of being assimilated by Verbobonc as bigger than the temple."* That's not synthesis — that's recognition.

He sees political atmosphere shift before others know something has changed. He noticed the door that opened by itself in the Hall of Trophies — Perception, not Investigation. He reads the prisoner: *he's telling the truth about the Deep Temple passage.* Insight, not interrogation.

He keeps track of details the way a monk keeps track of breath: not by trying, but because the attention is always there. Who moved. Who didn't. What was said. What conspicuously wasn't. He doesn't write this down. He doesn't need to.

## The Stillness

His most powerful weapon is silence. When the party sat with Kelno, Zinnia said nothing — and the room described him as sitting "menacingly." Kelno, who had been watching two weeks of temple war from hidden passages, responded to the silence with respect. No Charisma roll. Just presence.

This is not a performance. He doesn't cultivate stillness as a technique; it's what's left when everything unnecessary has been removed. He waits, he observes, and then he moves — and when he moves, *"every step was a decision made in the body before the mind caught up."*

He is also, physically, impossible to miss when seen. Six foot six, very white, blond elf — a distinctive figure in every room he enters. This is not a liability he resents; it's a variable he accounts for. He moves through spaces where he won't be seen (Cloak of Elvenkind, Stealth +7), and when he is seen, he controls what the seeing communicates. The stillness and the size work together: a tall, silent elf who watches you from across the room is not someone you forget.

His spy vocabulary surfaces naturally: "infiltration," "operational security," "leverage," "the mouse." These aren't affectations — they're just the right words for the things he's thinking about.

## The Conviction

There is one mode where the detachment breaks: when the situation calls for someone to mean it.

When the Broken Blades were traumatized — massacred, scattered, broken by Hartsch's betrayal — Zinnia rallied them. Not with strategy, not with a reading of the situation. With presence: *"I don't know what your plans are. But whatever your plans are, you are with us. We are with you."* The mercenaries pledged loyalty not to orders but to conviction.

This is Zinnia's tell. He cannot fake that. The operational persona can maintain cover through anything, but when he needs to give someone a reason to keep going, he reaches past the mask and the result is genuine. He is chaotic good underneath all of it — he has values that pull against the work sometimes, and when they pull hard enough, the pull wins. He doesn't do it often. When he does, it lands.

## The Mask (That Became the Face)

Zinnia doesn't struggle with his cover identity. He inhabits it so completely that the Water Temple guards heard a calm, authoritative voice deliver news of Alrrem's assassination and opened the door. The DM granted surprise *"for the quality of the roleplay."* That wasn't performance. That was professionalism.

And then, once the door opened and the party struck, he announced: *"We are the Obsidian Edge."* No hesitation. No transition. The deception served its purpose; the identity was the truth.

He has an acting background — a hobby from childhood, now a professional tool. He's good at it not because he's trained at deception but because he understands how people want to be seen, and he reflects that. The same perceptive machinery that reads rooms lets him give a performance that fits the audience precisely.

He doesn't share his past. When a stakeout reminds him of a previous operation: *"This reminds me of the time I was. Well, that's a secret mission. And maybe I shouldn't think about it."* He withholds by instinct — information is currency. He doesn't spend it casually. He trusts his spy leader (Augen Trust) more than he trusts anyone else in the world, including the party. That loyalty runs deep and is never discussed.

## The Professional Frustration

Zinnia is surrounded by amateurs, and it grinds at him. Calmer can't stay quiet. Zephyr takes unnecessary risks. Sequoia is competent but impatient. And they march into enemy strongholds without disguises or cover stories.

*"This amateur-hour infiltration of the temple has got to stop."*

He doesn't rage about it. He notes the problem, works around it, and moves on. The frustration shows as internal commentary, not confrontation — he doesn't teach, he compensates.

## The Movement

Zinnia fights like a Drunken Master: apparently unpredictable, then suddenly precise. He doesn't move with the economy of someone counting steps — he flows, disengages, reorients, strikes from an angle the enemy hadn't tracked. The chaos is a technique; the control underneath it is the actual skill.

His combat thinking is outcome-based and tactical, not sequential. *"If I kill the mooks, the captain will run. If I kill the captain, the mooks will run."* He decides the endpoint first, then moves toward it. When the endpoint is reached — when the target drops, when the breach is made — he steps back. He doesn't need the last blow. The execution was the point.

His most revealing combat moment is the Belsornig assassination: first-round strikes dealing 45 points of damage, reducing the Water Temple prophet to 1 hit point — and then he *stepped back.* Let Zephyr take the kill with the Dagger of Venom. Attribution was Zephyr's. The work was his.

He takes a critical hit with zero visible reaction. He adjusts. He continues. The narrative records the impact; it does not record distress.

## Speech Patterns — George at the Table

George is the quietest player at the table. Speaks roughly 5–10% as much as the other players, almost entirely in mechanics. No in-character voice for Zinnia. No narration. Pure function.

- Short tactical declarations: *"I'll take him." "I'll charge this guy." "I'll go behind the steam."*
- Mechanical questions: *"What's his armor class?" "Can I do bonus action?" "How far am I from—"*
- Single-word responses: *"Yep." "Dead." "Good." "True." "Word."*
- His one strategic moment: *"We have to be suspicious of him."* (About Kelno — direct and correct.)

He doesn't banter. He doesn't riff on narration. He doesn't play Zinnia's personality at the table — he plays Zinnia's actions and lets the GM's narration carry everything else. The inner voice of Zinnia is almost entirely a GM construction from the raw material of George's choices.

## Speech Patterns — Zinnia in Narration

In the summaries written from Zinnia's perspective, his inner voice is:
- Perceptive and terse: observations stated as finished reads, not reasoned conclusions
- Italicized internal monologue: *"Gotcha." "There must be a secret door somewhere here."* Things arrive, they don't develop.
- Terse with companions, expansive when he's tracking something: he'll give Spugnoir one word ("Tomorrow") but spend three sentences on what a room's arrangement tells him
- Occasional dry humor: *"The last ribbit"* (about a dead frog)
- Absent from scenes where he's purely in motion — when Zinnia is acting, the prose describes the body, not the mind

He uses spy vocabulary naturally: "infiltration," "operational security," "agents," "the mouse," "cover," "leverage." Not affectation — these are just the right words.

What he does NOT do: reason through to a conclusion step by step. He arrives. "I knew" not "I realized." "I read" not "I deduced." "I noticed" not "I determined." The cognitive mode is perception, not analysis.

## Things He'd Say

> *"We need information."* (His default opening position on any situation.)

> *"This amateur-hour infiltration has got to stop."* (About the party's approach to anything.)

> *"I don't know what your plans are. But whatever your plans are, you are with us. We are with you."* (When he means it — rare, and landing.)

> *"We are the Obsidian Edge."* (After the bluff has served its purpose. No ceremony.)

> *"This reminds me of the time I was. Well, that's a secret mission."* (His version of nostalgia.)

> *"Gotcha."* (Finding a blood trail, a secret door, a door that opened by itself.)

> *"We will wear these."* (Picking up cult robes — no discussion, just tradecraft.)

> *"I know how to stay quiet, patient, and wait."*

## Things He'd Never Say

- Anything about his past in specific terms — it's always "a secret mission" and he moves on
- An emotional reaction to violence or suffering out loud — he processes internally, not aloud
- "I trust them" about anyone outside the immediate party (and even inside it, provisionally)
- Small talk — every conversation has an operational purpose
- "I needed that kill" — he steps back when stepping back is right
- An apology for withholding information — information is shared on need-to-know, always
- Anything framed as "I reasoned that" or "I deduced" — he perceived it, he noticed it, he read it
