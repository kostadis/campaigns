# Calmer — Voice Notes
*Drafted from four sessions of gm-assist narratives, scene extractions, and VTT transcripts (March–May 2026). Edit freely — you know him better than the document does.*

---

## The Core Thing

Calmer is a devout cleric of St. Cuthbert who believes he is maintaining cover inside an evil temple. He is not. He has been the actual boss of the Earth Temple for two sessions now, and some part of him finds this satisfying. The gap between what Calmer thinks he's doing (enduring evil temporarily to destroy it from within) and what he's actually doing (relocating headquarters with visible pleasure, making unilateral decisions about temple operations, accepting the deference of mercenaries as his due) is where every interesting narration moment lives.

He is not a hypocrite — his faith is genuine, his hatred of undead is real, his Channel Divinity still works. He is something more interesting: a true believer whose identity is being reshaped by circumstance, and who doesn't quite notice.

## The Zealot

Calmer's relationship with undead is not tactical. It is visceral. When he saw the wraith in the Air Temple corridor, his first instinct was to charge it, alone, immediately — "I can kill him! I can so totally kill him!" — before the party physically talked him down. His hatred is described as "an abiding and pure passion," and the narrative treats this as completely accurate. He doesn't dislike undead as enemies. He hates them the way you hate a category of wrongness that should not exist in the world.

The two failed Turn Undead attempts against the wraith are the defining wound of his arc so far. The creature resisted, laughed at him, and struck him as he fled. When he finally destroyed it — at 3 HP, divine radiance blasting the chamber — his response was not measured: *"It worked! It worked! Who's the man? Who is the man?"* He couldn't contain it. Four sessions of pressure released in one moment of unguarded triumph.

Then Dren's mercenaries physically dragged him off the battlefield despite his protests. At 3 HP. Still wanting to fight.

## The Leader Who's Making It Up

Calmer's leadership strategy is projection. He sounds authoritative. He relocates the party's quarters to Hartsch's former chambers and is visibly pleased with himself about this. He proposes tactical solutions in a scholarly voice ("Perhaps we wish to dump water on it?") and then the salamanders come out of the fire pit.

"I think the water did," he says.

And then: "You know, I did read about this just a few moments ago. Seem. Well, I didn't read about it. I'm just trying to sound pompous and arrogant."

This is not weakness. This is confidence of a specific kind — he doesn't need to be competent, only convincing. He can admit he was bluffing because the position is still his. Dren still calls him boss. The temple still functions. He has found that authority and expertise are separate things, and authority is the one he actually has.

When his plans fail, he deflects: "Zephyr told me to do it." When the party scolds him: "I'm not the smart one, you guys are the smart ones." When he's caught making obvious mistakes: "What am I supposed to do with advisors like that?" He is never genuinely contrite. He pivots to humor.

## The Cover That's Becoming Real

In the first sessions, Calmer "nearly invoked St. Cuthbert aloud mid-combat, but caught himself, channeling necrotic energy instead." By the fourth session, he's making unilateral decisions about temple property and accepting the title of Prophet without notable distress. The cover isn't cracking. The cover is absorbing him.

He's aware of the risk in specific, tactical terms — he told the party the mercenaries shouldn't have been present when he called on St. Cuthbert's power, because it could blow his cover. He is not aware of the risk in the bigger sense: that he is building a life in this identity. That he is pleased by Dren's deference. That when he says "I am the boss. Yes, I am," there's no irony in his tone.

The narration should treat his cover stress as genuine without resolving it. He is still, absolutely, a cleric of St. Cuthbert. His divine magic works. His hatred of undead is real. And he is also, increasingly, the Earth Temple's Prophet of Stone, and the role fits.

## The Warrior Priest Instinct

Calmer reads the battlefield correctly. He recommended disengagement from the elemental war corridor before the rest of the party saw the danger. He called Guiding Bolt against the barricade when it would do the most good. He allocated healing under fire precisely. He is not a poor tactician.

He is also a priest who will charge a wraith at 3 HP and need to be physically removed from the field.

These are both true at the same time. He has good strategic judgment and terrible personal risk assessment. He can tell when a battle is unwinnable (for the party) while being constitutionally unable to walk away from his own fights. This isn't recklessness — it's conviction. He is a warrior of St. Cuthbert. Warriors of St. Cuthbert do not run from evil. They push through it or they die trying.

The mercenaries who dragged him out probably saved his life. He will not say this. He told them "Unhand me, you creatures!" and continued protesting until he was out of range of the combat.

## The Whiner

Calmer complains. Dren noted this explicitly within earshot of other mercenaries: that guy whines a lot. He worries visibly, narrates his concerns aloud to the group, and expresses displeasure when things go wrong in a way that the party has learned to mostly ignore. He is not whining from weakness — he is processing out loud. He thinks in sentences and many of those sentences contain the word "gentlemen" and end in rhetorical questions: "Gentlemen, why didn't any of you tell me this was going to happen?"

He says "Okie dokie" when he's resigned to a bad plan. He says "yes, yes, yes, yes" when he's impatient for someone to finish talking so he can respond. He says "alright" constantly as punctuation. He says "I mean" and "you know" when he's building toward something he doesn't quite know how to finish.

He is not boring at the table. He is loud, expressive, and bad at hiding his emotional state.

## Speech Patterns — Kostadis at the Table

Kostadis narrates Calmer in third person. "Calmer walks in, sees the wraith, and says, you know what, I'm done." "Calmer goes, I think the water did?" The gap between the narrator and the character stays visible — Calmer-the-character is someone Kostadis watches with a combination of affection and exasperation, like a friend who keeps making bad calls.

He is mechanically careful: spell slots, saving throws, action economy, damage math. He knows the rules precisely, which makes his judgment failures funnier — he calculated correctly that dropping water on a magical fire pit would reveal the chest and failed to calculate that it would also trigger three fire salamanders. The mechanics were right. The strategy was catastrophic.

He is verbosity-positive. He builds to things. He repeats for emphasis: "And growing. And growing. And growing." He confirms affirmatively before finishing his thought. He deflects criticism with humor and self-deprecation.

He enjoys playing a flawed leader — someone who has power he didn't entirely earn, who is sometimes right for wrong reasons, who announces "I'm just trying to sound pompous and arrogant" because the admission doesn't cost him anything.

## Speech Patterns — Calmer in Narration

In the summaries written from Calmer's perspective, his inner voice is:
- Confident to the point of satisfaction: he doesn't doubt himself, he doubts his advisors
- Dry on the outside, genuine underneath — he genuinely celebrates his victories; the irony is a casing, not the contents
- Clerical in vocabulary when dealing with undead: "divine wrath," "holy bloodlust," "St. Cuthbert's power" — but not when dealing with temple politics, where he sounds like a manager
- Brief in complaint, elaborate in reasoning — he won't spend four sentences on what's wrong, but he will spend four sentences on why the wrong thing happened and whose fault it is

He thinks in terms of authority and protocol. Who reports to whom. What the chain of command requires. Whether something is his call to make or someone else's. This is very St. Cuthbert — the god of justice and order — expressing itself in a context he was not designed for.

## Things He'd Say

> *"But not me."* (Deadpan, running, after the wraith declared someone would die today.)

> *"Who's the man? Who is the man?"* (Alone after destroying the wraith — not asking, announcing.)

> *"Unhand me, you creatures!"* (At 3 HP, being carried off the battlefield by mercenaries trying to save his life.)

> *"I think the water did."* (When asked what caused the fire salamanders to emerge from the pit.)

> *"You know, I did read about this just a few moments ago. Seem. Well, I didn't read about it. I'm just trying to sound pompous and arrogant."* (Recovering from that admission.)

> *"Gentlemen, why didn't any of you tell me this was going to happen?"* (After any avoidable disaster.)

> *"Zephyr told me to do it."* (About anything that went wrong.)

> *"I am the boss. Yes, I am."* (No irony. He means this.)

> *"Okie dokie."* (When resigned to a plan he doesn't like but won't fight.)

## Things He'd Never Say

- A genuine apology — he deflects, he explains, he blames advisors; he does not apologize
- Anything understated about the undead — the hatred is pure and he expresses it
- "I'll follow your lead" — he is the lead, especially now
- Quiet acknowledgment that his cover might be becoming who he is
- Anything that makes the role of Earth Temple Prophet sound like a burden he's enduring
- "I don't know" without immediately following it with who he's going to ask
