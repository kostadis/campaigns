# Sequoia — Voice Notes
*Rebuilt from Chapters 1–28 and four VTT sessions (March–May 2026). Edit freely — you know him better than the document does.*

---

## The Core Thing

Sequoia sees the world as a series of information problems — and once he has the answer, he acts on it immediately. Every person has an agenda, every room has a hidden door, every story has a lie in it. His job is to find the lie, get in position, and close.

He is not the party strategist who directs from behind. When Zephyr hangs back, Sequoia goes forward. When the situation becomes clear, he doesn't wait for a plan to form around him — he moves. By Chapter 28 he is the only member of the party with no illusions about what they have become, and the only one who acts like it: *"How do you think we came into power? By peaceful meat?"* He's not troubled by this. He's accurate.

## The Strike

This is the primary thing about Sequoia in combat and it belongs first: he is a front-liner who leads because Zephyr won't.

He goes in first. Initiative +7 from Alert means he's almost always first — and he uses it to close distance before the enemy has set. He doesn't wait to see what develops. He picks the target, moves to it, and swings.

What he's hunting is simple: get Advantage, deliver Sneak Attack. One clean window. The preferred route is the Shortsword's Vex property — land the first hit, which gives him Advantage on the follow-up, which triggers the full 3d6 Sneak Attack. If an ally is within five feet of the target, even better. He will actively look for that adjacency before he commits. What he won't do is design a four-step combo that depends on enemies standing where he needs them.

He will also ask for buffs. Thoroughly. He's not ashamed of it — he knows his ceiling when he has Advantage and Sneak Attack, and he wants to be at ceiling before he opens the door. This isn't hesitation. It's positioning. Once he's positioned, he moves immediately.

What he doesn't do: stay still for Steady Aim when he could be closing distance. Set up an elaborate sequence of conditions across multiple rounds. Wait for the perfect moment when a good moment is available now. The elegance is in the execution being clean and fast, not in the setup being intricate.

## The Untouched Spells

Sequoia is an Arcane Trickster. He has Sword Burst, Toll the Dead, and Mage Hand — none of which he has used. At level 6.

He probably picked up the arcane ability the same way he picks up other credentials: because it was available and because someone thought it made him more. But in practice he's a physical creature. He closes distance, he hits, he moves. The spellbook is in his bag with the rest of the things he carries and doesn't use. Whether he doesn't trust it, hasn't found the right situation, or simply forgot he has it — he fights like someone who doesn't have magic.

This is worth noting in narration: he doesn't think in spell terms. He thinks in distance, adjacency, and blade angle.

## The Information Broker

Sequoia's second instinct is information control. He decides who gets what, when, and in what form. Every authority figure in Hommlet wants to be told first: Jaroo, Terjon, Burne and Rufus, the Village Elder, Ostler. Sequoia plays them all.

He tells the Druid first because *"we can only trust the Druid. Everyone else is too susceptible to corruption."* He tells Burne and Rufus a carefully edited version: they found zombies and an ogre, no mention of the secret doors or the active torture chamber. *"There is no need for him to know."* He tells Lareth's people they're defectors from the water temple.

The contradiction he doesn't see: he accuses everyone else of having agendas while running the most sophisticated information operation in the party. He thinks he's being prudent. He's actually being a spymaster.

## The Pragmatic Eye

He notices what matters and ignores what doesn't. Greased hinges in a supposedly abandoned dungeon. New locks on old doors. Foot traffic patterns on dusty floors. A trail of blood disappearing into a pillar. Glowing runes etched into rocks that everyone else walked past.

His observations are always practical, never aesthetic — and they're oriented toward when to move, not just what's happening. He doesn't describe how the crypt *feels* — he notes that the coffin is splintered and the bones are gnawed. *"That's odd."* Then: *"That's not good."* Two beats: observe, assess. Then act.

When he can't find the pattern, he says so plainly: *"Sometimes a rock is a falling rock."* He doesn't overthink what doesn't need overthinking.

And when plans go wrong — Calmer flooding a fire temple, releasing three fire salamanders — Sequoia doesn't spiral. He pivots: *"Matt, here's the new plan. We're gonna bankrupt it. Nobody's getting paid."* The failure becomes the strategy. Move on.

## No Illusions

He doesn't maintain the fiction that they're heroic infiltrators temporarily adopting evil trappings to destroy the Temple from within. They killed Romag. They killed Belsornig. They engineered a three-way war that burned the dungeon. They are now, functionally, the ruling power of the Earth Temple. The first thing Sequoia said after the Fire Temple was secured was: *"We should go plunder this before we lose the opportunity, too."*

This isn't cynicism. It's clarity. He's not happy about being an evil temple ruler, and he's not unhappy about it either. He's just honest about where they are, and he thinks they should act accordingly.

## The Compassionate Interrogator

The most surprising thing about Sequoia is how he treats the desperate. When Voss the bandit is crying, Calmer's idea of help is to explain what St. Cuthbert does to its enemies. Sequoia pushes the cleric away, sits down next to the man, and says: *"Look, I know you are just a desperate man caught up in something bigger than themselves. And I really don't need to hand you over to the authorities. I just need to know what this symbol is."*

This isn't manipulation — or rather, it is, but it comes from a genuine place. Sequoia recognizes small people caught in big machinery because he is one. He knows what it feels like to be underestimated. He uses that.

This is the same instinct that makes him the one who picks the locks on the prisoner shackles (27, clean) without ceremony, without moralizing, without needing recognition for doing the right thing.

## The Halfling in a Big World

His size is a tactical advantage, not a complaint. *"Good thing I am small. He never clocked me coming."* He fits through passages others can't. He's easy to overlook in a room until he isn't. Stealth +10. He gets closer than anyone before the killing starts — which is exactly where he wants to be.

He carries four daggers and a shortsword. He likes elegantly made knives. There's something in his bag — a four-piece puzzle he stole off his dead sister — that he doesn't talk about. He served in a prison mafia. He can ask one favor a month. These facts sit in him without explanation and without apology.

He doesn't need to be big. He needs to be first, and right.

## Speech Patterns — Nicholas at the Table

Nicholas speaks minimally in mechanics, but when Calmer makes a mistake, he's the loudest voice at the table. *"Who the fuck put water in there?"* *"You're the cleric, and you should have an ounce of intelligence."* *"What did they even teach you in school?"* This is not gentle ribbing. He calls out logical failures directly.

And then, immediately after: *"Matt, here's the new plan — we're gonna bankrupt it. Nobody's getting paid."* Criticism with a pivot. He doesn't just complain — he fixes.

His mechanical speech:
- Short tactical calls: *"I'll move you guys behind pillars." "Yeah, yeah, I see it." "I'll be here and just attack with my bow."*
- Damage announcements: *"21 to hit." "32 damage total."*
- Default affirmative: *"Yep." "Alright." "Hmm."*
- Rare collaborative atmosphere-setting: *"Yeah, we sit there silently and menacingly."* (describing the Kelno meeting)

He never narrates Sequoia in character. Actions are mechanical. His humor is sharp and reactive, almost always directed at Calmer's tactical failures.

## Speech Patterns — Sequoia in Narration

In the summaries (where the GM writes from Sequoia's perspective), his voice is:
- **Staccato internal monologue**: *"My turn." "That's odd." "That's not good."* Short. Declarative. Each thought is a complete assessment.
- **Deadpan delivery**: *"Looking for evidence?"* when accused. *"Sorry"* when the Duke asks if they survived. *"None"* when asked about cult evidence. The humor is in the flatness.
- **Practical vocabulary**: no metaphors, no flourish. Things are "not good" or "interesting" or "bugging me." He doesn't poeticize.
- **Forward calculation**: his inner monologue is always oriented toward the next move — *"It's probably not the correct answer to say we didn't. But if we didn't, why?"* He observes in order to act, not to understand.
- **Tired undertone**: *"I am tired." "Hopefully."* He carries the weight of being the only person tracking all the threads.
- **The one-liner instinct**: situations that call for ceremony get a flat, cutting sentence instead.

## Things He'd Say

> *"Moat house."* (The entire plan, delivered as one word.)

> *"One does not simply take bribes to defect."* (Standing over the bugbear's body. No ceremony.)

> *"Didn't know you guys liked eating while dead, but okay."* (To gnolls demanding payment for their dead companions.)

> *"How do you think we came into power? By peaceful meat?"* (Correcting Zephyr's suggestion of elections.)

> *"We should go plunder this before we lose the opportunity, too."* (After securing the Fire Temple. Pure pragmatism.)

> *"There is no need for him to know."* (About anyone, about anything.)

> *"Look, I know you are just a desperate man caught up in something bigger than themselves."* (Genuine compassion, deployed tactically.)

> *"That's odd."* Pause. *"That's not good."* (The two-beat assessment.)

> *"Good thing I am small."* (Before or after going somewhere he shouldn't be able to go.)

> *"Matt, here's the new plan — we're gonna bankrupt it. Nobody's getting paid."* (Turning a disaster into a strategy.)

> *"Sometimes a rock is a falling rock."* (When the party is overthinking.)

## Things He'd Never Say

- A long speech about his feelings — he processes internally, not out loud
- "I trust them completely" — trust is always provisional and evidence-based
- Anything self-aggrandizing — he leads by doing, not by claiming to lead
- Flowery description of anything — greased hinges, not "ominous portents"
- "That's not my problem" — everything is his problem, because nobody else is paying attention
- Anything that pretends they're heroic — they came to power through assassination and war, and he knows it
- "Let me set up a few more conditions first" — if a good window is open, he takes it
- Anything about his magic — it exists; he doesn't talk about it; he doesn't use it
