# Sequoia — Voice Notes
*Drafted from Chapters 1-14 and the March 29th VTT session. Edit freely — you know him better than the document does.*

---

## The Core Thing

Sequoia sees the world as a series of information problems. Every person has an agenda, every room has a hidden door, every story has a lie in it. His job is to find the lie, decide who to tell, and control who knows what. He is not paranoid — he is correct. Everyone in Hommlet really does have a competing agenda, and Sequoia is the one person who tracks all of them simultaneously. This makes him the de facto leader of the party, but he leads by doing, not by declaring. He won't say "I'm in charge." He'll just say "Moat house" and start walking.

## The Information Broker

Sequoia's deepest instinct is information control. He doesn't just gather intelligence — he decides who gets it, when, and in what form. Every authority figure in Hommlet wants to be told first: Jaroo, Terjon, Burne and Rufus, the Village Elder, Ostler. Sequoia plays them all.

He tells the Druid first because *"we can only trust the Druid. Everyone else is too susceptible to corruption."* He tells Burne and Rufus a carefully edited version: they found zombies and an ogre, no mention of the secret doors or the active torture chamber. *"There is no need for him to know."* He tells Lareth's people they're defectors from the water temple. He deadpans to the Duke: *"Looking for evidence?"*

The contradiction he doesn't see: he accuses everyone else of having agendas while running the most sophisticated information operation in the party. He thinks he's being prudent. He's actually being a spymaster.

## The Pragmatic Eye

Sequoia notices what matters and ignores what doesn't. Greased hinges in a supposedly abandoned dungeon. New locks on old doors. Foot traffic patterns on dusty floors. A trail of blood disappearing into a pillar. Glowing runes etched into rocks that everyone else walked past.

His observations are always practical, never aesthetic. He doesn't describe how the crypt *feels* — he notes that the coffin is splintered and the bones are gnawed. *"That's odd."* Then: *"That's not good."* Two beats: observe, assess. That's his rhythm.

When he can't find the pattern, he says so plainly: *"Sometimes a rock is a falling rock."* He doesn't overthink what doesn't need overthinking.

## The Reluctant Leader

Sequoia never asked to be in charge. He leads because nobody else will do it right. Calmer is too dramatic. Zephyr is too impulsive. Zinnia is brilliant but operates solo. So Sequoia says "Moat house" and everyone follows. He says "Calmer, do your thing" and the cleric turns undead. He says "Let's go" and the meeting is over.

His leadership style is terse delegation: put the right person in the right spot and get out of the way. *"Alright, Calmer, do your thing."* *"Zephyr, let's go."* *"Keep him alive. We want to interrogate him."* He doesn't explain his reasoning unless pressed. If you're competent, you'll figure it out. If you're not, he'll roll his eyes and do it himself.

He is quietly exhausted by the politics. *"I am tired."* The village council meetings bore him. Festival planning terrifies him. *"Gotta go!"* He'd rather fight zombies than attend a committee meeting about decorations.

## The Compassionate Interrogator

The most surprising thing about Sequoia is how he treats the desperate. When Voss the bandit is crying, Calmer's idea of help is to explain what St. Cuthbert does to its enemies. Sequoia pushes the cleric away, sits down next to the man, and says: *"Look, I know you are just a desperate man caught up in something bigger than themselves. And I really don't need to hand you over to the authorities. I just need to know what this symbol is."*

This isn't manipulation — or rather, it is, but it comes from a genuine place. Sequoia recognizes small people caught in big machinery because he is one. He's a halfling in a world of human-sized politics, gnome-sized furniture, and ogre-sized threats. He knows what it feels like to be underestimated. He uses that.

## The Halfling in a Big World

His size is never a complaint — it's a tactical advantage. *"Good thing I am small."* He fits through passages others can't. He's easy to overlook. He notices things at ground level. But the world constantly reminds him of the mismatch: gnome taverns where even he feels cramped, human-scale politics where nobody thinks to consult the halfling, combat where the enemies literally look over his head.

He compensates with competence. A 27 on lockpicking. A 25 on initiative. Sneak attack damage that makes enemies stop looking over his head. He doesn't need to be big. He needs to be right.

## Speech Patterns — Nicholas at the Table

Nicholas is the quietest player at the table by a significant margin. His dialogue consists almost entirely of:
- Single-word confirmations: "Yep." "Yeah." "Right." "Okay."
- Mechanical declarations: "21 to hit." "32 damage total." "27."
- Terse tactical decisions: "I'll be here and just attack with my bow."
- Clarifying questions about rules: "Could Filbert be there for Sneak Attack?" "What type of trap?"

He never narrates in character during play. He engages with the game as a system — positioning, damage calculations, skill checks. When he does speak beyond mechanics, it's dry and brief: *"It ain't... oh, it is dead."* He processes the game faster than he speaks, resulting in frequent incomplete sentences and rapid-fire corrections: *"That's not... that's, 6 points of damage, just rolled my sneak attack again."*

His humor is deadpan and meta: "Dad, order food" (to the GM, his father). He tested Zoom's AI mid-session and was genuinely delighted when it worked. He's engaged, but his engagement is with the system, not the storytelling.

## Speech Patterns — Sequoia in Narration

In the summaries (where the GM writes from Sequoia's perspective), his voice is:
- **Staccato internal monologue**: *"My turn." "That's odd." "That's not good."* Short. Declarative. Each thought is a complete assessment.
- **Deadpan delivery**: "Looking for evidence?" when accused. "Sorry" when the Duke asks if they survived. "None" when asked about cult evidence. The humor is in the flatness.
- **Practical vocabulary**: no metaphors, no flourish. Things are "not good" or "interesting" or "bugging me." He doesn't poeticize.
- **Strategic narration**: his inner monologue is always calculating — *"It's probably not the correct answer to say we didn't. But if we didn't, why? I know — these guys are convinced this is just some agent of some other government. I'll pretend that I agree."*
- **Tired undertone**: "I am tired." "Hopefully." Sequoia carries the weight of being the only person tracking all the threads, and it shows.

## Things He'd Say

> *"Moat house."* (The entire plan, delivered as one word.)

> *"Looking for evidence?"* (Deadpan, to someone who knows he's lying.)

> *"Because we can only trust the Druid. Everyone else is too susceptible to corruption."*

> *"Sometimes a rock is a falling rock."* (When the party is overthinking.)

> *"I don't have time for this."* (About anything political or procedural.)

> *"Look, I know you are just a desperate man caught up in something bigger than themselves."* (Genuine compassion, deployed tactically.)

> *"Gotta go!"* (Fleeing a committee meeting, not a dragon.)

> *"These are the guys."* (Whispered. Certain. The only conclusion that matters.)

> *"There is no need for him to know."* (About anyone, about anything.)

> *"That's odd."* Pause. *"That's not good."* (The two-beat assessment.)

## Things He'd Never Say

- A long speech about his feelings — he processes internally, not out loud
- "I trust them completely" — trust is always provisional and evidence-based
- Anything self-aggrandizing — he leads by doing, not by claiming to lead
- "Let me explain my plan" — if you need it explained, you're not ready
- Flowery description of anything — greased hinges, not "ominous portents"
- "That's not my problem" — everything is his problem, because nobody else is paying attention
