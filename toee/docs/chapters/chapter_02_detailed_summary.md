# Chapter 02 Detailed Summary 

### Date: Planting 15, 579 

Zinia, always the master spy, decided that they needed information. The best way to get it was to talk to folks in a Tavern looking for someone to buy them a drink. 

Looking around, he noticed that these were all common folk, farmers, traders, and passers-by. 

It was midday, and these people had no business or chores. 

Precisely the kind of people who would be most interested in telling him what they knew. One talked about a Black Jay. He was a mysterious and forlorn herdsman in league with local bandits. This mysterious man would tip the bandits off. 

Zinia thinks, “Ah, so these folks don’t know the relationship to the Temple. Good.” 

The following person, a stout woman of indeterminate middle age, mutters that Y’dey, the head cleric of the village, has mysteriously disappeared. The priest, Terjon, is awfully closed-mouthed about the situation. She best that he played no small role in her disappearance as he is in charge now.

Zinnia reflects, “Distrust of central authority, and prone to conspiratorial thinking, Good.” 

Finally, the last one reveals his take on the local gendarmerie: “Burne and Rufus are nothing but lackeys for the Crown of Verbobonc. Once they complete their castle (“Burne’s Folly” is what I would call it), they will reveal their true motivations, and we will be under the thumb of Verbobonc’s Tyrannical Rule.” 

Zinnia connects the dots and explains to his companions, “The locals see the threat of being assimilated by Verbobonc as bigger than the temple. It’s probably related to their allegiance to the god Beory and Verbobonc’s theocracy. Verbobonc is committed to St. Cuthbert, and that god is committed to proselytizing and eliminating through conversion anyone who doesn’t at least acknowledge the importance of St. Cuthbert.”  

Zinia tells the group that the locals know nothing more of value. So, Zephyr leads the group to Jaroo, a local druid and follower of Beory. 

The grove is a place of worship. The caretaker has neatly pruned the trees, and the grass is well-tended. A carefully placed line of bluish stones sets off the path to a rock cairn where flowers, nuts, berries, and leaf garlands are placed. A small path leads beyond the shrine to a low-roofed wooden building placed under the great boughs of the central oaks of the thicket.

Zephyr shouts, and soon, an older man in a robe with a hood over his head and a flowing beard appears. 

“Have you come to contribute?” says the man, pointing to a collection box. 

A donation is made, and the robed man thanks them. 

“I am Jaroo, the keeper of this blessed grove.” 

Zephyr starts to ask him questions about what’s happening, but he notices that Jaroo looks at him with unmistakable disgust. 

Zephyr immediately realizes that Jaroo is a druid who thinks that Tieflings are outside the natural order and have no place in the prime material plane. He shrugs and lets Sequoia speak.

From the conversation, he learns that Jaroo was sent here to observe the events after the defeat of the Temple of Elemental Evil. Jaroo is upset that Verbobonc isn’t interested in only protecting Hommlet but is also committed to absorbing the faithful into its Church. 

Jaroo distrusts the intentions of Burne and Rufus, and Terjon's goals.   
When Sequoia tells him of the threats they encountered, Jaroo tells them of the Moathoase. 

“The mouthouse was once the outpost of the Temple of Elemental Evil, its watchtower an advance base for raids, looting, and destruction. From this area, servants of the Temple were to bring the Village of Hommlet and all the lands around it into subjection. The temple enslaved the  conquered folk, and then they were forced to construct yet another fortress further west, spreading the evil power of the Temple in ever-growing rings to encompass all of the lands around its base.”

Jaroo pauses, “This ended with the Battle of Emiry Meadows.” 

He thinks some more and continues, “ The circles of outposts that you and your friends have uncovered are too early, similar to the plots of the original temple, to be a coincidence.”

Jaroo then turns to them, “You must go and investigate the Moathouse. And when you have found what you are looking for, you must tell me and me alone. Do not tell anyone else because the agents of the Temple may even be here. As for Burne and Rufus, they may use whatever information they glean to restrict our freedoms.” 

From there, Sequoia leads the group to Burne and Rufus. Burne and Rufus are the local leaders and representatives of Verbobonc tasked with building a castle. Sequoia is confused about the purpose of such a vast military castle in a place like Homlet. To the south is the Gnarley forest. Past the forest is the Wild Coast to the southeast and the Kingdom of Celene to the southwest. Neither are serious threats. The theory that Jaroo has is that it’s about putting Hommlet entirely under the thumb of the Archibishop of Verbobonc, and a way to extend his power looks pretty reasonable. 

As he reaches the edge of the village, he notices the tower, the only part that has been built. 

This structure is some 55 feet tall, a smaller tower rising inside the greater at about 35 feet above the ground. Its entrance is accessible only by going up a curving flight of stone stairs, which terminate in a landing about 10 feet above the ground. The outer door of the tower lowers to form a bridge to the stone landing. There are several arrow slits around the tower, and it has a splay around the base to about 6 feet in height. The lower and upper battlements are machicolated, and the merlons are pierced for archers. Two men-at-arms watch from its roof.

The guard at the door asks them for their business. 

“We came in with the latest caravan from Narwell and have news about brigands.” 

The guard coughs and lets them in. 

Shuttered arrow slits flank the door to the room, and two murder holes are in the ceiling. Flanking the inner portal are two fireplaces, where most of the cooking is done by two to three servants. The lords of the place sit in judgment either here or at a table in the curved alcove. Trestle tables can be extended down and across to accommodate 40 people during a feast or revel. Several comfortable chairs are placed along the walls, and the walls themselves are hung with tapestries and decorated with shields, arms, and hunting trophies—12 shields, four long swords, two broad swords, four spears, two battle axes, and the heads and homes of various creatures. Ramps curving upwards and downwards lead to other levels.

Rufus is a powerfully built man of average height with blue eyes, a delicate nose, and golden blond hair. He is wearing white linen pants and a dark blue blouse that shows more of his chest than the Sequoia or anyone wants to see. 

Rufus introduces himself as the military leader of the area and asks Sequoia to explain everything he has learned. 

Midway through the narration, Rufus calls out to Burne, a rather youngish-looking man. Burne has a slight build, long brown hair, green eyes, and an aquiline nose. He’s wearing a mage robe with scuff marks and scratches on it. 

“Rufus, why didn’t you tell me we had guests? I would have worn the proper attire.” 

Rufus sighs, “It’s okay. You look fabulous. I am so sorry to tear you away from your experiments.” 

Sequoia immediately understands that they are an item. 

Burne asks, “So what’s so important?” 

Rufus explains that the heroes have discovered that there may be a resurgence of the Temple of Elemental Evil. 

Burne is horrified, “That’s impossible.” 

Rufus agrees and thinks momentarily, “My friends, if you were to find out that the temple was resurgent, that would be critical secret information. It would be essential that we present it to our boss in Verbobonc.”

Sequoia gets a hint but chooses to remain silent. Rufus wants him to volunteer to share information only with him, but Sequoia isn’t sure. 

Rufus sighs, “I have a better plan than relying on your good graces. I am the military commander of this area, and I am deputizing you. As my deputies, you are required to talk to me first. If you refuse my offer of being a deputy, I will have you arrested, or worse, if you explore the Moathouse. As my deputies, you are required to share any information with me, first. And then with anyone else.” 

Sequoia smiles and nods. He has seen too many people with too many conflicting agendas, each trying to control the information flow and accomplish something slightly different. Once he has information, he will decide what to share. 

### Zinia

“We need to go get our cleric, Calmer.” 

Zinia makes the long trek to the Temple of St. Cuthbert. A massive, imposing structure that is ridiculously too large for such a small village with so few souls. 

Inside, he finds Calmer, who excitedly introduces him to Terjon.

The high priest Terjon is happy to talk to Zinia alone, whom he recognizes as a recent convert. Zinia, for his part, does his best imitation of one. 

Terjon agrees with the idea that Calmer should join the group. But he does have one particular request that is so eerily familiar, 

“Whatever information you get must be shared with me and me alone. The safety of the good people of Oerth may hang in the balance.”

From there, Zinia heads to the Inn of the Welcome Wench, where he and his traveling companions secure reasonable accommodations for reasonable prices, except for Zephyr, who is a spendthrift and demands the best room.
