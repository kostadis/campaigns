# Chapter 03 A Smile and a Stone

### Date: Planting 16, 579 

Zephyr

The following day, the Zephyr sits for breakfast and waits for the arrival of Calmer and the man-at-arms that Ranos Davl has promised him. 

Calmer arrives first, his armor polished and his holy symbol, the cudgel dangling from his wrist. 

Zephyr scrutinizes him. He is short, 5’2”, has copper-red hair, hazel eyes, and a flattish nose. He also has that over-eager look that religious zealots have.   
   
Calmer asks, “Shall we begin?” 

Zephyr begs him to sit down, and then calls Ostler to bring Calmer some food. 

Calmer orders some boiled eggs, milk and bread. 

While Calmer waits for his food, Zephyr stares at him. The silence is uncomfortable and is interrupted when a man in studded leather armor walks in. 

“There’s the Tiefling. My boss Ranos said you hired me?” 

Zephyr looks at him carefully.

“My name is Thorne. My friends call me Duke because my name sounds like a throne.” 

Zephyr takes stock of Thorne or Duke. He is a rugged man in his late 30s with a weathered face marked by a few scars from past skirmishes. His brown hair is cropped short, and his piercing green eyes are hidden beneath the brim of a weather-beaten leather cap.

Zephyr says, “Well, since we are all here, let’s go outside and wait for the rest of the crew.” 

##  Zinia

The masked one had been watching Zephyr and their two new companions, trying to see if anything was off. He noticed nothing. 

He suggests that they head to the Moathouse since all that they needed to do had been done.   
![][image1]  
The journey is uneventful. He takes the road heading east towards Nulb. After a few hours of walking, he sees the track heading to the ruins. 

A scrub of thorns, thistles, weeds, and shrubs grows thickly along the edge of the track leading to the ruins. Even the track is mostly overgrown and cluttered with fallen branches and trees. Here and there, it is washed out, in other places a mire. Some game evidently still follows the pathway, however, for after a mile or sofaint traces can be seen. But even considering this, going is slow, and it takes over an hour to reach the place on horseback, or two to trudge along on foot. Considerable hacking and clearing is necessary to make the way passable, so double the time required for the first trip. After two miles, as the track turns more northerly, the land begins to sink and become boggy. Tall marsh plants grow thickly where cattails and tamaracks do not. Off to the left can be seen the jagged silhouette of the moathouse.A side path, banked high to cross over the wetland to either side, just north to the entrance of the ruin. The track here is only about 15 feet wide or so, with crumbling embankments making travel near the edge dangerous. The bogs stink. The vegetation appears dense and prolific, but somehow sickly and unhealthy, creepers and vines throwing their strangling loops over the skeletons of dead saplings and living bushes alike. The rushes and cattails rustle and bend even to a slight zephyr, and weird birdcalls, croakings, and other unwholesome sounds come faintly across the fen.

![][image2]

The Duke sets up camp just past the drawbridge that leads into the moathouse, wishing the group luck. 

Calmer is a bit insecure about this being his first adventure, so he lets Zephyr and Zinia advance first. 

Four giant frogs jump out of the pool near the bridge and attack. Although they catch the Zinia flatfooted, they miss him. When he has a chance to absorb the tactical situation, he sees that all the frogs missed. 

“The last ribbit,” he thinks. 

A few seconds later, the ground is drenched in the blood of giant frogs. 

Thurne shouts, “I can cook something nice with those legs. Tastes like chicken.” 

Zinia glares. 

He’s about to go on the drawbridge when Calmer shouts, “I think it’s time that I did what all great clerics of St. Cuthbert do, which is lead.”

Thurne shouts, “Do you want the horses?” 

Zinia watches as Calmer marches onto the drawbridge. At the last moment, the masked spy notices the bridge rotting. He’s about to shout when four more giant frogs jump onto the bridge. 

The frogs' and the party's weight causes the bridge to shatter. Zinia doesn’t fall in, but Sequoia and Calmer do. He looks around and sees Zephyr still standing on the bridge. Sequoia and Calmer have been injured from the falling debris, but so have two frogs. 

A frog chomps at Calmer and is knocked to death’s door. Zinia mutters to himself that this is not the kind of start that will ingratiate him with the clerics of St. Cuthbert. 

Zinia jumps into the water and strikes and kills one of the injured Frogs. 

From behind him, he hears another frog croak. It’s last. It is dead at Zephyr's hand.  

As Sequioa almost kills another frog, Zinia rushes to Calmer, pulls him out of the water, and patches his wound. 

When Zinia stands up, he sees the last frog is also dead from an arrow that went through the eye and into the amphibian’s skull. 

## Sequoia

“So you guys going to die before you even make it through the door,” shouts Thrune.   
Sequoia groans and drags Calmer to the campsite. Calmer mutters, “I just need a few moments.” 

Thrune can’t resist, “So are we calling it quits?” 

Calmer shouts, “No. We have come here to do a job and will do it.” Sequoia decides that he can lead, but Sequoia will observe. The door to the moathouse is hanging open on one great hinge. The other is splintered and holed but still in position, wedged and shored closed from the inside.

Past the door is a large courtyard, and to the northeast are some stairs. The stairs lead to the house portion of the moathouse. The doors at the top of the staircase are broken; one is flat on the floor, and the other is sundered. 

Zephyr shouts, “Hm, this is interesting. Some human and pack animal traffic comes this way.” 

Calmer says, “Perhaps the rogues would like to investigate and see what’s ahead? Let us get a jump on the enemy. I can stay here and guard the rear.” 

Sequoia can see the cleric’s fear and rolls his eyes \- “Zephyr, let’s go.” 

Past the door is a great hall. This was once a great audience chamber, as shown by the tattered banners and tapestries on the walls, destroyed furniture, and heaps of rotting cloth thrown into corners. Once richly appointed, it has been thoroughly searched, sacked, and despoiled. Leaves and dirt cover the floor, and cobwebs hang from walls and the ceiling above. Looking up, he sees that pieces of beams and chunks of stone poke through, indicating that the upper stories of the place are totally destroyed and likely to be impassable to anything larger than a rat. 

To the east and south are passageways, and to the northeast is a door. Sequoia examines the ground and whispers, “The foot traffic goes to the door.” 

Zephyr acknowledges him, and Sequoia maneuvers to the door to see if he can hear anything. 

When Zephyr asks if he should use a mage’s hand to open the door, Sequoia waves him off as the door is barricaded. 

A sudden bang is heard in the room just outside the door. “Zephyr has used an illusion to get the folks inside to open the door for us,” thinks Sequoia. 

The door opens, and a man in scruffy, dirty clothes with yellowing teeth peers through. He then retreats into the door, and there is muttering. Sequoia can’t understand what they are saying. He looks to Zinia and Zephyr, but they shake their heads as well. 

The two rogues and the monk co-ordinate with hand gestures. They will sneak into the room one at a time, attack, and retreat. 

Sequoia waits as Zephyr enters. He hears a body fall to the ground. As Zephyr exits, he indicates seven more left and that the leader is dead. Sequoia waits as Zinia enters. The satisfying sound of a body hitting the ground quietly precedes Zinia’s return. 

The rogue sneaks in the past through the door and sees the bandit leader dead on the ground, pooling in blood in the middle of the room. To his left is another dead bandit. Sequoia kills one with his shortswords and then leaves the chamber. 

The bandits recover from the shock of the surprise attack and start screaming that they have to get out of there. Three of the bandits escape to the south through an arrow slit that has been enlarged to allow easy retreat.

Sequoia kills one more of the bandits and then stands in front of the exit, blocking it. 

He turns to Zinia, “Keep him alive. We want to interrogate him.” 

Zinia knocks him out with a flurry of blows. 

Sequoia stands up, and just then, Calmer walks in and says, “I think it’s important that we callto  calm down.” 

The rogue stares at the cleric, wondering if that was intentional; the cleric's obvious glee makes it clear that it was. 

He groans. 

Sequoia then wakes up the bandit. The poor man wakes up, clears his eyes, and is shocked by a Tiefling, a cleric of St. Cuthbert, and Sequoia staring at him, their weapons covered in the blood of his gang. It’s too much, and the bandit cries. 

Under questioning, the bandit explains that he didn’t want to be a bandit, but there was this property that he had to sell, then there were the gambling debts and the drinking, and well, it just went downhill from there. 

He explains, “The Moathouse was a good hideout because nobody wanted to come here anymore. The Temple was dead, and adventurers picked this place clean, so nobody came here.” 

“You were working for the Temple,” Sequoia explains coldly and flatly. 

The Bandit, whose name is Voss, starts crying even more. Sequoia is baffled, and Calmer explains that membership in the cult is punishable by death.

Out of the corner of Sequoia’s eye, he sees a flat river rock inscribed with an eye and a tear. “What’s this?” 

Voss then starts to cry hysterically and in panic. Calmer offers to help, but apparently, Calmer’s idea of calming a man terrified of being killed by an Evil Cult is to explain to him what the Church of St. Cuthbert does to its enemies. 

Sequoia pushes the cleric away and sits down next to Voss. He says, “Look, I know you are just a desperate man caught up in something bigger than themselves. And I really don’t need to hand you over to the authorities. I just need to know what this symbol is.” 

Voss looks at him, “It’s what the big boss gave the boss who gave it to us. It meant we belonged.” 

Zephyr groans, “He’s too low down the totem pole to know anything useful.”
