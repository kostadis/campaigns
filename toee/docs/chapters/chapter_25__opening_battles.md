# Chapter 25 \- Opening Battles

---

## Zinnia — The Scalding Battlefield

The steam hit first.

Not the heat — the *steam*. That particular species of pain that gets inside your lungs before you've registered it's happened. We came up from the lower level into a corridor that had turned itself into something between a battle and a sauna, and my first thought wasn't *danger*, it was *assessment*. Stone floor. Bodies in Earth Temple brown and Fire Temple red, already cooling in the scalding air. Visibility: poor and narrowing. Bugbears behind an improvised barricade of pews and rubble, holding the stairway against an Earth Temple advance that had stalled somewhere in the haze ahead of us.

*Someone set up a very effective choke point.*

I almost appreciated it professionally, for about half a second.

Zephyr put an arrow into the mist from the edge of the corridor — precise, efficient, for once doing the sensible thing from distance. I noted it. Then I stopped noting things and started moving.

The cultist at the front of their line didn't see me coming. That's the point. Through the steam, into the billowing white heat, and then just — strikes, a sequence of controlled impacts, the kind of work where your body knows the geometry better than your mind does. The cultist dropped before the bugbears had finished processing that we were there. Clean. Fast. The momentum of the whole corridor shifted for one clear, perfect moment.

Then the bugbears reminded me what a barricade is for.

I've taken hits that hurt more. I've taken hits that did more structural damage. But there's a specific quality to a critical blow — the way it doesn't just land on you, it *reorganizes* your understanding of how the fight is going. Twenty-five points of impact, distributed across a body that had just committed fully to the attack and had nowhere to go. The sound it made wasn't something I want to think about.

I don't go down. That's not bravado; it's training. But I also don't pretend it didn't happen. I was aware of my own edges in a way I hadn't been thirty seconds before — the specific ache of ribs that had absorbed something they didn't enjoy absorbing, the recalibration that happens automatically when your body files a formal complaint. Zephyr and Sequoia had caught pieces of the same counterattack. I had caught the largest piece.

*You rushed in.*

I know. I know I rushed in.

The thing is, the barricade was the whole problem, and the cultist at the front was the hinge of the barricade. If you take the hinge, the door opens. That's not impatience. That's geometry.

What broke it, in the end, wasn't geometry. It was Calmer.

A single pulse of guiding radiance — the kind of divine light that doesn't ask permission — and the double doors at the barricade simply *stopped existing as an obstacle*. They flew open with the kind of finality that makes everyone on both sides pause for half a breath. Then the Earth Temple forces came through the gap like the tide deciding to try something different, and the Fire Temple line fractured into the exact kind of chaotic mess that we had specifically engineered this situation to produce.

I watched Zephyr work through the first bugbear with a methodical efficiency that I don't say enough good things about. Sequoia's finishing blow on the second had a certain satisfying weight to it. The barricade was gone. The stalemate was gone. The careful hold the bugbears had maintained was just — gone.

Which left the steam.

The steam was still there, and it was getting worse. Elemental violence has a way of feeding itself, and whatever was generating this particular environmental nightmare was not winding down. The mission was accomplished in the narrowest operational sense: the barricade broken, the advance resumed, the fire and earth factions in the process of tearing into each other exactly as intended. We had done what we came here to do.

*Time to not be here anymore.*

There's a specific discipline in the disengagement — recognizing that the chaos you've set in motion no longer belongs to you and no longer cares who you are. You're not retreating from failure. You're exiting the kill zone you personally created before it becomes indiscriminate about its victims. That's just operational hygiene.

My ribs had opinions about how quickly we should move.

I moved quickly anyway.

## Calmer — Confrontation with the Wraith

The corridor air changed before I saw it. That's the only way I can describe it — a drop in temperature that had nothing to do with stone or depth, a wrongness that sat behind my eyes like a headache forming. I turned the corner into the Air Temple passage and there it was, drifting in the dark ahead of us. Waiting. Patient in the way that things without hearts are patient.

My first instinct, if I'm being honest, was the same instinct I had the first time I ever came face to face with something that should not exist. Pure, animal want-to-be-elsewhere. Not fear exactly. Fear implies you're still calculating odds. This was older than that.

But I am a cleric of St. Cuthbert, and St. Cuthbert does not permit elsewhere as a destination when undead are in the corridor.

I gathered the light. I thrust it outward. Channel divinity — the real thing, the genuine article, the power that has cracked ghoul skulls and sent lesser dead screaming into whatever passes for oblivion on the other side. I aimed it at the wraith with all the conviction I possess, which is considerable.

The wraith looked at it. And made its save. The holy light dissolved into the cold air like breath in winter, leaving exactly nothing behind.

There is a particular quality to failure when it happens in front of an audience. The wraith didn't flinch. The corridor didn't tremble. The darkness simply remained exactly as dark as it had been, and the wraith remained exactly as present, and I stood there holding the afterglow of a divine power that had achieved nothing at all.

A lesser cleric might have retreated at that point. A wiser one, perhaps. But I have been killed once already in this gods-forsaken temple, revived at considerable cost to someone who claims not to care about me, and I did not survive all of that to be mocked by something that doesn't even have the decency to be alive when it defies me. I tried again.

I thought about it later: there was an abiding and pure passion in that second attempt. He meant hatred, and he was correct about that too. I hate this thing. I hate what it is, what it represents, the cold contempt of its existence, the way it simply *drifts* while I channel power that has worked before and should work now —

The wraith made its save again.

Two attempts. Two failures. The divine light of St. Cuthbert, Cudgel of Ortho, the Arbiter Eternal, dispersed twice into the air of a dank dungeon corridor while an undead abomination stood untouched and apparently unbothered.

*This is not fair*, I thought, which is not a thought with a great deal of theological dignity, but it was the honest one.

Then the wraith turned its hollow gaze on all of us, considering the room, and it said, with what I can only describe as serene confidence: *"Well, one of you is going to die today."*

Zephyr raised his pistol. The shot caught the wraith across what might generously be called its torso — five damage after the creature's resistance halved it — the only wound anyone managed to land on the thing before he broke for the secret passage. I am told I should credit this. I do. Zephyr wounded the wraith and then ran, which is functionally what the rest of us were about to do as well, so perhaps the lesson is simply that even recklessness improves when pointed at a door.

The wraith was hurt. The wraith was not stopped.

The party began dashing. One by one, boots loud on stone, the Air Temple corridor filling with the very specific sound of adventurers not fighting a tactical retreat but simply *running*. Sequoia. Zinnia. Zephyr already gone. This is the correct response to a wraith that has resisted two consecutive attempts at divine power and announced its intention to kill someone. I knew that.

*But not me*, I explained, to no one in particular, or possibly to St. Cuthbert directly, and I dashed.

My armor class is 20\. I want that on record. Plate mail, blessed and magical, the finest protection available to a cleric of my station and general level of being-placed-in-danger-repeatedly. I have good reason to believe I am harder to hit than most.

The wraith reached out as I sprinted and caught me across the back. Sixteen damage — cold, deep, the kind of cold that doesn't stop at skin — and then a stray fire bolt from somewhere in the chaos added eight more, because apparently the universe decided one insult was insufficient. I cleared the threshold. I made it through the passage. I was, technically, alive.

And behind me, echoing up the corridor as we scrambled upstairs, as the stone closed the distance between us and it, came the sound of the wraith laughing at me.

*Hysterically*.. It laughed at me *hysterically*.

Two failed turns. A divine power that worked on the chimera, that has worked before, that should by every measure of theological logic have worked today — and the wraith's only response to the entirety of my efforts was to chase me into a corridor and laugh.

I am going to need a moment before I speak about this further.

## Zephyr — Ambush by Ghouls

The smell arrived before the sound. That sick, wet rot — like meat left in standing water, but with something worse underneath it, something that didn't belong to the natural world at all. We'd pulled back to our own territory to wait out the temple war, and I'd been calculating odds in my head, counting corridors, thinking about Romag and Hartsch and the seventeen ways this whole operation could collapse before morning.

Then that smell.

*Oh god. This again.*

They came out of the dark in a shambling line — four of them, and I recognized the faces. That was the worst part. Not strangers. Not random undead dredged up from some nameless grave. Earth Temple members. People we'd seen in corridors, nodded to while maintaining cover, filed away as *useful* or *obstacle* in the ongoing arithmetic of survival. Now raised by something with necromantic ambitions and sent wandering down here to scavenge whatever the temple war left behind.

"But, aren't they supposed to be, like, fighting upstairs?"

The question hung there, genuinely unanswered, which I found deeply unpleasant. Things were supposed to be fighting upstairs. That was the plan. That was *my* plan. Ghouls lurching into our territory, stinking of fresh death and bad magic, was not in the plan.

Sequoia moved fast — bless him — already working to set something at the door. But the ghouls rolled forward like they had somewhere to be, and whatever trap he was laying, they hit it before it was ready. A 1\. The kind of number that makes you want to reconsider your life choices. The spring went early, the delay was nothing, and the ghouls kept coming.

Fine. Fine.

The nearest one hadn't reached me yet. Hadn't reacted. Was still in that lurching half-moment between shambling forward and noticing there was something in its way with opinions about being devoured. I've been paralyzed by ghouls before — we all have, at this point, the claws hit and your body just *stops*, which is a sensation I would describe as among my least favourite — so I have developed a strong professional interest in killing them before they get the opportunity.

The dagger was already in my hand.

Twenty-seven damage. Clean. The creature dropped where it stood like someone had simply switched it off.

*They attack. They die.* Except this time, they didn't even get the attack.

I was already tracking the other three when Calmer happened.

The man has been knocked unconscious more times than I can count, raised from the dead once, used as bait at least twice — that last one I will own, that was me, and I stand by the tactical reasoning even if the execution had some notes. He has been burned by fire priests and drowned by Thalsor's magic and told to *be ready to die* before a ceremony that I knew would probably try to kill him. He has been patient about this in his own particular way, which is to say he has not been patient at all, and there has been escalating friction, and the word Zinnia used was *identity drift* which I refuse to engage with directly.

None of that mattered in the moment Calmer let the undead have it.

The Turn Undead came out of him like something that had been stored under pressure for weeks. The holy wrath hit the room — all three remaining ghouls failing, every last one of them — and they *fled*. Not shambled. Not lurched. Fled in terror, taking damage as they ran, the divine power chasing them through the door like a creditor who's finally located a defaulter.

Calmer suddenly lost it. He was going *"yes, yes, yes, yes, yes, yes, yes, yes, yes, yes, yes, yes\!"* The cursing started. *This is how the power of—* and then more cursing. The sentence never finished. He didn't need it to.

I watched the war cleric come fully unhinged in the best possible way, and I had two thoughts at once. The first was: *this is what two failed attempts on the wraith looks like when it finally finds a target that can't resist.* The frustration releasing itself, all of it, through the conduit of divine power and vocabulary I won't repeat.

The second thought was quieter and I didn't linger on it.

The second thought was: *is that him, or is that something else wearing his voice?*

Because Calmer hates undead. Calmer always hated undead. That's not new — a cleric of St. Cuthbert spitting fire at animated corpses is the most theologically coherent thing he does. But the *yes yes yes yes yes* had an edge to it. The way Zinnia described it — the concern he won't say out loud while framing it as purely operational — I've filed it and I look at it sometimes. Two failed turns on the wraith. The ceremony, Romag's toast, the creeping months inside a temple where Evil is spelled with a capital letter and they keep expecting you to mean it.

Sequoia put an arrow in the back of the last one before it made it out the corridor. Clean finish. That's Sequoia.

The cursing faded. The smell didn't, not entirely. And I looked at the dropped ghoul — my ghoul, killed before it could act, the assassin's precision Zinnia respects and won't say he respects — and then at the door where the others had fled.

"Maybe we should go find that guy?"

Because the ghouls had been Earth Temple members. Recently dead, recently raised. Someone with necromantic power was working in this dungeon right now, during our carefully engineered war, with access to our people's corpses and apparent interest in deploying them.

That was a problem. That was several problems layered on top of each other.

The sounds of Calmer's breathing slowly returned to something resembling normal. I noted it. Filed it. Did not examine it further.

## Sequoia — Descent into the Second Level Corridors

The short rest had done what it could, which wasn't much. Calmer's colour had come back. Zephyr was still moving like something inside him wasn't quite right. The sounds beyond our territory hadn't stopped — distant impacts, shouted orders, the kind of echoing commotion that tells you a building is eating itself. We listened to it from borrowed stone floors and called it a rest.

When we moved back through the Air Temple, the wraith was where we'd left it. Same corridor. Same cold. Same hollow patience. Calmer didn't say anything, and neither did I. There's a particular kind of threat that's worse when it doesn't attack you — it just watches you go, already knowing you'll be back. We cleared the edge of its territory and kept moving, and I didn't look behind me when I heard it drift away.

The second level hit different the moment we reached it. Cooler air. The smell of blood and old stone. Broken bottles scattered across the floor like someone had celebrated early and poorly. Armor shoved against the walls — not placed, shoved, the way you clear a path in a hurry. Blood trails running into shadow and stopping nowhere near a body. The war sounds were still there, muffled by stone but present from every direction at once, which meant the fighting wasn't localized anymore.

I was reading the floor before I was reading the room.

That's the habit. Greased hinges, foot traffic, bloodstain direction. These aren't aesthetic details. They're a map of what happened and who survived it. The floor down here was telling me something had broken open — not just a skirmish, something structural. I just didn't have the full picture yet.

Then I saw the goblins.

They were moving through a hidden door that had no business being visible, hauling bags that clinked with the unmistakable weight of coin. Behind them, ghouls — ghouls — dragging looted weapons, threading the same passage with the same purposeful efficiency. Nobody scrambling. Nobody panicking. They weren't fleeing the battle.

They were shopping in it.

I knew the badge. Kelno's people. The Air Temple cleric who had sat across from Romag's table like furniture, quiet enough that I'd filed him under *background noise* and moved on. That was a mistake. I was looking at the correction. Kelno had read the board and decided his move was to let the other temples destroy each other while his people walked the corridors with empty bags and filled them.

I thought about how many times we'd been in a room, plotting and planing and never once giving him a thought. 

That's the thing about faction politics — you get fixated on the loud players. Romag's anger. The Fire Temple's fire. The Water Temple's ambition. You forget that the quietest person in the room is often the one who's already decided they don't need to win the argument, just outlast everyone having it.

Zephyr said it out loud, the way Zephyr does: *"So… We've done a good job. Right."*

The events confirmed it flatly. All four temples were in this now.

*"So, perfect for us to go down to the lower temple."*

He wasn't wrong. I watched the last goblin vanish into the passage — one bag, maybe sixty pounds of someone else's gold — and tallied it up. We'd engineered a war between three factions and a fourth had walked into the fire on its own terms. The Obsidian Edge had lit the match and stepped back, and now Kelno was standing in the smoke with a different set of burns and a full treasury.

That was either very good for us or a problem we hadn't designed for yet.

I wasn't sure which. I filed it under *both* and kept walking.

## Zinnia — The Hall of Trophies and Elemental Strife

The bones told a story.

I catalogued them the way I catalogue everything — automatically, without sentiment. The hall had once hosted celebrations, if you could call them that. Great banquets of conquest, probably. The trophies on the walls said as much: mummified heads, their leather-dry mouths still twisted in the expressions of their dying moments. Torn battle standards. Battered shields. A broken broadsword that someone had kept as a trophy, which meant someone had once been proud of breaking it. The tapestries showed raiding — figures in the act of taking, burning, dragging. Broad steps of dull black stone descended from the north wall into shadow, and the silence of the place pressed down like a thumb.

Ruins have a grammar. You can read them if you know how.

This one said: something powerful celebrated here, and now it is gone, and all it left behind were its worst moments.

*Noted.*

---

The heat reached us before the sound did.

Fire snakes and a wounded salamander, locked in combat with four water gargoyles — the collisions between them threw eruptions of scalding steam into the already close air, and the noise was a constant wet violence that didn't resolve into anything intelligible. I watched for a moment, parsing it. Fire versus Water. Neither faction was going to yield. The salamander was wounded but still moving, still contributing to the chaos. The gargoyles showed no sign of flagging.

No tactical objective. No leverage. No way in.

Just the violence itself, filling the chamber with heat and noise because violence, left uninterrupted, continues until it can't.

I was still watching when Zephyr — because of course it was Zephyr — decided he wanted to ask one of them a question.

"I wanna find one guy and ask him where the deep temple is."

The response was patient, as it usually is when Zephyr proposes something that requires the world to cooperate with him.

"I  notice that these are not humans… Does anybody here speak Primordial?" I wonder.

"Speak goblin," I speak goblin .

A beat.

"Alright, well, so we can't speak to the gargoyles. That's a problem."

"Alright," I said. "This plan is done."

I did not say anything. There was nothing to say that the situation hadn't already said. Whatever knowledge those creatures carried — whatever they might have told us about the deep temple's layout, its factions, its pressure points — it was locked behind a language none of us spoke, inside minds that had no particular interest in us.

Information asymmetry. The oldest problem.

---

I had noticed the door earlier. I always notice doors.

This one opened on its own.

Not dramatically — not with a gust of wind or the grinding of ancient mechanisms. Just: open, when it had been closed a moment before. Then closed again. The hall was full of noise from the elemental fight, full of heat and steam, and I was watching it between the billowing clouds as the party debated the uselessness of Goblin as a diplomatic language.

Watching us.

The thought arrived without alarm — I am a professional, and professionals don't waste alarm on hypotheticals. But I catalogued it. A door that moves on its own in a ruined hall is either a draft, a mechanism, or an observer. The hall was otherwise still. Drafts don't aim themselves. Mechanisms don't pause between movements like something checking whether you've noticed.

Someone, or something, was tracking our position.

I filed it. Asset or threat, TBD. Move south. The steam gives cover.

---

That was the exit strategy. No magic, no subtlety required — the elementals were generating enough obscuring haze on their own that slipping away south was simply a matter of patience and timing. We moved between the billows, staying low, staying quiet, letting the heat swallow the sound of our passage.

The salamander's shriek cut through once. Something was losing ground in there.

We didn't stop to find out which side.

The heat clung to my skin as we went, and the sounds of the battle faded behind us into the haze — and I kept the door in my peripheral attention until the geometry of the corridor finally took it from view.

Still watching us, or already forgotten?

I didn't know. That bothered me more than the elementals.

## Sequoia — The Prisoner's Bargain

The chamber smelled like fear and old stone. That particular combination — unwashed people, iron, the faint copper of something that had happened here before we arrived — I've learned to read it the way a sailor reads weather. It means people have been waiting a long time, and they've stopped expecting good news.

They were huddled against the far wall, chained at the wrist, and the moment we came through that door their voices hit us all at once.

*"Please, please don't leave us to die\!"*

I took stock without slowing down. Human, all of them. Thin. The kind of thin that isn't about food so much as about sustained terror. They'd been gathered for something, and that something was not going to be pleasant for them. I've seen enough of how this temple thinks to know what prisoners are for here. I didn't let myself think too long about the ones we hadn't found in time.

Zephyr was already moving — he has a nose for information, whatever else you want to say about him. He turned to the group and asked whether anyone knew about the Deep Temple.

Silence from most of them. And then one man, apart from the others slightly, looked up with the particular expression of someone who has been waiting to have something to trade.

*"I know about the Deep Temple,"* he said. *"But I'm only going to talk if you let — if you get me out of here."*

Smart. Or at least, experienced enough to know that information without leverage is just a gift, and people who've been chained in a temple dungeon don't have anything left to give away for free. I didn't blame him.

Zinnia had been watching the man the whole time. I saw him do that thing — the small stillness, the focused attention. He's good at reading people. Better than he lets on, and he already lets on plenty. Whatever he saw, it settled something, because he gave me the look that means: *this one's telling the truth.*

Right. So. The lock.

I stepped forward, crouched down, and got to work. Twenty-seven on the roll — the shackles came apart like they'd been waiting for me. There's a kind of satisfaction in a clean pick that I'd be embarrassed to admit to anyone. It's just physics, really. Everything has a mechanism. Everything has a point where it opens. You find the point, you apply the right pressure, and the thing gives way. It's the clearest version of how I think the world works.

The chains hit the stone floor.

That was when the others found their voices.

*"What about us, what about us?"*

Four still chained. Frightened. I looked at them — really looked, which I try not to do too long because it makes the arithmetic harder — and thought about how many locks I still had to pick tonight, and what we still needed to do before morning, and whether any of this was going to matter if we didn't get the information that was currently standing in front of us, rubbing his wrists, sizing us up.

Zephyr told them they'd have their turn. Two seconds. I kept my face neutral.

The freed man was doing his own accounting now. He looked at us — Earth Temple badges, weapons, the particular posture of people who've been fighting their way through something — and his expression shifted from desperate to cautious to something almost like wry.

*"The Deep Temple?"* he said. *"What do you want to know about the Deep Temple?"*

*"How do you get there?"* Zephyr asked.

The man actually laughed. Not a warm laugh. The laugh of someone who had been near enough to something terrible to find the question genuinely absurd.

*"Because nobody wants to get to the Deep Temple. Everybody wants to not — not ever be near the Deep Temple, but..."*

Zephyr asked why he was laughing.

I understood why he was laughing. I've been here long enough to understand that some things are funny because the alternative is something you can't look at directly. The laughter stopped when Calmer stepped forward, and the room changed in the way rooms change when Calmer means business. There's something about a priest who's survived being killed once that reads differently than before. The prisoner felt it.

Zephyr had tried persuasion first — I caught the flat delivery, saw it land badly. But with Calmer at his shoulder, he tried again, and the second attempt found its footing.

The man looked at us. He looked at the door. He looked at his own hands, free now and still faintly red from the iron.

*"I guess I would be pushing my luck if I insisted for more help, wouldn't I? My mother never raised — didn't raise me to be a fool."*

He paused, and there was something honest in that pause. A man deciding. Not performing surrender, actually deciding.

*"Alright. So here's the secret door to the Greater Temple."*

He pointed it out on our map. Precise. A hidden passage inside the Air Temple itself, knowledge he'd gotten directly from Kelno — which meant it was the kind of information that doesn't circulate widely, the kind you only know if you were close enough to matter or close enough to overhear.

I noted it. I noted him. Air Temple cultist, which made him a prisoner here for reasons of faction politics as much as anything else. Smart enough to trade. Smart enough to know the shape of what he was dealing with. His mother had raised him right, apparently.

The other four were still chained to the wall.

I had more work to do.

## **Zephyr — Beckoned**

---

The freed prisoner's directions were good. The door was real, the location confirmed, the path to the Deep Temple laid out in words Zephyr had turned over in his head until they sat flush and solid, like a key he could feel the weight of even when he wasn't touching it.

He'd had to work for those words. Not elegantly.

The interrogation hadn't been his finest hour — he'd leaned on the man harder than necessary, all bravado and borrowed threat, standing at the full height the horns gave him and hoping the prisoner would fill in the rest. The man had. Fear is efficient that way. Zephyr didn't find it satisfying exactly, but satisfying wasn't the point. The point was the information, and now he had it. Another notch in the victory belt, even if the notching had been sloppy.

So. The door existed. They knew where it was.

The moment he turned the location over in his mind — corridor junction, left passage, counting stones from the bracket — was the moment the runner found them.

An Earth Temple runner, collar damp with effort, mouth already open before he'd finished stopping. Hartsch wanted them. Hartsch wanted his Obsidian Edge assembled, upstairs, immediately. War council. The summons had the texture of *now*.

Zephyr looked at the runner, then at the corridor beyond him, then at nothing in particular.

He filed the directions away in the same place he kept everything he planned to collect later: the Rhennee debt, Captain Tolub's ship schedule, the exact weight of every favor he was owed. The Deep Temple wasn't going anywhere. Presumably.

The weight of it was the thing he didn't say aloud. They had it — finally had it — and now they had to go back up. Because Hartsch whistled and the Obsidian Edge came running, because that was the arrangement they had made, because Hartsch was useful and useful things required maintenance. The alliance was real. The leash attached to it was also real.

*This again.*

Not prejudice this time. Something else. The particular indignity of belonging to someone else's chain of command while you were trying to build your own.

He let the runner see him receiving the message calmly. Unhurried. The kind of man who goes where he chooses, and who happens to be choosing this. He gestured for the others to follow and started walking.

The door's location sat behind his eyes like a lit coal.
