Notable People of Nulb
The village of Nulb is home to an eclectic array of
powerful and dangerous figures. Each wields influence
in their own way, contributing to the chaos and intrigue
that define the settlement. Below is a summary of the
key individuals whose actions shape the fate of Nulb.
Madame Selentis (alias Lady Nysera
Krivaltis) Click here
Once thought dead after her family’s disgrace, Nysera
reemerges in Nulb under the alias Madame Selentis.
Installed by Hedrack himself, she commands a band of
hardened Temple-backed enforcers from the Old
Mayor’s Manor, overseeing smuggling and logistics in
service of the Temple of Elemental Evil.
Role: Temple Agent and Logistics Overseer
Motivation: To rebuild her power through loyalty to
the Temple and tighten her hold on Nulb.
True Allegiance: The Scarlet Brotherhood seeded
Elvanna in Nulb’s brothel years ago.
Sir Torvel Hawkes Click here
A scarred veteran of the Battle of Emridy Meadows,
Torvel abandoned his noble path to become Selentis’s
sworn protector, serving as both her shield and sword.
Role: Loyal Knight and protector of Selentis.
Motivation: To redeem Nysera’s honor through
devotion and military strength.
Selentis’s Bandits Click here
Assigned by Smigmal Redhand, these hardened
brigands patrol Nulb’s key sites and guard Selentis’s
operations. Their allegiance is less about loyalty to her
and more about Temple backing—but their presence
marks Selentis as a force to be reckoned with.
Role: Selentis’s Enforcers
Motivation: To enforce Selentis’s will and ensure
Temple operations in Nulb.
Romag, Prophet of Earth
Operating through Dick Rentsch and the Waterside
Hostel, Romag manipulates Nulb’s factions to advance
the Earth Temple. His quiet strength is subtle but
pervasive, undermining Selentis.
Role: Earth Temple Leader and Manipulator
Motivation: Expand the Earth Temple’s power.
Belsornig, Prophet of Water
Belsornig’s alliance with Captain Tolub strengthens the
Water Temple’s grip on piracy and trade. His network
destabilizes Selentis’s authority.
Role: Water Temple Leader and Pirate Patron
Motivation: Control Nulb’s waterways and
outmaneuver rival prophets.
Captain Tolub Click here
Master of the Iron Dagger, Tolub is equal parts pirate
and politician. His partnership with Belsornig funds his
ventures, but his growing ambitions put him into
frequent conflict with Selentis and her Temple masters.
Role: Pirate Captain of the Iron Dagger
Motivation: Dominate Nulb’s river trade and amass
wealth while strengthening the Water Temple.
Grud Squinteye Click here
Tolub’s ruthless right hand, Grud relishes bloodshed
and intimidation. His cruel reputation cements the
pirates’ stranglehold on Nulb’s docks.
Role: Lieutenant of the Iron Dagger
Motivation: Prove himself as the deadliest enforcer.
Dick Rentsch Click here
Owner of the Waterside Hostel, Dick poses as a genial
innkeeper while secretly serving the Earth Temple. He
uses his position to collect intelligence and funnel
resources for Romag, always scheming to undercut
Selentis’s growing power.
Role: Hostelier and Earth Temple Agent
Motivation: Expand Earth Temple influence.
Wat Click here
The Waterside Hostel’s bartender hides a more
dangerous role—an informer for the Fire Temple and

Wat stirs the pot in Nulb’s deadly rivalries.
Role: Bartender and Fire Temple Spy
Motivation: Aid the Fire Temple by sabotaging its

Skole Click here
The Boatman’s Tavern is Skole’s domain. Outwardly

Role: Proprietor of the Boatman’s Tavern, Water
Temple Collaborator
Motivation: Gain wealth and influence by balancing

Lodriss Click here
The hidden power behind the Boatman’s Tavern,

instincts make her a dangerous rival—or ally—

A Guide to Running a Paladin or Cleric in Nulb without
Breaking Temple Secrecy
Core Premise
A paladin or cleric entering Nulb is like a flare in the
dark—every faction notices at once. Normally, such holy
figures would be swiftly killed, but Hedrack’s orders
forbid unnecessary provocations:
“No martyrs. No crusades. Nulb must remain a den of bandits,
not a holy battlefield.”
Thus begins a dangerous game of shadow politics. The
Paladin is both a threat and a tool.
Faction Reactions
Hedrack (via Madame Selentis): Spies track the
party, feeding false leads about pirate feuds and
splinter cults. Goal: waste their time.
Romag & Waterside Hostel: Friendly outlaw
innkeeper through Dick Rentsch. Wat spreads
stories of “bogus cults in the swamps.”
Belsornig & Boatman’s Tavern: Pirates pick staged
fights; false intel about smugglers and fire-cults.
Alrrem’s Agents: Deflect inquiries with fiery zeal, but
always redirect zealots toward Earth’s Shattered
Monastery or Water’s River Fort, promising
“heretics” ripe for cleansing.
Smigmal Redhand: Prefers ambushes outside town
to discredit the Paladin.
Feldrin & Gefreid Patris: Delay shipments through
the Hunting Cabin; avoid exposure.
Running the Stay at the Hostel
Day 1: Quiet observation. Locals grow silent, Dick
plays genial host.
Day 2–3: Staged brawls, gossip of splinter cults,
minor thefts.
Day 4+: Coordinated effort to frame or discredit the
Paladin, forcing them to leave.
DM Note: Stress the tension. NPCs always seem to
know more than they say. Conversations hush when the
Paladin enters. Shadowy figures linger at the edges.
Constable Edrick Malvaar
The Paladin may seek out the Constable for law and
order. Instead they find a broken man:
“Law in Nulb? Friend, I’m a scarecrow in a storm. The badge
shines, but the wind blows me where the pirates tell me.”
Malvaar quietly deflects the Paladin toward “safe”
targets—rivals of the faction currently keeping him alive.
Reactions:
If pressed for justice: He warns the Paladin of
“splinter cults” in the hills, parroting the faction that
currently owns his leash.
If threatened: Malvaar folds instantly, begging for his
life. He has no real power and knows it.
DM Use: Malvaar is a narrative mirror—showing the
cost of compromise, the collapse of law, and the futility
of one honest badge in a den of knives.
DM Guidance
Every NPC Has a Script: Locals are briefed on how
to handle outsiders.
False Leads with Just Enough Truth: Outposts exist,
but they’re rival strongholds.
Social Pressure: Prices rise, rooms vanish, allies
disappear. Subtle hostility escalates.
Optional Reward: The “patron” faction may grant
coin or gear, tightening false alliances.
Misdirection Through Rivalries
Temple leaders weaponize their own rivalries to divert holy adventurers. Each faction sends the Paladin toward an
enemy outpost—real cultists, but of another Temple.

1. Waterside Hostel of Nulb
seedy stronghold of secrets on the bank of
Imeryds Run. The Waterside Hostel is a
cracked jewel in Nulb’s gutter crown—a
tangle of tavern, inn, and haven for outlaws
that clings to the riverbank like a leech.
Stone foundation. Timber bones. Smokestained rafters. Its rooms creak, the ale is
sour, if something bites you in the night, pray it’s a rat.
Owner: Dick Rentsch (covert operative of the Earth
Temple under Romag)
Function: Inn, tavern, smuggling waystation
Atmosphere: Perpetual tinderbox—kegs of menace
“Rooms are cheap. Lives cheaper.” —Local saying
scratched into the outhouse door.
Physical Features
Two stories: field-stone foundation, patched timber
walls; lean-to sheds and a rickety gallery facing the
river. The roofline sags under too many winters.
Tavern Room: Low ceiling, smoke-thick air, lantern
light the color of old butter. Sound stacks under the
beams; whispers carry farther than you think.
Semi-Private Rooms (B–D): Cramped bunk rooms
with warped doors and rusted latches. Mildew,
sweat, and old blood in the boards. “Privacy” is
something you rent by the hour, lose by the minute.
Hostile Hospitality
Dick Rentsch: The gruff publican mask hides a
patient recruiter and sorter for the Earth Temple. He
weighs every guest for utility: coin, leverage, or
sacrifice. He smiles with his mouth, listens with his
eyes, and remembers everything.
Wat (barkeep): Quiet, sharp, and loyal—to Alrrem’s
Fire Temple. The bar is a cold war line: Earth vs.
Fire under the same roof. Each man tolerates the
other, Hedrack demands no schisms in Nulb.
Serving Staff
Dala & Pearl (cutpurse wenches; level-3 thieves):
pour ale, lift purses, start rumors.
Two manservants (level-2 fighters): sleep in the loft
above the gallery with chain, shields, light
crossbows, and orders to crack heads fast.
Hostel Structure (for DMs)
The Waterside was a decent inn but The Temple’s rise
rotted it into a predator den. Dick and Wat run a
laundering and staging house.
At night, the place transforms into a boiling pot of
violence and plotting. Brawls are common, secrets are
whispered over spilled ale, and death is always just one
drink away.

buys in bulk.

The Waterside Tavern at Night
By day, the Waterside is just another miserable tavern
in a miserable town. But at night, under the smoky haze
and guttering lanterns, it becomes something else: a
crucible of tension, where secrets flow with the ale and
violence simmers beneath the surface.
Wat – Night Bartender, Hidden Snake Click here
When the sun goes down, Wat takes the bar. Calm,
careful, always watching. He smiles with his mouth,
never his eyes. To the drunks, he’s just another barkeep.
To those who look closer, he’s something else entirely—
an agent of a rival Temple faction, using the tavern.
“One more for the road? Just remember who poured it.”
Dala – Cutpurse, Lover, Eyes of the Room Click here
Dala moves through the tavern like a knife in the dark—
fast, quiet, smiling just enough to be dangerous. She’s
loyal to Dick, both as a lover and operator, and serves as
his eyes and ears among the nighttime crowd.
Pearl – Pickpocket, Watt’s Paramour Click here
Pearl plays the harmless wench act well—soft voice,
fluttered lashes, gentle touches. It’s all theater. She’s a
seasoned cutpurse, and Wat’s paramour, quietly helping
him unravel Dick’s hold on the tavern. She limits her
thefts to the drunk and the distracted—but if Wat asks,
she’ll go deeper. “She dances between tables, and walks
away with your purse.”
The Manservants – Bouncers, Brutes, Quiet Muscle
Two veterans guard the tavern floor once the lights dim
and the drinks spill. They act like hired fists—and they
are—but there’s a reason no one throws a second punch
in the Waterside after dark. They don’t need orders to
act—they just nod, grab, and break. “They don’t talk.
They just hurt you.”
Tensions Behind the Bar
Wat (Fire Temple → Alrrem): Plays the quiet
barkeep, feeds Pearl daytime tasks and Fire intel;
steers inquisitive guests toward Earth or Water
“splinter” problems—never Fire assets.
Dick Rentsch (Earth Temple → Romag): Smiles and
sorts; keeps a private favors-ledger; tolerates Wat
only under Hedrack’s “no schisms” orders.
Dala (night shift, Dick’s lover): Lifts purses from Firefriendly marks, runs Dick’s errands, passes keys and
gossip upstairs; will cut for him.
Pearl (day shift, Wat’s paramour): Picks only drunks,
hides Fire messages and coin, nudges do-gooders
toward rivals or out of town.

First Visit to the Waterside
Dick Rentsch sizes up the adventurers as they step into the
dimly lit tavern room of the Waterside Hostel, his eyes
narrowing slightly as he leans back against the worn bar. The
clink of mugs and low murmurs fill the background, but
Dick’s voice cuts through, gruff and unwelcoming.
“Ah, fresh faces in Nulb,” he begins, his tone dripping with
a mix of curiosity and suspicion. “Don’t see many new folks
‘round these parts without a reason. So, what brings you to our
little slice of paradise, eh? Don’t suppose you’re here for the
scenic views.” He chuckles darkly, a sound that doesn’t quite
reach his eyes. Dick straightens up, crossing his arms over
his broad chest, his gaze still fixed sharply on the
adventurers. “Listen, I run a tight ship here, and while you’re
welcome to fill your bellies and wet your whistles, understand
this—“ his voice drops, a stern edge creeping in, “I don’t
tolerate trouble. You start any, and you’ll find yourselves out on
your arses quicker than you can draw a blade. Got it?”
He pauses, letting his warning sink in, then continues, the
undercurrent of threat remains. “Now, tell me, what’s your
business in Nulb? Looking for work, trouble, or something else?
Be straight with me—it’ll save us all some time.”
Nightfall in the Tavern: Five Types
When dusk falls and the lanterns gutter low, the tavern
at the Waterside Hostel changes skin—becoming a
volatile crossroads of factions, fortunes, and quiet
knives. What looks like noise and drink is really sorting
and surveillance under Dick Rentsch’s and Wat’s eyes.
Day shift — Pearl (quiet, watchful)
“You plant yourselves like you own the table. You pay first. You
want small beer, river ale, or the black pitch that sticks to your
tongue? I’ve got bog grog if you like forgetting, marsh gin if you
like pain, and river whiskey if you like both—unless you’re
foolish enough for the Black Bottle. What’ll it be?”
1. Villagers
Fisherfolk, mud-cutters, and marsh scavengers from the
Nulb surrounds drift in for bitter ale and a short
forgetfulness. Most are lightly armed, if at all.
Presence: 2d6 early evening; half remain by last call.
Behavior: Wary of outsiders; speak names softly.
They know who’s new, who’s lying, and who’s
dangerous.
Hooks: A quiet nod toward a “wrong” crew (bandits
posing as traders); a missing cousin last seen near
the Hunting Cottage; rumors of “splinter cultists” in
the fens (bait from someone else’s mouth).
Tells: They look to Dick before they answer.
2. Rhennee Bargefolk
Nomadic riverfolk moor their flats and come under
Dick’s strict eye—coin first, always. Cloaked, close-knit,
and superstitious, they drink in a circle and leave in one.
Presence: 1d6+2; sometimes none for weeks.
Admittance: Pay in advance; tolerated, watched.
Hooks: Smuggling runs, river-only items, cryptic
rumors about pirate beacons or vanishing lights near
the old fort upriver. Trust is the price.
Tells: Never drink alone. Never forgive a slight. A
wrong word earns a cold stare and a colder river.
3. Bandits & Mercenaries
Ex-soldiers, blades-for-hire, and ambitious thugs fill
benches with their backs to timber. Some are passing
through; others are shopping for work—clean or not. A
Crimson Reaper or three might be here between jobs.
Composition: Small crews (1d4+1) or lone knives;
sometimes a recruiter in a clean cloak.
Motivation: Coin, cover, or a chance to join a faction.
Caravans: Overland trains swing through in warmer
months; the same men who “guarded” them last
week might “rob” them tomorrow.
Hooks: Protection rackets that lead to rival turf; a
“lost ledger” someone wants found (or destroyed); a
bounty with a suspicious sponsor.
4. River Pirates & Smugglers
Hardened by the Imeryd’s Run, they drink hard and
trade harder. Most Water crews prefer the Boatman’s
Tavern across the way, but a few show at the Waterside
to eavesdrop, fence small goods, or test Earth loyalties.
Trade Focus: Contraband, weapons, bodies to move,
and goods that don’t ask questions.
Interactions: Will deal with PCs—but never without
eyes on the door and a hand near steel.
Hook Potential: Smuggling routes, illegal crossings,
stolen manifests, whispers of a river-fort where tides
run wrong. They respect strength, fear open magic.

5. Temple Agents
Not all sinners wear their shame. Beneath the noise:
Earth Temple screeners, Fire Temple listeners, and the
occasional Greater Temple courier passing like a
shadow. They look like brigands, drovers, sellswords—
until you notice how they sit back-to-wall and how the
room tilts around them.
Presence: 1d2–1d4 “civilians” each night; roles vary.
Engagement Strategy: It can take days of careful
talk, observation, and cross-referencing to spot
patterns: who arrives after who, which table empties
when Dick stands, who never seems drunk.
Leave-Time Lurkers: Off-duty cult troops drift
through in twos and threes. They speak little of “the
Temple,” but stories leak—on purpose—guiding
zealots toward rival outposts (Earth → blame Water,
Fire → blame Earth/Water).
Undercover Operations: PCs won’t grasp the
Temple’s full reach here, but they may realize some
“bandits” are auditioning recruits or marking targets
for a handoff to Selentis’s guards.
Running the Room (DM Quick Use)
Which turf? The Waterside is Earth-leaning (Dick), with
Fire eyes (Wat) on the tap—Water business mostly stays
at the Boatman’s.
Watch the wenches: Dala (night, Dick’s lover)
nudges talk toward Earth’s interests; Pearl (day,
Wat’s paramour) steers curious do-gooders toward
Fire-friendly “splinter cults.”
Neutral with teeth: Brawls pop; feuds don’t—unless
someone important wants them to. If steel shows,
the loft guards start with crossbows.
Signal the net: A raised lantern or a wrong song
verse can summon “concerned locals” (bandits), a
“constable check” (Malvaar in defeat), or a quiet
Temple pickup that empties a table without a sound.

4. Boatman’s Black Market Hall
A Bazaar of Shadows
The Indoor Market Hall of Nulb is no innocent
gathering of farmers and craftsmen. This is a shadowy
bazaar—a place where masks are worn as often as
blades, and every smile carries the scent of betrayal.
Facing the village side of Nulb and operating under the
unspoken blessing of the Temple of Elemental Evil, this
market is where contraband breathes, and morality
withers.
The hall connects to the Boatman’s Tavern via an
internal door and includes large iron-bound oak doors
facing the street for patrons. Inside, a mix of open-air
stalls, curtained booths, and shelf-lined storage creates
a chaotic maze of illicit commerce. Lanterns cast amber
light across muddy flagstones, while incense and
swamp smoke disguise the rot of desperation.
General Layout and Access
Main Doors (Village Side): Two large, iron-bound
oak doors provide access for patrons. Usually
guarded by hired thugs who demand a visible
weapon or bribe.
Tavern Door: A connecting door leads into the
Boatman’s Tavern, allowing fluid movement
between drink and deal-making.
Warehouse Access: A third reinforced door leads to
the warehouse section, used for loading bulk
shipments or smuggled goods.
Market Features
Booth Rentals: Ramshackle booths, crates-ascounters, and curtain-shrouded tables line the hall.
Merchants pay a 10% tithe to Lodriss.
General Supply Section: A corner of tall wooden
shelves holds used common goods—rope, rations,
flint, tallow, torches, cheap weapons—no questions.
Artifact Alley: A narrow row of stalls specializing in
looted relics, cursed trinkets, and counterfeit holy
items. Some of it is real. All of it is dangerous.
The Shadow Nook: A curtained alcove where
poisons, alchemical brews, and deadly flora are sold
in hushed tones.
Slave Cage (Hidden): Iron cages located in the

First Steps into Nulb’s Black Market
The iron-bound oak doors groan as you push them inward,
coughing on the sudden shift from open air to smoke-thick
gloom. The hall is dim, stinking of mold and boiled leather,

Lanterns sway from rusted chains, casting crooked shadows

4. Boatman’s Warehouse
The Veins of Illicit Trade
Occupying nearly a third of the Boatman’s Tavern and
Market structure, the Warehouse of Nulb is the
lifeblood of the town’s shadow economy. Facing the
swamp-stained piers, this vast, sagging structure hums
with constant activity: loading, lifting, shifting, and
hiding. By day, it appears a busy trade depot; by night, it
becomes the artery of the Temple’s supply network,
where goods vanish from the manifest and reappear
bound for the dark halls of Elemental Evil.
Structural Layout & Entry Points
Pier-Side Cargo Doors: Two pairs of towering ironbound oak doors open to the Nulb docks. Wagons
and barges pull in under cover of fog, guided by dim
lanterns and thick rope lines.
Interior Access: Heavy oak doors connect to both the
Indoor Market Hall and the Boatman’s Tavern,
allowing fluid movement of goods, guards, and
informants.
Basement Access: A set of stone steps near the back
wall leads down into the basement storage and
secret prisoner room (see below).
Second Floor Access: A set of rickety wood stairs
hugs the western wall, climbing up to a gallery level
above. A wooden crane platform, centered in the
floor opening, is used to hoist crates from dock
wagons or basement lifts. A worn winch system
remains in service, though prone to loud creaks.
Main Floor Inventory
The main floor is a cluttered tangle of:
Crates, barrels, and pallets of standard goods.
Dried fruits, smoked meats, cheap wine, bulk cloth,
rope, wax, tools, nails, wagon parts, and mundane
arms and armor.
Stamped goods from Dyvers, Verbobonc, and Wild
Coast merchant houses.
Second Floor: Private Quarters &
Smuggler’s Cache
Crane Platform: Suspended over the warehouse
floor, this platform allows cargo to be lifted to or
from the second level.
Locked Cargo Room: Tucked behind a reinforced

Temple-bound goods: ritual supplies, sacred idols,
rare alchemical reagents, and blackmail documents.

Investigation or Thieves’ Tools to open).
Servant & Guard Rooms: A secured hallway leads to
the small sleeping quarters for Lodriss’s personal

hallway is patrolled and trapped against intruders.
Basement Level: Contraband Storage &
Secret Prison
The stone staircase from the main level descends into a
cool, dark storage space. Illicit trade goods such as
banned books, magical arms, stolen relics, elemental
charms, and poisons are stored here in moisture-proof
crates. A false wall in the rear of the basement leads to
the Secret Prisoner Room:
Five reinforced slave cages with shackles, lined with

Used for holding captives prior to transfer to Captain

Guarded discreetly by Crimson Reaper enforcers
under direct supervision of Lodriss.

You step into the warehouse and are hit by the stench of

4. Boatman’s Brothel
A Den Above the Mire
Perched above the reeking smoke and clamor of the
Boatman’s Tavern lies a place where shadows breathe
and perfume masks decay. Known by whisper and curse
as Elvanna’s Loft, the brothel occupies the second floor
of the tavern’s pier-facing structure—a crooked gallery
of rot and velvet. Here, in rooms cloaked with incense
and stained velvet, pleasure is a commodity, secrets are
currency, and the Temple of Elemental Evil recruits its
next acolytes.
The brothel’s ceilings sag with damp mildew, its walls
hum faintly with enchantments gone stale, and the
creak of each board underfoot feels like a warning.
Though it offers carnal delights and intoxicated respite,
Elvanna’s Loft is anything but comforting—it is a
trapdoor into corruption, a velvet sheath for the
Temple’s blade.
“The madame’s perfume is sweeter than blood—but not by
much.” — Naika, to a nervous client fumbling a coin purse
The Brothel’s Structure
A narrow stairwell near the tavern stage winds upward
to the second floor, depositing visitors into a lowceilinged, smoky lounge. At the top, beneath a warped
beam and a moth-eaten banner, sits a massive man with
arms like tree trunks and an iron cudgel across his lap.
The lounge itself is dimly lit by hanging lanterns with
cracked glass. A battered wooden table surrounded by
mismatched chairs stands near the center of the room.
A filthy, threadbare rug sprawls beneath it, its original
pattern long obscured by boot scuffs and spilled drink.
Several smaller side tables, greasy with age, line the
walls for serving drinks. In one corner, a faded lounge
couch—its fabric worng—offers a semi-private space for
hushed dealings and negotiations.
Several locked wooden doors line the lounge’s
perimeter, each leading deeper into the brothel’s
twisted warren of secrets and sin.
This is Mardun the Brute, a fixture as permanent as
the scent of mold and musk. He watches all who enter
with glowering suspicion. Behind him, the hall divides
into a series of doors—each leading to chambers stained
by whispered transactions and darker rites.
Elvanna’s Private Chamber
The largest and most richly furnished room in the loft,
though “luxury” is relative. Velvet curtains droop from
rusted hooks, incense smolders in cracked braziers, and
a carved bedframe—reinforced with iron rings—rests
beneath a faded painting of a drowned city. Hidden
behind a tattered tapestry is a small shrine to the Elder
Elemental Eye, with offerings of blood-waxed coins and
black candles.
“Upstairs? You go with coin, or you go with chains.
Either way, you’re paying.” — Mardun the Brute
Mardun the Brute – Door and Doom
Click here Once a gladiator in the Pits of Elredd,

The Courtesans’ Quarters
Four beds, no privacy. A patchwork curtain provides the
illusion of division. Makeup kits, soiled gowns, charms,
and token jewelry are scattered across warped tables.
Here, the working girls sleep in shifts and pray for easy

Services and Tactics of the Loft
While the brothel offers the promise of companionship
