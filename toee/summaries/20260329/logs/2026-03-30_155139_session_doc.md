## Section — Zephyr — Ambush by Ghouls

The smell arrived before the sound. That sick, wet rot — like meat left in standing water, but with something worse underneath it, something that didn't belong to the natural world at all. We'd pulled back to our own territory to wait out the temple war, and I'd been calculating odds in my head, counting corridors, thinking about Romag and Hartsch and the seventeen ways this whole operation could collapse before morning.

Then that smell.

*Oh god. This again.*

They came out of the dark in a shambling line — four of them, and I recognized the faces. That was the worst part. Not strangers. Not random undead dredged up from some nameless grave. Earth Temple members. People we'd seen in corridors, nodded to while maintaining cover, filed away as *useful* or *obstacle* in the ongoing arithmetic of survival. Now raised by something with necromantic ambitions and sent wandering down here to scavenge whatever the temple war left behind.

"But, aren't they supposed to be, like, fighting upstairs?"

The question hung there, genuinely unanswered, which I found deeply unpleasant. Things were supposed to be fighting upstairs. That was the plan. That was *my* plan. Ghouls lurching into our territory, stinking of fresh death and bad magic, was not in the plan.

Sequoia moved fast — bless him — already working to set something at the door. But the ghouls rolled forward like they had somewhere to be, and whatever trap he was laying, they hit it before it was ready. A 1. The kind of number that makes you want to reconsider your life choices. The spring went early, the delay was nothing, and the ghouls kept coming.

Fine. Fine.

The nearest one hadn't reached me yet. Hadn't reacted. Was still in that lurching half-moment between shambling forward and noticing there was something in its way with opinions about being devoured. I've been paralyzed by ghouls before — we all have, at this point, the claws hit and your body just *stops*, which is a sensation I would describe as among my least favourite — so I have developed a strong professional interest in killing them before they get the opportunity.

The dagger was already in my hand.

Twenty-seven damage. Clean. The creature dropped where it stood like someone had simply switched it off.

*They attack. They die.* Except this time, they didn't even get the attack.

I was already tracking the other three when Calmer happened.

The man has been knocked unconscious more times than I can count, raised from the dead once, used as bait at least twice — that last one I will own, that was me, and I stand by the tactical reasoning even if the execution had some notes. He has been burned by fire priests and drowned by Thalsor's magic and told to *be ready to die* before a ceremony that I knew would probably try to kill him. He has been patient about this in his own particular way, which is to say he has not been patient at all, and there has been escalating friction, and the word Zinnia used was *identity drift* which I refuse to engage with directly.

None of that mattered in the moment Calmer let the undead have it.

The Turn Undead came out of him like something that had been stored under pressure for weeks. The holy wrath hit the room — all three remaining ghouls failing, every last one of them — and they *fled*. Not shambled. Not lurched. Fled in terror, taking damage as they ran, the divine power chasing them through the door like a creditor who's finally located a defaulter.

The GM described it. He said Calmer was going *"yes, yes, yes, yes, yes, yes, yes, yes, yes, yes, yes, yes!"* The cursing started. *This is how the power of—* and then more cursing. The sentence never finished. He didn't need it to.

I watched the war cleric come fully unhinged in the best possible way, and I had two thoughts at once. The first was: *this is what two failed attempts on the wraith looks like when it finally finds a target that can't resist.* The frustration releasing itself, all of it, through the conduit of divine power and vocabulary I won't repeat.

The second thought was quieter and I didn't linger on it.

The second thought was: *is that him, or is that something else wearing his voice?*

Because Calmer hates undead. Calmer always hated undead. That's not new — a cleric of St. Cuthbert spitting fire at animated corpses is the most theologically coherent thing he does. But the *yes yes yes yes yes* had an edge to it. The way Zinnia described it — the concern he won't say out loud while framing it as purely operational — I've filed it and I look at it sometimes. Two failed turns on the wraith. The ceremony, Romag's toast, the creeping months inside a temple where Evil is spelled with a capital letter and they keep expecting you to mean it.

Sequoia put an arrow in the back of the last one before it made it out the corridor. Clean finish. That's Sequoia.

The cursing faded. The smell didn't, not entirely. And I looked at the dropped ghoul — my ghoul, killed before it could act, the assassin's precision Zinnia respects and won't say he respects — and then at the door where the others had fled.

"Maybe we should go find that guy?"

Because the ghouls had been Earth Temple members. Recently dead, recently raised. Someone with necromantic power was working in this dungeon right now, during our carefully engineered war, with access to our people's corpses and apparent interest in deploying them.

That was a problem. That was several problems layered on top of each other.

The sounds of Calmer's breathing slowly returned to something resembling normal. I noted it. Filed it. Did not examine it further.
