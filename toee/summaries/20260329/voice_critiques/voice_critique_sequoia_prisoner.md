## Flags

### [1] Mechanical scan A — em-dash (appositive)

> I saw him do that thing — the small stillness, the focused attention.

**Why:** Narration-level em-dash before an appositive list. Colon is cleaner and more Sequoia (direct, declarative): "I saw him do that thing: the small stillness, the focused attention."

### [2] Mechanical scan A — em-dash (result clause) / table-voice intrusion

> Twenty-seven on the roll — the shackles came apart like they'd been waiting for me.

**Why:** Two issues. First, the em-dash connects cause to result where a colon or period would be cleaner. Second, "on the roll" is dice-table reference language that breaks the in-character narration frame — other sections anchor mechanics in fiction rather than referencing the dice directly.
**Suggested rewrite:** "The lock gave cleanly. The shackles came apart like they'd been waiting for me." — or keep the roll number and drop the table reference: "Twenty-seven. The shackles came apart like they'd been waiting for me."

### [3] Mechanical scan A — em-dash pair (parenthetical)

> I looked at them — really looked, which I try not to do too long because it makes the arithmetic harder — and thought about how many locks I still had to pick tonight

**Why:** Em-dash pair around a long parenthetical interruption. Comma parenthetical preserves the rhythm: "I looked at them, really looked (which I try not to do too long because it makes the arithmetic harder), and thought about..."

## Verdict

The best Sequoia voice in the document. The lock-pick satisfaction paragraph ("There's a kind of satisfaction in a clean pick that I'd be embarrassed to admit to anyone. It's just physics, really. Everything has a mechanism. Everything has a point where it opens.") is exactly the spec — one-liner instinct, practical vocabulary, forward-oriented reasoning. "His mother had raised him right, apparently" is perfect deadpan. All three flags are mechanical (em-dashes, one table-voice slip). No re-narration needed; spot-edit the punctuation.
