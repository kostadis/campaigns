# Chapter 26 Attrition 

## Zephyr — The Sealed Greater Temple

The goblins were the first thing we saw when we pushed into the Air Temple.

Moving fast toward Kelno's territory, arms piled with everything they'd stripped from the dead. Equipment, robes, the kind of things you take when you're not sure there will be another opportunity. They kept their eyes down. We kept ours forward. Nobody said anything.

*This again.* The war I'd helped arrange, moving around me in looting parties.

The Water Temple door had burn marks on it. The Fire Temple had tried to force an entry and failed, or stopped, or been stopped. Hard to say which. The bodies along the passage didn't clarify matters. Looted already, by the time we got there. Everything useful gone.

The wraith was at the entrance to the Greater Temple when we arrived. Feasting. Not hunting. Feasting, which is a distinction that mattered to my continued good health. He looked up from whatever unfortunate corpse had his attention and called out with the warmth of something that finds everything amusing.

"Your turn will come! Don't worry, my little pets! Your turn will come."

The last time I'd stood this close to him, I'd come away with something missing: not a wound exactly, more like a portion of myself that the cold reached into and found worth keeping. That feeling has not entirely left.

Sequoia said, "Lucky me."

I agreed with the sentiment.

Calmer, naturally, saw an opportunity.

"I can kill him! I can kill him, I can so totally kill him!"

"It's okay," I said.

I meant: *the wraith knows things.* Calmer apparently heard the first word and not the rest, because he subsided, still looking at the wraith with that hungry ecclesiastical expression he gets when he's found something worth smiting. I appreciate the energy. I have learned not to rely on it as a tactical plan.

We pressed on toward the Greater Temple entrance. I had, in point of fact, never been through.

"Have you gone through the Greater Temple?" I asked.

No one had.

"Oh, we could go there."

Someone agreed. We moved.

The door didn't cooperate. Something held it from the other side: braced, or barricaded, or committed to not opening. "Or dash through," I said. Calmer began hammering at it instead, which achieved the same noise with considerably less tactical elegance, and the wraith found this very funny.

I turned back to face him.

The wraith had been here the whole time. Watching everything we'd done in this temple: the dead we'd made, the war we'd triggered, the bodies lining every passage we'd walked through. Information was information, and the wraith was currently the only entity in the room willing to talk.

"And Gary Race," I said, "can you tell us what's happening?"

He looked at me with the amusement of something that has eaten recently and is deciding whether to eat again.

"I could tell you," he said, "or I could kill you—"

He paused. His attention drifted to the bodies around him, the ones we'd arranged, directly or otherwise.

"But you provided me so much tasty food! Food!"

Then: "I want to thank you about something."

"Yes," I said.

I let it run. The wraith wanted to be thanked. It had been alone in this corridor for weeks, feasting on whatever the war left behind, with nobody willing to call it by name and ask it what it knew. And here was someone doing exactly that. I gave it that. I could feel the room tilt: the wraith no longer performing menace but something closer to collegial warmth. Peer to peer. I'd given it an audience. The price of the audience was conversation, and conversation was exactly what I'd come for.

"With all the bodies you've created," he announced, with the satisfaction of a man presenting a ledger that has resolved favorably, "I now have minions."

The specters materialized one by one. He counted them out. One, two, three. Four, five. Mostly Earth Temple forces. I recognized the remnants of robes, the insignia, the general silhouette of people we had, in one way or another, contributed to killing. Six, seven.

Seven specters. Raised from bodies we were responsible for.

*Good,* I thought. *The wraith is building something. We need to make sure it builds it for us.*

"Why don't you go fight the other temples?" I said.

He did not answer. He was busy being pleased with himself.

Then the voice.

Cold. From behind the door Calmer had been hammering. Every one of us stopped where we stood.

"The Greater Temple is sealed until the battle is over. No one gets in, no one gets out."

The wraith laughed harder.

"Where are you?" I asked the door.

The wraith answered instead.

"You see, what you don't understand, you poor little fools, you poor little delightful little fools — this happens every so often, and when it does, the Greater Temple seals the lesser temples and waits for the battle to end. You're going to be trapped in here like the rest of us, and maybe you'll be like one of my servants."

He said "my servants" with obvious satisfaction. He was counting on it.

I let it settle. We were inside. The door was sealed. The wraith had seven specters and a month's supply of Earth Temple dead to expand the collection. The voice on the other side had decided the war was not yet over.

"Who is… what is the Greater Temple?" I asked.

"The Greater Temple," the wraith said, with the tone of something explaining things it finds obvious, "is the deep temple. It's the people who are really in charge."

The people who are really in charge.

I'd been wrong about who sat at the top before. Romag. Then Hartsch. Then us, for a moment. Every time I thought I'd found the ceiling, another one appeared above it.

"I'd like to meet them," I said.

The voice from behind the door answered.

"Survive the fight, and maybe you'll get a chance."

Terms. Don't die in the war we started, and there would be an introduction. I've worked with worse opening offers.

I looked at the seven specters. I looked at the wraith. I looked at the door.

"Why don't we camp out next door?" I said.

Nobody moved toward camping.

The seven specters were, as it turned out, a relevant consideration. The wraith had them and was not going to stop having them. The door was sealed. We were, by the wraith's own enthusiastic assessment, excellent raw material for future servants.

"Alright," I said. "We'll get out and come back later."

The wraith shouted after us as we moved.

"I can't wait till you come back here! I will be waiting!"

He meant it warmly. The absolute confidence of something that has already decided the outcome and is merely permitting time to catch up.

The tiefling kept walking.

*Survive the war. The door opens when the battle ends. We'll be on the right side of it.*

## Zinnia — Return to the Earth Temple

The arrowheads were the first thing I saw.

Two positions, six guards, all of them reading our emergence from the stairwell as a threat. Which, given the state of the second level, was a reasonable assessment. The party had been gone. Things had not gone well. The gap between our departure and our return had been filled with sounds they couldn't account for and a body count they couldn't afford.

Their leader got his voice out before his men got their fingers to the triggers.

"STOP! Stop! Don't fight!"

Then the recognition. Then the question.

"Where the hell have you been?"

Zephyr gave him the short version. "Uh, we've been fighting."

"Where?"

"We opened the door for you."

It was enough to get us moving and not enough to get us trusted. That gap was still open. The leader took it, set it somewhere between "acceptable" and "later," and redirected us.

"Yeah, well… well, after you open the door, you guys disappeared. Things have not gone well. Hartsch needs you right now. Go to the temple."

"We're going," Zephyr said.

We went.

---

I counted the soldiers before I looked at Hartsch.

The number was wrong. Not collapse-of-the-faction wrong, but wrong enough that I counted them twice before I trusted what I was seeing. Whatever had happened while we were on the second level had cost the Earth Temple more than it should have for a faction holding its own ground. These were men who had started this war expecting to win it quickly. The quick part was behind them.

Then I looked at Hartsch.

Exhausted. Dirty. Bloodied in ways that suggested proximity to the fighting, not command distance from it. He had been in it, or near enough. The muffled boom of something two levels below, or in a corridor we weren't in, settled into the stone under our feet and went quiet.

Hartsch looked at us.

"What do we do next?"

Four words. The authority transfer was complete.

I had watched this happen with other commanders, on other operations: the moment when a man who has been pretending to be in charge finally stops pretending. Hartsch had held the Earth Temple together through a three-way war he didn't fully understand, with forces he was losing, and now he was asking us. The Obsidian Edge. The people who had engineered this situation from underneath him.

*Information he doesn't get to have. We built this; he inherited it.*

Zephyr took it first.

"We wait it out."

Hartsch stared at him. "What do you mean we waited out?"

"So… we'll wait till the other temples stop fighting. Just to… okay, because… but right now, it's not the best time to go on the offense."

Hartsch's voice went sharp.

"But that… but that is Romarg's passive strategy!"

There it was. A name that stung him, attached to a strategy that shamed him, and Zephyr had just handed it back as a reasonable plan. I caught it. Hartsch would rather lose aggressively than win the way that man had. Whatever it cost him.

That was leverage. I didn't say so.

Zephyr pivoted. "Hmm… well, the situation is evolving."

"What do you mean, the situation is evolving?"

"Well, you can go to the Obsidian Edge and… we'll hold on for it here with a couple of troops."

Hartsch looked at him. "Is that wise? For me to go on the vanguard?"

"Well, take half the troops, we'll stay here with the other half. We can't leave our territory undefended."

He was past strategy now. I could see it in his hands. The questions weren't tactical. He didn't know where to stand anymore.

"Where should I put the troops? It's all gone to hell!"

"No, it's fine, it's fine. Just go… go fight the… the water in the fire temple. I'll hold down for it here. You got it."

Zephyr was improvising. I could see it register: the soldiers' eyes going to each other instead of to him. I looked at the soldiers around us. Bloodied. Fewer than before. This was supposed to have been an easy win, and they knew whose faction had started the war. They were watching us, the Obsidian Edge, waiting to see if we were about to disappear on them again.

"Alright, we will leave the vanguard."

Hartsch's shoulders changed.

"So, uh, we'll charge once more to the Water Temple!"

"Yes, let's go."

But Hartsch wasn't done. "So, should we… should we deploy any of our forces to defend anything, or should we just, like, charge straight ahead? Like… is there anything we should do to protect anything?"

"No. Come, everyone in charge, we will make a final stand. It's time we got it to Temple."

The planning found its footing. Someone put the question about relative strength: who was winning, who was weakest, where to push.

"Who's the strongest? The Water Temple. The weakest is the Air Temple. The next is the Fire Temple."

Zephyr absorbed this and turned to us.

"Okay, here's the plan, guys. We'll go after the Air Temple. Take all their treasure. And from there, we'll see if we attack that water."

It was the right call. I had read the same conclusion in Hartsch's face when he listed the order. He had not said it with pride. He had listed it the way a man lists damage he's already accepted, in order, without argument, like reading something he already knew.

Hartsch looked around the room. Something in him settled. Not confidence. Something close enough to move on.

"Very well! This is the most heavily defended part of the territory. We will stay here for the night, and we will attack again."

We rested.

The sounds reached us from corridors we weren't in. The war was running on its own momentum. Good. It didn't need us to keep burning.

## sequoia — War Council and Hartsch's Blunder

The war room smelled like men who hadn't slept. Hartsch was in the middle of it, asking what to do next like a man who'd never once had to answer that question himself.

Zephyr said wait. Let the temples bleed each other out. It was the correct answer, the cheap answer, the one that cost us nothing.

Hartsch didn't like it. He brought up Romag: what Romag would have wanted, what Romag built, all of it in the voice of a man reaching for borrowed authority. We'd killed Romag. I didn't say that. Nobody in the room knew it but us, and there is no need for him to know.

So we asked which temple was weakest. Air. Thin guard, thinner walls. Zephyr proposed we hit it, and Hartsch agreed too fast. Agreement was the only thing he could produce reliably. Then he asked for the night to sort deployment.

That was fine. That was the part where a leader sleeps on it and wakes up with orders.

We didn't sleep in the main temple. Zephyr wanted the Romag room. *The special room. Because we're apostles, apparently.* The dead man's quarters, the one place in this building nobody would think to bother us. I watched Hartsch hand the whole question of where the apostles slept off to Zephyr, and Zephyr hand the question of deployment back to Hartsch, *you're the leader, you should be able to do this*, and I thought: nobody in this room wants to hold the thing they're holding.

*Are we sure they're still alive?* I meant the Broken Blades. The men we'd recruited. The men whose lives were now sitting in the hands of a panicking quartermaster who couldn't decide where to put a chair.

Hartsch asked to sleep on it. We let him. We went to the dead man's room.

In the morning he was gone.

I knew before we found the note. An empty office in a building this nervous isn't a man stepping out for air. *There you go.* I'd half expected it. Being half-right is still wrong.

The note was on his desk in his own hand. *I couldn't wait! I sent the Broken Blades to attack!*

I read it twice. Not because it was hard to read. Because I recognized it.

I know that man. I've redistributed treasure when nobody was looking. I've taken quests without asking the others. I've decided, on my own, in the dark, that I knew better than the people standing next to me. *I couldn't wait* is a sentence I have lived inside of. The difference I tell myself is the consequence. I don't know if it's true.

Hartsch was the version of me with no eye for consequence and no patience and a war to play with. We'd put him at the top of the Earth Temple because he was pliable. Pliable is just another word for a man who'll do anything as long as someone else seems to want it, including invent the someone.

*That's odd.* No. *That's not good.*

Back at the temple there were fewer people standing in it than the night before. Empty spaces where guards had been. An attack in the night, and it had cost us more than we could spare. Hartsch confessed it in pieces: he'd sent the Blades down to stage a raid on the Water Temple, and heard nothing back, and now if we lost them we'd lose the guards too, and *you have to do something.*

Zephyr slapped him. Walked up and slapped the panic right across his face. It didn't take. *What do you want me to do, what do you want me to do, what do you want me to do*, the same three words on a loop. He was stuck in the question.

Then Zinnia looked at the map where he'd marked his glorious initiative, and looked at him, and got the rest of it. I watched his face go still the way it does when the lie underneath finishes coming up.

He hadn't just sent them without asking.

He'd told them the orders came from us.

There it was. The whole man, in one move. He'd made the unilateral call and then pinned the name to it that wasn't his, ours, so that when the Blades died in the water it would be the Obsidian Edge that sent them. I'd have admired the instinct if it weren't aimed at me. A small man caught in a big machine, reaching for the only lever he understood: someone else's authority.

So the Air Temple was off. You don't run a two-pronged raid when one prong is already pinned and bleeding under the wrong banner.

"Okay, so we'll go straight to the Broken Blades," Zephyr said.

Use them as the distraction. Let Hartsch advance from the east, if Hartsch could find east. Go pull our men out of the water before the lie killed them.

The plan had switched. It switches. You don't spiral. You pivot.

We went to get them back.

## zinnia — The Failed Raid and Rescuing the Broken Blades

The wraith was where it always was, at the choke point, seven specters in a line behind it. The same choke point it had always been, better staffed. Necrotic cold sat in the corridor ahead of us, the kind that doesn't pass when the thing that makes it moves on. We had abandoned the Air Temple assault to come back here. Operationally, that was a problem for later. Right now there was a dead thing between us and the men we'd sent to die.

Zephyr didn't slow down. "I don't have time for this one," he told it.

The wraith found that amusing. *What do I get in exchange?* It looked us over and offered terms: live bodies, or intelligence. *Tell me where the next attack is coming so I can feast on the dead.* A merchant. Even here, even dead, everyone is running an angle.

"Water temple," Zephyr said. No hesitation. Zephyr gave up Water Temple dead in exchange for passage. The wraith got what it wanted. We got through. I noted what it now knew about us.

The thing deflated. *Oh, okay, that wasn't very exciting. Feasted on them enough. I was hoping for some Earth Temple blood.* I marked it. A wraith with a grudge against the faction we wear the badges of is leverage, and it is also a threat, and the two are not separable. Zephyr told it its turn would come. It smiled, and it let us pass, and I watched it calculate the whole way through. *It remembers we came past. It remembers we paid in someone else's people.* Good intelligence and bad debt, in the same transaction.

Then the door, and behind the door, the slaughterhouse.

Dren Halveth had me by the arm before I'd cleared the threshold. Battered, bloodied, his eyes those of a man who'd already counted his dead twice and gotten the same number. Behind him the survivors stood, hands slick to the wrist, staring at nothing. The stench of opened flesh filled the whole chamber. These were the Broken Blades. We had recruited them. Dren had liked Sequoia. The man who liked Sequoia and the man standing in this doorway were the same person with a lot of dead between them.

"You didn't say it would be a slaughterhouse!" he said. "Romag's replacement, Hartsch is a mad man! We're being fed to a meat grinder."

"It was not us who sent you here, but Hartsch," Zephyr said. "However, we are still deeply saddened by your — whatever happened here."

*Hartsch.* I let the name sit. He'll be dealt with. That wasn't reassurance for them. It was a note to myself, the kind you don't say out loud because saying it spends it. He's our nominal superior and he's a liability and the day is coming when those two facts resolve.

But Dren needed something now, and condolences weren't going to hold these men together. They were one bad word from breaking and running into the corridor we'd just bargained our way through, straight into the thing that wanted Earth Temple blood. I don't rally people. I observe, I wait, I move. This was the other thing, the thing I almost never reach for, because it can't be faked and faking it is all I do.

I stepped in front of them. Gore-soaked, hollow-eyed, finished men. I'd showed up. That mattered more than anything I could say. I'd walked in personally, past the wraith, into the room nobody sane would enter. So I said the true thing.

"I don't know what your plans are. But whatever your plans are, you are with us. We are with you."

I knew it the moment it left me. The exact word. I could see it land. Something behind Dren's eyes came back. The men straightened. They looked at me and gave it back, the same words, a pledge: *whatever your plans are, you are with us. We are with you.* Not loyalty to an order. Loyalty to the person who showed up when the order left them there. I don't do this often. When I do, it lands.

Zephyr moved them out. "Come backwards, we'll hold the line for now." Fall back to the Earth Temple, rest there, but if they were true warriors, he told them, they could stay with us. Half a retreat, half a recruitment. He was already recruiting. He never leaves a room without adding to the count.

They saw the problem before we named it: the wraith blocked the only exit, seven specters at its side, a choke point that would keep killing them. "Alright, we'll deal with this, Dren," Zephyr said.

The blades looked to Calmer. "Do you need our help, boss?"

"I don't need any help to deal with the undead," Calmer said. Then, quieter, the hint pitched at us and not at them: it would be better if they weren't here to see him call on St. Cuthbert's power. *Operational security.* Even now, even with these men pledging themselves to us, the one true thing about him is the thing he can't let witnesses see.

"Alright, calm," Zephyr said. "Let's go."

## calmer — Battle with the Wraith and Specters

I had laid the ground before I crossed the threshold. A weapon of conjured light hung at my shoulder, a thing of judgment that moved when I willed it. And the chamber itself, I filled it. A golden aura of summoned spirits, the radiance of a power I have not said aloud in this place in longer than I want to count, pooling across the brown marble and its veins of black, waiting for something dead to step into it. To any wretched, hollow thing that should not exist in the world, that aura is fire. To me it is the warmest thing I have felt down here.

So I charged in. Boldly. I want that recorded.

And the Wraith, the one that had laughed at me twice, that had stood between us and the way down for too long, turned its empty face and said, almost delighted, "Oh, you're back!"

"Uh, yeah," Zephyr said.

Then the room moved.

Sequoia hit a specter and it came apart, gone before it understood it was dying. Zephyr stepped into a second one, his blade finding the seam before the thing had so much as twitched, and it unraveled. He drifted to a third and clipped it, a glancing thing, nothing fatal, but their numbers were already falling in our favor and I let myself believe, for one breath, that this would be clean.

It was not going to be clean.

The Wraith flew. It crossed into my golden field and the radiance bit into it. I felt the working strain and pull, saw it stagger and slow in the light. Good. *Good.* And then it reached me anyway.

I have been hurt before. This was not that. This was cold pouring in where warmth should be: not a wound, an absence, something reaching past the plate and the prayers and taking. Not heat applied. Heat *removed.* I felt the floor of myself drop out and knew, instantly and completely, that I was at the very bottom of what a man can hold and still stand. And worse, I knew this particular cold does not mend. No light I carry would fill that back in. I was going to walk the rest of this with the door open.

Something else came after the cold, some second clawing thing trying to follow the first into me. And it didn't. I held. Clean, complete, the kind of holding that surprises even the one doing it. I shut the door in its face and it did not get through.

The specters swarmed then, three of them grasping at me, and missed, every one, and paid the toll for being in my light: the gold searing them as they reached. One lost its nerve and tried to flee the aura. *No.* I caught it on the way out, before I could think it through, the answer already leaving me, the old draconic gift flaring in my chest, a strength it could not meet, a fist of force that flung it back broken into the radiance it was running from. Zinnia finished it before it could try again.

I was at the very bottom of myself, one touch from gone, and I knew it. I want that recorded too, because of what I did next.

I did not heal. I did not run. I am a warrior of St. Cuthbert and we do not run from the dead. And besides, there was nothing left in me that could undo what the Wraith had taken. So I reached for the thing I had reached for twice before, in two other rooms, on two other nights, and had it die in my hands both times. The creature had *laughed.* The rite of turning, the call that breaks the dead. The third time.

I called it. The light went out of me in a single blast of judgment, and the room of dead things flinched as one. And they could not hold. The Wraith could not hold. Specter after specter could not hold. They came apart in the radiance, all of them but one. Four nights of carrying this, and they just came apart.

The Wraith was dead. The thing that had mocked me. *Dead.*

I could not contain it. I did not try.

"It worked! It worked!" The words came out of me without permission, without dignity, four nights of it failing in my hands and now this. "Who's the man? Who is the man?"

"That's you," Zephyr said.

Zinnia put down the last surviving specter: missed the first strike, took it on the second, and when it still refused, still clinging to the last of itself, he flowed into another and it was simply over.

The passage was clear. No more cold thing standing guard over the way down, no more creature that murders your friends and stands them back up as itself. Two roads to the second level now, and one of them open. A real victory, the strategic kind, the kind Hartsch's war wanted.

I stood in the cooling gold with the door still open in my chest, unable to be healed past the bottom of myself, and I was, against all sense, grinning.

## sequoia — The Defector's End

We were turning to go up. First level, the war council, Hartsch waiting on us like we owed him something. Calmer had just put the wraith down and had nothing left in him. I could see it in how he stood, all the color gone, nothing left.

Then movement. Down the corridor, against the brown marble, a bugbear stumbling toward us with a sack of coins, both arms around it. Babbling. Kelno had promised him safety. Kelno had promised him gold.

Zephyr read it wrong first. "So… You're for the water temple."

No. Not water. I watched the understanding change on his face the same time it changed on mine. This one was ours.

"Where are you from?" Zephyr pushed. The answer came out of the babbling. Earth Temple. *Our* wall. One of the bugbears who was supposed to be standing between us and the things that wanted us dead, and he was walking out with somebody else's money in his hands.

"And you're leaving?" Zephyr again. Then, when that didn't land: "Okay. Remember who's the most powerful temple, bugbear?"

Calmer didn't wait for the appeal to work. He came in low and loud with next to nothing left in him, a breath from collapse, the wraith's cold still in his chest, and he said, "Don't mess with me, man! I just killed the Wraith!" Then, louder: "This is how the earth temple rules, with power and force!" He was getting into it. Too into it. Somewhere under the elemental cult armor the cleric was supposed to be wearing a costume, and the costume had started doing the talking. Not my problem right now.

The question came around to me. Chase him. Intimidate him. Threaten him. Find out what's going on.

"I think he's okay."

I didn't mean it the way it sounded. I meant the situation was clear, the read was finished, there was nothing here that needed working out. He was taking money from Kelno to change sides. That's the whole of it. A man who sells the wall he's standing on doesn't get a second wall.

"Uh, we're gonna go kill him, Ben."

I said the rest of it before anyone had moved, before he'd even understood it was happening. "One does not simply take bribes to defect." Because that's the whole reckoning of it. You can be bought. Everyone can be bought. But the buying happens before you take the post, not after, once you're inside the wall you're inside it, and the only exit anyone's going to honor is the one that doesn't have you walking through it.

Good thing I am small. He never clocked me coming.

The first cut found exactly where it needed to go. Clean. He never even turned. It took most of him in the one exchange. He was still standing. Barely. I had one more in me and there was no reason to save it.

He dies.

I stood over the body. "One does not simply take bribes to defect."

Then the part that actually mattered. I went through the sack: twenty gold, and I turned a coin in the light because something was bugging me about the weight of it. The stamp wasn't Kelno's. Wasn't water at all. Air Temple insignia, pressed clean into every piece.

*That's odd.*

*That's not good.*

Kelno hadn't bought him. Somebody upstairs had let us believe Kelno was the one pulling at our bugbears, and the real hand was a different one entirely. The gnolls came out of the wall to prove it, a secret door I hadn't found yet, which meant they'd been close enough to watch the whole thing, furious, talking about money. "We paid good money for that! Now we will get our money, and get food!"

"Didn't know you guys liked eating while dead, but okay."

Air Temple. Kelno had been recruiting, busily, badly, reaching over the Earth Temple's wall and peeling people off one frightened bugbear at a time. We engineered a three-way war and then went and stood at the top of one of the three sides, and the other two had noticed, and this was what noticing looked like: gold with the wrong stamp on it, in the hands of a man who used to guard our wall.

I pocketed the coins. Evidence and payment both. No sense leaving them for the gnolls.

## zephyr — Ambush of the Air Temple Gnolls

The bugbear was still settling into the floor when the secret door coughed up its replacements.

Three gnolls, hunched and reeking, spilling out into the corridor with the indignation of creditors. *"We paid good money for that!"* one of them snarled, and I understood the whole situation before the second sentence arrived. *"Now we will get our money, and get food!"* The bugbear had been a purchased defection. We had killed their investment. And now they wanted a refund and a meal, in that order, and they had come to collect both from the people standing over the corpse.

Air Temple gnolls. Kelno's recruitment drive, apparently, running door to door. *This again.* Everyone in this temple was buying everyone else, and the loyalty lasted exactly as long as the coin was warm.

"Didn't know you guys liked eating while dead, but okay," Sequoia said, no setup, no emphasis, and I will admit the line was clean enough that I let it stand. He has been getting funnier the deeper into this place we go. It's the only thing in here that's improving.

Then Dren stepped up with his two mercenaries and put his hands on Calmer.

The cleric did not take it gracefully. *"Unhand me, you creatures!"* Bellowed, full-throated, the over-eager zealot's outrage at being denied a fight he had no business standing in. But the man had nothing left in him. Necrotic cold from the wraith still sat in his chest, and whatever well he draws his healing from was scraped dry; he could not patch himself past the thin margin he was running on. So Dren dragged him back up the corridor, all protest and no leverage. I let it happen. At three hit points the cleric was a resource I couldn't use; Dren was doing us a favor. The balance of the skirmish rearranged itself around his absence.

Three of us now. No healer. Whatever finished these gnolls had to come out of me and Sequoia, and the body knew it before the thought did.

We moved first. We always do: these things never see us coming until we're already inside their reach, and the gnolls were too busy salivating to set themselves.

One of them turned his yellow eyes on me. *"Tiefling flesh!"* it howled, delighted, like a man discovering a delicacy he'd thought extinct. *"Haven't had tiefling flesh in ages!"*

There it was. The world charging me for existing again, except this one wanted to do it with its teeth.

"Come and get it," I said.

I didn't flinch. I never do, not for this. A dryad once asked if I'd crawled out of the Abyss to torment her; a gnome guard once asked *or whatever you are*; and now a starving Air Temple dog wanted me on a spit. Different mouths, same bill. The threat gets solved the same way every time.

I went in on the first one. The opening swing didn't land the way I wanted, sometimes the blade just doesn't fall true, but the second found the gap, and the dagger went where daggers go. The strike from concealment is the only blow that matters. Thud. Steel through the soft place under the ribs, the full weight of the ambush behind it, and the thing folded most of the way to dead in a single beat. Zinnia drifted in and finished what I'd left twitching. One gone.

The survivor got his one good moment then. Caught me across the side, hard enough to register, not hard enough to mean anything. *Not lethal.* The other one lunged and missed entirely. Two dogs, one scratch between them. That's the going rate when you let me set the terms.

Sequoia took the second. Stepped in, tagged it clean, opened it up. The thing had nothing left to lose and lost it anyway.

Which left the last one for me.

Sequoia slid in beside it to give me the angle, and I took the angle. Dagger high, dagger low, then the third from the blind side with everything behind it. Thud. The gnoll didn't so much die as stop. They attack. They die. That's the only eulogy they earn.

And then it was quiet in that ugly brown-and-black corridor, and three of us were standing, and the secret door hung open on whatever the gnolls had crawled out of.

"This leads to the water temple?" I said, already counting what might be behind it.

Air Temple, came the correction. Of course. A whole new route, and we owned the doorway now.

"Well, I think we can go in." But Sequoia was right and I knew it before he said it: no Calmer, no healer, three people and a door into someone else's stronghold. We needed him out front for whatever was in there. He absorbs what the rest of us can't afford to absorb. Without him that job doesn't get done.

"We'll have to go without him." Then, looking at the dark on the far side of it, I corrected myself. Not an assault. Not yet. "Recon mission."

We have ridiculous stealth. A door is just a door when you're the thing nobody hears coming.
