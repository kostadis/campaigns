## Zinnia — Return to the Earth Temple

The arrowheads were the first thing I saw.

Two positions, six guards, all of them reading our emergence from the stairwell as a threat. Which — given the state of the second level — was a reasonable assessment. The party had been gone. Things had not gone well. The gap between our departure and our return had been filled with sounds they couldn't account for and a body count they couldn't afford.

Their leader got his voice out before his men got their fingers to the triggers.

"STOP! Stop! Don't fight!"

Then the recognition. Then the question.

"Where the hell have you been?"

Zephyr gave him the short version. "Uh, we've been fighting."

"Where?"

"We opened the door for you."

It was enough and not enough, the way these things always are. The leader absorbed it, filed it somewhere between "acceptable" and "later," and redirected us.

"Yeah, well… well, after you open the door, you guys disappeared. Things have not gone well. Hartsch needs you right now. Go to the temple."

"We're going," Zephyr said.

We went.

---

I counted the soldiers before I looked at Hartsch.

The number was wrong. Not collapse-of-the-faction wrong — but wrong enough that I ran the subtraction twice. Whatever had happened while we were on the second level had cost the Earth Temple more than it should have for a faction holding its own ground. These were men who had started this war expecting to win it quickly. The quick part was behind them.

Then I looked at Hartsch.

Exhausted. Dirty. Bloodied in ways that suggested proximity to the fighting, not command distance from it. He had been in it, or near enough. The muffled boom of something two levels below — or in a corridor we weren't in — settled into the stone under our feet and went quiet.

Hartsch looked at us.

"What do we do next?"

Four words. The authority transfer was complete.

I had watched this happen with other commanders, on other operations — the moment when a man who has been pretending to be in charge finally stops pretending. Hartsch had held the Earth Temple together through a three-way war he didn't fully understand, with forces he was losing, and now he was asking us. The Obsidian Edge. The people who had engineered this situation from underneath him.

*There is no need for him to know.*

Zephyr took it first.

"We wait it out."

Hartsch stared at him. "What do you mean we waited out?"

"So… we'll wait till the other temples stop fighting. Just to… okay, because… but right now, it's not the best time to go on the offense."

Hartsch's voice went sharp.

"But that… but that is Romarg's passive strategy!"

There it was. A name that stung him, attached to a strategy that shamed him, and Zephyr had just handed it back as a reasonable plan. I filed that. Hartsch would rather lose aggressively than win the way that man had. Whatever it cost him.

That was leverage. I didn't say so.

Zephyr pivoted. "Hmm… well, the situation is evolving."

"What do you mean, the situation is evolving?"

"Well, you can go to the Obsidian Edge and… we'll hold on for it here with a couple of troops."

Hartsch looked at him. "Is that wise? For me to go on the vanguard?"

"Well, take half the troops, we'll stay here with the other half. We can't leave our territory undefended."

He was past strategy now. I could see it in his hands. The questions weren't tactical — they were the sound of a man whose map had stopped making sense.

"Where should I put the troops? It's all gone to hell!"

"No, it's fine, it's fine. Just go… go fight the… the water in the fire temple. I'll hold down for it here. You got it."

Zephyr was improvising, and the room could feel it. I looked at the soldiers around us. Bloodied. Fewer than before. This was supposed to have been an easy win, and they knew whose faction had started the war. They were watching us, the Obsidian Edge, waiting to see if we were about to disappear on them again.

"Alright, we will leave the vanguard."

Hartsch's shoulders changed.

"So, uh, we'll charge once more to the Water Temple!"

"Yes, let's go."

But Hartsch wasn't done. "So, should we… should we deploy any of our forces to defend anything, or should we just, like, charge straight ahead? Like… is there anything we should do to protect anything?"

"No. Come, everyone in charge, we will make a final stand. It's time we got it to Temple."

The planning found its footing. Someone put the question about relative strength — who was winning, who was weakest, where to push.

"Who's the strongest? The Water Temple. The weakest is the Air Temple. The next is the Fire Temple."

Zephyr absorbed this and turned to us.

"Okay, here's the plan, guys. We'll go after the Air Temple. Take all their treasure. And from there, we'll see if we attack that water."

It was the right call. The Air Temple was weakest; hitting it first would consolidate our position before the harder fight. I had read the same conclusion in Hartsch's face when he listed the order — he had not said it with pride. He had said it like a man reading a ledger that wasn't in his favor.

Hartsch looked around the room. Something in him settled. Not confidence. Something close enough to move on.

"Very well! This is the most heavily defended part of the territory. We will stay here for the night, and we will attack again."

We rested.

The war continued elsewhere. I could hear it in the stone.
