## Flags

### [1] Generic truism

> It was enough and not enough, the way these things always are.

**Why:** "The way these things always are" is a narrative truism any POV character could say. Zinnia's spec says he notices and names the specific detail — he would identify the precise operational sense in which the answer was enough (they moved) and the sense in which it wasn't (the soldiers are still watching us).
**Suggested rewrite:** `It was enough to get us moving and not enough to get us trusted. That gap was still open.`

### [2] Literary metaphor over perception

> The questions weren't tactical — they were the sound of a man whose map had stopped making sense.

**Why:** "Whose map had stopped making sense" is an elaborate literary metaphor. The revised spec grounds Zinnia's reads in specific observable detail — body language, hands, voice, position. He read this moment in Hartsch's hands ("He was past strategy now. I could see it in his hands") — that line is already doing the work. The "map" metaphor adds explanation where the physical read was enough.
**Suggested rewrite:** Cut the "map" clause, or replace: `The questions weren't tactical. He didn't know where to stand anymore.`

### [3] Analytical reasoning chain where a perception beat belongs

> It was the right call. The Air Temple was weakest; hitting it first would consolidate our position before the harder fight.

**Why:** The revised spec is explicit: Zinnia doesn't reason to conclusions step by step — he arrives at them, then confirms through perception. The sentence "I had read the same conclusion in Hartsch's face before Zephyr finished saying it" would be the correct Zinnia move. Instead the narration first walks through the tactical logic (which is Sequoia's mode), then names the perception. The order should be reversed, or the reasoning dropped.
**Suggested rewrite:** `It was the right call. I'd read the same conclusion in Hartsch's face before Zephyr finished saying it.` — cut the reasoning chain entirely; the perception confirmation is sufficient.

### [4] Impressionistic room-read

> Zephyr was improvising, and the room could feel it.

**Why:** "The room could feel it" is atmospheric and impressionistic — the kind of collective-mood sentence that any narrator might write. Zinnia's Perception reads specific observable details: faces, posture, hands, eyes. He would name what he saw that told him the room had registered Zephyr's uncertainty, not the ambient effect.
**Suggested rewrite:** `Zephyr was improvising. I could see it register — the soldiers' eyes going to each other instead of to him.`

## Verdict

The scene's strongest passages are the soldier count, the Hartsch hands read, and "Something in him settled. Not confidence. Something close enough to move on." — all clean Perception-mode Zinnia. The four flags cluster around moments where the narration slides from specific observation into literary metaphor or ambient impression. Spot edits; no re-run needed.
