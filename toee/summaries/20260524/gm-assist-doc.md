# Chapter 27 Dungeon Boss

## calmer — The Aftermath and the Secret Door

The wraith was dead. I held onto that. Whatever else was happening to me, whatever cold had crawled into my chest and stayed there, the wraith was dead and I had been the one to kill it. Twice it had laughed at the holy power I called down in His name. Twice the holy radiance had hit the wraith and simply ceased — the way divine light ceases when it finds nothing that recognizes it, and twice I had felt the outrage of it — the warrior of St. Cuthbert who could not turn a thing that should not exist. And then, on my feet by the grace of nothing but stubbornness, with nothing left in me but reflex and the body's last argument against lying down, I had called the light one final time and it had answered. The chamber went white. The wraith came apart.

*Who's the man,* I thought now, somewhere far down under the cold. *Who is the man.*

The man was being carried.

Hands had me. Many hands, rough hands, the wrong hands — Dren's people, hauling me up off the marble by the arms and the plate, my heels dragging across the brown stone with its black veins, my mace knocking against someone's shin. I tried to tell them. I think I tried. *Unhand me, you creatures.* The words came out, or didn't. The corridor tilted. They were taking me up, away from the stairs, away from the dark passage where the work still was, because — I understood this dimly, the way you understand a thing you would never agree to if you were awake — because they had been told to keep the boss from getting himself killed.

I am the boss. Yes, I am. And the boss does not run from evil.

But they carried me anyway, and the temple slid sideways, and the voices started coming through the floor.

*"You were about to enter the Earth Temple."* That was right. We were. I was. The secret door the gnolls had cracked open for us, the one we'd marked weeks ago — *"Discovered it a while ago."* Yes. I knew that door. I should be at that door.

*"Who wants to lead here?"*

Me. I wanted to lead. I always —

*"My lead,"* said Zephyr, and the cold turned over in my chest.

Of course. His lead. It is always his lead. I built a whole argument once about what my opinions were worth and he watched me drift through it the way he watches me now, waiting to see how much of it I still believed. *Be ready to die,* he'd told me, in that tone, and the wraith had agreed with him — *one of you is going to die today* — and here I was being dragged out of the fight feet-first while the rest of them went down without me.

The voices kept coming. *Stairs leading down. A passageway to the northeast. The lower temple, also probably barricaded.* The Greater Temple had pulled its doors shut and let the lesser ones go to war in the dark, and our whole holy purpose now was to do nothing and stab the survivors. *Let them keep fighting and periodically stab one in the back, and the battle ends.* I would have said something pompous about that. I had read about this just a few moments ago. Well — I hadn't read about it. But I would have made it sound like I had.

*"This time, calmer,"* Zephyr said. *Who's in front? This time, Calmer.*

And I surged — I swear I came up off the dream-floor and reached for the front of the line, *yes, yes, yes, yes, finally* — and the corridor folded shut around me like a hand.

*Calmer is not with you guys. It's just a dream.*

Not with them. Just a dream. Just the cold talking, just the bad audio of a body being hauled up a flight of stairs while the people who matter walk down into the part of the temple where everything happens. I was not in front. I was not even in the room. They were going through the door and I was going up.

Somewhere behind me, or below me, one of Dren's mercenaries fell in to follow them — orders to protect the boss, except the boss was the one being removed, so the boss was me, and I was elsewhere. They'd leave the man at the doorway to watch the rear. He'd be no use deeper in. The mercenaries are anything but stealthy, all clatter and breathing, and Zephyr would lead, and Zinnia and Sequoia would follow, quiet, into the dark, into the Air Temple, into the work — and I would be upstairs, warm and safe and useless, the prophet of stone wrapped in a blanket by men who thought they were doing me a kindness.

*One of you is going to die today.*

The cold sat in my chest and did not leave.

*But not me.*

I didn't even believe it. That was the worst part. I said it to the dark anyway, the way I'd been saying His name to the dark for weeks now — quiet, where no one could hear, where it couldn't blow my cover — and then the stairs took me up and the voices went under the floor and were gone.

## zephyr — A Bargain in the Air Temple

Calmer wasn't with us. Deeply injured, parked with Dren's mercenaries somewhere behind a curtain so he wouldn't get himself killed reaching for a god he can't name in here. Fine. I had the others. And I had the only thing in this temple that ever told me the truth: four starving prophets, all chaotic, all hungry for the same chair, and the simplest way to win a war between starving men is to let them eat each other and stab whoever's still chewing. Let them slaughter each other. That wasn't a bad outcome. That was the outcome.

"Let's go export," I said, which is not how the word goes, but nobody corrected me, and we went.

The southwest passage gave us a curtain. I peeked. Someone was sitting in a chair in a room the color of old ash, beckoning like he'd ordered us off a menu.

"Come, sit down, I've been waiting for you."

"Sit down," I said, which made no sense as a reply, and I sat anyway.

Everything gray. Carpet, plaster, hangings, two upholstered chairs, a crock half-full of mead, lamps of dull metal throwing barely enough light to see by. And on the shelves between the scrolls and the foodstuffs — skulls. Human, demi-human, arranged like a man arranges things he's stopped thinking about. A narrow bed in the corner. *I've heard of worse in the Abyss. But this is the room of someone who plans to be alive in the morning.*

"I am Kelno."

"Hello, Kelno," said Sequoia.

Old. Older than any cultist has a right to get. Tired in the bones and cunning behind the eyes, the two things sitting together because one had bought the other. He tapped a sheet of paper. Assassins had reached Hartsch's antechamber an hour ago. By tonight our puppet would be meat. Alrrem was already on his pyre. That left him, and Belsornig, and — most interestingly — us.

He asked for a reaction. We gave him none.

"Yeah," Sequoia said. "We sit there silently and menacingly."

He appreciated the menace. Then he laid it out, and I felt the terms shift as he talked — the weight on each side of the deal rearranging itself without him raising his voice.

When the war ended, the great doors would open, and whoever stood on top of the upper temple became the Greater Temple's new partner. *That is going to be you. I have noted for a week.* I could kill him — worst guards in the dungeon, he admitted it himself — and then walk into the deep temple blind, no goblin eyes, no informants, no bribery network. Or I could walk out with him as my number two. His goblins, mine. His informants, mine. The throne, mine. His little office, his.

"Y'all wanna take the deal?" Sequoia asked the room.

"We have to be suspicious of him," Zinnia said.

She was right. She has a way of finding the part of a thing that doesn't hold — the place where it was already going to give. But I wanted to hear him say more first.

He showed us the note he kept tapping. Belsornig — *by the cannon of the crushing wave*, the man liked the name — the assassination plot against Hartsch, two men through the north service hall, the bar that should've been left unset. And at the bottom, the line that made the whole performance honest: *If the obsidian edge appear in your hall, withdraw to the steam corridors. Do not engage. They are not to be killed.*

"Are you trying to kill us?" Sequoia asked.

He wasn't. That was the enemy's note. Even the man trying to murder our puppet had given orders to leave us breathing. *Can it be that we've become something a man steps around?*

"Kelno," I said. "So — are you for or against? The pimple." My mouth tripped over the word and I let it.

He smiled and gave me nothing. Loyalty to the power of air, he said, the way a man swears loyalty to weather.

"So you're — you're for Chaos, or — for order."

"Yes, I am for chaos, but I'm also for living."

*There it is.* That was the genuine article. Not loyalty — appetite, the clean honest hunger of a man who only wants to wake up tomorrow. I respect that. I trust it more than I'd ever trust a vow.

He asked if anyone had explained what starting a civil war actually meant. They hadn't. I hadn't known there were rules.

"What happens when a conflict ends?" I asked.

The doors open. Whoever's on top leads the lesser temples.

"And who won the last competition?"

"That would have been the former head of the Earth Temple."

I sat in the gray light with the skulls watching and let it land.

"Okay," I said. "So we must align ourselves with the victories."

"We are the victors," Sequoia said.

And he was right, and that was the thing I'd been circling without letting myself touch. We let Hartsch get killed. We let Romag get killed. We had a cleric pretending to be an earth cultist, which made us the fourth group, which meant the chair at the top was already ours — we just hadn't sat down. *This is starting to be a very lucrative operation.*

"Okay, let's — let's make this — let's do that plan."

Belsornig. Yes.

"Very well," Kelno said.

He pointed us toward the Water Temple — lightly guarded now, he observed, as good a time as any to strike.

"We'll get, I cut up the mercenaries," I said. "Yes, I'll take the deal. We won't kill you for his news."

"I think this is the beginning of a wonderful relationship."

I let him have that. Then, because a new partner is owed a formality: "You know, as a formality, since you're a new partner, I just wanna know — do you have anything hiding around here?"

A ledger. Payments out to a dozen names. And a little note that said *set out the second chair* — two weeks old. He'd put the chair out two weeks ago and sat across from it, waiting for us.

"Must have, like, atrophied or something," I said. Then: "So he knew — he knew that we would start a war? And grow this."

He knew we were a wild card he could leverage. The minute he saw what we did to Romag, the weakest of the four temples decided we might be the way he survives. I understood the logic. I'd have done the same. *Which is exactly why* — "But also safety, so we should not trust it."

"Okay, but look — Kelno has troops because he controls it, right? Well, I tell them, just post some guards, to make sure no one enters the water temple. Just so we can conduct our business with the owner."

"So you can conduct your business," he said. "Alright."

I stood. The chair he'd kept warm for two weeks held my shape now.

## sequoia — The Captain's Report

Dren came down the processional corridor while we were going up it, which meant he'd already done the one thing we'd asked. Calmer was upstairs. Safe. Or as safe as anything in here.

He looked at me first. He always did. Some recruitment negotiation weeks back left him with the idea I was the reasonable one. I'm not. I just talk less.

"Oh my god," he said, "that guy whines a lot." A pause. "He won't shut up."

He meant Calmer. The injured cleric I'd watched bleed in three different rooms now. Dren had carried him up two flights of brown marble and the thanks he wanted was to be heard out about it.

"He's a worrier at heart," Zephyr said.

That was generous. Calmer wasn't a worrier. He just couldn't say his own god's name in here, so it came out sideways, as complaint. But Dren didn't need that, and there was no need for him to know.

Then the report.

"The captain, the captain? March is finished." Dren said it fast, the way men say things they ran to deliver. "Belsornig's people are at his door. He's screaming for you."

So. That was the war we'd built, arriving on schedule. The Fire Temple folding inward. Water Temple knives at the captain's threshold. Somewhere south of us the smell of charred gnoll had been hanging in the corridors all morning, and now it had a body count.

Dren wanted that understood — that he was reporting, not deciding. "You didn't put me in charge of mercenaries, because I tell you what to do." He shrugged, a soldier's shrug. "You tell me."

I noted it. A man who knew exactly what he was for. Rare, in this place.

"Alrrem's on his pyre," he went on. "Belsornig pulling his treasury out the south route." Then the assessment, clean as a quartermaster's ledger. "Low. Healthy. Vulnerable. Your calls."

Three temple leaders. One burning, one robbed, one screaming. We did that. Not by accident. We took bribes from the Fire Temple, sold a chimera, knifed Romag at his own table, and pointed three armies at each other until they marched. One does not simply take bribes to defect and end up the rightful lords of anything. We ended up here anyway.

Zephyr was already weighing routes, the grim arithmetic of which corpse to loot first. The captain screaming for rescue. Belsornig's treasury walking out a back door. I let him run it. He's better at the math when there's more than one prize in the room.

We moved into the next chamber. Dren came with us, and that was the problem.

"Sounds good, boss." He said it to me, of course. "What do you want me to be doing?"

There was the trap. Dren was a mercenary in a temple full of mercenaries who would all sell us for a copper if the war tilted. He wouldn't. I didn't know why. Maybe it was the negotiation. Maybe he just liked having someone tell him the truth about what he was for. Either way he was the only blade in this entire stone box that pointed our direction and meant it.

Which meant he could not come into the water tank. A loyal man is an asset. A dead loyal man is a waste. We had one.

Zephyr fumbled it out loud, the way he does when he's solving a problem in front of an audience. "You could stay here with — what's his name. Kelno." Then he caught himself. Calmer was upstairs. We took him up. "Oh. Shit."

I didn't say anything. There wasn't a need. The right answer was sitting in the room already: put Dren where the cleric was, and let the only loyal man in the temple guard the only man who couldn't run.

"Take your mercenaries and stay back," Zephyr landed on, finally.

"Take a vacation," somebody muttered.

Dren didn't argue. He didn't take orders, except from us, and that was the whole point of him. "Okay, boss." He gathered his two men off the wall, gave me one last look — the look that still thought I was the reasonable one — and they headed out, back toward the stairs, back toward Calmer.

We went the other way. Toward the water.

The dripping had started somewhere above us, in a ceiling that hadn't been dripping an hour ago.

That's not good.

## zinnia — Entry into the Water Temple

Dren said "okay, boss," and that was the last easy thing.

We came up on the Water Temple and the corridor opened into a hall the size of a barracks, the ceiling arched and lost somewhere in the shadow high overhead. The walls were slabs of polished stone, blue swirled into deep green, and some old magic had been laid into them — a soft, cloudy luminosity that bled out of the stone and floated in the damp air, so the whole chamber felt submerged. Like standing at the bottom of the sea. Even the air smelled of salt.

I drank it in. That is the job, before anything else: read the room. Bronze everywhere, all of it gone to verdigris, that green crust that says nobody has touched this in a long time, or nobody dares. A great sheet of bronze set into the west wall. An altar near the middle of the hall, round and squat, its concave top holding a shallow basin of water, and at the bottom of the basin — coins, a scatter of gems, seashells. An offering. South of it, in the mouth of an alcove, an idol of serpentine stone: a writhing knot of fish and eels and octopi sculpted into one horrid mass, the height of a tall man and more, with a seaweed drapery hung across the wall behind. In each corner a triangular plinth ran up toward the vaulted dark, and atop each one a bronze gargoyle squatted under its coat of green.

Four watchers in the corners. A god in the alcove. Treasure in the font.

*Touch nothing.* Not the time and place to pull for treasure. Sequoia agreed in a word — "Yep" — and that settled it. We were not here to be tempted.

"Let's sneak in," Zephyr said, and we did. This part I know. This part is breath held and footfall placed and the body made small in a room built to make you feel small. Zephyr moved the way he moves when it matters — no announcement, just presence, closing the distance before the room processed it. I moved behind him and the hall did not notice us — not the gargoyles, not whatever prayed at that altar. We crossed the whole of it and nothing stirred. Clean. *Gotcha.*

Two doors at the far end, east and west. The intelligence said the bad guys were through one of them. Zephyr wanted the west — fewer rooms to cross, less chance of stumbling into something. Sound. I'd have chosen the same. He put his ear to the door first, the way you do, listening for the thing on the other side before you give it a reason to listen back. The plan was simple: hear nothing, then break it open before they can stand. Zephyr is an assassin; if we take them unawares, the first blow is already a killing one. Surprise is the whole margin. You build it carefully and you spend it once.

So we would smash the door. Splash through it. Take them while their hands were still finding their weapons.

The trouble was the door, and the trouble was me. We sized up who had the shoulders for it. Zephyr's no good for that work, never has been. Sequoia about the same. I'm the one built to put a body through a barrier — so I set myself, and I hit it.

It didn't give.

I bounced. The door held and threw me back, and the impact went up my arm and nowhere else useful. Someone moved to help. I set again and hit it a second time, everything I had, the strongest of us against a door that wasn't even locked so much as stubborn — and it threw me again. Twice. The same flat, jarring nothing, twice.

*This amateur-hour infiltration has got to stop.* I have thought that about every one of them. Calmer can't stay quiet. Zephyr takes the risk for the flourish. And here it was my own shoulder that botched the one thing I was put on the door to do.

Behind the door, the two men went still, then loud. They'd felt it — the door shuddering, something heavy throwing itself at them twice and not getting through. They shouted a name I couldn't make out through the bronze, muffled, a question more than a command.

"You're not supposed to be here. Who is this?"

The margin was gone. We had crossed that whole stretch of glowing water-light without a sound, and lost it all on a door.

## zephyr — Infiltration of the Water Temple

The west door. Always the west door — fewer rooms, fewer surprises, fewer chances for a war zone to introduce me to something it had been saving. I put my ear to the brown marble and listened. Nothing. The plan was to burst in.

The plan died when Zinnia hit the door and the door hit back.

She rolled her shoulder into it and bounced. Tried again, with help, bounced again. *That was a sorry effort,* she said, which was not the door's problem so much as a confession. Two muffled voices came through the wood now, fully awake, a name shouted that I couldn't make out, then the question that ends so many infiltrations: *You're not supposed to be here? Who is this?*

Sequoia, without missing a beat: "Housekeeping!"

It did not land. I sighed. Zinnia tried informants. I tried Kelno — *We are messengers for Kelno* — and felt the thing wobble in the air, because messages from Kelno, in a temple at war with itself, is the wrong kind of password. So I changed the trade.

There was a war on. War means news, and news means doors open for the people who carry it. We were messengers, then — bearing word of an assassination, a letter, a coin to prove it. Zinnia produced the proof. The voices behind the door paused, and the pause is where you win or lose. *Yes,* one said, *and what is it?* News for the big boss. News for Belsornig.

And then they did the thing tired men do when they think they're safe — they talked. "They're dead, then, with Alrrem dying, and those fools… All that remains is for us to defeat those pompous asses known as the Obsidian Edge."

*This again.* The world had been calling us pompous behind closed doors for a while now. I could forgive it. We were about to introduce ourselves properly.

The door opened. The bluff held so clean we didn't just get in — we caught them flat-footed, the whole opening exchange ours to spend before they could lift a hand. Then they saw us, and saw the shapes wearing the wrong faces, and started to say the word — *the Obsidian Edge?* — and that was the last useful thing either of them said.

They were cult fanatics, and not entirely stupid: blades of conjured force already hanging in the air, hovering, ready for trouble. Trouble had simply come dressed as a courier.

Sequoia and Zinnia took the first one apart. The second was mine.

Surprise, for an assassin, is not an advantage. It is a sentence already finished before the man knows it started. The blade went in where it always goes. Automatic. Done. Sequoia stepped over the body and ended the rest of it, and the way to the inner sanctum stood open, quiet, smelling of damp stone and old water.

And waiting in my hand: the Dagger of Venom. Kelno's gift. *I've been saving this to kill Belsornig for some time. If you wouldn't mind doing me the honors?* I'd taken his deal in seconds because his answer had been honest — for chaos, but also for living. An honest man with a poisoned knife and a grudge. I respected all three. *Well, I'll give it to you. Take it,* I'd said, and the dagger had gone around the circle, and now it was coming back.

Belsornig, Prophet of Water, was preoccupied. He'd heard his henchmen die so fast he'd registered nothing at all. We crossed the chamber like the floor owed us silence — and it gave it, every footfall swallowed, not a breath out of place. He never turned.

Then it happened all at once. They turned to fight. They died.

Sequoia opened him up. Zinnia charged and burned the water out of him. I came in last, missed the first, found the second exactly where I wanted it. A staggering toll, all of it landing on a man who'd been reading a scroll. He swayed. One more hit and there'd be nothing left to question.

*Maybe we should keep him alive? No?* The thought had value — a Prophet talks, and talk is leverage.

"No, let's just kill him," Sequoia said.

Fair. *Look, I'll take the poison dagger.* I crossed the last step, found the soft place under the ribs, and drove Kelno's gift home. The Prophet of Water folded onto his own wet floor.

And a gong rang. Deep, enormous, rolling out through the whole second level. *Are they opening the door?* I asked, dagger still warm in my fist — but it wasn't a door. It was the temple announcing that something had changed hands.

We turned for the stairs in the Air Temple, meaning to go home, to wash the water-stink off and report.

The bugbears came up to meet us instead. All in black — the black of the Greater Temple — and they didn't reach for weapons. They announced a name like an order. *Barkinar.* The Greater Temple, they said, wished to speak with the new rulers of the Upper Temple.

"Yes, we will go see him," I said.

"No. He will see you." They looked past me, counting heads. "Where is the cleric?"

"I will summon him."

"The cleric is in charge, of course."

*Of course he is.* "Yes," I said, and the useful lie settled into place the way it always does. "He's hurt. He will be fine."

## zinnia — Summons to the Greater Temple

We were at the foot of the stairs in the Air Temple, ready to climb back to our quarters, when they came up out of the dark in black.

Bugbears. A column of them, and the black they wore was not Earth Temple black — it was the deeper black, the black of the thing below the thing. I read that before I read anything else. The color was a credential. They were telling us who they answered to without a word.

They came from the secret door we had left barred. They came from the eastern door. They came from the western door. Three approaches, converged at once, and the converging was the message: *we were always here, on every side of you, and you did not know.*

One of them announced, gruff and flat, that Barkinar wished to speak to the new rulers of the Upper Temple.

Zephyr said, "Yes, we will go see him."

The bugbear corrected him without heat. "He will see you." A small word, *you* against *we*, and it carried the whole hierarchy in it. We did not summon. We were summoned.

They looked around the room. Where was the cleric? Zephyr told them he would summon him — that he was hurt, but he would be fine. One of them said, as though stating a known fact, "The cleric is in charge, of course." I let that sit where it landed. They had built a picture of us from the outside, and in their picture Calmer wore the crown. *Let them keep it.* A wrong read in the enemy's hands is leverage in mine.

Then Zephyr offered them meat. "Are you hungry? Would you like some meat?" I have stopped being surprised by him. The bugbears were not sure either.

That was when I saw them.

Not the bugbears in front of us — the others. It landed all at once, the way a wrong room lands: they stood like men who had stood here before, easy, owning the floor — and I knew. The Greater Temple had not kept its forces below. They had forces on this level, in the parts of the map we had never walked, sitting quiet behind brown marble while we ran our little war for them floor by floor. We had been operating inside a house that was never empty. We had simply never opened the right doors.

*Gotcha.* Except this time the catch was on me. I had not seen them, and I am the one who sees. That stayed with me.

A voice came up from the stairs leading down. Unhurried. The voice of someone who had been in this room before and knew what happened in it.

"That was efficient. Come down, bring your puppet master if he is alive. We should talk."

Puppet master. So they had watched the strings too, and named the hand that pulled them. There was no point pretending. We went down.

Barkinar brought Calmer with him, half-carried, assuming — as everyone assumed — that the cleric was the chief of us. The room at the bottom was built to remove the question of whether we could fight our way out. A hill giant. Ogres at the walls. Tables and doors and the certain knowledge in the body that anything that started here ended here. Beside me they set Kelno, and Barkinar looked at him and smiled. I filed that. A man who smiles at the prisoner he has been watching from hidden passages for two weeks is a man who already knows more than he is saying.

"Romag. Hartsch. Alrrem. Belsornig," Barkinar said. "Problems I had on a list yesterday morning, and four problems gone by sundown. You will forgive me if I'm impressed."

I said nothing. Silence has never cost me anything, and it was the only honest thing in the room.

He gave us the inventory of our new holdings the way a steward hands over keys. Leaderless cultists in the Fire Temple. Trolls on contract in the Water Temple. A treasury unaccounted for. Mercenaries who needed a chain of command. The chest we had delivered to Romag, which the Greater Temple had an interest in recovering. None of it urgent enough, he said, to flay us alive tomorrow — and when he said *tomorrow*, the threat sat under the word, real, waiting. But the upper temple should look organized when he came back through these steps. A week.

We came here to take this temple apart. We had become it instead. I sat with that for a moment without moving my face.

"Be careful of the people downstairs," he went on. "One or two you might want to meet. Senshock — our Lord Wizard — has opinions about new arrivals. He is ambitious and paranoid. He is also useful. Be civil, and let him underestimate you." A second name, set down lighter, for later: Falrinth, a wizard hiding from the Greater Temple for reasons of his own. "We could talk about Falrinth later. For now, organize your house. We'll be in touch."

*Let him underestimate you.* I had been doing that to people for years. Now it was advice handed down to me by the one man in the temple I could not yet read.

Before any of us spoke, Barkinar turned. He said something quiet to one of his ogres, and walked back down into the dark.

A week to organize the house. The house we came to burn.
