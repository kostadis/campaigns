Dren said "okay, boss," and that was the last easy thing.

We came up on the Water Temple and the corridor opened into a hall the size of a barracks, the ceiling arched and lost somewhere in shadow thirty feet up. The walls were slabs of polished stone, blue swirled into deep green, and some old magic had been laid into them — a soft, cloudy luminosity that bled out of the stone and floated in the damp air, so the whole chamber felt submerged. Like standing at the bottom of the sea. The buttresses and arches threw a tracery of dimness across everything. Even the air smelled of salt.

I drank it in. That is the job, before anything else: read the room. Bronze everywhere, all of it gone to verdigris, that green crust that says nobody has touched this in a long time, or nobody dares. A great sheet of bronze set into the west wall. An altar near the middle of the hall, round and squat, its concave top holding a shallow basin of water, and at the bottom of the basin — coins, a scatter of gems, seashells. An offering. South of it, in the mouth of an alcove, an idol of serpentine stone: a writhing knot of fish and eels and octopi sculpted into one horrid mass, eight feet of it, with a seaweed drapery hung across the wall behind. In each corner a triangular plinth ran twenty feet up, and atop each one a bronze gargoyle squatted under its coat of green.

Four watchers in the corners. A god in the alcove. Treasure in the font.

*Touch nothing.* Not the time and place to pull for treasure. Sequoia agreed in a word — "Yep" — and that settled it. We were not here to be tempted.

"Let's sneak in," Zephyr said, and we did. This part I know. This part is breath held and footfall placed and the body made small in a room built to make you feel small. Zephyr moved the way he moves when it matters — no announcement, just presence, closing the distance before the room processed it. I moved behind him and the hall did not notice us — not the gargoyles, not whatever prayed at that altar. We crossed the whole of it and nothing stirred. Clean. *Gotcha.*

Two doors at the far end, east and west. The intelligence said the bad guys were through one of them. Zephyr wanted the west — fewer rooms to cross, less chance of stumbling into something. Sound. I'd have chosen the same. He put his ear to the door first, the way you do, listening for the thing on the other side before you give it a reason to listen back. The plan was simple: hear nothing, then break it open before they can stand. Zephyr is an assassin; if we take them surprised, the first blow is already a killing one. Surprise is the whole margin. You build it carefully and you spend it once.

So we would smash the door. Splash through it. Take them while their hands were still finding their weapons.

The trouble was the door, and the trouble was me. We checked who had the shoulders for it. Zephyr's no good for that work, never has been. Sequoia about the same. I'm the one built to put a body through a barrier — so I set myself, and I hit it.

It didn't give.

I bounced. The door held and threw me back, and the impact went up my arm and nowhere else useful. Someone moved to help. I set again and hit it a second time, everything I had, the strongest of us against a door that wasn't even locked so much as stubborn — and it threw me again. Twice. The same flat, jarring nothing, twice.

*This amateur-hour infiltration has got to stop.* I have thought that about every one of them. Calmer can't stay quiet. Zephyr takes the risk for the flourish. And here it was my own shoulder that botched the one thing I was put on the door to do. The surprise was gone. We had carried it forty feet across that glowing floor, and spent it on a door.

Behind the door, the two men went still, then loud. They'd felt it — the door shuddering, something heavy throwing itself at them twice and not getting through. They shouted a name I couldn't make out through the bronze, muffled, a question more than a command.

"You're not supposed to be here. Who is this?"

The margin was gone. We had crossed forty feet of glowing water-light without a sound, and lost it all on a door.
