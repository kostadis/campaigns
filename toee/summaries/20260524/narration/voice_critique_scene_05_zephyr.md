## Flags

### [1] Transcribed table-talk embedded unprocessed

> *But I'd rather not go into many different rooms, so I should just go to the west.*

**Why:** This italicized line reads as the player's out-of-character reasoning at the table ("I'd rather not go into many different rooms") dropped into the narration without being transformed into Zephyr's interior voice. Zephyr's interiority runs on staccato theatrical logic — the surrounding prose ("The west door. Always the west door — fewer rooms, fewer surprises, fewer chances for a war zone to introduce me to something it had been saving.") is exactly right. The italicized line undercuts it by sounding like a rules-of-thumb explanation rather than the character's compressed cognition.

**Suggested rewrite:** *Fewer rooms. Fewer chances for whatever this war had saved for us to introduce itself.*

---

### [2] Over-literary, not Zephyr's economical self-narration

> "Yes," I said, smooth as the rehearsed curse I've worn for years, "but he is — he's hurt. He will be fine."

**Why:** "Smooth as the rehearsed curse I've worn for years" reaches for elegance in a moment where Zephyr's internal voice is characteristically flat and efficient. The spec is explicit: he uses the Tiefling lie automatically and never gives it interiority beyond noting that it is rehearsed. He doesn't romanticize his cover — he wears it. The political_maneuvering example shows Zephyr noting a deflection flatly: "I let him have that." He wouldn't pause inside the lie to describe its texture poetically.

**Suggested rewrite:** "'Yes,' I said, and the useful lie settled into place the way it always does. 'He's hurt. He will be fine.'"

---

## Verdict

Otherwise a very strong scene — the Belsornig sequence ("They attack. They die."), the "This again" on being called pompous, and the handling of Kelno's dagger are all on-spec. The table-talk flag is the more structurally important catch; the "rehearsed curse" line is a light touch. Two spot edits. The closing exchange with the bugbears ("Of course he is.") is the scene's best moment and needs nothing.
