Calmer wasn't with us. Deeply injured, parked with Dren's mercenaries somewhere behind a curtain so he wouldn't get himself killed reaching for a god he can't name in here. Fine. I had the others. And I had the cold arithmetic, which was the only thing in this temple that ever told me the truth: four prophets, all chaotic, all hungry for the same chair, and the simplest way to win a war between starving men is to let them eat each other and stab whoever's still chewing. Let them slaughter each other. That wasn't a bad outcome. That was the outcome.

"Let's go export," I said, which is not how the word goes, but nobody corrected me, and we went.

The southwest passage gave us a curtain. I peeked. Someone was sitting in a chair in a room the color of old ash, beckoning like he'd ordered us off a menu.

"Come, sit down, I've been waiting for you."

"Sit down," I said, which made no sense as a reply, and I sat anyway.

Everything gray. Carpet, plaster, hangings, two upholstered chairs, a crock half-full of mead, lamps of dull metal throwing barely enough light to see by. And on the shelves between the scrolls and the foodstuffs — skulls. Human, demi-human, arranged like a man arranges things he's stopped thinking about. A narrow bed in the corner. *I've heard of worse in the Abyss. But this is the room of someone who plans to be alive in the morning.*

"I am Kelno."

"Hello, Kelno," said Sequoia.

Old. Older than any cultist has a right to get. Tired in the bones and cunning behind the eyes, the two things sitting together because one had bought the other. He tapped a sheet of paper. Assassins had reached Hartsch's antechamber an hour ago. By tonight our puppet would be meat. Alrrem was already on his pyre. That left him, and Belsornig, and — most interestingly — us.

He asked for a reaction. We gave him none.

"Yeah," Sequoia said. "We sit there silently and menacingly."

He appreciated the menace. Then he laid it out, and I felt the terms shift as he talked — the weight on each side of the deal rearranging itself without him raising his voice.

When the war ended, the great doors would open, and whoever stood on top of the upper temple became the Greater Temple's new partner. *That is going to be you. I have noted for a week.* I could kill him — worst guards in the dungeon, he admitted it himself — and then walk into the deep temple blind, no goblin eyes, no informants, no bribery network. Or I could walk out with him as my number two. His goblins, mine. His informants, mine. The throne, mine. His little office, his.

"Y'all wanna take the deal?" Sequoia asked the room.

"We have to be suspicious of him," Zinnia said.

She was right. She has a way of finding the part of a thing that doesn't hold — the place where it was already going to give. But I wanted to hear him say more first.

He showed us the note he kept tapping. Belsornig — *by the cannon of the crushing wave*, the man liked the name — the assassination plot against Hartsch, two men through the north service hall, the bar that should've been left unset. And at the bottom, the line that made the whole performance honest: *If the obsidian edge appear in your hall, withdraw to the steam corridors. Do not engage. They are not to be killed.*

"Are you trying to kill us?" Sequoia asked.

He wasn't. That was the enemy's note. Even the man trying to murder our puppet had given orders to leave us breathing. *Can it be that we've become something a man steps around?*

"Kelno," I said. "So — are you for or against? The pimple." My mouth tripped over the word and I let it.

He smiled and gave me nothing. Loyalty to the power of air, he said, the way a man swears loyalty to weather.

"So you're — you're for Chaos, or — for order."

"Yes, I am for chaos, but I'm also for living."

*There it is.* That was the genuine article. Not loyalty — appetite, the clean honest hunger of a man who only wants to wake up tomorrow. I respect that. I trust it more than I'd ever trust a vow.

He asked if anyone had explained what starting a civil war actually meant. They hadn't. I hadn't known there were rules.

"What happens when a conflict ends?" I asked.

The doors open. Whoever's on top leads the lesser temples.

"And who won the last competition?"

"That would have been the former head of the Earth Temple."

I sat in the gray light with the skulls watching and let it land.

"Okay," I said. "So we must align ourselves with the victories."

"We are the victors," Sequoia said.

And he was right, and that was the thing I'd been circling without letting myself touch. We let Hartsch get killed. We let Romag get killed. We had a cleric pretending to be an earth cultist, which made us the fourth group, which meant the chair at the top was already ours — we just hadn't sat down. *This is starting to be a very lucrative operation.*

"Okay, let's — let's make this — let's do that plan."

Belsornig. Yes.

"Very well," Kelno said.

He pointed us toward the Water Temple — lightly guarded now, he observed, as good a time as any to strike.

"We'll get, I cut up the mercenaries," I said. "Yes, I'll take the deal. We won't kill you for his news."

"I think this is the beginning of a wonderful relationship."

I let him have that. Then, because a new partner is owed a formality: "You know, as a formality, since you're a new partner, I just wanna know — do you have anything hiding around here?"

A ledger. Payments out to a dozen names. And a little note that said *set out the second chair* — two weeks old. He'd put the chair out two weeks ago and sat across from it, waiting for us.

"Must have, like, atrophied or something," I said. Then: "So he knew — he knew that we would start a war? And grow this."

He knew we were a wild card he could leverage. The minute he saw what we did to Romag, the weakest of the four temples decided we might be the way he survives. I understood the logic. I'd have done the same. *Which is exactly why* — "But also safety, so we should not trust it."

"Okay, but look — Kelno has troops because he controls it, right? Well, I tell them, just post some guards, to make sure no one enters the water temple. Just so we can conduct our business with the owner."

"So you can conduct your business," he said. "Alright."

I stood. The chair he'd kept warm for two weeks held my shape now.
