## Flags

### [1] Cliché simile

> the holy radiance had broken against it like water on stone

**Why:** "Water on stone" is a stock metaphor for futile impact that carries no Calmer-specific weight. The combat example renders the same theological failure as "the holy light dissolved into the cold air like breath in winter" — atmospheric, temperature-coded, registered through the dungeon's own sensory world. "Water on stone" borrows nothing from Calmer's clerical vocabulary or from the cold that the wraith planted in his chest.

**Suggested rewrite:** "the holy radiance had hit the wraith and simply ceased — the way divine light ceases when it finds nothing that recognizes it."

---

### [2] Voice spec conflict

> twice I had felt the shame of it — the warrior of St. Cuthbert who could not turn a thing that should not exist

**Why:** Calmer does not process his failures as shame. The spec is explicit: he deflects, blames advisors, pivots to humor, or spins the failure into complaint — he does not admit shame. His emotional register for the failed Channel Divinity attempts is closer to theological outrage or injured dignity. "Shame" is too inward and self-judging for a man who would follow a failure with "What am I supposed to do with advisors like that?" The framing that follows — "the warrior of St. Cuthbert who could not turn a thing that should not exist" — is correct; the word that introduces it is off.

**Suggested rewrite:** "twice I had felt the outrage of it — the warrior of St. Cuthbert who could not turn a thing that should not exist."

---

## Verdict

Strong scene overall — the "Who's the man" / "But not me" / "prophet of stone wrapped in a blanket" moments are exactly right, and the ending (saying the saint's name to the dark, where it can't blow his cover) is the best Calmer line in the document. Both flags are single-word-or-phrase corrections rather than structural problems; this scene does not need re-narrating, just two spot edits.
