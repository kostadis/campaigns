# Chapter 29: The New Rulers of the Temple


---

## sequioa — The New Rulers of the Temple

Four prophets dead. I counted them the way I count anything I'm responsible for, which is carefully, and against my own interest.

Romag, knifed at his own celebration. Hartsch, who summoned us to a war council and got one. Alrrem, manipulated into a fight he didn't survive. Belsornig. Four men who ran the elemental factions of this place, and every one of them was in the ground or worse, and every one of them had a name we'd written into a ledger before we put him there.

The brown marble was the same. The black veins running through it. The bronze sconces throwing their thin crescent light. Somewhere below us the war we'd lit was still burning — fire in the southern corridors, water in the low passages, and the creak of something structural nobody had stopped yet. We had built that.

Then Barkinar came up from the Greater Temple and told us, in the flat voice of a man delivering inventory, that the Upper Temple was ours.

Not ours to wreck. Ours to run.

There was a list. There is always a list. "Here's the things that you have been tasked to do," he said, like a foreman handing off a shift. "You have to pacify the Fire Temple, resolve the troll contract, audit and secure the Water Temple treasury, formalize Dren's command, recover the chest of Rao." A pause. "Oh, deal with Kelno." Another pause, the worst one. "And figure out who this damn necromancer is, if he's still alive."

I let that settle. Pacify. Audit. Resolve. Formalize. These are not words you use about a thing you are tearing down.

"Do you understand your problems?" he asked.

I understood them fine. That was the problem. The chest of Rao alone — Brother Eldin had warned Calmer not to hand it to Romag, and we handed it over anyway, and now it was on the recovery list like a misplaced requisition. The thing we'd given to one boss was now homework assigned by the next boss.

There were warnings, too, the kind a man gives you when he's already decided you're staff. A Lord Wizard named Senshock, ambitious, watching. A wizard named Falranth, hiding somewhere in the walls. Threats, but delivered as personnel notes. Watch this one. Mind that one. The way you brief a subordinate.

"One of the things that you realize," Barkinar said, "is that your new management has said you need to do these things." He looked at me when he said it. "Does that make sense, Sequoia?"

It made sense. It made the worst kind of sense.

"So, you know — much to your horror, you got bosses."

I have been a lot of things in this temple. Deputized by Rufus while reserving the right to tell him nothing. Adopted into a clan. Toasted by a man I helped kill. I had not, until that exact moment, been an employee.

"You were supposed to destroy the temple," I said, "not join."

Nobody argued. That was the part that landed. Zephyr didn't have a line. Calmer didn't have a sermon. We had come down here to burn this place out from the inside, and we'd been so good at it that the inside had promoted us.

"Yeah," Barkinar said. "So apparently you now have bosses. Your management. So, yeah. That's your problem now."

Management. We had assassinated our way to the top of an organization and the reward was a chain of command above us.

Then Dren, who has always liked me more than is wise, explained the one rule that mattered. By the temple's own law, only clerics could rule the factions. "And there's currently no cleric in the Fire Temple," he said.

I saw Calmer's face change before he opened his mouth. I have watched this man go pale at rituals he was supposed to bless. I have watched him nearly say a name out loud in a place where the name would get him killed. This was not that. This was a man hearing a vacancy.

"I am a cleric," he said. "Of the Earth Temple. Of the Earth God." He was already moving. "I will need to go read up on some of the rituals."

And he walked into Hartsch's room — Hartsch, dead a day, the books still where he'd left them — and started turning pages.

I stood in the cold marble hall and watched the cleric of St. Cuthbert go study how to rule a temple of fire.

We came to destroy this place.

We got jobs.

## zephyr — New Management and the Earth Temple

The list came at me like an invoice. Pacify the Fire Temple. Resolve the troll contract. Audit and secure the Water Temple treasury. Formalize Dren's command. Recover the Chest of Rao. Oh — and deal with Kelno. And figure out who this damn necromancer is, if he's still breathing. I sat in Hartsch's old war room with the brown marble cold under my boots, listened to the bill itemize itself, and thought: *this is starting to be a very lucrative operation.* It is also starting to look like a job. There is a difference, and I am the only one in the room who keeps track of it.

Dren came in picking his teeth with the same dagger he picks everything with. "So boss," he said, "am I in charge?"

"I'm the boss," I said.

"I don't think so, Zephyr, my buddy ol' pal." He didn't even slow down on the dagger. "Last time I checked, Barkinar said the cleric was."

*This again.* I felt it land the way I always feel it land — the small recalibration, the door that doesn't open for me. So I let it. "He is our boss," I said, and watched Calmer drink it in like wine.

"Yes," Calmer said, to nobody, to the marble, to the elder elemental god whose authority he is now wearing like a borrowed coat. "I am the boss. Yes, I am."

Fine. The cleric is the boss. I am the man who decides what the boss decides.

I turned to Dren — the Water Temple man, the one we pulled out of the wreckage, the only resource in this dungeon whose loyalty I'd buy without a discount. I told him nothing ahead was cheap. Obstacles. But he had to stay close, because we trusted him — for now. The two most expensive words I own. *For now.*

He wanted the promotion. All the Earth Temple forces, reporting up through Calmer. He wanted it badly enough that I didn't have to do any work to give it to him, which is its own kind of suspicious, but a man who wants something is a lever, and a lever is a gift. "Well, we were happy with his loyalty, so," I said. "Of course. We'd be okay with him having certain control of the troops. To Calmer, of course."

To Calmer. Of course. I said it twice the way you sign a contract twice.

It cost me something to land it clean — the way any investment costs, the principal up front, the return delayed, the whole thing uncertain until it isn't. It gave. Sequoia, beside me, could not have sold ice in a steam corridor; whatever he tried slid off the room like water off marble. But mine held.

"Yes, yes," Calmer said, lit up like a sconce. "I think that is an excellent idea. I think we should hold a ceremony of sorts. What do you think?"

"For sure, yeah, yeah."

"The first thing, though — we should change our headquarters. Hartsch's chambers are far more comfortable than the current ones."

"Yeah, of course. You could lay down camp there." I watched him settle into the dead man's room like he'd been measured for it. Zinnia watched too, longer than I did, the way she watches when something doesn't add up — and I couldn't tell either, whether the cleric loves the power or just loves the cushions. The man is ambiguous now. That's new. That's worth a sigh.

Then Calmer turned commander on us. "Dren — set up a meeting in the Earth Temple for later today. Bring all the troops. Announce there will be changes in the management."

*Management.* Sequoia's face did something complicated, then settled into horror. "You were supposed to destroy the temple," he said. "Not join."

I let that one breathe. He's not wrong. He's just early.

When the day's options came down to *which corpse-pile to loot first*, I dug in. "We should first lay the law in the area. Make them know we're the Chiefs." We hadn't even met the new leaders. There are no new leaders, I was told. So I said the only sensible thing.

"I asked Dren — shouldn't they be electing new leaders?"

Dren laughed. Not a chuckle. A whole-body laugh, the dagger going still.

"What the fuck you mean, elect?" Sequoia said. "Democratic society. How do you think we came into power? By peaceful means?"

I am perfectly aware how we came into power. I held the knife for some of it. But a structure that runs on assassination still has to run on *something* between assassinations, and nobody in this temple has ever once asked the question. "Alright, Dren," I said. "Explain to me."

So he did, picking his fingers raw. Alrrem and his last apprentice had been summoning a fire elemental — "to use to kill us all," he said, fond as a man telling a fishing story — and they'd failed, and died. By the rules, only a cleric can rule a temple. And there's no cleric left in the Fire Temple.

"I am a cleric of the Earth Temple," Calmer announced. "Of the Earth God. I'll need to go read up on the rituals." And he vanished into Hartsch's room toward a wall of books.

I wanted to go finish the Fire Temple — meet them, kill the remaining. Dren shook his head like I was a slow child. "This gives us a one-time opportunity to plunder that temple. We've got a week to steal whatever the F we want, and after that, he expects things to be run properly."

A week. A window with a price tag and a closing time. *Now* we were speaking my language.

"Alright," I said. "Let's go steal everything."

Dren beamed. "NOW you're talking! Calmer, you good with that plan? I don't want Zephyr here stepping on any toes."

Calmer emerged from his study with dirt smeared across his face. "Yes, yes! Just practicing the rituals, just practicing the rituals." He wiped it off, smiling like a man who'd buried something. "Nothing, nothing, nothing to see here. Shall we?"

"Let's go!" Sequoia said.

I stood, and filed the dirt away with everything else I notice and never mention.

## zinnia — Entering the Fire Temple

We could go down the front entrance now. That was the difference, and I felt it the way I feel a room rearrange itself — not a door we picked a lock on, not a passage we crawled through behind a Cloak of Elvenkind. The front. The beaten brass doors. We owned the building, or near enough that no one left alive would say otherwise.

There were two ways into the Fire Temple, and we stood and weighed them, which is the kind of thing amateurs do too long. Sequoia ended it the way he ends things.

"We should go plunder this before we lose the opportunity, too," he said. Then, warming to it: "Man, here's the new plan of how we're gonna take down the temple. We're gonna bankrupt it. Nobody's getting paid."

*One does not simply take bribes to defect.* But this — this was cleaner. Take the money. Leave nothing to fight over. I had no argument with it.

The brass doors swung. We walked in.

Red granite, scores of flambeaux, the air thick with heated metal and sulfur — it sat at the back of my throat before I'd taken two steps. There was no doubt what this place was. Tapestries on the north wall, fire and burning forms worked into the cloth. The torches threw light, but it was the granite that held my eye: blue and mica flakes in the polished stone, and they did not sit still. They leapt. They danced, as if the rock had caught fire somewhere underneath and the flame was trying to get out. A trick of the light. Probably. The whole hall carried rust-colored motes drifting in the air that wasn't quite air.

A brass tube hung from the ceiling on brass chains, long as a fallen tree and twice my reach across, a shallow fire pit beneath it and low brass stands stacked with charcoal blocks to either side. Ritual furniture. I logged it and moved my eyes on.

"Do you keep going in?"

"Yes," Zephyr said.

East and west, past two rows of pillars, great copper cauldrons flanked the floor, and before each one an octagonal brass table set with sixteen bowls and a copper dipper. I looked into the nearest pot. Some glowing amber liquid, lit from within. I didn't touch it. You don't drink from a Fire Temple cauldron and you don't put your hand in to find out what it is.

At the south end stood the altar — a block of translucent golden apatite, three feet to a side and nine feet long, the light from the room sinking into it and coming back wrong. Skulls grinned from its flanks, inlaid with hammered gold. An eight-pointed fire symbol crowned it, set in wrought gold. Money. All of it money, hanging on the walls of a room nobody was getting paid out of anymore.

And before the altar, the fire pit. Diamond-shaped, longer than a man north to south, deep enough that I couldn't see the bottom without leaning, and its bottom was a bed of glowing coals. Tiny forms moved across them. Cavorting. Small things that belonged on the elemental plane of fire and not in a room I was standing in.

I stopped reading the room as inventory and started reading it as a problem.

There was something wrong about the pit. Not the fire — the *intent* under the fire. The way the rest of the hall arranged itself around it, the altar set so the worshippers would face it, the heat coming off it that felt less like temperature and more like attention. It was magical, and it was evil, and the two were the same thing in this place. That landed first.

There was only one place in a temple that worships fire where you put something you can't afford to lose.

*Gotcha.*

The treasure chest was in the pit. I'd have staked my cover on it.

"Because of the Fire Temple," Zephyr said, like it was the simplest thing in the world.

It was. Of course it was. Where else would they put it.

## calmer — The Fire Pit Disaster

The fire pit sat in the center of the chamber, coals glowing, the heat already inside my armor before I'd crossed the room. We had agreed — and by we I mean my expertise in the criminal and the demonic mind had agreed — that the most likely place anyone would stuff a treasure chest was right there, in the one spot guaranteed to cook you alive. "Oh, yes," I'd said, "I mean, where else would you stuff your treasure chest, right?" The reasoning was airtight. I want that noted. The reasoning, in isolation, was airtight.

Zephyr was already feeling around the edges of the problem. "Alright, who's… resistant to fire. Maybe me?"

"Perhaps we wish to dump water on it?" I offered. A flask of water. A magical fire pit of evil nature. Steam, presumably. Steam reveals things.

I looked at Zephyr. He looked back. "Yeah, I dropped the water, it's okay."

So I poured.

The water hit the coals and rose back up as steam. I studied it. I waited for the chest to reveal itself in the clearing vapor. Nothing revealed itself.

"Well," I said, "that didn't seem to work."

Then I noticed the small shapes in the pit. And they were growing. And growing. And growing.

"I think the fire's growing," Sequoia said.

It was not the fire.

"Who touched it?" Sequoia demanded.

"I think the water did?" I said.

"Who the fuck put water in there?"

"Bummer," said Zephyr, unhelpfully.

"Zephyr told me to do it!"

"We don't —" Sequoia turned on me with the full weight of a man who freed prisoners on principle. "You're the cleric, and you should have an ounce of intelligence."

"What do you mean I should have an ounce of it?" That stung. Mostly because we were now spending the one breath we had to act on the question of whose fault it was. I drew myself up. "You know, I did read about this just a few moments ago. Seem — well, I didn't read about it, I'm just trying to sound pompous and arrogant, but I do think that perhaps mixing water in the Fire Temple was a mistake."

"No shit, Sherlock."

"Come to think of it," I allowed, "that was probably not the best decision we could have made."

"Thanks, Captain Obvious."

This is the thing about advisors. I turned to Zephyr. "What am I supposed to do with advisors like that?" Then to the one I actually trusted. "Zinnia, you have experience in the realms of subterfuge. I shall be looking to you for advice."

"Not much," said Zephyr.

"Sequoia?"

"Y-don't look at me!"

"It's — team? Team? We're a team, right?"

"You put water in here!" Sequoia said. "Good idea, let's put water in the fire, like, what the — are we thinking?"

I had, in fact, been thinking. About leverage. About what a clever operative inside an enemy faction might do with the inverse of this principle. "You're gonna suggest putting fire in the water temple," Sequoia said before I could open my mouth.

"Is that a bad idea?" I asked. Sincerely. I wanted data.

Zinnia did not dignify it. Nobody did. Somewhere two levels down, water was finding a corridor it hadn't been invited into, and I filed the idea away anyway.

"Run?" Sequoia tried. "Y'all wanna, like, dip?"

"But the treasure's in there, you said!"

"You know what else is there? Like, big fiery elementals, probably."

"Well, yes, but if we want the treasure, we have to kill them! How are we supposed to blunder the Fire Temple if we don't kill them?"

"Well, maybe it could be a boon to the Fire Temple!"

"Well, yes, but now if the monsters are gonna come out, we kill them, and then we go and grab the treasure!" The logic, again, airtight. "Well, yes, but the damage has been done; we might as well deal with it. There was probably a secret way to do this, if I had time to read all of the books."

"Well, maybe you should have read," Sequoia said. "We sent you to the library to go read up on this!"

"Okay, well, I — well, how about we hide behind the pillars? I suggest I'm going to hide behind the pillars."

"Let's go hide behind the pillars," Sequoia agreed, and started shoving people into cover — Zinnia, Zephyr, both of them swept behind the brown marble columns like luggage. "Behind pillars."

Three salamanders came out of the pit. Long, coiling, the heat coming off them in waves I could feel through steel. They stood. They sniffed the air, tasting the room for prey.

We were all caught flat-footed. Every one of us. The salamanders moved before we had our footing. Nobody had briefed me on this possibility, which I consider a significant oversight. "Barely quicker than the rest of you," Zephyr said of his own reflexes — and his were the fastest of us, which tells you everything.

"Gentlemen," I said, "why didn't any of you tell me this was going to happen?"

"Well, you could've just thought about it, you know?" Zephyr said.

"Yo, you did not just —" Sequoia stared. "Calmer did not just say that. You put water to the Fire Temple!"

"Well, yes, but I — I'm not the smart one, yous guys are the smart."

"Yes, you are! By all objective metrics, you are!"

There was no time for objective metrics. "This is enough to work with," I said, and called the cold into my skin — frost rising off the plate, a rime of it crawling up my arms until the heat of the room broke against it. Anything that touched me now would feel winter for its trouble. "That's how I'll deal with him."

"You could have just gone into the fire," Zinnia observed.

The first salamander prowled toward the pillars, hunting Sequoia. It couldn't find him — he'd gone to ground perfectly, breath held, not a scrape of leather giving him away. It turned, baffled, and looked for an easier mark.

I was the easier mark. I always am. I am a man in shining plate standing in the open with frost steaming off him.

So I leaned into it. "ATTACK ME NOW! I am an enemy of all that is, fire!"

"Holy shut up!" Sequoia hissed from behind his column.

The second salamander obliged. It lunged, coils whipping out to wrap and crush — and I twisted out of the constriction, the grip closing on empty air and cold. It couldn't hold me. The third, finding the others as hidden as smoke, came for me too, the same speared lunge, the same coiling grab. I set my feet and refused it. The grip failed again, sliding off frost and steel.

Two of them, fully grown, fully furious, and neither could get a hold on me.

*But not me.*

## zephyr — The Fire Temple Skirmish

The three of them came up out of the fire pit. The bill for the Chimera, two levels overdue, arriving at last. Salamanders. Bodies of moving heat. The Fire Temple, finally meeting the people who burned its Chimera.

Calmer turned to us with the look of a man discovering weather. "Gentlemen, why didn't any of you tell me this was going to happen?"

I had a knife in my hand and a wall at my back. "Well, you could've just thought about it, you know?"

That set Sequoia off — *you put water to the Fire Temple* — and I let it run, because the accounting on the water was mine and I wasn't paying it in the middle of a fight. *I had to throw in the water.* Calmer asked. That's a defensible expense.

Calmer wrapped himself in his shield — frost and flame braided together, the cold edge of it crawling out from his plate — and screamed at three creatures of living fire that he was the enemy of all that is fire. *Holy shut up*, said Sequoia, and he wasn't wrong, but it worked. The man made himself the loudest, brightest, most obvious thing in the room. *Louder*, I thought. We really should have named him Louder.

Because that was the gift. I went still and the shadow took me — clean, no scuff, no breath — and the salamanders looked everywhere and found nothing but a yelling cleric. Sequoia nearly gave the whole thing away, his foot finding the one loose stone in the temple, then clawing the luck back at the last second while the rest of us held our breath. Not my problem. I was already gone. They couldn't see me. They couldn't smell me. They saw Calmer.

So I reckoned it the only way I do. Are they made of fire, or only wearing it? A blade through that skin says they're wearing it. Fine.

The tiefling breathes.

I came out of the dark on the one that had never moved. It never knew the room had a fourth occupant. The first cut opened it, and then the real work — the strike that lands before the thing knows it's in a fight, every ounce of patience cashing out at once, the blade going in to the guard and the heat hissing where the steel parted it. Then the second knife, just to round the bill. The salamander folded around the wound like the fire had been let out of it.

Thud. The biggest single thing I've ever put into a living creature, all of it spent before it could spend anything on me.

This is starting to be a very lucrative operation.

Zinnia came across the floor next, closing the gap to make the strike heavier, hit the second one hard enough that it staggered — and then she was gone again, out of reach before the heat could answer her. He knows the rule. I watched him take it.

Calmer tried to ring his death-bell at one of them and the magic slid off it. Sequoia stepped in and finished the bloodied one with a clean stroke — *one, two, three, let's get rid of this guy* — and then learned what the heat pouring off them costs just for standing close.

The survivor turned on him. Spear of fire, then the arms, and Sequoia came out of it looking thin. "Man, you can still take half of what's left of me in a single breath."

"You're gonna wanna stay as far away from these guys as possible."

"I'm staying close." Of course I was. Distance is for people who haven't already won the opening.

The thing speared Calmer too, and the frost shield bit it back harder than it had bitten — the cold pouring off the cleric's armor into a creature that hates cold worse than anything, and it broke and ran. Calmer swung his Mace of Smiting at its back and hit nothing but the smell of it.

Then I overreached. Took the measure of what I could do, traded my second knife for a better angle, came out of the dark again — and missed. Twice. With every edge I'd built myself. Both knives finding air. *This again.* The good rounds get paid for too.

Sequoia, one solid hit from done, peeled off the line to beg healing and put an arrow somewhere into the masonry on his way out. Zinnia struck and ran a third time, patient as a closing account. Calmer mended the halfling and lit the last salamander with a column of clean light.

And then we stopped, the survivors bloodied, the corridor reeking of char and burned scale and the wet-iron smell of Calmer's frost still dissipating in the air.

"Easy fight," I said. "Fire Temple, has much to fear from us."

They wanted to talk about leadership after that. About the water. About whose temple this was anyway. I let them — but I had one thing to put on the record. "If he's the boss, he has to act like the boss."

Amateur hour, boss. That's what happens when you promote a man above his qualifications.

*I made a note to brief him before the next one.*
