We could go down the front entrance now. That was the difference, and I felt it the way I feel a room rearrange itself — not a door we picked a lock on, not a passage we crawled through behind a Cloak of Elvenkind. The front. The beaten brass doors. We owned the building, or near enough that no one left alive would say otherwise.

There were two ways into the Fire Temple, and we stood and weighed them, which is the kind of thing amateurs do too long. Sequoia ended it the way he ends things.

"We should go plunder this before we lose the opportunity, too," he said. Then, warming to it: "Man, here's the new plan of how we're gonna take down the temple. We're gonna bankrupt it. Nobody's getting paid."

*One does not simply take bribes to defect.* But this — this was cleaner. Take the money. Leave nothing to fight over. I had no argument with it.

The brass doors swung. We walked in.

Red granite, scores of flambeaux, the air thick with heated metal and sulfur — it sat at the back of my throat before I'd taken two steps. There was no doubt what this place was. Tapestries on the north wall, fire and burning forms worked into the cloth. The torches threw light, but it was the granite that held my eye: blue and mica flakes in the polished stone, and they did not sit still. They leapt. They danced, as if the rock had caught fire somewhere underneath and the flame was trying to get out. A trick of the light. Probably. The whole hall carried rust-colored motes drifting in the air that wasn't quite air.

A brass tube hung from the ceiling on brass chains, long as a fallen tree and twice my reach across, a shallow fire pit beneath it and low brass stands stacked with charcoal blocks to either side. Ritual furniture. I logged it and moved my eyes on.

"Do you keep going in?"

"Yes," Zephyr said.

East and west, past two rows of pillars, great copper cauldrons flanked the floor, and before each one an octagonal brass table set with sixteen bowls and a copper dipper. I looked into the nearest pot. Some glowing amber liquid, lit from within. I didn't touch it. You don't drink from a Fire Temple cauldron and you don't put your hand in to find out what it is.

At the south end stood the altar — a block of translucent golden apatite, three feet to a side and nine feet long, the light from the room sinking into it and coming back wrong. Skulls grinned from its flanks, inlaid with hammered gold. An eight-pointed fire symbol crowned it, set in wrought gold. Money. All of it money, hanging on the walls of a room nobody was getting paid out of anymore.

And before the altar, the fire pit. Diamond-shaped, longer than a man north to south, deep enough that I couldn't see the bottom without leaning, and its bottom was a bed of glowing coals. Tiny forms moved across them. Cavorting. Small things that belonged on the elemental plane of fire and not in a room I was standing in.

I stopped reading the room as inventory and started reading it as a problem.

There was something wrong about the pit. Not the fire — the *intent* under the fire. The way the rest of the hall arranged itself around it, the altar set so the worshippers would face it, the heat coming off it that felt less like temperature and more like attention. It was magical, and it was evil, and the two were the same thing in this place. That landed first.

The rest came right behind it, the way these things do — not reasoning, just arrival. I have spent enough time inside the criminal mind, and we have all spent more than enough time now inside the demonic one. Where do you hide what you can't afford to lose, in a temple that worships flame? You put it where no one sane would reach. You stuff it in the fire.

*Gotcha.*

The treasure chest was in the pit. I'd have wagered the whole take on it.

"Because of the Fire Temple," Zephyr said, like it was the simplest thing in the world.

It was. Of course it was. Where else would they put it.
