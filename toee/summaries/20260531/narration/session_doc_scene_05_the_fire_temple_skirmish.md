The three of them came up out of the fire pit like the pit had been waiting for an excuse, and the corridor filled with the smell of a war we'd started two levels up arriving on schedule down here. Salamanders. Bodies of moving heat. The Fire Temple, finally meeting the people who burned its Chimera.

Calmer turned to us with the look of a man discovering weather. "Gentlemen, why didn't any of you tell me this was going to happen?"

I had a knife in my hand and a wall at my back. "Well, you could've just thought about it, you know?"

That set Sequoia off — *you put water to the Fire Temple* — and I let it run, because the accounting on the water was mine and I wasn't paying it in the middle of a fight. *I had to throw in the water.* Calmer asked. That's a defensible expense.

Calmer wrapped himself in his shield — frost and flame braided together, the cold edge of it crawling out from his plate — and screamed at three fire elementals that he was the enemy of all that is fire. *Holy shut up*, said Sequoia, and he wasn't wrong, but it worked. The man made himself the loudest, brightest, most obvious thing in the room. *Louder*, I thought. We really should have named him Louder.

Because that was the gift. I went still and the shadow took me — clean, no scuff, no breath — and the salamanders looked everywhere and found nothing but a yelling cleric. Sequoia nearly gave the whole thing away, his foot finding the one loose stone in the temple, then clawing the luck back at the last second while the table held its breath. Not my problem. I was already gone. They couldn't see me. They couldn't smell me. They saw Calmer.

So I did the math the only way I do it. Are they made of fire, or only wearing it? A blade through that skin says they're wearing it. Fine.

The tiefling breathes.

I came out of the dark on the one that had never taken a turn. It never knew the room had a fourth occupant. The first cut opened it, and then the real work — the strike that lands before the thing knows it's in a fight, every ounce of patience cashing out at once, the blade going in to the guard and the heat hissing where the steel parted it. Then the second knife, just to round the bill. The salamander folded around the wound like the fire had been let out of it.

Thud. The biggest single thing I've ever put into a living creature, all of it spent before it could spend anything on me.

This is starting to be a very lucrative operation.

Zinnia came across the floor next, ran the distance to make the strike heavier, hit the second one hard enough that it staggered — and then she was gone again, out of reach before the heat could answer her. She knows the rule. I watched her take it.

Calmer tried to ring his death-bell at one of them and the magic slid off it. Sequoia stepped in and finished the bloodied one with a clean stroke — *one, two, three, let's get rid of this guy* — and then learned what the heat aura costs just for standing near them.

The survivor turned on him. Spear of fire, then the arms, and Sequoia came out of it looking thin. "Man, you can still have half my HP in one round."

"You're gonna wanna stay as far away from these guys as possible."

"I'm staying close." Of course I was. Distance is for people who haven't already won the first round.

The thing speared Calmer too, and the frost shield bit it back harder than it had bitten — the cold pouring off the cleric's armor into a creature that hates cold worse than anything, and it broke and ran. Calmer swung his Mace of Smiting at its back and hit nothing but the smell of it.

Then I overreached. Read my own abilities, traded my second knife for a better angle, came out of the dark again — and missed. Twice. With every advantage I'd built myself. Both knives finding air. *This again.* The good rounds get paid for too.

Sequoia, one solid hit from done, peeled off the line to beg healing and put an arrow somewhere into the masonry on his way out. Zinnia struck and ran a third time, patient as a closing account. Calmer mended the halfling and lit the last salamander with a column of clean light.

And then we stopped, the survivors bloodied, the corridor stinking of cold and char.

"Easy fight," I said. "Fire Temple, has much to fear from us."

They wanted to talk about leadership after that. About the water. About whose temple this was anyway. I let them — but I had one thing to put on the record. "If he's the boss, he has to act like the boss."

Amateur hour, boss. That's what happens when you promote a man above his qualifications.

"Yep, yep," I said. "Guess they're in this place."
