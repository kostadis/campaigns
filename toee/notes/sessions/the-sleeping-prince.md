# The Sleeping Prince — The Vampire in the Iron Coffin

Co-GM working doc. Not canon yet — staging for the cleanup-tenday session(s) after the temple war, in which the Prince Thrommel quest is introduced. Two acts: a **political thriller** (three handlers, sequenced, racing Barkinar's review) that delivers the party to a coffin chamber on Level 3, and an **Indiana Jones / Mummy set-piece** in which they must figure out that the "vampire" in the iron coffin is a prince.

Party: the Obsidian Edge — **Calmer** (War Cleric of St. Cuthbert, undercover; now installed as the Earth Temple's chief cleric — the key figure for Act 2), plus **Zephyr**, **Sequioa**, and **Zinnia** (the assassin/rogue/monk trio; Zinnia is the most perceptive — the right target for Falrinth's contact). Level **~8** (the Fire Temple salamanders this session were sized for L8; bump if they level on the way out — confirm). **Timeframe: the one-week window Barkinar granted them** (chapters 27–28) to organize the upper temple and plunder the Fire Temple. The session ends when **Barkinar returns for his end-of-week check-in** — that return is the clock, not a first meeting (he's already met them).

---

## The Win Condition (state plainly to yourself)

This session lands if, by the time Barkinar's boots hit the upper temple, the party has been touched by **all three handlers** (Falrinth → Mother Screng → Lucius), understands that **someone important is sleeping below**, and *wants* to go get him — and ideally has descended to area 334 and faced the coffin. The emotional target: they walk into a tomb expecting a monster and walk out (or don't) having understood it was a man. The political thriller is the *delivery system*; the vampire reveal is the *payload*.

You do **not** need Thrommel extracted this session. Reaching the coffin and cracking (or failing) the illusion is a complete, satisfying climax. Extraction can be the cliffhanger.

## The Default Trajectory (what happens if the party does nothing)

- **Falrinth makes his probe regardless.** The illusory hooded man visits Zinnia (per `notes/falrinth_strategy.md`). If the party ignores it, he simply has more time and they have less warning about Lucius.
- **Y'dey keeps waiting.** She has waited months as Mother Screng. She does not surface unless the party comes to Nulb or gives Murfles a reason. If they never go to town, this vector stalls — which is *fine*; it just means Falrinth and Lucius carry the session.
- **Lucius probes anyway.** He wants to know what the party knows. If they show no interest in the deep levels, his probe is gentle and he relaxes (bad for the party long-term — he stops watching them). If they show interest, his probe sharpens into a *test*.
- **Thrommel sleeps on.** The Prince has been in stasis for years. Nothing about him changes. The clock is entirely political: Veluna and Furyondy have written him off as dead. The window is that *this crew, right now,* owns the upper temple. No one will ever be this well-placed again. That's the "opportunity of a generation" — and it is real, not hype.
- **Barkinar arrives** to inspect his new "lieutenants." If the party has done nothing interesting, the inspection is a flat loyalty check. If they've been poking at the deep levels, Barkinar's arrival becomes a pressure valve — see the Clock.

## The Clever Play (what the optimal party would do — so you know when to nudge)

1. **Treat Falrinth's bait as intelligence, not a command.** Don't commit; go to town to verify.
2. **Let Mother Screng surface.** Bring Calmer to the herb shop. Y'dey hands them the truth Falrinth only hinted at: a living hostage of immense political value, and a *clean* reason to extract him (Veluna/Furyondy gratitude, a counterweight to the Temple).
3. **Stall Lucius.** Reveal nothing. Use the meeting to learn what the Temple is protecting without showing they already know.
4. **Use their dungeon-boss access** to reach Level 3 *before* Barkinar's review boxes them in — they have a narrow window where they command the upper temple but Barkinar hasn't yet locked down the deep levels against them.
5. **In the chamber: look before they strike.** The whole set-piece rewards investigation over reflex. The party that "kills the vampire" loses the prince.

The three handlers all point at the same room for opposite reasons. The party's job is to notice that and decide *whose* version of "free the sleeper" they're actually serving.

## Scene 1 — Falrinth's Probe (the opener)

### Setup
- **When:** late at night, the first or second night of the cleanup tenday, in the party's claimed quarters on Level 2.
- **Who:** Falrinth via an illusory image (he never risks himself). He addresses **Zinnia** — most perceptive, the right read per his dossier.
- This is the contact already scripted in `notes/falrinth_strategy.md` — use that pitch verbatim if you like. **The one addition for this arc:** he dangles the sleeper.
- **Now extra-loaded:** Barkinar already spoke Falrinth's name to the party (ch28 — "a wizard hiding from the Greater Temple, to be discussed later"). So when the image names itself, it lands on a name they've *already heard from their own boss.* Falrinth knows this and uses it — he's getting ahead of whatever Barkinar plans to tell them about him.

### The beat
Run the candle-in-the-window probe as written (scarab warning, the Lucius name-drop with golden eyes, "do not commit, stall"). Then, as the image begins to fade, he adds the hook — almost an afterthought, which is how you make players lean in:

> "One more thing, since you now hold these wretched halls. There is a room below — deep, forgotten, on the level beneath your feet. The holy gentlemen of this place keep something in it. A *sleeper.* I have never been able to reach it; my leash is short and the gates are watched. But you... you have keys I do not. If you ever wish to make Verbobonc and all its banners come crashing down on this Temple — and oh, they would come, for *this* — wake what sleeps in the iron coffin. Ask your golden-eyed friend what he is so afraid you'll find. *Then* signal me."

The image is gone before they can question it.

> **GM-only — what Falrinth actually wants.** He does NOT care about the Prince. He wants Veluna and Furyondy to march on the Temple, because a coalition siege is exactly the kind of chaos that keeps Verbobonc's gaze fixed *here* and away from Lolth's real work (the Demonweb invasion prep — `falrinth_strategy.md`). Freeing Thrommel is, for Falrinth, a *detonator*, not a rescue. He won't say "prince" because he wants the party hungry to find out, and because the less he commits, the safer he is. He also genuinely doesn't know whether they're already Lucius's tools — this is still a probe.

### Branch — party tries to interrogate the image
It's a one-way *Magic Mouth*-style projection with limited interactivity; Falrinth anticipated three or four questions and pre-loaded vague answers. Press too hard and it just repeats "signal me" and fades. **Failure isn't punished, but they get nothing more** — the point is to send them looking elsewhere. Which is town.

### Branch — party reports the image upward immediately
They're the bosses now — there's no puppet to report to (Hartsch is dead; Calmer holds the Earth Temple). Reporting it *up* means reporting to Barkinar / the Greater Temple. Viable, and Barkinar would be *very* interested that Falrinth made contact — but it puts the party on record as having dealt with a known fugitive, and Lucius's network will hear of it. See the Clock. A careful party keeps Falrinth's contact to themselves until they understand it.

### Branch — party goes straight down to Level 3 that night
Possible! They own the upper levels. But the bronze gates to the Greater Temple are watched, and going deep *before* they have cover or a reason is how they trip Lucius's tripwires early. Let them — but Lucius's probe (Scene 3) then comes *hard and fast* and the Barkinar clock accelerates. Reward the boldness with danger, not a closed door.

### NPC notes
- **Falrinth** — oily, transactional, evaluating not befriending. Tic: theatrical apology for the "theatrics." Wants: information and a useful catspaw. Never says "prince."

## Scene 2 — Mother Screng Reaches Out (the truth, in town)

Falrinth made them curious enough to go to town — for information, for resources, for a sage who knows what "a sleeper in an iron coffin under the Temple" might mean. That's the doorway **Y'dey** has been waiting at for months.

### Setup
- **When:** a day or two later, in **Nulb** (the herb shop) or **Hommlet**, whenever the party goes to town to dig.
- **Who:** first **Murfles** ("Hruda," the surly "daughter") makes contact and tests them; then **Y'dey / Mother Screng** reveals herself — *especially* if Calmer is present (see `notes/nulb_allies.md`, Beats 1–3).
- This pays off the long-dangling **"Y'dey is missing"** thread. She was never missing. She went undercover. Terjon's evasiveness was *protection*, not guilt.

### The beat — Murfles tests, then Y'dey surfaces
Run the herb-shop contact from `nulb_allies.md`. Murfles asks too-specific questions; if the party passes (and especially if Calmer answers a Cuthbertine call-and-response correctly), Mother Screng lets the disguise slip — for Calmer:

> "You are Calmer of the Earth Temple, they say. I am Mother Screng of the herb shop, they say. Both true. Both also incomplete."

Then she gives them what Falrinth would not — **the whole truth**:

> "There is a man beneath that Temple. Not a relic, not a rumor. **Prince Thrommel** — Grand Marshall of Furyondy, Provost of Veluna, betrothed of her Ladyship Jolene. The kingdoms buried him as dead ten years ago. He is not dead. He sleeps, bound by the Temple's foulest art, and they have hidden him so cleverly that no power of Good has ever laid eyes on him — because I could never get *in.* For ten years I have sat in this shop and waited for someone who could.
>
> You hold the upper halls now. You can walk where the Church of St. Cuthbert cannot. Bring him out — alive — and you do not merely wound this Temple. You hand Veluna and Furyondy the one thing that turns a quiet purge into a holy crusade. This is the opportunity of a generation. There will not be another."

> **GM-only — what Y'dey actually wants.** Exactly what she says. She is the *honest* vector — no hidden agenda, which is itself disorienting in this campaign. Her only blind spot: she doesn't know about Hedrack's coup, the Orb, or that Falrinth and Lucius are also steering the party at the same room. She thinks she's the only one who knows the Prince lives. She is wrong, and that's the dramatic irony — three people are sending the party to the same coffin and she thinks she's alone.

### What Y'dey can warn them about (the useful intel)
- **The "vampire" disguise** — IF her network's intelligence is good. Decide how much she knows (Open Decisions). Best dramatic value: she knows he's hidden by "the Temple's foulest art" and warns them *"do not trust your eyes down there,"* but does NOT know the specific vampire-illusion. That preserves the Act 2 reveal while justifying the party bringing *true seeing* / *dispel* tools.
- **Fragarach** — she may know the Prince's holy sword was lost with him. A grail-detail to plant: "He carried a blade called Fragarach. Find the man, find the sword."
- **Murfles and Otis** as operational support topside (extraction logistics, a safe route out of Nulb, healing).

### Branch — Calmer is NOT brought to town
Y'dey is far more cautious. Murfles still makes contact but Y'dey stays hidden, passing the Thrommel truth through Murfles in fragments. The reveal is colder, slower — but still lands. Don't gate the whole vector on Calmer; gate the *warmth* of it on Calmer.

### Branch — party is hostile / suspects a Temple trap
Reasonable paranoia — they're undercover and a Nulb herbalist just named their deepest secret. Y'dey expects this. Her proof: she knows things only a real St. Cuthbert canon would (Cuthbertine liturgy, the name of Calmer's seminary, the Chest of Rao's true significance to Veluna). Let Calmer's player adjudicate whether it rings true — **players are the authority on their character's read.**

### NPC notes
- **Y'dey / Mother Screng** — tired, patient, fiercely faithful, ten years deep in a cover that's eaten her life. Tic: speaks in St. Cuthbert's plainspoken cadence even as "Screng." Wants: the Prince out, full stop.
- **Murfles ("Hruda")** — surly, plain, sharp. Tests before she trusts. Wants: protect Y'dey.

## Scene 3 — Lucius Wants a Meeting (the trap)

Now the screw turns. Falrinth named him ("a flattering man with golden eyes"). The party may already be wary. **Lucius requests an audience** — framed as Temple business, post-war housekeeping, getting to know the upper temple's new managers.

### Setup
- **When:** later in the tenday, after the party has been to town (Lucius's surface network — Zert at the Welcome Wench, Madame Selentis — will have *noticed* them digging). Could be at the Temple or a neutral meet.
- **Who:** **Lucius Graeme / "Aldren,"** human cleric, golden-eyed, warm, dangerous. He communes with Zuggtmoy directly. He is the coup's operational hand (`notes/hedrack_strategy.md`).

### The beat — a warm interrogation disguised as a welcome
Lucius is charming, generous, complimentary. He congratulates them on the war. He hints at "greater responsibilities." And woven through it, he is *fishing*:

> "You've been to Nulb, I hear. And Hommlet. Restocking, no doubt — war is thirsty work. Tell me, did anyone down there fill your heads with the usual ghost stories? People love to imagine this Temple is full of buried treasures and sleeping kings." *(a light laugh, golden eyes steady on each of them in turn)* "There's nothing below but rock and the faithful's work. I'd hate for clever operators like you to waste a tenday chasing a Nulb herbalist's bedtime tale."

> **GM-only — what Lucius actually wants.** To learn **whether the party knows about Thrommel.** The Prince is the Temple's crown-jewel hostage — leverage over two kingdoms. If the party reveals they know, they become a liability Lucius reports straight to Hedrack, and their cover is functionally blown (they'd be watched, isolated, possibly removed). If they convincingly *don't* know, Lucius relaxes and the secret stays buried — and the party keeps their access. **Lucius mentioning the "sleeping king" ghost story himself is the tell that he's testing them** — an innocent man wouldn't raise it. A sharp player (Insight) catches that he's the one who brought it up.

### The core tension — this is the scene where the quest can DIE
This is the one handler who can *close* the quest instead of opening it. If the party blurts what they know:
- **Cover-blown branch:** Lucius's warmth doesn't drop — that's what makes him scary. He just *files it.* Within a day, their access to the deep levels quietly tightens; a "reassignment" is proposed; Barkinar's inspection turns into a containment. The Prince becomes much harder to reach. **This isn't a fail-state — it's a harder game.** Now they must reach the coffin against active suspicion, or move *fast* before the net closes.
- **Stall branch (the clever play):** they play dumb, maybe feed Lucius a *different* thing they "found in town" to satisfy his fishing (a rumor about Senshock, a bandit lead — anything that isn't the Prince). Lucius leaves satisfied. They keep their access AND now *know for certain* there's something below worth this much misdirection. Falrinth's "ask what he's so afraid you'll find" pays off in real time.

### Branch — party tries to flip or threaten Lucius
He's too well-placed and too calm. He deflects, and the attempt itself tells him they're a problem. Save any Lucius confrontation for much later in the Hedrack arc — he is not killable-or-flippable in a single welcome meeting, and trying tips the whole coup's hand. If they push, he smiles and leaves, and the Barkinar clock slams forward.

### NPC notes
- **Lucius Graeme** — warm, golden-eyed, generous, lethal. Never raises his voice; never drops the smile. Tic: compliments that are also probes. Wants: to know what they know. Will never say "Thrommel" — naming the Prince would be the very leak he's hunting.

## The Barkinar Clock (the deadline that caps Act One)

Barkinar already installed the party and **gave them one week** to organize the upper temple (ch27–28). His **return at the end of that week** is the deadline this whole session runs toward.

- Ideally, **all three handler reveals happen before Barkinar's week-end return.** That return is the wall the clock runs into.
- Barkinar is a *sincere Iuz operative* who genuinely believes the party are legitimate Iuz-aligned assets (`hedrack_strategy.md`). He does **not** know about the coup, and he doesn't know the party are digging for the Prince — *unless Lucius told him.*
- **The Falrinth collision.** Barkinar already told the party Falrinth is "a matter to be discussed later." At the week-end check-in he may well *task them with finding/dealing with Falrinth* — at the very moment the party has already been secretly contacted by him. That's a delicious squeeze: do they admit Falrinth reached out? Pretend they've never heard from him? Use the tasking as cover to pursue the sleeper (since Falrinth lives on the same level as the coffin)?
- **Barkinar is also the party's plausible route deeper.** They've already been down to his audience chamber once; a week-end debrief, or a Falrinth-hunting mandate, gives them sanctioned reason to be on Level 3 — cover to reach area 334.
- **If Lucius was tipped:** the check-in is colder, with more muscle, and Barkinar asks pointed questions about where the party has been. Tense standoff.
- **If Lucius was stalled:** the check-in is a gruff but routine loyalty review — and, via the Falrinth tasking, a door to the deep.
- **Parallel pressure:** Barkinar also wants the **Chest of Rao** recovered (delivered to the late Romag; now in Earth Temple hands). A useful errand that can pull the party between town and the deep levels, and a thing Lucius/Selentis can watch them chase.

> **GM-only — timing the clock.** Don't let Barkinar arrive until the three handlers have all touched the party. If players are moving fast, Barkinar is "still a day out." If they're dawdling, a runner announces Barkinar is "ascending tomorrow" to light a fire. The clock serves the story; it is not a fixed turn-count.

# ACT TWO — The Inky Chamber (Indiana Jones / The Mummy)

The political thriller has done its job: the party knows there's a sleeper, wants him, and has a window (Barkinar's arrival) to reach Level 3. Now the register **flips** — from loaded conversations to a torchlit tomb and a puzzle that punishes reflex. This is **area 334, the Inky Chamber.** Run it like the opening of a *Mummy* film: dread, dust, a sarcophagus, and a horrible thing that is not what it looks like.

### Getting there
- The coffin chamber is on **Dungeon Level 3** (the Greater Temple level), near the Inky Chamber, area 334. Falrinth's lair (337) is on this level too — note he's *near* the prize, which is why he knows of it.
- Access opens via the party's **dungeon-boss authority** (they've already been escorted down to Barkinar's audience chamber once), via a **Barkinar-sanctioned Falrinth hunt** (Falrinth is on Level 3, near the coffin), or by sneaking the bronze gates during the week-end debrief. The descent itself can carry one or two Indiana-Jones obstacles if you want texture (a watched gate, a gargoyle perch, a collapsing war-damaged stair) — but keep them light. **The room is the set-piece, not the corridor.**

### The room — layered reveal (run it slowly, one layer per action)
Pace this like peeling an onion. Each layer should make the party *more* sure it's a vampire, right up until it isn't.

1. **First look (read aloud):**
   > "A bare hexagonal room, thick with dust and old cobwebs, forgotten by everyone. By the door, a rag-clad skeleton — some cleric who died here of causes no one recorded. The longer you look into the dark of this place, the more a vague, crawling unease settles over you. The room appears empty."
2. **Detect magic** → a strong pulse from the **northwest** corner. **Detect evil** → the *whole room* gives off a dim radiation of malign power. *(This is the hook that makes Calmer's instincts fire. Lean into it.)*
3. **Investigate the NW corner:**
   > "A black iron coffin stands against the northwest wall. Its heavy iron lid lies beside it — and inlaid in the lid is a silver cross. Atop the lid rests a forgotten scroll case."
4. **The sarcophagus is invisible.** *Dispel magic, detect invisibility, dispel illusion,* or *dust of appearance* reveals a lidless stone sarcophagus on a stand.
5. **Inside (the gut-punch):**
   > "Within lies a vampire — a corpse clad in black, the flesh hale and unrotted, the face almost peaceful. A wooden stake stands driven through its heart."
6. **The truth (only with *true seeing*, *gem of seeing*, or similar):**
   > "The vampire flickers and is gone — illusion peeling away like burnt paper. In its place: a handsome, mail-clad man with golden hair, in a white surcoat quartered with the arms of Furyondy, Veluna, and the red antlers of the Knights of the Hart. A gold belt and dagger at his waist; a gold chain at his throat bearing a crown and crescent moon; a matching ring on his left hand. His shield rests beneath his feet. He is not dead. He breathes — once, slowly — and does not wake."
   The same true-sight reveals the **silver cross in the lid is no cross at all — it is a magical broadsword, Fragarach,** sheathed in illusion.

### The central trap — the party's own instincts
> **GM-only — this is the whole point of the scene.** The cult built this as a *jape at the forces of Good.* The scroll in the case spells it out:
> *"Let the Good ones use their newfound strength to seal in the horror of darkness to prove their faithfulness to right and justice. We who wrote this lacked the power to do so."*
> (The case also holds a scroll of two *Strength* spells — the "newfound strength" the jape refers to.)
>
> A vampire. A silver cross. A stake through the heart. Malign evil radiating from the room. **Every reflex a paladin or a cleric of St. Cuthbert has been trained on says: destroy it, burn it, seal it, leave it staked.** Calmer is the detonator — his orthodoxy is being *weaponized.* If the party acts on instinct — pours holy water, burns the coffin, "finishes" the staked vampire, re-seals the lid and walks away — **they have done exactly what the Temple wanted: left Prince Thrommel entombed (or worse).** The malign aura is the illusion's seasoning, not the man.
>
> The scene rewards the party that *stops and looks* over the party that *acts.* That's the Indiana-Jones beat: the trap isn't a dart-trap, it's the hero's certainty.

**Make them earn the doubt.** Cues that something is off, for players without *true seeing*:
- A staked vampire that radiates evil yet has never risen in a forgotten, sealed room — vampires don't *stay* staked politely for years.
- The mocking scroll explicitly daring the Good to "seal in the horror." Why would evil *want* good clerics to destroy a vampire? (It wouldn't — unless the vampire is the lie.)
- The "silver cross" is in the *lid that was removed* — displayed, not protecting anything. Odd.
- Detect-magic's *strong* pulse is disproportionate to a mundane staked corpse.

If they have no true-sight magic at all, this is where **Y'dey's warning** ("do not trust your eyes down there") and **Falrinth's** ("wake what sleeps") are meant to make them hesitate and *try dispelling the illusion* rather than destroying the occupant. If they're still about to torch it, give Calmer a passive-Wisdom or a St. Cuthbert *moment of doubt* — a beat where the "evil" feels staged.

### Waking the Prince
- **Removing him from the chamber** wakes him: groggy and amnesiac for **1d4 turns**, uncertain of his own name and past. Then he settles — cautious, circumspect, taciturn. A decade in stasis under an enemy's illusion makes a man careful.
- **Fragarach:** if the sword is in his sight when he comes to, *he speaks a word and it flies to his hand* — a fantastic visual; make sure a PC is holding or has revealed it. If they left it behind, he doesn't know where it is but asks whether he was found wearing a blade.
- **First words** (cautious, regal, disoriented): *"...How long. Tell me plainly — how long was I in the dark? And whose banners fly over Furyondy now?"*

### The extraction tension (cliffhanger fuel)
- They are on **Level 3, in the heart of the Greater Temple, with Barkinar present or nearby**, holding the one secret the Temple would kill to keep. Getting a groggy paladin lord *up and out* through territory they only half-control is the heist climax.
- If Lucius was tipped, the net is already closing — they may be carrying Thrommel past the very people hunting for the leak.
- **Failure here is material, not lethal:** if they're caught extracting him, they don't necessarily die — they might lose the Prince (re-taken, re-hidden, or "martyred"), lose their cover, lose Barkinar's trust, or be forced to flee the Temple entirely, turning the campaign's whole board over. Losing the Prince *again* after finding him is the cruelest, most playable failure.

## Anticipated player questions

- **"Why would Falrinth help us free a prince?"** → He won't say. (GM: he wants the coalition siege, not the rescue.) If they ask Y'dey or reason it out, let an Insight/Intelligence check surface that whoever wakes the Prince *also* brings a war down on the Temple — and ask themselves who benefits from that.
- **"Is Mother Screng a trap?"** → No — she's the honest vector. Her proof is Cuthbertine liturgy and knowledge only a real canon would have. But let them stay paranoid; the campaign has earned their distrust.
- **"Can we just tell Lucius we know, and bluff that we're loyal?"** → Dangerous. Telling Lucius = telling Hedrack. Leave the *stall* open and the *confession* costly. Don't forbid it — let them choose the hard road.
- **"Can we kill Lucius / Barkinar now?"** → Not viably this session. Both are load-bearing for the Hedrack arc. Attempts tip the coup's hand and accelerate the clock. They *can* try; the cost is the whole board lighting up early.
- **"It's a vampire — do we destroy it?"** → Let them. Don't warn them out of character. The reveal only means anything if the danger of getting it wrong is real. (Have Y'dey's/Falrinth's warnings and the staged-evil cues be the lifeline.)
- **"What's the silver cross / the sword?"** → True-sight reveals Fragarach. Without it, it reads as a vampire-hunter's silver cross — another layer of the trap.

## Stage directions (timing / silence cues)

- **Falrinth's "one more thing"** — drop it *as the image is fading,* so they can't interrogate it. Curiosity is the hook; denial of follow-up is the barb.
- **Y'dey's reveal** — let the disguise-slip land in **silence.** Don't rush past "Both true. Both also incomplete." Let Calmer's player sit with it.
- **Lucius raising the "sleeping king" story himself** — say it lightly and *move on fast,* as if it's nothing. The sharp player catches that he's the one who brought it up. Don't flag the tell; let them find it.
- **The room, layer by layer** — one reveal per player action. Do NOT volunteer that it's an illusion. Make "the room appears empty" do real work; make the staked vampire feel like the answer.
- **The flicker to Thrommel** (true-sight) — this is the money shot. Read it slowly. Then be quiet and let them react.
- **Thrommel's first breath** — a beat of silence before "How long was I in the dark?" Let the table feel the decade.
- **If a PC moves to destroy the "vampire"** — do not editorialize. Ask "are you sure?" only if you'd ask it of any irreversible action, no more. The weight should come from the fiction, not a GM nudge.

## Open decisions (for you — I won't decide these)

1. **How much does Y'dey know about the disguise?** Recommended: she knows he's hidden by "foul art" and warns "don't trust your eyes," but does NOT know the specific vampire-illusion — preserves the Act 2 reveal while justifying the party packing *true seeing* / *dispel*. Your call.
2. **Does the party have access to *true seeing* / *gem of seeing* / *dispel illusion*?** This determines whether Act 2 is solvable by magic or must be solved by *reasoning* (the staged-evil cues). Both are good; decide what tools they have and tune the cues accordingly. If they have none, lean hard on the warnings + the mocking scroll.
3. **Lucius's order — before or after town?** I've placed him *after* town (so his surface network has noticed them digging). If you'd rather he move first and pre-empt Y'dey, swap Scenes 2 and 3 — but then he's probing before they know anything, which is less tense. Recommended order stands: Falrinth → Y'dey → Lucius.
4. **Is Barkinar tipped by Lucius?** Depends entirely on whether the party leaks to Lucius in Scene 3. Decide the consequence live, based on their play.
5. **Does the extraction resolve this session or cliffhang?** Reaching + cracking the coffin is a complete climax. I'd cliffhang the *extraction* (Prince in hand, Barkinar between them and the surface) unless you want a longer session.
6. **Fragarach's whereabouts.** Canon puts it *in the coffin lid* (the "silver cross"). Keep it there for the great "it flies to his hand" beat — or, if you want a side-quest, have the cult moved it elsewhere and make recovering it a follow-up.
7. **The Lareth landmine.** Falrinth "appreciated" that the party killed Lareth (his ally). If they ever connect Falrinth to Lareth, his motives get murkier. Decide whether that surfaces here or stays held for the Hedrack arc.
