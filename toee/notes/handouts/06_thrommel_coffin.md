# HANDOUT — The Iron Coffin (Area 334)

Two artifacts for the Inky Chamber set-piece (see `notes/the-sleeping-prince.md`, Act Two). **A** is a found object — the mocking scroll in the case atop the coffin lid. **B** is a reveal prop — what the party sees when the vampire-illusion is pierced, used to identify the sleeper as Prince Thrommel. Surface them in that order, and never in the same beat.

---

## ARTIFACT A — The Mocking Scroll

> **GM context:** A brass scroll tube lies atop the removed iron coffin lid, beside the inlaid "silver cross." **Passive discovery — the party must open the case.** Inside are *two* scraps of parchment: one is a usable scroll of two *Strength* spells (real, castable); the other is the taunt below. The two are deliberately paired — the spell scroll is the "newfound strength" the taunt refers to. Whoever wrote this wanted a Good-aligned finder to read the taunt, feel the dare, and use the *Strength* to "finish the job" on the staked horror — sealing the Prince forever. **This note is the bait of the whole trap.**
>
> Written roughly ten years ago by the Temple scribes who hid the Prince — collective, anonymous, signed only with the golden skull. They never expected the right kind of hero to find it. That is why it is so pleased with itself.
>
> **Tone for read-aloud:** a sermon delivered to an empty room. Smug, unhurried, theologically cruel — the voice of someone savoring a joke they set a decade ago and may never see land. Read it slowly enough that the dare lands *before* the party realizes it is a dare.

---

```
                          Goodmonth, 569 CY
              In the Year of the Sleeping Throne

  To the bold and the bright, the clean of hand and the
  pure of purpose ─ should any such ever stand where you
  now stand:

  Welcome. You have come far, and down, and in the dark,
  and we know what you are by the smell of you. You are
  one of the GOOD ones. We made this room for you.

  Look in the box. You will find a horror there ─ a thing
  of night, fanged and foul, pinned through its black
  heart as your kind pins such things. We could not end
  it. We confess this freely. We lacked the power. The
  stake holds it; the cross binds it; and there it has
  lain, patient as patience, waiting for hands holier
  than ours to do what we could not.

  That is your office, is it not? To seal away the dark.
  To prove, by the sealing, that you are what you say you
  are. We have even left you the means ─ strength, freely
  given, in the second leaf of this case. Take it up.

  Let the Good ones use their newfound strength to seal
  in the horror of darkness, to prove their faithfulness
  to right and justice. We who wrote this lacked the
  power to do so.

  Burn it. Bind it. Bury it deeper. Walk away clean and
  righteous and certain, as your gods would have you be.

  We will be laughing. We are laughing now, as we write,
  ten years before your torchlight ever finds this ink.
  Faithfulness is such a reliable blade. One need only
  hand it to the faithful and point.

                                        ─ The Faithful
                                    [a golden skull, stamped in wax]
```

---

> **What this reveals — player-side (what they can work out):**
>
> - **Someone wanted Good heroes to "seal the horror."** That is *backwards.* Evil does not beg paladins to destroy its vampires. "Let the Good ones use their newfound strength to seal in the horror of darkness" — read suspiciously, this is a dare designed to make them act. (Insight / a sharp player: *why would the cult want this?*)
> - **The thing in the box is bait, not a prize.** The whole note is built around getting them to "finish" or "seal" the occupant. The harder it pushes, the more a careful party should *stop pushing.*
> - **The date — Goodmonth, 569 CY — is ten years gone.** Whatever is in that coffin has been there a decade, staked and quiet. Vampires do not lie politely staked for ten years in a forgotten room. (The "small detail" that should itch.)
> - **"Strength, freely given, in the second leaf."** The *Strength* scroll is real and usable — and its placement is a tell. They were *meant* to cast it and lay hands on the coffin.
>
> **What this reveals — GM-side (levers it opens):**
>
> - This is the **fulcrum of the set-piece.** A party that takes the note at face value and destroys/seals the "vampire" murders Prince Thrommel and never knows it — the cruelest, most playable failure in the arc. A party that reads the note as a *trap* is rewarded with the doubt that makes them look closer (dispel, detect, true-sight).
> - **Calmer is the intended victim of the joke.** It is aimed squarely at clerics and paladins — at "your office… to seal away the dark." If Calmer's player leans into orthodoxy, the trap nearly springs itself. That tension is the scene; don't defuse it for them.
> - **If they "seal the horror" and leave:** do not correct them at the table. Let them walk out righteous. The reveal comes later — Y'dey, or Falrinth, asking *"and the sleeper? did you bring him?"* — and the dawning horror is the payload. The Prince can be "re-lost" (re-hidden by the Temple, or genuinely destroyed) — your call on whether it's recoverable.

> **GM use:**
>
> - Reveal only when they open the case. Don't volunteer the contents.
> - Read it *before* they pierce the illusion, so the dare and the staked vampire reinforce each other.
> - If a PC moves to cast the *Strength* scroll and physically reseal/destroy the coffin, **do not warn them.** The lifelines are the note's own contradictions and the warnings the handlers planted ("don't trust your eyes down there" / "wake what sleeps"). Let the fiction carry the weight.
> - The single line the cult-scribe ghost of this note "says," if a PC ever throws it back in a captured cultist's face later: *"Oh, that old jape? It has worked more often than prayer has. The righteous are the easiest hands to point at a thing — you need only tell them it is dark."*

## ARTIFACT B — The Sleeper Revealed (heraldry card)

> **GM context:** This is **not a found object.** It is what the party sees the instant the vampire-illusion is stripped away (*true seeing*, *gem of seeing*, *dispel illusion* on the occupant, or *dust of appearance*). Hand it across the table *physically* at that moment — the prop-flip from "a staked monster" to "a crowned man" is the money shot. A PC with heraldry, History, or any Furyondy/Veluna/Knights-of-the-Hart background identifies the arms; otherwise the regalia alone tells them this is no vampire.
>
> **Tone for read-aloud:** reverent, hushed, like a tomb-light catching gold. After you read it, stop talking.

---

```
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                    │
  │   The vampire flickers — and is gone, peeling      │
  │   away like burnt paper.                           │
  │                                                    │
  │   In the sarcophagus lies a HANDSOME MAN in mail,  │
  │   golden-haired, his face at rest. He wears a      │
  │   WHITE SURCOAT, quartered:                        │
  │                                                    │
  │     · the arms of FURYONDY                         │
  │     · the arms of VELUNA                           │
  │     · the red ANTLERS of the KNIGHTS OF THE HART   │
  │                                                    │
  │   A gold belt at his waist, a dagger upon it.      │
  │   At his throat, a gold chain bearing a CROWN      │
  │   AND CRESCENT MOON. A matching ring on his left   │
  │   hand. His shield rests beneath his feet.         │
  │                                                    │
  │   He is not dead. As you watch, he draws one       │
  │   slow breath — and does not wake.                 │
  │                                                    │
  │   ────────────────────────────────────────────    │
  │                                                    │
  │   These are the arms of PRINCE THROMMEL,           │
  │   Grand Marshal of Furyondy, Provost of Veluna,    │
  │   betrothed of her Ladyship Jolene ─ lost, and     │
  │   mourned as dead, these ten years.                │
  │                                                    │
  │   And the "silver cross" in the coffin lid is no   │
  │   cross at all. It is a sword. It has a name.       │
  │                                                    │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

---

> **What this reveals — player-side:**
>
> - **The "vampire" is a man, and a famous one.** The staked horror was an illusion over a living prince in stasis. Everything the mocking scroll dared them to do would have killed him.
> - **The "silver cross" is the sword Fragarach in disguise** — revealed by the same true-sight. If they earlier left it / pried it / ignored it, that now matters (it flies to his hand when he wakes within sight of it — the great visual).
> - **The decade lands.** Two kingdoms buried Thrommel as dead ten years ago. He has been here, breathing, the whole time. The crown-and-crescent device is Furyondy-and-Veluna joined — the marriage that never happened.
>
> **What this reveals — GM-side:**
>
> - The reveal is the *turn* of the whole arc: the political thriller resolves into a rescue with enormous stakes (a living Furyondy-Veluna royal = a holy war against the Temple, exactly what Y'dey hopes for and Falrinth is gambling on).
> - **Identification check, if you want one:** History or a relevant background, DC ~15 — but the regalia (royal arms + crown-and-crescent betrothal device) should let *any* party grasp "this is no vampire, this is a king's man" even on a failure. Don't gate the gut-punch behind a roll; gate only the *name* behind it, and let Y'dey supply the name later if they miss it.
> - Waking him: removed from the chamber he rouses, groggy and amnesiac **1d4 turns**, then cautious and taciturn. First words (cold, regal, disoriented): *"...How long. Tell me plainly — how long was I in the dark? And whose banners fly over Furyondy now?"*

> **GM use:**
>
> - Hold this card back until the illusion is *actually* pierced. If they never pierce it, they never get this card — they get the mocking scroll's outcome instead. The two artifacts are the fork in the scene.
> - Hand it over in silence. Let them read it themselves. Do not narrate over it.
> - The single line Thrommel will say later, if a rescuer asks why the Temple kept him alive rather than killing him: *"A corpse ends a war. A hostage postpones one. They were saving me for a worse day than mine."*
