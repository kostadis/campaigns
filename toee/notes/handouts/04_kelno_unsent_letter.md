# HANDOUT — Kelno's Unsent Letter

> **GM context:** Folded in quarters, tucked between two of the books on Kelno's shelf — not on the table. **The party will only find this if they search the chamber.** Kelno does not hand it over and would not have offered it. If found and read in front of him, his expression closes; he says nothing.
>
> The letter is addressed to a dead apprentice named Marad — the same name labeled on one of the skulls ("Marad — gone to Lolth"). It is dated three weeks ago. It was never sent because Marad was already dead when Kelno wrote it. Kelno keeps writing letters to Marad and not sending them. This is the most recent.
>
> **Tone for read-aloud:** monastic, contemplative, bitter. The same dry exactness as the ledger, but the cracks show. This is who Kelno actually is when he is not negotiating.

---

```
                                            Plant 6, 579

  Marad ─

  You were right about the gnolls. I will not pretend you were not.
  The trouble is that being right and being alive are different
  states and you held only the first.

  I have been keeping count. I have kept your name. You are the
  twenty-third on the shelf. I do not say this to grieve. I say it
  because I want you to know that the accounting continues. I am
  not the kind of man who lets the numbers slip.

  Alrrem will die. I will not be the one to do it but he will die.
  Belsornig will die. He is the more difficult of the two but his
  arrogance is reaching the threshold past which men who hate him
  begin to act. I have arranged for some of the hate.

  I am not a powerful priest. I never was. You knew this and
  pretended otherwise out of kindness. The Elder Eye does not
  whisper to me. I have read the texts and I am familiar with the
  shape that the whispering would take if it occurred, and I am
  certain that it has never occurred to me. I do not have the gift
  the others claim to have. I never did. I was given this office
  because the previous prefect was sacrificed by his brother and
  there was nobody else willing to stand in the gray room and lie.

  I lie well. That is my gift. The gift the temple wanted of me.

  But I am tired of it, Marad. I would like very much to be the
  cause of someone's death other than my own apprentices. I have
  arranged the gnolls and the bugbears and the goblins and they
  have done what I needed but they have not made up the count.

  There are some travelers coming. I have not met them. They are
  not, by the count of their actions, what this place would call
  evil — by which I mean they have killed only the deserving and
  have lied only the necessary amount. I think they will arrive
  in my chamber within the week. I have set out a second cup of
  mead. I would like them to take it. I have prepared what I
  intend to say.

  If they refuse, I will fight. I will not last long. The mace
  is good but the body inside the chain mail is fifty-six years
  old and tired. So I would prefer they accept the cup.

  If they accept, I will let them count for me.

  I miss you. The gnolls were stupid. You were not. The temple
  wastes good apprentices and keeps tired prefects and that is
  the truest thing I have written today.

                                                      ── K.
```

---

> **What this reveals about Kelno (player-side):**
>
> - **He does not have actual divine power.** "I do not have the gift the others claim to have. I never did." Kelno is an *administrator* who got the high-priest seat by elimination. He prays and the rituals work because Zuggtmoy (per the GM-only canon) grants the spells regardless of belief, but he himself has never felt the connection. He has the office because nobody else would take it.
> - **He is grieving.** The skulls are not trophies — they are a memorial. He keeps the count because he failed the people on the shelf.
> - **He is tired.** This is a man at the end. The pitch the party heard from him in his chair is not bravado; it is a man trying to retire alive by attaching himself to the people he judges least bad.
> - **The "second cup" line.** He prepared the cup of mead for them three weeks ago. They have been *the plan* for three weeks. The party should land on this with appropriate weight.
> - **"I will let them count for me."** The killings he wants — Alrrem and Belsornig — he wants the party to do *for* him. Not because he can't (the *dagger of venom* canon line: he's been saving it for one or the other). Because he wants the *count* to belong to someone who deserves to make it.
>
> **What this reveals about Kelno (GM-side):**
>
> - This is the moment Kelno becomes a *character* rather than a faction-mascot. He is the most sympathetic of the four elemental priests by a long mile. The party can treat him kindly without compromising the campaign — they may even genuinely come to like him.
> - The "no actual divine power" thing is a real lever later. If the party ever brings him to Y'dey or to a Cuthbertine cleric for honest evaluation, the verdict — *"this man has been carrying the office without the office's gift"* — opens an interesting redemption path. He could be a *secular* survivor inside the new regime, useful and harmless.
> - **If Kelno dies in the negotiation** (party chooses to execute him after the pitch), this letter — if found — should hit the party hard. Suggested: have the party find it *after* the killing, not before. The retroactive recontextualization is the punishment for the easy choice.

> **GM use:**
>
> - Reveal this only if the party actively searches. Don't volunteer it.
> - If they search and read it before deciding Kelno's fate, the deal-and-spare path becomes much more emotionally weighted.
> - If they kill him first and find it after, leave the table silent for a beat. Don't fill the silence.
> - If they take the deal and bring it up later — *"we found this in your bookshelf, why didn't you give it to us"* — Kelno's answer is the campaign's quietest moment so far: *"Because I wrote it to a man who is dead, and showing it to you would have been asking you to be my friend. I did not have the right to ask that of strangers. Now perhaps I do."*
